## 1. Jurisdictional limits — MAXIMUM AMOUNT YOU CAN SUE FOR  
Rhode Island small claims court handles claims for money damages up to **$5,000** per case ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=Small%20claims%20cases%20are%20handled,The)). This $5,000 cap applies regardless of whether the plaintiff is an individual, a business (e.g. corporation, LLC, partnership), or a government entity – all are bound by the same monetary limit (no higher cap for businesses). The $5,000 limit is **exclusive of interest and court costs**, meaning you can still add applicable interest and allowed costs on top of the $5,000 principal claim ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE10/10-16/10-16-1.htm#:~:text=tax%20for%20money%20only%20not,general%20public%20in%20which%20the)). There is no separate lower cap for certain plaintiffs; for example, unlike some states, Rhode Island does **not** impose a lower small-claims limit on businesses suing consumers – the limit is $5,000 across the board. 

Rhode Island law historically limited small claims to $2,500; however, legislation in **2021** raised the maximum to $5,000 ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE10/10-16/10-16-1.htm#:~:text=P,1%2C%20effective%20July%203%2C%202021)). You cannot circumvent the $5,000 limit by “splitting” a larger claim into multiple suits. If your total claim exceeds $5,000, you generally *must* either abandon (waive) the amount above $5,000 or file in a higher court. Once a small-claims judgment is entered on a cause of action, you can’t file a second case for the leftover amount – the courts will treat that as an impermissible claim-splitting and bar the second suit (your first case **resolves the entire claim**). In practice, if you are owed more than $5,000, you have to choose: limit your claim to $5,000 in small claims (essentially giving up the rest), or file a regular civil lawsuit in District or Superior Court for the full amount. There is no annual limit on the *number* of small claims cases you can file (Rhode Island law doesn’t set a yearly filing cap), but the court can sanction abusive filings. For example, if a plaintiff repeatedly files meritless or duplicative small claims, the court may treat it as an abuse of process ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-FAQ.aspx#:~:text=If%20you%20are%20a%20plaintiff,%E2%80%8B)) ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-FAQ.aspx#:~:text=If%20you%20are%20a%20plaintiff,%E2%80%8B)). However, unlike some states, Rhode Island does not impose a strict numeric limit on how many cases one can file per year. The key is simply that each case independently meets the criteria (and isn’t splitting one claim into pieces). 

## 2. What types of cases qualify — EVERYTHING YOU CAN SUE FOR  
Rhode Island small claims are *only for certain types of disputes*. In Rhode Island, small claims jurisdiction is **narrower** than many states: it is mainly limited to cases **“ex contractu”** (arising from contracts or agreements) and certain consumer transactions. By law, small claims may be used for actions “based on a contract, a retail sale, or services rendered” where the amount sought is $5,000 or less ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=Small%20claims%20cases%20are%20handled,The)). In other words, if someone owes you money under a contract or sale, you likely can sue in small claims. But claims arising from pure **torts** (like accidents or personal injury that aren’t rooted in any contract or sale) are **not** eligible for small claims in Rhode Island. The small claims statute specifically covers contract-based claims, claims for money due from the sale of goods or services to the public, and claims based on negotiable instruments (like checks or promissory notes) – **not** general injury or property damage tort claims ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE10/10-16/10-16-1.htm#:~:text=tax%20for%20money%20only%20not,general%20public%20in%20which%20the)) ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE10/10-16/10-16-1.htm#:~:text=plaintiff%20seeks%20to%20recover%20an,of%20interest%20and%20costs%2C%20the)). Below is an exhaustive list of which types of cases *can* be filed in RI small claims, with examples and special rules, followed by which types **cannot**.

### Contract & money-owed disputes  
- **Breach of written contract:** Yes – small claims covers any civil claim “ex contractu” (from a contract) up to $5,000 ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE10/10-16/10-16-1.htm#:~:text=tax%20for%20money%20only%20not,general%20public%20in%20which%20the)). If someone fails to perform a written agreement or violates its terms causing you financial loss ≤ $5,000, you can sue for those damages. *Example:* You paid a contractor $3,000 under a written home repair contract and they never did the work – you can sue for the $3,000 back. Or if a vendor delivered defective goods under a written purchase agreement and you want a refund of $2,500, that is a proper small claim. **Controlling law:** R.I. Gen. Laws § 10-16-1 allows contract-based money claims in small claims ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE10/10-16/10-16-1.htm#:~:text=tax%20for%20money%20only%20not,general%20public%20in%20which%20the)). No special pre-suit steps are required beyond the general recommendation to demand performance or payment first (though not legally mandated). Just be sure the dispute is truly about a *breach of the contract* (failure to do what the contract requires or doing it improperly causing damage).

- **Breach of oral / handshake agreement:** Yes – oral contracts are just as enforceable as written ones in many situations, and Rhode Island small claims can hear oral contract disputes up to $5,000 ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE10/10-16/10-16-1.htm#:~:text=tax%20for%20money%20only%20not,general%20public%20in%20which%20the)). *Example:* You lent your friend $1,000 based on a verbal promise to repay, and they didn’t – that’s an oral contract (a promise for a promise), and you can sue for the $1,000. Another example: You agreed verbally to pay a painter $500 to paint a room, but they never showed up – you could sue to get any deposit back or damages from having to hire someone else. Make sure you have some evidence of the agreement (texts, emails, witnesses, part performance) since nothing was written. Oral agreement cases are eligible as long as they are essentially contract claims for money owed.

- **Unpaid personal loans (friends/family IOUs):** Yes – a loan is essentially a contract (the borrower’s promise to repay in exchange for the loaned money). If you loaned someone money and they haven’t repaid as agreed, you can sue in small claims for up to $5,000. *Example:* You loaned an acquaintance $800 cash and they promised verbally to pay back in a month but didn’t – you can bring a small claim for $800. It’s wise to have any proof (a signed IOU, a text message acknowledging the loan, etc.). Rhode Island doesn’t require any extra steps for loan collections beyond what’s needed for any contract claim.

- **Unpaid promissory notes:** Yes – a promissory note is a written promise to pay (essentially a written loan agreement), which is a contract. Small claims will handle suits to collect on promissory notes up to $5,000. *Example:* You sold a used car and the buyer signed a promissory note to pay you $200/month, but they stopped paying with $1,500 remaining – you can sue for the $1,500 balance due on the note. Because a promissory note is a negotiable instrument, it falls squarely under small claims jurisdiction ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE10/10-16/10-16-1.htm#:~:text=plaintiff%20seeks%20to%20recover%20an,of%20interest%20and%20costs%2C%20the)). There’s no special procedure beyond filing the claim; just attach a copy of the note to your suit if you have it.

- **Unpaid commercial invoices / accounts receivable:** Yes – if you own a small business and a customer or client owes money for goods delivered or services rendered (especially if evidenced by invoices or an account statement), you can use small claims as long as the amount is ≤ $5,000. *Example:* A freelancer issued an invoice for $2,000 of work completed and the client never paid – this is essentially a breach of contract for services, which is allowed ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=Small%20claims%20cases%20are%20handled,receive%20a%20judgment%20for%20money)). Or a supplier sold $4,500 in goods to a customer on account and wasn’t paid – a claim for the price of goods sold is eligible. In Rhode Island, such open accounts and debts for sales/services are explicitly within small claims jurisdiction (they fall under “contract or retail sale” claims). There are no special prerequisites, though sending a final demand or invoice copy before suing is prudent. 

- **Unpaid rent (residential or commercial):** Yes – a lease is a contract, so claims for unpaid rent or other sums due under a lease can be brought in small claims if the amount is within $5,000. *Example:* A landlord is owed two months’ back rent of $1,800 after a tenant moved out – the landlord can sue in small claims for the $1,800. Both residential and commercial rent claims are allowed *for money only*. **Important:** Small claims can only award money – it **cannot be used to evict a tenant or regain possession** (that requires a separate eviction proceeding in District Court’s landlord/tenant docket, not small claims). So a landlord could sue for **unpaid rent or damage** after the tenant is out, but not for an eviction order. Likewise, a tenant could use small claims to recover money (like a security deposit) but not to force repairs or regain possession. For rent claims, there’s no special notice required beyond any notice the landlord already gave during tenancy; once the tenancy is over or rent is clearly in arrears, the landlord may proceed to small claims for the money. (For eviction, Rhode Island has a separate process with required notices; but those go to eviction court, not small claims.)

- **Bounced / dishonored checks:** Yes – Rhode Island small claims explicitly covers actions based on a **negotiable instrument**, which includes checks ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE10/10-16/10-16-1.htm#:~:text=plaintiff%20seeks%20to%20recover%20an,of%20interest%20and%20costs%2C%20the)). If someone gave you a check that bounced (NSF – non-sufficient funds), you can sue for the amount of the check and any bank fees you incurred. *Example:* A customer paid by check for a $300 item, the check bounced, and they haven’t made good – you can file a small claim for $300 + any bank charge (e.g. a $20 returned check fee). **State-specific quirk:** Some states allow suing for multiples of the check amount as a penalty; Rhode Island does **not** have a special treble-damages statute for bad checks in small claims. Your recovery is generally limited to the check’s value plus actual costs (e.g. bank fees). (The maker of a bad check can also face criminal penalties, but those are pursued by police/DA separately; as a civil matter you’re just getting the money owed.) It’s wise (though not required) to send a written demand to the check writer giving them a short time to pay up before suing – this sometimes prompts payment and, if not, it documents your attempt. But there’s no statutory demand-letter requirement for NSF checks in Rhode Island.

- **Open book accounts / running tabs:** Yes – if you have an open account or tab where someone has accumulated charges (for goods or services) and they haven’t paid, that’s a form of contract debt. Rhode Island small claims will handle these as long as the amount due is ≤ $5,000. *Example:* A local hardware store had an arrangement to let a contractor buy materials on credit (a running account) and the contractor now owes $3,500 on account – the store can sue in small claims for that amount. Such claims often fall under “goods sold and delivered” or “services rendered” which are explicitly within small claims scope ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=Small%20claims%20cases%20are%20handled,receive%20a%20judgment%20for%20money)). Just be prepared to show the account statements or ledger of charges and payments.

- **Co-signer / guarantor disputes:** Yes – if you co-signed a loan or guaranteed someone’s debt and *you* ended up paying it (or the creditor is coming after you), small claims can be used between co-signers to recover what’s owed. For instance, if you co-signed a $4,000 car loan and the primary borrower defaulted, and you had to pay the bank, you can sue the primary borrower for the $4,000 you paid on their behalf (since by law you have a right to reimbursement – an “ex contractu” claim implied by the co-signer arrangement). Or if you guaranteed a lease and had to cover rent, you could likewise sue the tenant. These are contract-based rights (either express or equitable subrogation) and are allowed in small claims. Make sure to have proof of your payments and the agreement that made you a guarantor. (Note: If the creditor itself is suing the guarantor, that’s just a normal debt collection which also can be in small claims if ≤ $5,000.)

### Consumer disputes  
- **Refunds for defective products:** Yes – if you bought a product (goods) as a consumer and it turned out defective or not as promised, you can sue the seller for up to $5,000 in small claims. A product sale is a contract, and Rhode Island allows small claims for damages “resulting from a retail sale of tangible personal property to a member of the general public” ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE10/10-16/10-16-1.htm#:~:text=tax%20for%20money%20only%20not,general%20public%20in%20which%20the)). *Example:* You purchased a new appliance for $1,200 and it never worked properly, and the store refuses to refund or replace – you can sue for your $1,200 back (or the cost of repair, etc.). Another example: You bought a used car “as is” for $4,000 and it immediately had major undisclosed problems; while an “as is” sale limits some claims, if the seller breached an express warranty or engaged in misrepresentation, you could sue for the repair costs or diminished value (say $1,500) as damages. Rhode Island’s consumer protection laws (R.I. Gen. Laws § 6-13.1, the Unfair Trade Practice Act) allow consumers to recover actual damages (which could be doubled or trebled for willful violations) ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/BillText14/HouseText14/H8104A.htm#:~:text=admissible%20in%20any%20subsequent%20criminal,or%20services)), and you can bring such a claim in small claims **if it’s within the $5,000 limit**. (However, note that if a statute entitles you to double or treble damages that would put the total over $5,000, you’d have to either waive the excess or file in a higher court – small claims won’t award more than $5,000 total even if a law might technically permit it.) No special pre-filing notice is required under Rhode Island’s consumer protection law (unlike some states that mandate a demand letter), so you can proceed directly if the seller won’t voluntarily remedy the issue.

- **Refunds for services not performed or done poorly:** Yes – paying for a service implies a contract that the service will be provided with reasonable care or as agreed. If you paid for a service and the provider failed to perform or performed so poorly that it’s essentially worthless, you can sue to get your money back (or the cost to fix their work). *Example:* You paid a wedding photographer a $1,000 deposit, but they never showed up – you can sue for the $1,000 deposit. Or you hired a lawncare company for the season at $600 and they only came once – you can claim a refund for the unused portion. These fall under “services rendered” in the small claims statute ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=Small%20claims%20cases%20are%20handled,receive%20a%20judgment%20for%20money)). Be clear on the amount of loss – usually the amount you paid, or the cost to hire someone else to redo the job properly (up to $5k). There’s no special state-required notice, but sending a demand outlining the failings and requesting refund is a good idea to document the dispute.

- **Auto repair overcharges or shoddy work:** Yes – disputes with mechanics and body shops are common small claims. If a mechanic did unauthorized work, overcharged beyond the estimate, or did poor quality work causing you loss, you can sue for the overcharge or cost of corrective repairs. *Example:* A mechanic quoted $500 but charged $1,000 without approval – you can sue for the $500 difference. Or if you paid $800 for a repair that was done incorrectly, causing engine damage costing $2,000 to fix, you might sue for that $2,000 in damages. These claims are essentially breach of the service contract or possibly a breach of warranty on the workmanship. Rhode Island’s Motor Vehicle Repair Act (if any) may have protections, but even without a specific statute, basic contract law covers this. Be sure to have evidence (invoices, any written estimate, perhaps a second mechanic’s report on the shoddy work). No special filing requirements. If fraud was involved (e.g. charging for parts not actually replaced), that could also be a claim (often under consumer protection laws), still filed in small claims if under $5k.

- **Home repair/contractor disputes (incomplete work, defects, abandonment):** Yes – if you hired a contractor or handyman and they either didn’t finish the job or did it improperly, you can seek the amount it will cost you to have the work completed or redone (or a refund of what you already paid, up to $5,000). *Example:* You paid a contractor $4,500 to remodel a bathroom, but they only did demo and disappeared – you can sue to get your $4,500 back. Or the contractor’s work was so substandard that it’ll cost $3,000 to fix – you could sue for that $3,000. These are service contract breaches squarely within small claims ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=Small%20claims%20cases%20are%20handled,receive%20a%20judgment%20for%20money)). Rhode Island does require contractors to be registered and bonded for larger jobs (under the Contractors’ Registration and Licensing Board), and there may be an administrative complaint process as an alternative – but you are *not* required to go through the board to use small claims if you just want your money. (However, if the amount is large, the Board might be better or required; at the small-claims level it’s usually fine to proceed in court.) No special pre-suit notice beyond perhaps giving the contractor a chance to cure (which is often good practice to show reasonableness).

- **Appliance/electronics warranty failures:** Yes – if an appliance or electronic item fails and the seller or manufacturer won’t honor a warranty or refund, you can sue for the cost of repair or replacement (whichever is appropriate) up to $5,000. *Example:* You bought a new TV for $1,200 with a one-year warranty, it died after 3 months, and the store/manufacturer refuses to fix or replace it – you can sue for the $1,200 (or the cost of repair if you had to pay one). This is effectively a breach of the warranty contract. Federal law (Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act) allows consumers to use small claims for warranty enforcement, and Rhode Island small claims can grant a money judgment for the value of the lost product or repair. Be sure to have proof of purchase and any warranty terms. 

- **Cancelled or no-show service providers (e.g. photographers, caterers, DJs, movers):** Yes – if you paid a deposit or full amount for a service tied to an event/date and the provider bailed or didn’t show, that’s breach of contract. You can sue for what you paid and any additional costs you incurred to find a last-minute replacement, as long as the total is ≤ $5k. *Example:* You gave a wedding caterer a $2,000 deposit and they canceled a week before the event and won’t refund – you can claim the $2,000 (and maybe any extra you had to pay another caterer on short notice, if within the cap). Or a moving company never showed on moving day and you had to hire last-minute labor for $500 – you could sue the original mover for any deposit plus extra costs. No special notice rules; just standard contract claim.

- **Dry cleaning or property loss/damage in custody (e.g. at hotels, valets, repair shops):** Yes – when you give property to someone for service or safekeeping (bailee situations) and it’s lost or damaged, you can sue for the value (up to $5k). *Example:* A dry cleaner lost your suit worth $300 – small claim for $300. Or a valet parking service returned your car with $1,000 in damage – you can sue for the $1,000 repair cost (assuming they deny liability otherwise). These claims can be framed as breach of contract (implied contract of bailment) or negligence, but since they arise from the service relationship, Rhode Island’s small claims likely will hear it as part of the service rendered to the public ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE10/10-16/10-16-1.htm#:~:text=tax%20for%20money%20only%20not,general%20public%20in%20which%20the)). Just be prepared to prove the item’s value and that the defendant had possession and caused the loss. (Note: Some tickets or receipts have fine print limiting liability – those can complicate things but you can still bring the claim and let the court sort out enforceability of those disclaimers.)

- **Gym/subscription/membership cancellation refunds:** Yes – if you canceled a gym membership or other subscription per the contract terms and the company failed to provide a refund you’re entitled to, you can sue for that amount. *Example:* You paid a yearly membership of $600 up front, canceled after a month following the contract’s procedure (e.g. within a trial period), but no refund was issued – you can claim the refund due. Rhode Island law regulates health club contracts (e.g. R.I. Gen. Laws § 5-50) but you don’t need to invoke specific statutes in small claims; it’s sufficient to show you followed cancellation terms and are owed money. Similarly, if a service auto-renewed without proper notice and won’t refund, that can be a small claim. Rhode Island’s consumer protection might cover improper auto-renewals, but again the court’s focus will be the money unjustly kept. Provide copies of contracts, cancellation notices, etc. No special pre-suit steps beyond giving them a chance to honor the refund.

- **Auto dealer/used-car disputes (lemon law, odometer fraud, undisclosed defects):** Possibly – these can be complex, but if the claim is essentially for money under a contract or warranty and within $5k, you can use small claims. Rhode Island’s **Lemon Law** (R.I. Gen. Laws § 31-5.2) generally applies to new cars and may require arbitration with the manufacturer for buyback – you likely wouldn’t use small claims for a true “lemon law” repurchase because new car prices exceed $5k and the law has its own process. But for used cars or smaller auto issues: if the dealer made specific promises or there was odometer fraud or violation of R.I. Deceptive Trade Practices, you can sue for your damages. *Example:* You bought a used car for $4,500 and the dealer lied about the condition (say, the frame was bent or the odometer rolled back). You could claim the difference in value or cost of repairs in small claims. If the dealer’s conduct violated the consumer protection statute, the court can award your actual damages (and possibly up to triple by statute, but again capped by the small-claims limit unless you waive the excess). These cases might border on fraud (a tort), but since they arise from a **sales transaction**, they fall under “damages from a retail sale” which is allowed ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE10/10-16/10-16-1.htm#:~:text=tax%20for%20money%20only%20not,general%20public%20in%20which%20the)). There may also be a Rhode Island Used Car Lemon Law (covering certain used car sales warranties), which if applicable gives you rights to return the car or get repairs – you could enforce those in small claims as a breach of the statutory warranty. No pre-suit requirement beyond any notice to dealer you choose to give.

- **False advertising / deceptive trade practices:** Yes – if a business’s false advertising caused you to purchase something or incur a loss, you can sue for the losses under the Unfair Trade Practice/Consumer Protection law. Rhode Island’s Unfair Trade Practices Act (UTPA) allows a consumer to recover **actual damages** (and if willful, potentially twice or treble damages) ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/BillText14/HouseText14/H8104A.htm#:~:text=admissible%20in%20any%20subsequent%20criminal,or%20services)). In small claims, you’d be limited to $5,000 max. *Example:* A retailer falsely advertised a laptop as having certain features which it did not, and as a result you spent $800 on it – you could sue for a refund of $800 due to the misrepresentation. Or a service provider guaranteed a result they never delivered. Small claims is a proper venue as long as you’re only seeking money. **Note:** UTPA does allow attorney’s fees and punitive damages in some cases, but in small claims you won’t have attorney’s fees (since typically you’re self-represented and attorneys’ fees aren’t applicable), and punitive damages generally aren’t awarded in small claims. You’ll focus on the direct monetary loss. There is no statutory requirement to send a formal demand letter under RI law (unlike Massachusetts’ 30-day demand, for example); you may file without further ado, though documenting the deception and your reliance on it will be key.

### Property damage  
*Important:* Pure property damage claims **not** arising from a contract or consumer transaction are **not eligible** for Rhode Island small claims. For instance, if someone negligently crashes into your fence or a tree falls from a neighbor’s yard causing damage, those are tort claims (not based on a contract) – Rhode Island requires such cases to be filed as regular civil suits (in District Court or Superior Court depending on amount). Small claims in RI is **generally limited to contract-related money disputes**. However, if the property damage is connected to a contract or service relationship, it can fit under small claims. Here are scenarios: 

- **Vehicle collisions (accidents):** *Not eligible in small claims.* A car accident claim is a tort claim for negligence, not a contract or sale. Even if the damages are under $5,000, you cannot file it in RI small claims court – it must be filed as a regular civil case (District Court handles claims up to $5,000 exclusively and up to $10,000 concurrently with Superior Court ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil.aspx#:~:text=The%20District%20Court%20has%20exclusive,of%20the%20defendants%20in%20the)), but it will follow the normal civil procedure, not small-claims procedure). So if another driver hit your car causing $3,000 damage, you have to sue in District Court regular civil docket (or pursue their insurance). **You cannot use small claims for auto accident damages** in Rhode Island. (One workaround: if there was some *contractual* element – e.g. you lent your car to someone under an agreement and they wrecked it – you might frame it as breach of an agreement, but generally accidents = tort).

- **Damage to personal property by another person (general negligence or intentional):** *Not eligible*, unless it’s tied to a contract. For example, if a neighbor broke your window (either accidentally or intentionally), that’s a tort claim (property damage). Rhode Island small claims does not cover general torts. You would need to sue in regular District Court (if under $5k) for negligence or vandalism. Similarly, if someone stole from you or destroyed your property, those are not small-claimable here (again, outside the contract context). 

- **Damage by a tenant to a landlord’s property:** *Yes, potentially*, but usually handled as part of the lease contract claim after move-out. If a tenant leaves rental property with damages beyond normal wear and tear, the landlord’s claim for those damages can be brought in small claims as a breach of the lease (lease typically says tenant must return premises in good condition). *Example:* A tenant caused $2,000 of damage to floors and didn’t pay for repairs – landlord can sue for $2,000 in small claims after tenancy. This is effectively a contract claim (breach of the rental agreement’s terms to care for premises). Often landlords just subtract from the security deposit; if damages exceed the deposit, small claims is appropriate for the balance. (If it’s an ongoing tenancy and you want eviction for damage, that’s separate – eviction court.) Landlords should document the damage and cost to fix. No special prerequisites beyond perhaps an itemized bill or demand to the ex-tenant.

- **Damage by a landlord to a tenant’s property:** Generally *not eligible* unless you can tie it to a contract duty. If a landlord negligently or intentionally damaged a tenant’s belongings (say during an illegal eviction or failure to fix a leak that ruined your stuff), that is essentially a tort or possibly a violation of landlord duties. You could attempt to frame it as breach of the implied contract of tenancy (e.g. covenant of quiet enjoyment) or a violation of specific landlord-tenant statutes. It might be safer to pursue in a regular civil suit, but if the amount is small (under $5k), you could try small claims by characterizing it as money owed under the lease relationship. For instance, Rhode Island law implies a covenant that the landlord won’t negligently damage your property; a breach of that could be argued. However, caution: small claims judges might dismiss it if it looks purely like a tort. If you’re a tenant in this scenario, also consider a complaint under Rhode Island’s Residential Landlord-Tenant Act in District Court housing session. In sum, this kind of claim is borderline for small claims – proceed with clarity that it’s about the landlord failing contractual/ statutory obligations, not just a random tort.

- **Tree/fence/boundary disputes resulting in damage:** *Not eligible in small claims.* These are typically tort or property-law claims (e.g. negligence, nuisance, trespass). If a neighbor’s tree fell onto your property and caused $4,000 damage, that is not based on any contract between you and the neighbor, so it’s outside RI small claims jurisdiction. Such cases belong in the regular civil court. Similarly, disputes about property lines, encroachments, etc., cannot be adjudicated in small claims (and often require injunctive relief or declarations, which small claims cannot grant).

- **Pet damage (dog bites or pet destroys property):** *Not eligible as a small claim* if it’s just based on the act of the pet, because that’s a tort (owner liability for pet’s actions). Example: Your dog bites someone ($2,000 medical bills) or their dog damages your rug – those are not contract claims. These would be handled as regular civil personal injury or property damage suits, not in small claims. The only way a pet-related claim would fit small claims is if it’s linked to a service: e.g. you paid a pet sitter and due to their negligence your pet was injured (then you might sue the sitter for vet costs as a service breach), or you paid for dog training that was not provided. But **injury caused by animals** themselves is not within the small claims category in RI.

- **Damage during a move (by movers or by friends helping):** If you hired professional movers and they broke or lost your items, yes – that can be a small claim because you had a service contract with the movers. *Example:* Paid movers $800 and they badly damaged your furniture causing $1,500 in losses – you can sue the moving company for $1,500 in small claims (breach of contract/bailment). Many moving companies limit liability in the contract (e.g. by weight), but you can still bring the claim; the judge will consider any enforceable limitations. Since it’s tied to the service you purchased, it’s within jurisdiction ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE10/10-16/10-16-1.htm#:~:text=tax%20for%20money%20only%20not,general%20public%20in%20which%20the)). On the other hand, if friends volunteered to help you move and dropped your TV – there’s no contract (just a favor), so you cannot sue in small claims (you’d technically have a tort claim for negligence, but not a contract). 

### Landlord–tenant (financial only — possession/eviction is separate)  
Small claims is often used for **post-tenancy money disputes** between landlords and tenants. Remember, *eviction cases* (to recover possession) are handled in a different process in District Court and **cannot** be done in small claims. But if the issue is purely money (unpaid rent, deposit refunds, damages), small claims is a common forum, provided the amount is ≤ $5,000. Key landlord-tenant money issues in RI:

- **Unreturned security deposits:** Yes – this is one of the most frequent small claims nationwide. Rhode Island landlords must return a residential security deposit (with an itemized deduction statement if applicable) *within 20 days* after the end of tenancy ([www.hemlane.com](https://www.hemlane.com/resources/rhode-island-security-deposit-laws#:~:text=match%20at%20L124%2020%20days,address%20provided%20by%20the%20tenant)) ([www.hemlane.com](https://www.hemlane.com/resources/rhode-island-security-deposit-laws#:~:text=20%20days%20after%20the%20end,address%20provided%20by%20the%20tenant)). If the landlord *wrongfully withholds* all or part of a deposit, the tenant is entitled to **double the amount wrongfully withheld**, plus court costs and reasonable attorney’s fees ([www.hemlane.com](https://www.hemlane.com/resources/rhode-island-security-deposit-laws#:~:text=landlord%20wrongfully%20withheld%20any%20portion,double%20the%20amount%20wrongfully%20withheld)). For example, if your $1,000 deposit wasn’t returned and the landlord had no valid reason to keep it, you can sue for $2,000 (double) under R.I. Gen. Laws § 34-18-19. The small claims court can and does award that penalty to tenants ([www.hemlane.com](https://www.hemlane.com/resources/rhode-island-security-deposit-laws#:~:text=landlord%20wrongfully%20withheld%20any%20portion,double%20the%20amount%20wrongfully%20withheld)). Be aware: to trigger the double-damages, the withholding must be in “bad faith” or without good cause – essentially the court must find the landlord had no legitimate reason. The tenant does **not** need to send a separate demand letter; the law imposes the double penalty automatically if the court finds wrongful withholding after 20 days. Just ensure you provided a forwarding address (if you didn’t, the landlord’s duty is still to make reasonable efforts, but providing it helpfully avoids disputes). If only part of the deposit was wrongfully kept, double damages apply only to that portion ([www.hemlane.com](https://www.hemlane.com/resources/rhode-island-security-deposit-laws#:~:text=landlord%20wrongfully%20withheld%20any%20portion,double%20the%20amount%20wrongfully%20withheld)). Small claims is ideal for these because deposit amounts are usually under $5k. Bring evidence of when you moved out, the deposit amount, and any communications. (Note: Rhode Island also requires landlords to pay **5% annual interest** on residential security deposits, payable to the tenant once a year or at tenancy end ([www.hemlane.com](https://www.hemlane.com/resources/rhode-island-security-deposit-laws#:~:text=So%20in%20summary%2C%20Rhode%20Island,annual%20interest%20on)) ([www.hemlane.com](https://www.hemlane.com/resources/rhode-island-security-deposit-laws#:~:text=20%20days%20after%20the%20end,address%20provided%20by%20the%20tenant)). If your landlord didn’t pay required interest, you can claim that too, though it’s often small dollars.)

- **Refund of prepaid last month’s rent:** Yes – if you paid first and last months’ rent in advance and then, for example, moved out early or otherwise the last month wasn’t used or was double-paid, you can claim a refund. Often this ties into security deposit accounting. If a lease terminated early or you were overcharged, small claims can handle it as a contract refund. 

- **Habitability / repair-and-deduct counterclaims:** *Scenario:* The tenant withheld rent or paid for repairs because the landlord failed to maintain the property. Technically, under the RI Residential Landlord-Tenant Act, a tenant can do “repair and deduct” or withhold for serious habitability issues after notice. If a landlord sues the tenant for unpaid rent (in eviction court or afterwards in small claims), the tenant’s defense or counterclaim for breach of the landlord’s duties (e.g. no heat, so value was less) could be heard. However, small claims might not be the initial forum – habitability issues are usually raised in eviction proceedings or as a counterclaim if the landlord sues for rent in small claims. If a tenant instead preemptively wants to recover money they spent on repairs due to landlord’s breach, they could initiate a small claim for that expense. *Example:* The landlord ignored a broken furnace, so tenant paid $300 to fix it and wants reimbursement. The tenant can sue for $300 in small claims as damages for landlord’s breach of the lease’s implied warranty of habitability. This is allowed because it’s essentially enforcing a duty under the rental contract. Provide evidence of the condition, notice given, and repair costs. Keep in mind, this overlaps with housing code enforcement – but those do not yield money judgments, so small claims is appropriate for the money part. 

- **Unpaid rent or damages (landlord against former tenant):** Yes – after a tenant moves out, a landlord can sue for any unpaid rent owed under the lease and any property damage beyond normal wear, as long as the total is ≤ $5,000. *Example:* Tenant skipped out owing 2 months’ rent ($1,600) plus left $1,200 in damages beyond the $800 deposit – landlord could sue for $2,000 (rent + damages minus deposit applied). These are standard contract damages. Landlord should itemize the damages and give credit for any deposit already held (to avoid being penalized for wrongful deposit withholding). Landlords in RI cannot get *double* damages for property harm – only tenants get double for deposit violations – but the landlord can recover actual repair costs. No special notice requirement (the security deposit statute doesn’t prevent the landlord from suing; it just penalizes them for not following procedure). If the amount exceeds $5k, landlord would need to go to regular court or waive some amount.

- **Wrongful retention of tenant’s property after move-out:** If a landlord unlawfully keeps a tenant’s personal belongings or throws them out contrary to law, the tenant might have a claim. This could be seen as **conversion** (a tort), but since it arises from the tenancy, a tenant could argue it’s covered by landlord-tenant contract obligations. Rhode Island law (Residential Landlord Tenant Act) has procedures for abandoned property, etc. If a landlord violates those and causes loss, the tenant could try small claims for the value of items (e.g. landlord locked you out and kept your furniture). However, because it sounds like a property tort, success may vary. Still, given it’s related to the lease, a small claims magistrate might hear it. For a clear-cut example: landlord agreed to hold your belongings for a week after you moved but then disposed of them – you could claim that was a breach of an agreement. No guarantee in small claims if framed wrong, but there’s a reasonable chance as a contract/bailment issue.

- **Dispute over fees (cleaning fees, late fees, pet fees, lock/key fees, parking fees):** Yes – if the lease had certain fees and one party disputes them, those are contractual obligations. Landlord could sue for unpaid accrued late fees (though courts often scrutinize if they’re enforceable), or a tenant could sue to recover a fee improperly charged or taken from a deposit. *Example:* Landlord deducted a $200 “cleaning fee” from your deposit but the unit was left clean – you can sue for that $200 if it was wrongful (again under deposit law, likely winning double $200 if truly improper). Or if a tenant never paid required pet fees and landlord wants to recover them, that’s a claim. These hinge on the lease terms and reasonableness (some excessive penalties might not be enforceable by the court). But small claims can adjudicate the amounts and decide if the fee was owed or not. 

### Employment & wages  
Wage disputes are often ideal for small claims, but note: Rhode Island’s Department of Labor and Training (and federal DOL) provide administrative avenues for wage claims. Still, a worker (especially not highly paid, given the $5k cap) can choose small claims for quick relief on straightforward wage issues. Also be aware of any penalty wage statutes.

- **Unpaid final paycheck / earned wages:** Yes – if your employer (or former employer) didn’t pay you wages you’re owed (hours worked, last paycheck, etc.), you can file a small claim for those wages (up to $5,000). Rhode Island law requires employers to pay employees timely; failure to do so gives the employee a right to wages plus possibly penalties. The RI Department of Labor can take complaints, but you are *allowed* to sue directly in court for your wages. *Example:* Your final paycheck of $800 was never delivered – sue for $800. Or your employer routinely shorted you 5 hours per week and it adds up to $2,000 – you can claim that. Rhode Island, like federal law, allows possible **wage penalties**: R.I. Gen. Laws § 28-14-20 provides that an employer who fails to pay wages could be liable for an additional **double the amount in damages** plus attorneys’ fees in a civil action. A small claims court can award such statutory damages (again, mindful of the $5k cap). So if you’re owed $1,000 in wages, the court might award $2,000 total under the law for willful nonpayment. (If that pushes over $5k, you’d have to forego the excess or use a different court.) **Note:** You do not have to file a wage complaint with the state first – you can go straight to small claims. Just ensure you have evidence of hours worked and pay rate. No special pre-suit notice is required, though sometimes a demand letter citing the law may spur payment or strengthen a willfulness claim if ignored.

- **Unpaid commissions or bonuses:** Yes – provided you have an agreement (even if implied by company policy) that you earned a commission or bonus, you can claim it as wages owed. *Example:* A sales employee earned $3,000 in commissions per a plan, but after leaving the company the employer refused to pay – you can sue for that $3,000. Rhode Island’s wage laws include commissions in many cases. Be sure to clarify the commission was earned (e.g. sale closed, you met quota) according to the plan. If the dispute is over whether the performance targets were met, you’ll need evidence. But fundamentally it’s a contract/wage claim and fine for small claims. (If it’s an especially complicated formula or requires extensive evidence, the small claims court might struggle, but for moderate complexity it should be fine.)

- **Unreimbursed business expenses:** Yes – if your employer owes you reimbursement for expenses (mileage, supplies, travel) under a company policy or agreement, you can sue for those amounts. *Example:* You spent $600 on work travel (with approval) and the company never paid you back – that’s a straightforward claim. It’s essentially a contract/implied contract claim (you expended funds on employer’s behalf). Keep receipts and any company policy or email approving it. No special labor agency involvement needed for expense reimbursement; it’s not exactly “wages,” so the state DLT might not handle it, but a court will as a contract debt.

- **Misclassification disputes (e.g. you were treated as contractor but should be employee):** Small claims can’t *decide your status* for tax or labor law purposes, but it can award money owed that stems from it. For example, if you were misclassified and therefore the company didn’t pay you overtime that an employee would get, you could sue for the overtime pay under wage laws. The misclassification angle would be part of proving you were effectively an employee entitled to overtime. This might be somewhat complex for small claims (which lacks formal discovery), but it’s possible for simpler situations. Alternatively, you might go to the RI DOL for a ruling. In any event, small claims can only give money, not a determination of status beyond what’s needed to decide the money claim. 

- **Note on Labor Department vs. court:** Rhode Island’s Department of Labor and Training, Wage & Hour Division, is a resource for wage complaints – often without needing a lawyer. It might be cheaper and yield penalties. However, it can take time. Many employees use small claims for speed and because a court judgment can include statutory damages and interest. It’s your choice; you are not required to exhaust administrative remedies for wage claims (except certain specific scenarios like if it’s a union matter or already in process with DOL). If your goal is just money and it’s under $5k, small claims is a good route, especially if you have clear documentation.

### Personal services / professional disputes  
These involve paying someone for a personal service and being dissatisfied or not getting what was promised. They are usually contract claims: you paid for X service and it wasn’t delivered properly, so you seek a refund or damages.

- **Disputes with hairdressers, barbers, salons (botched service):** Yes – if a salon service goes awry (e.g. hair coloring burns your scalp and you had medical bills, or they ruined your hair requiring costly correction), you can seek monetary damages. This is a service contract claim. *Example:* You paid $200 for a hair treatment that was done improperly and you had to pay another stylist $250 to fix it – you might sue the first salon for $250 (the cost to fix their mistake). Note, however, if you suffered personal injury (like burns or hair loss), that edges into personal injury tort – but you could still frame it as the service was done negligently in breach of contract and claim related costs (small claims won’t handle serious injury claims well, especially if value is above $5k). For purely economic losses or minor incidental medical costs within $5k, it’s feasible. No special rules; just evidence of what went wrong and the cost to rectify.

- **Tutoring, lessons, coaching not delivered:** Yes – if you paid for classes, tutoring sessions, music lessons, etc., and the provider didn’t deliver them or they were far below promised quality, you can sue for a refund or part-refund. *Example:* You paid a tutor $500 for 10 sessions, they only did 3 – you can claim the value of the 7 missed sessions ($350). Or you joined a paid coaching program that did not provide the advertised content – claim back your fee. These are straightforward contract claims. Provide any agreement or advertisement of services and proof of payment.

- **Pet boarding/grooming/veterinary disputes:** If you paid for pet services and something went wrong, yes, you can claim financial loss. *Example:* You paid a kennel $300 to board your dog, and the dog was injured due to their negligence incurring $1,000 in vet bills – you could attempt to recover the vet bills and perhaps refund of boarding fee. Because you had a service contract with the kennel, this fits as a damages-from-service claim. Keep in mind, if a pet is injured or killed, the damages in court are usually limited to economic value/vet bills (emotional distress over a pet’s injury is generally not compensable). For grooming: say the groomer charged $100 but shaved your pet bald against instructions, causing a skin condition – you might get the $100 and vet treatment cost. Vet malpractice cases (if a vet severely harms an animal) can be complex and often exceed $5k; serious ones may need to go to a higher court or through professional complaint channels. But small claims can handle simpler monetary aspects (e.g. refund of a botched procedure cost, or cost to treat an issue caused by the vet if it’s within the limit).

- **Medical billing disputes (when not covered by insurance or surprise billing situations):** Potentially – if you were overcharged or charged for services not provided, and you have a direct agreement with the provider, you could sue for money. Example: a dentist billed you for two crowns but only did one – you could claim the overcharge. However, if it’s an issue of medical malpractice (service done incorrectly causing injury), that’s beyond small claims (and typically requires expert testimony and is above $5k). But straightforward billing errors or a provider’s failure to honor a self-pay discount, etc., can be handled. Also “surprise billing” (if you got an out-of-network charge improperly) now often is handled by law via arbitration, but small claims might see some cases if law doesn’t cover it and patient tries to recoup money paid. Check if specific state/federal protections apply first. 

- **Daycare or preschool refund disputes:** Yes – if you prepaid a daycare or preschool and withdrew your child, and the facility refuses to refund unused tuition contrary to the contract or policy, you can sue for that refund. *Example:* You paid $1,000 for a month, and after one week you had to pull your child out and the daycare promised a pro-rated refund but never paid – claim the $750 balance. Or if the daycare closed suddenly and didn’t refund the remaining days you paid for. This is a contract issue. Provide the enrollment agreement or receipt and any terms on withdrawal. Also, if a daycare lost or damaged some of your child’s property or injured your child (not seriously), small claims could handle the replacement cost or related bills (anything more serious should go through insurance or higher court).

### Loans, debts, and financial  
Many informal financial arrangements between individuals can be resolved in small claims if someone doesn’t pay up. We covered personal loans under contract disputes; here are some other money disputes:

- **Recovery of money lent to a friend or family member:** Yes – as noted, a loan is enforceable in small claims. Just repeat: document the loan if possible (even a text “I’ll pay you back” helps). If the person gave some excuse (e.g. “I’ll pay when I get my tax refund”) you can still sue once a reasonable time has passed or a set deadline passed.

- **Disputes over jointly-paid expenses (roommates, ex-partners, group travel):** Yes – if you and someone agreed to split expenses and they didn’t pay their share, you can claim their portion. *Example:* You fronted $3,000 for a joint vacation for you and a friend who promised to pay you back half ($1,500), but after the trip they didn’t – you can sue for $1,500. Or roommates: you paid the whole utility bill or security deposit and roommate didn’t reimburse their agreed half. These are essentially oral contract agreements to share costs. Small claims often sees ex-roommates hashing out deposit splits, last month’s rent splits, furniture cost splits, etc. Just clearly outline the agreement and proof you paid the whole bill.

- **Disputes over wedding/engagement gifts and rings after cancellation:** These can be tricky, but the common legal rule: an engagement ring is a conditional gift – if the engagement is broken, the ring typically should be returned to the giver (especially if the giver wasn’t at fault, though Rhode Island law on fault may not apply – many jurisdictions treat it as no-fault needed, just return). So if you gave a ring and your ex-fiancée refuses to return it, you could sue for its value (if ≤ $5k) in small claims, framing it as recovery of a conditional gift. Conversely, if you’re the recipient being sued, you might end up a defendant in small claims. Gifts in contemplation of marriage often must be returned if marriage off. Other gifts between partners: generally, a gift (like birthday gift) isn’t recoverable. But if it was agreed to be shared property or a loan, that’s different. These cases sometimes come down to he-said/she-said on intent. Small claims can adjudicate it if the value is within limit. Provide any evidence (receipt for ring, text saying “please return ring”). No special rule; just general contract/gift law.

- **Jointly titled property after a breakup (where state allows in small claims):** If two people co-own an item (say, both names on a car) and one keeps it, small claims cannot change title or order specific performance. However, one owner might sue the other for the value of their share if effectively a buyout is needed. This is complicated for small claims because it edges into equitable relief (which small claims doesn’t do). Often these need to be resolved in a higher court or via settlement. If the amount is small and clearly one paid more, small claims might entertain a reimbursement claim. Proceed with caution; it might get tossed as beyond small-claims scope. For example, if ex-roommates bought a TV together for $600, one kept it, the other might claim $300 – small claims could handle that as effectively a debt owed. That’s straightforward if both agree on the facts. If disputing who should keep what, small claims won’t decide property division.

- **Recovery of cash deposits/earnest money:** Yes – if you put down an earnest money deposit on, say, a vacation rental, or a purchase (that’s not real estate, since real estate deals often exceed $5k and have separate law), and the other party wrongly kept it, you can sue. Example: A hall rental required $1,000 deposit, you canceled in accordance with the contract terms but they refused refund – claim the $1,000. Or a contractor required a $500 deposit and never did any work – get the $500 back (very common scenario, and courts often side with consumer if no work done). These are contract breaches. If the contract said “deposit nonrefundable,” it might not be recoverable unless that clause is unenforceable under circumstances (like if they didn’t suffer any loss or it’s unconscionable). But one can certainly bring the claim and argue fairness.

- **Can a bank, credit card, or collection agency sue a consumer in small claims?** Yes, creditors (including banks and debt collectors) *may* use small claims court to collect debts, but in Rhode Island there’s no prohibition on that. Small claims is part of District Court, which has jurisdiction for any civil case under $5k ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil.aspx#:~:text=The%20District%20Court%20has%20exclusive,of%20the%20defendants%20in%20the)), so a collection agency could file there. In practice, many debt collectors file regular civil suits (especially if using attorneys, they might prefer formal rules or need more than $5k). But if the amount is small, they might opt for small claims for speed. Rhode Island does not bar assignees of debt or collection agencies from using small claims (contrast California, which bars *most* collection agencies from small claims). So yes, a creditor can sue you there. However, if the plaintiff is a **business entity** (like a corp or LLC collection agency), remember the rule that if the entity has over $1 million in assets it must have an attorney (and attorneys generally *cannot* represent parties in the *hearing* of small claims, but a business >$1M wouldn’t be allowed to appear without one). This effectively means large financial institutions might not use small claims because they’d need a lawyer who can’t fully participate at trial (in Rhode Island, attorneys *are* actually allowed to represent parties in small claims if the party is required to have one – see Section 13 below for representation rules). In any event, individuals being sued by a creditor in small claims should know they can raise any defenses (e.g. dispute the debt, ask for proof). 

### Vehicle-specific  
(Some of these overlap with earlier categories like consumer or property damage, but focusing on common vehicle scenarios:)

- **Accident damage where insurance won’t cover or denies claim:** As noted, car accident claims cannot be filed as small claims in RI because they are torts, not contract. So if another driver hit you, small claims is **not** the right venue (even if insurance didn’t pay – you’d file a regular civil suit against the driver). However, if the accident involves a *contractual* element – e.g. you lent your car to someone who wrecked it contrary to an agreement to be careful or to pay for damage, you might attempt to sue them in small claims for the damage cost under a quasi-contract theory. That’s attenuated; likely it would still be seen as deriving from a tort. Best to avoid small claims for typical accident injury or damage recovery.

- **Mechanic/body shop disputes:** Yes, as covered under consumer disputes, those are allowed in small claims (breach of service contract). Sue for cost of improper repairs or losses.

- **Dealership disputes:** Yes, if it’s about money and within $5k (used car sales, warranty promises, odometer, etc., as discussed). Not if it’s seeking to revoke the deal above $5k or anything requiring the car’s return (small claims can’t order the dealer to take back a car, but can award monetary damages). 

- **Towing/impound disputes:** Possibly – if a private tow company unlawfully towed your car or overcharged you, you could sue for the fee back under consumer protection theories. *Example:* A tow company charged $300, but city law says max $150 for that tow, you might claim the overcharge. Or if they damaged your car while towing (that’s property damage tort – tough in small claims because not contract with you unless you called the tow). If your car was towed from private property improperly, RI law (R.I. Gen. Laws § 39-12.1) contains provisions on towing – some states allow statutory damages for wrongful tow, but RI’s law primarily regulates charges and notice. You could use small claims to get a refund of excessive fees or for damage if you argue the tow contract included safe handling (again borderline tort). If your car was impounded by police, different process (likely not small claims at all).

- **Parking damage when you can identify the responsible driver:** If someone dented your parked car and you caught them or have their info, that’s a tort claim (property damage). Unfortunately, as explained, you can’t bring that in RI small claims – it needs to be a regular civil suit. (If the person admitted fault and *agreed* to pay for damages, but then didn’t, you might try to frame it as a breached agreement: e.g. other driver signed a note “I’ll pay your repair cost.” That written promise itself is a contract you could enforce for money. This is a nuanced approach – essentially turn the tort into a contract via their promise. If you have that in writing, small claims could work because now it’s an agreement to pay money.)

### Personal injury (small/minor)  
Most personal injury claims (slip-and-fall, accidents, etc.) are **not** allowed in RI small claims because they are tort-based, not contract. Even if the dollar amount is small, the case doesn’t qualify jurisdictionally. For example, a minor fender-bender causing $1,000 of medical bills still must go through a regular civil lawsuit, not small claims. The only time a personal injury might come into small claims is if it’s entwined with a contract: e.g. you were a customer on a premises and part of your claim is effectively breach of their contractual duty to provide safe service, etc. That’s uncommon. In general, **personal injury cases belong in the regular court system**, regardless of amount, in Rhode Island. (Also, small claims has no mechanism for the detailed evidence and expert testimony often needed in injury cases, and $5k is often too low for the value of even “minor” injuries after medical bills and pain and suffering.) So if you were physically injured, plan to use District Court (if seeking up to $5k or $10k) or Superior Court (for larger claims). One exception: if you want only out-of-pocket losses that are clearly under $5k and *the other side is amenable*, you could theoretically both agree to handle it informally in small claims – but legally, the plaintiff can’t force it into small claims if it’s a tort. 

### Specific statutory claims (state-specific)  
Rhode Island has various statutes that create causes of action or extra damages. Many can be pursued in small claims if the monetary demand is within $5,000. A few notable ones:

- **Bad-check statute multipliers:** As discussed, unlike some states with explicit bad-check penalties (double or triple the amount after 30-day demand), Rhode Island’s main tool is the general civil theft statute (which can award treble damages for conversion of money) or writing a bad check can be pursued criminally. Civilly, you’ll typically just get the amount plus bank fee. Rhode Island does have a civil statute on bad checks in commercial transactions (R.I. Gen. Laws § 6-42 might cover check deception in commerce), but small claims plaintiffs usually just sue for face value. No explicit small-claims-only statute for NSF checks exists.

- **Consumer protection (UDAP) – R.I. Unfair Trade Practice (UTPA):** R.I. Gen. Laws § 6-13.1-5.2 allows a consumer who suffers loss from an unfair or deceptive act to sue for actual damages or $200, whichever is greater, and the court **may** award up to three times actual damages for willful or knowing violations, plus attorney’s fees ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/BillText14/HouseText14/H8104A.htm#:~:text=H8104A%20,or%20services)). You can invoke this in small claims for a consumer transaction problem. If you do, remember you won’t have an attorney (so fees aren’t an issue), and punitive trebling would be within the judge’s discretion. But even a single damages award compensates you. Small claims judges can and do consider UTPA in appropriate cases, but often if it’s a straightforward breach or misrepresentation, they just award the actual loss. If you truly want treble and it’s significant, a higher court might be better, but for under $5k actual losses, small claims is acceptable. No pre-suit demand needed in RI (unlike some states’ consumer laws).

- **Lemon law:** Rhode Island’s new car Lemon Law requires arbitration and is for cars under warranty that have substantial defects – typically you’d get a replacement or refund from the manufacturer, not something handled in small claims. If the amount you seek is small (maybe reimbursement for towing or incidental costs due to a lemon?), you could use small claims, but major lemon law remedies will exceed $5k and go through the proper process. Used car implied warranty law (for dealers) – if a used car under 30 days/1000 miles breaks and dealer won’t honor the limited implied warranty, you could sue for repair costs or to unwind the deal if value is small. But often the value of a car is high, beyond small claims. Check the specific statute (RI’s used car law is under R.I. Gen. Laws § 31-5.4), which might limit you to returning the car for a refund – that’s rescission, which small claims can’t order, so you’d instead sue for the money you paid and offer to return the car (effectively doing rescission via a money judgment). That might be complex for small claims.

- **Identity theft / fraud-related money damages:** Rhode Island has an Identity Theft Protection Act, but that mainly has fines for businesses that leak data, not direct consumer suits. However, if someone fraudulently took money from you (scam) for under $5k, you could sue them for fraud – but since fraud is a tort, it’s not small-claims eligible by default. If you can recast it as breach of contract (e.g. you paid for a product that was never delivered – that’s both fraud and contract breach), you can use small claims on the contract theory. There’s no special statutory penalty in small claims you can invoke for being defrauded; you just get what you lost. 

- **Statutory damages under wage/labor laws:** As mentioned in Employment section, Rhode Island wage statutes allow up to double damages and attorneys’ fees for willful nonpayment of wages ([www.hemlane.com](https://www.hemlane.com/resources/rhode-island-security-deposit-laws#:~:text=landlord%20wrongfully%20withheld%20any%20portion,double%20the%20amount%20wrongfully%20withheld)). You can absolutely request those in a small claim. The court has the power to award the statutory extra damages. For example, R.I. Gen. Laws § 28-14-19, -20 (wage payment law) – small claims is a fine forum for that if wages ≤ $5k. Also, Rhode Island’s minimum wage law and overtime law (which mirror federal FLSA) allow civil actions with possible liquidated damages (usually double pay for willful violations). Those too can be pursued. The small claims court will be familiar with these concepts if you cite the statute.

- **Other examples:** Rhode Island has specific causes of action like the **Rhode Island Deceptive Trade Practices** (which we covered), **fair debt collection practices** (the federal FDCPA allows consumers to sue debt collectors – that must be filed in federal or state general jurisdiction court, but if your damages + statutory $1,000 penalty are under $5k, you could file in state small claims for the $1,000 statutory plus any actual damages. However, note: RI small claims doesn’t allow attorney reps usually, so many FDCPA suits are filed in higher court with counsel. Still, it’s possible pro se in small claims if you’re knowledgeable). **Telephone Consumer Protection Act (robocall)** – that’s federal ($500 or $1500 per call) but can be brought in state courts; a few people have done small claims for robocall penalties if they have details, and that’s permissible if total under $5k. **Security deposit double damages** we mentioned. **Landlord’s failure to provide essential services** – under RLTA a tenant can sue for damages or reduced rental value; small claims could handle that money claim (e.g. you lived without heat for a week, want half a week’s rent back). **Auto odometer fraud** – federal law allows $10,000 minimum damages, so that’s above small claims. **Door-to-door sales 3-day cancellation** – if a seller violated that (R.I. Gen. Laws § 6-28-3), you can get your money back – ideal for small claims if under $5k. In summary, any statutory cause of action that basically says “you can recover a sum of money” and doesn’t mandate a different forum can be pursued in small claims if your demand is within the limit.

### Other state-specific claim types  
*(Additional claim types commonly seen in Rhode Island small claims or allowed by RI law, beyond those listed above):*

- **Auto repair shop storage fees / mechanic’s liens:** If a mechanic holds your car claiming you owe money (mechanic’s lien) and you dispute the amount, you might pay under protest and then sue in small claims to recover the overpayment or improper charges. Rhode Island allows mechanic’s liens on cars for unpaid repair charges (but they must follow certain notice). If you believe you were forced to overpay (to get your car back), a small claim for the difference is possible. This is essentially a contract dispute over a reasonable charge.

- **Utility bill disputes:** If you were over-billed by a utility (electric, gas, etc.), often you’d go through the PUC or their dispute process, but you could bring a claim for money if, say, a cable company charged you for services after cancellation or failed to honor a promotional rate. Those are contract issues – e.g. you might sue Cox Cable for $300 of wrong charges. (Utilities regulated by tariffs might argue the PUC has exclusive jurisdiction on rate disputes, but for straightforward overbilling or breach of contract, small claims can be used by consumers.)

- **Homeowner Association (HOA) or condo fee disputes:** If you live in a condo or HOA and have a dispute over fines or fees (under $5k), you could end up in small claims. HOAs might sue owners for unpaid dues (provided under $5k) – that’s a contract/covenant issue and allowed. Conversely, an owner might sue the HOA for money owed (maybe reimbursement the HOA agreed to pay or damage the HOA caused to your unit). Suits by owners against HOAs for failing to maintain property usually seek injunctive relief (not possible in small claims) or large damages, so not common in small claims. But a simple dispute like “HOA double-charged me $500” is fine for small claims. 

- **Insurance subrogation claims:** If your insurer paid a claim and then sues the at-fault party for under $5k (say for a small auto claim), technically the insurer stands in your shoes. However, because the underlying claim is tort (accident), they shouldn’t use small claims. In practice, insurance companies in RI do not use small claims for subrogation – they go to regular District Court (often filing in the insured’s name anyway). So you won’t usually see that in small claims.

- **Small business vs. vendor disputes:** If you’re a small business and you have a dispute with a supplier or service provider (e.g. you paid for advertising that wasn’t delivered, under $5k), you can file in small claims (the business can appear if meeting the representation rule). Likewise a vendor could pursue a small business client for an unpaid invoice through small claims (less formal than a full case). These B2B small claims are fine as long as it’s contract-based. 

- **Bank account errors or wrongful fees:** If a bank wrongly took fees from your account or a money transfer failed and you lost money (under $5k), you might sue the bank in small claims for the amount. Banks often have arbitration clauses for account disputes – if so, the bank could move to compel arbitration instead of court. But if no arbitration or the bank doesn’t invoke it, a small claim could proceed. It’s contract (account agreement) and allowable. 

- **Bills and debts where you *admit* owing but can’t pay:** If you are on the **defendant** side and you owe money, Rhode Island small claims will still enter judgment against you if the plaintiff proves it. But note: often small claims encourages mediated payment plans. For example, if you owe a medical office $1,200 and they sue, you might agree in mediation to pay $100/month. The court could then incorporate that or note the agreement. If you default, the judgment would then be entered. While this isn’t a *type of claim*, it’s a scenario to be aware of: small claims is commonly used by medical offices, tradespeople, landlords, etc., to get relatively small amounts, and if you can’t really dispute owing it, the advantage is you might get a structured payout or at least put the matter to rest with a judgment.

### Counterclaims  
If you are sued in small claims as a defendant, you **can file a counterclaim** against the plaintiff if you also have a dispute. The counterclaim process in Rhode Island small claims is established by statute: a defendant may file a counterclaim for money. The same $5,000 limit typically applies to counterclaims ([us.vlex.com](https://us.vlex.com/source/14841/chapter/chapter-10-16-small-claims-and-consumer-claims-425197767#:~:text=,Order%20of%20New)). You file the counterclaim in writing with the court (usually by the answer deadline). **Timing:** You generally must file the counterclaim when you submit your Answer (within 20 days of service) ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/FAQs.aspx#:~:text=You%20must%20file%20your%20answer,judgment%20may%20enter%20against%20you)) ([us.vlex.com](https://us.vlex.com/source/14841/chapter/chapter-10-16-small-claims-and-consumer-claims-425197767#:~:text=,Order%20of%20New)). The court provides a form or you can write a brief statement of your counterclaim on the answer form.

- **Counterclaim over the $5,000 limit:** If your counterclaim against the plaintiff exceeds $5,000, Rhode Island law provides a mechanism: the case may be removed out of the small claims process and into the regular civil docket. In particular, if a defendant’s counterclaim **exceeds $5,000**, the small claims statute effectively says the normal rules of District Court apply thereafter ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-12/9-12-10.htm#:~:text=Except%20as%20otherwise%20provided%2C%20in,be%20paid%20by%20the%20clerk)). In practice, the District Court would likely transfer the case to its regular civil calendar (or treat the small claim as if it were a regular civil action) so that the entire dispute (claim and counterclaim) can be resolved together. The defendant would need to pay the regular filing fee difference and possibly the case would proceed with formal rules. The defendant could also **choose** to waive any amount over $5,000 and cap their counterclaim at $5,000 to keep it in small claims. If they don’t waive, the plaintiff (or defendant) can request transfer. Rhode Island statutes encourage keeping it in small claims if possible, but not at the expense of a defendant losing their right to full recovery. So effectively, a counterclaim > $5k will bump the case out of small claims unless trimmed down.

- **What if the counterclaim is unrelated?** In small claims, typically the counterclaim should arise from the same transaction or dispute, but even if not, as long as it’s a claim the defendant has against the plaintiff, the court may hear it (Rhode Island’s small claims rules are less formal about joinder; they likely allow any counterclaim). But practically, most counterclaims are either a defense tactic or related. For example, a landlord sues for rent, tenant counterclaims for property damage due to leak – those will be heard together.

- **Counterclaim procedure:** Use the court’s **Answer/Counterclaim form** (if provided) or write a statement referencing the case. There is *no additional fee* to file a counterclaim in RI small claims (the defendant already doesn’t pay an answer fee; counterclaim is free). Serve it on the plaintiff (the court clerk will usually mail a copy of your answer/counter to the plaintiff). The hearing might be postponed if the plaintiff needs time to prepare for your counterclaim.

- **If counterclaim exceeds plaintiff’s claim:** If a counterclaim is valid and higher, the court doesn’t dismiss the plaintiff’s claim – it either transfers or the plaintiff will have to defend and possibly end up paying the difference if the defendant prevails. If transferred to the regular docket due to size, both parties will get notice and the case proceeds under normal rules (possibly with discovery, etc., and the right to jury trial if demanded).

- **Example:** You’re sued for $4,000 by a contractor for unpaid work, but you believe the work was defective and it will cost $6,000 to fix – you have a counterclaim of $6,000 against them. That’s above $5k. You could either waive the $1,000 and counterclaim for $5,000 (keeping it in small claims), or file for the full $6,000 and expect the case to be removed to regular District Court (where it would be consolidated and you might want a lawyer). It’s a strategic choice.

- **If you counter-sue for more than the cap knowingly:** The court will likely advise removal. If neither party objects, sometimes the magistrate might handle it up to $5k and ignore the rest by consent – but that’s not standard, since the defendant would be forfeiting part of their claim. Generally, the safer course is as described: either waive excess or transfer.

- **Failure to file counterclaim:** If you have a claim against the plaintiff and you do *not* raise it as a counterclaim, you might or might not lose the chance to sue later. In District Court civil procedure, compulsory counterclaim rules could apply. It’s wise to assume you should bring it now if it’s related, to avoid later being barred by res judicata. So don’t hold back a small counterclaim thinking you’ll sue separately – you may be precluded. Bring all related claims now.

### What you CANNOT sue for in small claims (Rhode Island-specific exclusions)  
Rhode Island’s small claims court is **limited to claims for money damages** arising from contracts, sales, or services (plus taxes) – it explicitly does *not* cover many other matters. Here is a list of claims that **cannot** be filed as small claims in Rhode Island (either because of the subject matter or the type of relief needed):

- **Eviction or unlawful detainer:** You cannot use small claims to evict a tenant or otherwise obtain possession of real property. Evictions must be filed as a landlord-tenant action in District Court Housing division, following specific procedures (notice, termination of tenancy, etc.). Small claims can’t grant a judgment of possession or order someone out – it can only award money. If you mistakenly file a small claim trying to evict, it will be dismissed or transferred to the proper eviction docket.

- **Family law matters (divorce, child support, custody, alimony):** Not allowed in small claims. Those belong in Family Court (a completely separate court in RI). Even purely money issues arising from family matters (like child support arrears or property division) cannot be relitigated in small claims – they must go through Family Court enforcement. If, say, an ex-spouse owes you $2,000 under a divorce decree, you enforce it via Family Court contempt, not a small claims suit. Small claims has no jurisdiction over family law or domestic relations.

- **Defamation (libel/slander):** A claim for defamation is a tort claim (reputation harm). It’s not within small claims purview both by subject (tort) and by nature (often seeks more than $5k and is complicated). Even if you kept damages under $5,000 (say you only want $1,000 for slander), you cannot pursue it in small claims in RI – you’d file in regular court. Additionally, defamation cases involve non-monetary elements (like retractions or injunctions often sought, which small claims can’t do). So no defamation suits in RI small claims.

- **Title to real estate:** Small claims cannot determine or affect title to real property. Any dispute over who owns land or interests in land (boundary disputes, adverse possession, enforcing real estate contracts for conveyance) must go to Superior Court (which in RI has exclusive jurisdiction over equitable matters and title matters). Similarly, **foreclosures, liens on property, partition of property** – none of those can be done in small claims.

- **Malpractice (medical, legal, etc.):** Professional malpractice claims typically far exceed $5k and require expert testimony, but beyond that, they are negligence tort claims. So you cannot bring a medical malpractice case in small claims. Even a “small” malpractice (e.g. a simple mistake causing $4,000 extra cost) is not suitable – it’s outside the jurisdiction. Those must go to Superior Court (and medical malpractice in RI has special pre-suit notice and possibly tribunal requirements). Same for legal malpractice or any licensed professional negligence.

- **Federal claims exclusively handled in federal court:** Small claims can’t be used for matters like bankruptcy (which is federal and handled by U.S. Bankruptcy Court) – e.g. you can’t sue someone in small claims for violating a bankruptcy stay or for a dischargeability issue. Also ERISA (benefits), federal civil rights claims (42 U.S.C. §1983) – these typically belong in higher courts. If you have a straightforward claim that also implicates a federal law, you can still bring it if not preempted – e.g. you can sue under federal Telephone Consumer Protection Act in state small claims because Congress allowed state courts to have jurisdiction. But some federal matters are exclusively federal.

- **Class actions or multi-plaintiff suits:** Small claims is designed for individual claims. You cannot file a class action lawsuit in small claims court. Each claim must be an individual plaintiff versus defendant. If a bunch of people have the same claim (e.g. 10 consumers each burned by the same product for $500), each must file separately (or one can file and the others might testify, but you cannot consolidate beyond perhaps the court informally hearing them together if both sides agree). Certainly no formal class certification happens in small claims.

- **Specific performance or injunctive relief:** Small claims cannot order someone to do *or not do* something, only to pay money. So if your goal is to make a contractor finish the work or to force a neighbor to remove a fence, small claims is the wrong forum. Similarly, you can’t get restraining orders, orders to repair property, etc., in small claims. Only a dollar judgment. Sometimes, as a practical matter, the prospect of a money judgment will induce the action (e.g. sue for cost of fence removal and the neighbor might remove it to avoid paying), but the court won’t directly compel the action.

- **Cases against the State of Rhode Island or the United States or certain public entities:** While the statute doesn’t flatly prohibit suing government in small claims, issues of sovereign immunity and jurisdiction come up. You generally cannot sue the federal government in a state court small claims for money damages without following the Federal Tort Claims Act process (which requires filing with the agency, etc., and typically goes to federal court). Suing the State of Rhode Island or a city/town for money damages usually requires compliance with notice of claim statutes and often must be filed in Superior Court. For example, claims for state negligence (like your car is damaged by a state vehicle) require a notice to the Attorney General and have a 3-year limitation ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=match%20at%20L73%20failed%20to,90%20days%20after%20decision)) ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=34,1)). If you tried to do it in small claims without proper notice, it could be dismissed. Moreover, certain claims may be under exclusive jurisdiction of specific courts (e.g. traffic tribunal for some matters, workers’ comp court for job injuries). So as a rule, you cannot just drag the government into small claims unless it’s a routine matter like a small contract the government hasn’t paid (even then, they might remove it or claim immunity). Practically, such suits are not seen in small claims.

- **Workers’ compensation:** Not allowed – work injury benefits are governed by the Workers’ Compensation Court and system. You can’t sue your employer in small claims for an on-the-job injury or for comp benefits; those must go through the comp system. Even wages for time out due to injury are comp issues if injury-related. So anything that properly falls under workers’ comp exclusivity cannot be a small claim.

- **Probate and estate matters:** If someone owes you money from a deceased person’s estate, you must file a claim in Probate Court, not sue the estate in small claims (unless the estate explicitly agreed to pay a debt after probate closed – complicated). So, small claims doesn’t handle will contests, estate administration, or claims against an estate (there’s a probate process for that). However, if an executor or someone explicitly contracted with you (post-death) and failed to pay, that could be a personal claim against that person, which might be allowed if unrelated to estate duty. But generally stick to Probate.

- **Admiralty, maritime, and other special jurisdiction matters:** Not in small claims – e.g. if you have a claim for damage to a boat on navigable waters, that’s maritime law and usually federal or superior court. Small claims doesn’t do maritime liens, etc. Similarly, certain regulatory matters (e.g. appeals of tax assessments, public utilities issues) have their own forums. 

- **Tax disputes:** Actually, *tiny exception:* R.I. Gen. Laws § 10-16-1 includes “any action to recover the amount of any tax for money only not in excess of $5,000” ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE10/10-16/10-16-1.htm#:~:text=%C2%A7%C2%A010)). This suggests that if you as a taxpayer believe you overpaid a state or local tax up to $5k, you could use small claims to recover it (which is unusual). However, many tax disputes require going through tax appeal procedures, so it’s not commonly done. It might be aimed at town taxes or excise taxes. For example, if you accidentally paid a $200 municipal fee twice and the town won’t refund, small claims could be used to get the $200 back. But for actual contested taxes, you normally have to follow statutory remedies (like an appeal to the tax assessor board or Superior Court for larger tax cases). So while the statute lists taxes, use caution – there may be preconditions (the inclusion in the law likely just clarifies that straightforward money claims for taxes can be handled, but in practice, those are rare).

- **Cases where the defendant has bankruptcy protection:** If the person or business you want to sue is in an active bankruptcy (automatic stay) or has received a discharge of that debt in bankruptcy, you cannot pursue them in small claims (or any court) for that debt. The bankruptcy stay/discharge trumps. If you file anyway, the case will be halted or dismissed, and you could face penalties for willfully violating the stay. Always check if the debtor filed bankruptcy. If so, seek relief in bankruptcy court, not small claims.

- **Anything requiring an equitable order or declaratory relief:** Small claims can’t issue injunctions, can’t do declaratory judgments (e.g. declaring who owns what or interpreting a contract for future effect), can’t quiet title, reform a contract, or enforce a lien (beyond granting a money judgment that you can then use to lien property). It only awards money for proven debts or damages.

Rhode Island’s statutes or court rules may also *explicitly* carve out other exclusions. For example, claims under specific acts that mandate arbitration or administrative process first cannot start in small claims. If a law says “before suing under this chapter, you must go to agency X,” then small claims is off-limits until that’s done. One instance: **Human rights/discrimination claims** – you can’t sue for discrimination damages in small claims; those must go through the Rhode Island Commission for Human Rights (and then court). **Public utility billing disputes** – likely must be addressed by the PUC before court. **Cases involving title to personal property (replevin)** – small claims might allow you to sue for the value, but it can’t order return of the item. **Penal fines** – you can’t use small claims to contest a fine or ticket (those have their own tribunals).

In summary, **if it isn’t a straightforward claim for money owed under a contract or statute, small claims is probably not the right venue in Rhode Island.** When in doubt, consult the District Court clerk or an attorney – filing a disallowed case will just waste time and filing fees.

### Damages — what you can and cannot recover in small claims  
Rhode Island small claims can award only **monetary damages**. No injunctions or specific performance (as noted). Within that monetary award, here’s what is and isn’t recoverable:

- **Compensatory damages (actual out-of-pocket loss):** Yes – the core of any small claim is compensatory damages. This includes money you paid and didn’t get the benefit of, cost to repair or replace damaged property, wages lost, etc., as long as these are a direct result of the defendant’s breach or wrongdoing and can be quantified. The judge will expect you to prove the amount by receipts, estimates, bills, or credible testimony. You can also recover incidental costs directly caused by the breach (e.g. the cost of sending certified mail demand, some travel costs if it directly resulted from the issue, etc., at judge’s discretion).

- **Punitive or exemplary damages:** Generally **not available** in small claims. Punitive damages (meant to punish beyond actual loss) are rarely awarded in any Rhode Island court and require egregious conduct and usually a higher court. Small claims judges do not award separate punitive damages. The only exception is when a statute effectively allows a form of punitive-like damages (e.g. double deposit, treble consumer damages, double wage damages) – those are considered **statutory damages** rather than common-law punitive, and *can* be awarded because the statute authorizes them to make the plaintiff whole or encourage compliance. But pure punishment damages without statutory basis are not given. So, do not expect to get something like “$1,000 for distress” or “$5,000 to teach them a lesson” unless a law specifically says so.

- **Statutory multipliers and penalties:** Yes, if applicable. As discussed, Rhode Island statutes provide for multiple damages in certain cases (double for security deposit wrongfully withheld ([www.hemlane.com](https://www.hemlane.com/resources/rhode-island-security-deposit-laws#:~:text=landlord%20wrongfully%20withheld%20any%20portion,double%20the%20amount%20wrongfully%20withheld)), up to treble for consumer willful violations ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/BillText14/HouseText14/H8104A.htm#:~:text=admissible%20in%20any%20subsequent%20criminal,or%20services)), double for willful wage violations, etc.). A small claims court **can** award these, subject to the $5k maximum. The practice is: if your base damages are $X, and a law says you get 2x or 3x, the court could award 2X or 3X *as long as* the total doesn’t exceed $5,000 (or if it does, you essentially are capped at $5,000). For example, say you proved a $3,000 wage nonpayment and willfulness; double would be $6,000, but the court in small claims would likely just give the max $5,000 (or you’d have had to file in a higher court to seek the full $6k). So you effectively cannot get the full benefit of a multiplier if it pushes the sum over the jurisdictional limit – you don’t get an exception to the cap for that. Within the cap, judges often do honor statutory penalties to the extent possible (they might award 2x deposit even if it makes $2,000 on a $1,000 deposit case, since that total $2k is under the cap). So if you have a case with a statutory multiplier, feel free to claim it and cite the statute.

- **Attorney’s fees and court costs:** In Rhode Island small claims, **attorney’s fees** are generally not applicable because parties typically appear without lawyers. Even if you happen to be an attorney representing yourself, you cannot “charge” your own fees to the other side (pro se can’t get attorney fees). The only scenario is if a statute explicitly grants attorney’s fees to the prevailing party (like the consumer protection law or wage law). If you did hire a lawyer (which is uncommon in small claims, and note that in RI small claims, attorneys can participate for certain corporate parties or if the court allows, but generally it’s not routine), you normally wouldn’t recoup those fees in small claims unless the law mandates it. For example, the deposit law allows **“reasonable attorney’s fees”** to a prevailing tenant ([www.hemlane.com](https://www.hemlane.com/resources/rhode-island-security-deposit-laws#:~:text=Additionally%2C%20the%20prevailing%20tenant%20is,to%20court%20costs%20and%20reasonable)); a small claims judge could award, say, a nominal amount or actual documented fee if an attorney helped you file. But since most don’t have counsel, it’s moot. As for **court costs:** Yes, the court can award the filing fee and service fees you spent. Rhode Island’s small claims statute § 10-16-13 allows certain costs as part of judgment ([us.vlex.com](https://us.vlex.com/source/14841/chapter/chapter-10-16-small-claims-and-consumer-claims-425197767#:~:text=,16)). Typically, if you win, the judgment will include reimbursement of your **filing fee ($55)** and the **service of process fee** (what you paid the sheriff or constable, usually $45-$100 depending). It may also include any statutory surcharges (the $17.50 e-filing fee, etc.). Essentially, the winning side can recover the basic costs of bringing the case. These are relatively small but important. The clerk/judge usually adds them automatically if you win. If you settled or something, costs might be part of negotiation.

- **Interest (pre-judgment and post-judgment):** Yes – interest can be recovered. **Pre-judgment interest:** In Rhode Island, certain claims (especially tort and contract claims in higher courts) automatically add statutory interest from when the cause of action arose (often 12% per annum simple interest as per R.I. Gen. Laws § 9-21-10 for tort, and case law extends to contract). In small claims, you should explicitly ask for interest and let the magistrate decide. Often, for liquidated amounts (like a unpaid loan on a certain date), the court will award interest from that due date to judgment at the statutory rate (currently Rhode Island’s statutory interest on judgments is **12% per annum** ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2009/title-9/chapter-9-1/9-1-13#:~:text=%C2%A7%209,shall%20accrue%2C%20and%20not%20after)), simple interest). For unliquidated damages (like you didn’t know the amount until fixed by judgment), interest might run from the date of judgment only. It’s a bit discretionary in small claims if not argued. It’s wise to include a claim for “interest as allowed by law” in your complaint form. **Post-judgment interest:** This accrues on the judgment amount until paid, at the statutory rate (12% per year) **automatically by law** (R.I. Gen. Laws § 9-21-8) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2009/title-9/chapter-9-1/9-1-13#:~:text=%C2%A7%209,shall%20accrue%2C%20and%20not%20after)). You don’t need to ask for post-judgment interest – it’s statutory. But if the judgment is not paid immediately, when you go to enforce or ultimately satisfy it, interest from the judgment date is added. For example, a $5,000 judgment will accrue about $600/year in interest until paid. The clerk can help compute this when execution is sought. 

- **Pain and suffering / emotional distress:** Generally **not** awarded in small claims (and not in contract cases anyway). Since small claims deals with contract and consumer issues, emotional distress damages are rarely available (contract law doesn’t usually allow them, and small claims wouldn’t handle the proof needed). If you have some minor inconvenience or stress, the court is not going to put a dollar on it. They stick to economic losses. The only time you might see a token recognition is if a statute includes it (none in RI small claims context do for emotional harm), or sometimes for a breached vacation or something extremely personal, a judge might award a bit extra – but that’s not typical or guaranteed. So assume you cannot get money for intangible suffering in small claims. 

- **Lost opportunities or speculative damages:** The court will be conservative. If you claim “because you didn’t have my car fixed, I missed out on a $10,000 job opportunity,” that’s speculative and above cap; even if under cap, unlikely to be awarded without solid proof the opportunity was concrete. Small claims sticks to fairly direct damages.

- **Punitive aspects via statutes:** As covered, double/treble mandated by statute are fine (like a mini-punitive measure embedded in law). But if you want to punish someone outside that, no.

- **Court-ordered out-of-pocket expenses for attending court:** Typically no separate compensation for your time or travel to court. Costs are limited to filing and service fees (and possibly witness subpoena fees if you had to pay those). You cannot bill for hours missed at work to attend the trial, etc. That’s considered part of the burden of litigation (unless your lost wages are actually part of the claim itself, e.g. you’re suing for lost wages from an accident, but then we’re in tort territory which as we said isn’t small claims material generally).

- **Collection costs after judgment:** If you have to pay a sheriff $20 to execute the judgment or record a lien, those costs can sometimes be added to the judgment as allowable *post-judgment* costs. Rhode Island law allows certain post-judgment costs of enforcement to be charged to the debtor. For example, the fee for an execution writ ($20 in District Court ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=Civil%20Filing%20Fee%20%20,%2480.00))) can be included. When you actually do it, the clerk issues the execution and adds that fee on. Similarly, if you record a judgment lien in Land Evidence records, you might not recover that from defendant unless in an execution proceeding. But interest covers a lot of the carrying cost.

To summarize: **Small claims will make you whole for your actual monetary loss, and apply any statutory enhancements you’re clearly entitled to, up to the $5,000 cap.** You won’t get pain-and-suffering or generalized punitive damages, and you can’t get non-monetary relief. It’s a court of limited remedial scope – money, money, money.

## 3. Where to file  
All small claims cases in Rhode Island are filed in the state’s **District Court**, which is the court of limited jurisdiction for certain civil matters. Within District Court, small claims cases are handled on a special calendar but *not* a separate court. 

- **Name of the court:** Officially, you will be filing in the District Court of Rhode Island, Small Claims division. (Sometimes people refer to it as “People’s Court” informally, but the proper designation is District Court). When you fill out forms, it will say “District Court” with a checkbox or indication for small claim. 

- **Geographical venue (which county or division):** Rhode Island is a small state but has multiple divisions of District Court. You must file your small claim in the appropriate **division (county) of the District Court**. Generally, venue is proper either where the **defendant lives or has their business**, or where the **contract was made or to be performed**, or where the **cause of action arose** ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=34,1)) ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=39%20%20,3%20months%20from%20last%20day)). As a practical rule: 
  - If your claim is against an individual, file in the court division for the county where that person **resides** (or is doing business).
  - If it’s against a business, use the county where the business’s principal office or operation is located.
  - If it’s over a transaction that occurred at a certain location (e.g. a store where you bought a product), you can file in the county of that location.
  - For example, if you live in Providence but you’re suing someone who lives in Newport, the case should be filed in the *Second Division* District Court (Newport County) where the defendant resides. If you file in the wrong division, the defendant can object and the case may be transferred or dismissed.
  - Rhode Island’s District Court Divisions: **Sixth Division** (Providence/Bristol County) – covers Providence and Bristol Counties; **Third Division** (Kent County); **Fourth Division** (Washington County); **Second Division** (Newport County). Providence County is by far the busiest, so many small claims end up in Sixth Division (Providence). If your claim is in Providence/Bristol area, it will likely be heard at the Providence District Court (One Dorrance Plaza). For Kent, at the Kent County courthouse in Warwick; Newport at Newport courthouse; Washington at Wakefield.
  
- **If you file in the wrong venue:** Rhode Island law allows transfer for improper venue. According to R.I. Gen. Laws § 9-4-4, if an action is brought in the wrong county (or wrong division of District Court), the court may, in its discretion, transfer it to a proper county/division ([www1.goramblers.org](https://www1.goramblers.org/research/pdf?dataid=VML99-3202&title=rhode-island-renters-rights.pdf#:~:text=Rhode%20Island%20Renters%20Rights%20,late%20with%20their%20share%20of)). The case doesn’t have to be dismissed outright unless the defendant demonstrates prejudice. In practice, if you accidentally file in Providence but it belongs in Newport, the judge may simply order it moved to Newport (especially if the defendant shows up and asks, or even on their own). This causes delay and inconvenience, so it’s better to pick the right location first. If the defendant doesn’t object and appears, sometimes the case will just proceed and venue defect is waived. But best not to risk it; follow the venue rules.

- **Online filing (e-filing):** Yes, Rhode Island has implemented an **electronic filing system (Odyssey File & Serve)** for civil cases, including small claims ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=shall%20be%20assessed%20on%20a,%E2%80%8B)). Attorneys are required to use e-filing. **Self-represented litigants (pro se)** in Rhode Island *may* e-file but are not strictly required – you can still file paper at the clerk’s office if you prefer (as a pro se, you’re exempt from mandatory e-filing, though it’s encouraged). The **e-filing portal** can be accessed via the Rhode Island Judiciary website. After making an account, you can file your small claim complaint and upload documents electronically. The court charges a $17.50 “case processing” fee for e-filing (or even for paper filings that get scanned) plus a small technology surcharge ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=Civil%20Case%20Processing%20Fee%20to,%E2%80%8B)) – these are automatically added to your filing fees. If you pay by credit card online, there’s a 3.25% fee as well ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=Civil%20Case%20Processing%20Fee%20to,%E2%80%8B)). Alternatively, you can go in person to the District Court clerk, fill out the complaint form and pay by check/cash; the clerk will enter it into the system (and that scanning triggers the same $17.50 fee). The bottom line: e-filing is available and speeds things up. The **Odyssey File & Serve** site for RI courts is the place to file if you choose to use it (link is on RI Judiciary site under e-filing). 

- **Which court clerk’s office to file with:** If filing on paper, go to the District Court clerk in the division of proper venue. E.g., for Providence venue, go to the 6th Division District Court clerk (located at the Garrahy Judicial Complex in Providence). For Kent, to the Noel Judicial Complex in Warwick, etc. The Rhode Island Judiciary website lists addresses for each division’s clerk ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil.aspx#:~:text=The%20District%20Court%20has%20exclusive,originally%20in%20the%20Superior%20Court)). 

- **What if you file in the wrong court (e.g., Superior instead of District)?:** If you mistakenly filed a $3,000 claim in Superior Court (which generally deals with >$10k cases), the Superior Court would likely lack jurisdiction because District Court has exclusive jurisdiction under $5,000 ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil.aspx#:~:text=The%20District%20Court%20has%20exclusive,of%20the%20defendants%20in%20the)). The case could be transferred down to District (by a motion) or dismissed for lack of jurisdiction and you’d have to re-file in District Court. It’s important to choose District Court for small claims from the start. On the complaint form, you actually indicate it’s a small claim so it’s assigned accordingly. 

- **Multiple defendants or locations:** If you have two defendants in different counties, venue can be any county where at least one defendant resides or where the claim arose. So choose one of the appropriate venues. 

- **Venue for out-of-state defendants:** If the person you want to sue lives out of state, you can still file in Rhode Island *if* the claim has a connection here (they did business here, contract was here, etc.). You then serve them out of state (but note, the court must have jurisdiction under long-arm statute). Often, small claims involving out-of-state defendants are tricky because service and enforcement are harder. But you’d typically file in the RI county where the transaction happened or where you reside (if that’s where contract was made) and assert jurisdiction because of the defendant’s contacts. It might be easier if the defendant is a business that operates in RI. If the only connection is you live here and they live elsewhere with no RI ties, the court may not have jurisdiction at all.

- **Removal to higher court:** In Rhode Island, if a case is properly a small claim but a defendant wants a jury trial, there isn’t a direct “removal” to Superior except by appealing after judgment. However, for regular District Court cases (>$5k up to $10k), a defendant can demand removal to Superior Court before answering ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil.aspx#:~:text=wherein%20the%20amount%20in%20controversy,originally%20in%20the%20Superior%20Court)). That doesn’t apply to small claims per se, since small claims has its own procedure for appeals (de novo in Superior after judgment). So you won’t have a defendant remove a small claim to Superior pre-trial; they either counterclaim above limit (triggering transfer) or they let it be and, if they lose, *appeal* to Superior for a new trial (see section 11 on appeals).

**Key point:** File your small claim in the right **Division of the District Court**, and consider e-filing for convenience. The Rhode Island Judiciary provides a “**District Court Small Claims**” information page and likely the forms online for each division. If in doubt, call the clerk’s office of the division and ask where to file for a particular city/town – they will tell you which county’s court covers that area.

## 4. Filing fees  
Filing a small claim in Rhode Island involves paying several fees, which are uniform statewide (not varying by county). Here is the fee breakdown:

- **Small claims filing fee:** $\$$**55.00** – This is the base fee to initiate a small claim case in District Court ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=Civil%20Filing%20Fee%20%20,%2480.00)). It’s a flat fee for claims up to $5,000 (Rhode Island does not have tiered fees by amount; it’s one fee for any amount in small claims range).

- **Electronic filing fees:** If you e-file or if the court scans your paper filing into the system (which they do), there is a one-time **Case Processing Fee of $17.50** and a **Technology Surcharge of $3.25** added ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=Civil%20Case%20Processing%20Fee%20to,%E2%80%8B)). So effectively, the total to file becomes $55 + $17.50 + $3.25 = $\$$**75.75** (plus any card processing fee if applicable). These extra fees go to the e-filing vendor and court tech fund. If you file in person on paper, the clerk will still apply these fees because the case will be entered into the electronic system (the rule is those fees apply to *all* civil cases in District Court) ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=shall%20be%20assessed%20on%20a,%E2%80%8B)).

- **Payment methods:** Online you can pay via credit/debit (with 3.25% fee on the total) ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=Civil%20Case%20Processing%20Fee%20to,%E2%80%8B)) or electronic check (usually a flat small fee). In person, you can pay by cash, check, or money order to “RI District Court”. If you use a card at the clerk, likely the same percentage applies.

- **Service of process fees:** Serving the defendant is a separate cost. In Rhode Island, small claims summons can be served by a sheriff or a certified constable ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=Service%20of%20a%20summons%20can,by%20using%20the%20following%20methods)) ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=1,at%20the%20office%20of%20the)). The typical **sheriff’s fee** for personal service is around $\$$**45-60** depending on mileage and number of parties (sheriffs have a set fee plus mileage). Constables may charge similar or slightly more. For example, serving one defendant in-state might cost ~$45. If you have two defendants or difficult service, costs rise (each additional address or person is usually another fee). You will pay the server directly. If you win the case, you can add the service fee to your judgment as a recoverable cost ([us.vlex.com](https://us.vlex.com/source/14841/chapter/chapter-10-16-small-claims-and-consumer-claims-425197767#:~:text=,16)). There is also a fee if you choose **certified mail service** (which is allowed on some small claims by court permission or if defendant is out of state) – that would be postal fees (around $7–$15) plus possibly a mailing service fee. However, RI’s small claims rules emphasize personal or abode service by sheriff/constable ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=Personal%20service%20of%20a%20summons,made%20in%20the%20following%20manner)) ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=Service%20of%20a%20summons%20can,by%20using%20the%20following%20methods)); certified mail isn’t standard unless court okays it for a minor claim or defendant in another state (and even then, you’d need a return receipt signed by defendant to count). Budget for sheriff service as the norm.

- **Motion fees:** In small claims, typically there are no motion filing fees. For example, if you need to file a motion to continue or a motion to vacate a default, District Court doesn’t charge extra for those in a pending case. (Superior Court has motions fees in some places, but District small claims doesn’t.) The initial filing is the main fee.

- **Trial/hearing fees:** There are no additional fees to have the hearing/trial. Your $55 at filing covers the entire case through judgment.

- **Counterclaim fee:** There is no fee for a defendant to file a counterclaim in small claims. The defendant just files the answer/counterclaim within 20 days ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/FAQs.aspx#:~:text=You%20must%20file%20your%20answer,judgment%20may%20enter%20against%20you)), and no payment is required.

- **Jury trial fee:** Not applicable – small claims are heard by a judge (or magistrate) without a jury, so there’s no jury fee. If somehow the case is transferred out of small claims and a jury trial is claimed in regular District Court or on appeal in Superior, then fees associated with that court would come into play (e.g., Superior might require a jury deposit).

- **Appeal fee:** If a defendant appeals a small claims judgment to Superior Court (allowed for defendants only), there is an **appeal filing fee of $100** (that’s the standard fee to claim an appeal from District to Superior) plus a statutory $50 attorneys’ fee to be paid as part of costs ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-12/9-12-10.htm#:~:text=with%20the%20clerk%20of%20the,attorney%E2%80%99s%20fee%20of%20fifty%20dollars)) (more on this in Appeals section). So appealing will cost about $150 (and possibly bond). But for filing the initial small claim case, that’s not relevant – just good to note for the losing defendant later.

- **Fee waivers:** If you cannot afford the filing fee, you can apply for a fee waiver (in forma pauperis). Rhode Island courts allow indigent litigants to proceed without fees if approved. You typically need to file a form or motion to proceed in forma pauperis, including a financial affidavit. The court will waive fees if you demonstrate inability to pay (for example, if you receive public assistance or have very low income). This would waive the $55 and associated surcharges and possibly service fees (sheriffs may still bill, but the court can order the state to cover sheriff costs in indigent cases). Ask the clerk for the fee waiver forms before filing. It might be a one-page form listing income, expenses, any public benefits. A judge will review it (often on the spot or within a day). If granted, you can file your case without paying. **Note:** The $17.50 e-file fee is also waived when the underlying filing fee is waived. Rhode Island has a general statute for waiver of fees for indigents (R.I. Gen. Laws § 9-25-18) and court rules about it.

- **Serving fee waiver:** If you are indigent and need the sheriff to serve, mention it in your waiver request. The court might include a line in the order allowing service by sheriff without prepayment. Sometimes the sheriff will still invoice later, but the court can order costs to be waived or paid by the state. Alternatively, the court may permit you to serve by certified mail to save cost if the judge is convinced of hardship and that mail is likely to reach the defendant. But generally, yes, fee waivers can cover all court fees.

- **Recoverable fees if you win:** If you win the case, the judgment can include: **“Costs”** such as the $55 filing fee, $17.50 processing fee, $3.25 tech fee, and your service fee (and statutory $50 if it was an appealed case). For example, your judgment might say “$1,000 damages + $75.75 costs = $1,075.75 total.” ([us.vlex.com](https://us.vlex.com/source/14841/chapter/chapter-10-16-small-claims-and-consumer-claims-425197767#:~:text=,16)) By law, at least $25 minimum costs must be taxed in District Court civil cases ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-12/9-12-10.htm#:~:text=Sundays%2C%20and%20legal%20holidays%2C%20after,attorney%E2%80%99s%20fee%20of%20fifty%20dollars)), so winners always get at least that. In small claims, they’ll give actual costs as long as it meets that minimum. So yes, if you paid $100 total outlay, expect ~$100 added to judgment. If you lose, you typically do **not** have to pay the other side’s costs in small claims (since usually they didn’t pay fees to defend, except maybe service fee for a counterclaim, which is rare). The court has discretion to award costs to the prevailing party – so if you lose, the defendant *could* ask for their parking or something but usually that’s denied except for extraordinary documented costs. There’s no attorneys’ fee shifting unless statute as discussed.

**Summary of likely filing expenses:**

Always check with the clerk or the court’s website for the current fees because they can change by legislative action (though $55 has been stable for years, and surcharges too). As of 2026, the above figures are current ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=Civil%20Filing%20Fee%20%20,%2480.00)) ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=Civil%20Case%20Processing%20Fee%20to,%E2%80%8B)).

## 5. Statute of limitations  
A statute of limitations is the time deadline to file a lawsuit. In Rhode Island, the limitations periods depend on the type of claim (and they apply equally in small claims as in regular court). Filing a case after the limitations period has expired can result in dismissal if the defendant raises it as a defense. Below is a breakdown of common limitation periods (“SOL”) by claim type relevant to small claims, under Rhode Island law (R.I. Gen. Laws § 9-1-13, § 9-1-14, etc.):

- **Written contracts:** **10 years** – Rhode Island’s default for civil actions not otherwise specified is 10 years ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2009/title-9/chapter-9-1/9-1-13#:~:text=%C2%A7%209,shall%20accrue%2C%20and%20not%20after)). A written contract breach is not specially listed elsewhere, so the general limitation of 10 years from the breach applies. For example, if someone signed a written agreement on Jan 1, 2020 and breached it on Jan 1, 2021, you have until Jan 1, 2031 to sue. (Note: This is quite generous compared to many states.) If the contract is under seal or a court judgment, see below (judgments are 20 years). But ordinary written contracts: 10-year SOL.

- **Oral contracts:** **10 years** – Interestingly, Rhode Island does not differentiate a shorter period for oral vs written in the statute; both fall under the general “all civil actions not otherwise provided for” which is 10 years ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2009/title-9/chapter-9-1/9-1-13#:~:text=%C2%A7%209,shall%20accrue%2C%20and%20not%20after)). Many states have shorter for oral, but RI’s law, by omission, means oral contract claims also use the 10-year catch-all. So if you had a handshake deal and it was breached, you technically have up to a decade to bring the claim. (However, proving an oral agreement after many years can be tough due to fading evidence, but legally the door is open for 10 years unless defendant uses laches or similar equitable argument.)

- **Sale of goods (UCC contracts):** **4 years** – Contracts for the sale of goods are governed by the Uniform Commercial Code. R.I. Gen. Laws § 6A-2-725 sets a 4-year statute of limitations for breach of a sales contract ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=6,10%20years)). This is an exception to the 10-year catch-all. So if your claim is for breach of a contract for sale of goods (e.g. you bought equipment or merchandise and it was defective or wasn’t delivered), you have 4 years from when the breach occurred (usually date of delivery or date it should have been delivered) to file. *Example:* You bought a computer on Jan 1, 2022 that failed to conform, you must sue by Jan 1, 2026. Note: Parties can reduce this to not less than 1 year by contract, but not extend it. So check the contract; but absent a change, 4 years.

- **Negotiable instruments (e.g. checks, promissory notes):** If a promissory note has a due date, the action is essentially contract – likely 10 years, but if it’s a *negotiable instrument under UCC Article 3*, often 6-year period might apply for notes (UCC 3-118 generally provides 6 years for notes). Rhode Island has adopted UCC, so a suit on a dishonored check or note might be subject to a 6-year limit under UCC Article 3 (need to confirm RI’s enactment). However, R.I. Gen Laws § 6A-3-118: a check should be sued on within 6 years of dishonor. Since 6 years < 10, that specific might govern. To be safe, aim for 6 years on negotiable instrument claims. (But many times people sue on notes as general contracts within 10, which might slip by if not challenged. Best practice: 6-year limit on notes & checks.)

- **Store accounts/open accounts:** If it’s a sale of goods on account – arguably UCC 4-year applies as it’s sale of goods. If it’s an open account for services, 10-year might apply (since service is not “goods”). In Rhode Island, there’s no separate “open account” statute listed; so default catches it if service.

- **Property damage (to personal property):** **10 years** – If based on negligence (tort) causing property damage, Rhode Island’s statute for tort property damage is not explicitly separate, so it likely falls under the 10-year catch-all as well (since 9-1-14 specifically mentions personal injury but not property). However, practically, property damage claims are considered “injuries to the person or property” often 3 years. Let’s clarify: R.I. Gen. Laws § 9-1-14(b) states *“injuries to the person”* 3 years ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=9,3%20years)). It doesn’t mention injuries to property. Many jurisdictions pair them, but Rhode Island’s compilation (see § 9-1-13) didn’t list property under 3 years. It likely means property damage gets the 10-year default ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=6A,1%20year)) ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=9,3%20years)). Indeed, the compiled list ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=9,3%20years)) shows “Injuries to the person, generally – 3 years” and does not separately list property. So, bizarrely, Rhode Island allows up to 10 years to sue for property damage. (But again, tort claims rarely wait that long, and evidence issues aside, if you tried to sue for a car accident after 8 years, a defendant might argue laches or that the 3-year should apply by analogy. But statute-wise, it seems 10.)

- **Personal injury (bodily injury):** **3 years** – Actions for personal injuries must be filed within 3 years of the date of injury (or date of accrual) ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=9,3%20years)) ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=,2%20years)). This includes things like car accidents, slip-and-fall injuries, etc. The clock typically starts when the injury occurred, but Rhode Island does recognize a “discovery rule” in some cases (e.g. medical malpractice (which has its own section), or if you discovered an injury later). However, in straightforward accidents, assume 3 years from accident date. Note: For minors or incompetents, the period can be tolled until disability is removed (e.g. a minor has until 3 years after turning 18) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/title-9/chapter-9-1/section-9-1-14-1/#:~:text=,one%20%2821%29%20years%20of%20age)). But these are rarely relevant in small claims since those are not in small claims scope anyway (small claims not for PI, as discussed – but good to know if someone tries to bring a small PI like property damage to a car in District Court, 3 years is time for personal injury, 10 for property technically).

- **Fraud (common law):** If considered a tort, likely 10-year default (not specifically enumerated). However, fraud might be argued to be an “action for which no other limit is provided” = 10 years. Rhode Island also has a statute that the limitation for fraud runs from discovery of the fraud (if it’s an equitable case). Generally, assume 10 years but you must have discovered it late to go that long or else laches may apply.

- **Professional malpractice:** For medical malpractice, Rhode Island has a 3-year statute (with a 7-year outside limit except for foreign objects) in § 9-1-14.1 ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2005/title9/9-1-14.1.html#:~:text=Justia%20law,malpractice%20shall%20be%20commenced%20within)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/title-9/chapter-9-1/section-9-1-14-1/#:~:text=,one%20%2821%29%20years%20of%20age)). Legal malpractice likely 3 years (since it’s not otherwise specified, could argue 10 but courts have treated it as akin to personal injury or some as contract – unclear, but probably 3 from when act or when client knew). These aren’t for small claims though usually.

- **Debt collection suits:** If it’s a written credit card agreement or promissory note, 10 years (or 6 if note under UCC). If it’s store card (goods), 4 years. Many credit card debts end up being 4 if it’s technically sale of goods, but credit card issuers often argue 10 since it’s a revolving credit contract. Rhode Island case law on that isn’t obvious here; safer view: 10-year on standard credit agreement (which is written contract to loan money), 4-year on store credit for goods.

- **Judgments:** If you have a judgment and want to enforce it (not initial filing, but enforcement timeline), a Rhode Island judgment is enforceable for **20 years** ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=9,3%20years)) (R.I. Gen. Laws § 9-1-17 sets 20 years for judgments and for contracts under seal) ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=9,3%20years)). You can also renew it before expiration to extend further. This is relevant because if you win a small claims judgment, you have up to 20 years to collect on it (though interest accrues, better to collect sooner). But if you have an old judgment beyond 20 years, it’s expired – you can’t enforce anymore.

- **Government-related claims:** Some have notice deadlines. For example, claims against a city/town for injury or damage typically require notice of claim within 60 days of the incident (R.I. Gen. Laws § 45-15-9 for tort claims vs municipalities requires notice in 60 days or else in absence of notice the recoverable amount might be capped) ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=match%20at%20L98%20highways%2C%20causeways,30%20days)). So even though you may have 3 years to sue a city for negligence, if you didn’t notify within 60 days, your claim could be limited or barred. Always research specific notice requirements if suing a government.

- **Tolling exceptions:** Rhode Island tolls (pauses) the statute for minors until age 18 (then time runs) for personal injury ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/title-9/chapter-9-1/section-9-1-14-1/#:~:text=,one%20%2821%29%20years%20of%20age)). Also tolling for mental incompetence (until competency restored) up to 3 years after ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/title-9/chapter-9-1/section-9-1-14-1/#:~:text=,one%20%2821%29%20years%20of%20age)). Fraudulent concealment of a cause of action can toll until discovery (R.I. Gen. Laws § 9-1-20). E.g. if a defendant hid their wrongdoing, the clock starts when you discovered or should have discovered it. Also, if the defendant is out of state (not amenable to service) for a period, that period may not count (common tolling in many states). Military service or bankruptcy stay can toll the time to file or enforce. If someone files bankruptcy, the statute to sue them is tolled during the automatic stay. Always consider these if near expiration.

- **“Date of breach or accrual” details:** For contract, accrual is when the breach happened (when payment was due and not made, or when performance due and not given). For tort, accrual is typically the date of injury. For example, if a check bounced on Jan 1, 2023, cause accrues then for you to sue the check writer. If a loan was payable on June 1, 2020 and unpaid, accrual June 1, 2020 for SOL.

- **Check the precisely applicable section:** Rhode Island General Laws § 9-1-13(a) – 10 years for all not otherwise provided ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2009/title-9/chapter-9-1/9-1-13#:~:text=%C2%A7%209,shall%20accrue%2C%20and%20not%20after)), § 9-1-13(b) – product liability 10 years from purchase (which in fact is a statute of repose) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2009/title-9/chapter-9-1/9-1-13#:~:text=%28b%29%20,purchased%20for%20use%20or%20consumption)), § 9-1-14 – 1 year for slander (words spoken), 3 years for personal injury ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/title-9/chapter-9-1/section-9-1-14/#:~:text=%C2%A7%209,words%20spoken%20or%20personal%20injuries)) ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=9,3%20years)), § 9-1-14.1 – 3 years for professional malpractices (with discovery rule specifics) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2005/title9/9-1-14.1.html#:~:text=Justia%20law,malpractice%20shall%20be%20commenced%20within)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/title-9/chapter-9-1/section-9-1-14-1/#:~:text=,reasonable%20diligence%2C%20have%20been%20discovered)), etc.

Rhode Island not being a “short SOL” state except for a few categories means most small claims folks file well within time.

## 6. Pre-filing requirements  
Before filing a small claim in Rhode Island, it’s wise to take certain steps, though the law imposes few mandatory pre-filing requirements. Unlike some states that mandate a demand letter or mediation before suit, Rhode Island generally does not require those for small claims – but there are exceptions for specific types of disputes and good practices that effectively function as requirements if you want to maximize your case’s success. Here’s what to consider doing before you file:

- **Demand letter (recommended but not required):** Rhode Island law does *not* universally require a demand letter before a small claims suit. However, it is highly recommended to send one. A **demand letter** is a written notice to the potential defendant that outlines your claim (what is owed or what went wrong), the amount you seek, and a timeframe to resolve before you sue. For instance, you might write: “On March 1, 2026, I paid you $500 for X which was never delivered. I demand a full refund of $500 within 10 days or I will take legal action in small claims court.” This letter serves a few purposes: it can prompt the other side to pay up without court; it documents that you gave them an opportunity (which in court shows reasonableness); and if you have any statutory claim requiring notice, it may satisfy that. **No Rhode Island statute requires a generic demand for all small claims**, but **specific cases might:** e.g., for a **bad check**, some states require a 30-day demand for treble damages – Rhode Island doesn’t have a treble-damage statute, so no statutory demand needed. For **security deposits**, not required – the statute automatically penalizes wrongful withholding after 20 days, no extra notice needed ([www.hemlane.com](https://www.hemlane.com/resources/rhode-island-security-deposit-laws#:~:text=landlord%20wrongfully%20withheld%20any%20portion,double%20the%20amount%20wrongfully%20withheld)). For **consumer fraud**, RI’s consumer act does not require a pre-suit notice (unlike Massachusetts which does) ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/BillText14/HouseText14/H8104A.htm#:~:text=admissible%20in%20any%20subsequent%20criminal,or%20services)). So legally, you could file without any letter. Still, sending one is a good idea. Ensure you keep a copy and send it in a verifiable way (e.g. certified mail, or email with read receipt) so you can later prove the other party had notice. If the defendant responds or offers partial payment after a demand, you might resolve or you might still end up suing – but at least you tried. 

- **Mandatory mediation or arbitration pre-suit:** Rhode Island does not mandate pre-filing mediation for small claims. However, note that *after* filing, if you are a self-represented plaintiff, the court will refer the case to mediation once an answer is filed ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=)). That is an internal process. **Pre-suit**, there’s no court-ordered ADR. That said, if there is a **contract clause** requiring arbitration or mediation before litigation, you must follow that or risk your case being stayed or dismissed. E.g. some contractor agreements or gym contracts have an arbitration clause. If so, the defendant can move to compel arbitration and the court will likely honor it (federal arbitration law is strong). So, check your contract with the party: if it has an arbitration clause, you may need to go through that instead of small claims (some arbitration clauses explicitly carve out small claims as allowed – if so, you’re fine). If the clause requires mediation first, do it before filing (because the court could consider the case premature if you ignored a contractual dispute resolution step). **Landlord-tenant:** No pre-suit mediation required for deposit or rent claims. **Labor/wage claims:** Not required to go to Dept of Labor first, you can directly sue (the law allows either route). **Auto repair disputes:** No official requirement to, say, notify the Auto Body Licensing Board or any such – though you could complain to them separately. So in summary, no general pre-filing mediation required, but heed any contract’s dispute resolution terms.

- **Notice periods (specific to certain disputes):** A few specific situations have notice prerequisites:
  - If suing a **city/town** for negligence (say a city vehicle hit your fence), you are supposed to file a written notice of claim with the city clerk within 60 days of the incident, per R.I. Gen. Laws § 45-15-9 ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=match%20at%20L98%20highways%2C%20causeways,30%20days)). Not doing so could limit your recovery to a set amount or bar it. So do that before suing (just a simple letter to city of intent to claim).
  - If suing the **state** or a state agency, Rhode Island law (§ 9-31-1 et seq.) waives immunity but you generally should present the claim to the agency or attorney general within 3 years (the SOL is 3 years but they might also require a notice under some contexts; the statute isn’t as strict as cities).
  - **Defective products** – no special notice to manufacturer required by law *unless* you’re invoking warranty and the warranty requires giving the seller a chance to cure. Actually under UCC, to recover for breach of warranty, the buyer must within a reasonable time after discovering the defect notify the seller (R.I. Gen. Laws § 6A-2-607(3)(a)). If you don’t notify the seller of the breach, you may be barred from any remedy. So if you bought goods and they’re defective, before suing, ensure you communicated the problem to the seller or manufacturer within a reasonable time. That counts as a form of pre-suit notice (which often happens naturally – you likely complained or attempted a return, which suffices). If you haven’t, do so now (send an email or letter: “the product you sold me is defective in X way, please fix or refund.”). That way they can’t claim lack of notice as a defense.
  - **Construction contractor disputes:** Rhode Island has a “Right to Repair” law for contractors? Some states do, requiring homeowner to give contractor chance to fix defects before suing. Rhode Island’s Contractors’ Registration Board encourages mediation for disputes over $1,000. But if you’re going straight to small claims, it’s not legally required to notify the contractor. Still, practically, telling the contractor “I’m unhappy and will sue if not resolved” is wise. If a complaint is filed with the CRLB, that can also resolve or provide evidence. Not mandatory though.
  - **Home improvement contracts** under RI law must have certain terms, but no pre-suit requirement beyond noticing the contractor of defects maybe within a certain time if contract states.
  - **Auto accidents** (again not in small claims jurisdiction for suit, but if you were going to try to recover a deductible or something from the other driver, typically you would have already made an insurance claim. There’s no legal requirement to send the person a demand before suing for property damage, but it’s good practice).
  - **Insurance claims:** If your claim is against your insurance (like they owe you under a policy), most policies require you to file a proof of loss and wait a certain time. Also, RI has Unfair Claims Practices regs that encourage negotiation. Usually by the time you’d sue your insurer in small claims (maybe for a small health claim or auto damage they refused to pay), you’d have exhausted their claim process. Be sure to comply with policy notice requirements (if you didn’t formally make a claim, the suit will be premature).
  - **Commercial small claims:** If a business is suing a customer, no particular pre-step is required (though sending a final demand or invoice is customary).
  
- **Attempt to settle or mediate voluntarily:** While not mandatory, it may save time and fees. Rhode Island Legal Services or community mediation centers might offer free mediation pre-suit. Or simply negotiating one more time might resolve it. Because once you file, the defendant may become more defensive or even not show (leading to default but then collection issues). If you can work it out beforehand, that’s ideal. However, don’t let the clock run out waiting – be mindful of the SOL.

- **Gather your evidence and documents:** It’s not a formal requirement, but effectively before filing you should collect all relevant paperwork: contracts, receipts, emails, texts, photos, etc. Especially any document that shows the defendant’s liability or the amount owed. Also get the correct legal name and address of the defendant (critical for filing and service). If it’s a business, look up if it’s an LLC or corp via RI Secretary of State’s business database and note the official name and agent for service. Suing the correct entity is key; doing this homework before filing will prevent needing amendments or risking dismissal for wrong party.

- **Check the defendant’s military status:** This is more relevant after filing (for default judgment you’ll need to state whether defendant is in military service). The Servicemembers Civil Relief Act requires plaintiffs to declare if the defendant is in active military, because active military have protections against default judgments. It’s wise to do a quick check (SCRA website or asking them) beforehand. Not a filing requirement per se, but something to be prepared for.

- **Pre-filing for specific forums:** If your case could go to a specialized forum (like a wage claim to Dept of Labor, a discrimination claim to Human Rights Commission, etc.), decide if you want to use that instead of small claims. Sometimes those agencies are free and will pursue for you. If you choose small claims anyway, you typically can (unless law says administrative route is exclusive, which for discrimination it is until you get a right-to-sue). For wage claims, parallel jurisdiction but if DOL has started, you might not need to file separate suit.

In summary, **Rhode Island imposes no blanket pre-suit requirements** for small claims, but as a practical matter: send a demand letter (particularly for consumer and debt cases), ensure any contract-required or statute-required notices are done (e.g. UCC warranty notice, city claim notice), and have all your ducks in a row. By doing so, you increase your chances of either resolving the matter quickly or winning in court without delay. If you're unsure of a specific requirement for your type of case, doing a quick check of Rhode Island General Laws for that topic or consulting an attorney can save you from a misstep.

## 7. Forms required  
Filing a small claim in Rhode Island involves preparing a few specific forms. The Rhode Island Judiciary provides standard forms for small claims proceedings. The required forms (and some optional or subsequent forms) include:

- **Complaint form (Small Claims Notice of Suit):** This is the main form to start your case. In Rhode Island, the District Court small claims complaint is often titled **“Complaint (Small Claims)”** or may be combined with a summons. The form number can vary; it might not have a statewide number on the public site due to e-filing, but it’s available from the clerk. It typically asks for:
  - Plaintiff’s name and address
  - Defendant’s name and address (if a business, the business name and an officer/agent’s name)
  - Amount of claim (the dollar amount you are suing for)
  - A brief statement of the claim (e.g. “Defendant failed to repay loan of $___ made on [date].” or “Breach of contract for services on [date] – paid $___, no services received.”)
  - Date and signature line for the plaintiff (or plaintiff’s attorney).
  - If filed on paper, the clerk upon filing will assign a case number and a hearing date and will issue the summons portion.
  The Rhode Island Legal Services website or the court clerk can provide a **Small Claims Complaint form**. Also, the court’s computer system will generate a **“Notice of Hearing and Summons”** once you file, which you then serve. If e-filing, you fill in an electronic version. If paper, you fill it out in duplicate. **Where to get it:** You can get the small claims complaint form at any District Court clerk’s office or possibly online (the RI Judiciary “Forms” page under District Court might list “Small Claims Complaint”). For example, form DC-53 might be a small claims complaint form in some older references. Check the RI Judiciary forms directory ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Pages/forms.aspx#:~:text=Rhode%20Island%20Judiciary%20Justice,Forms)) or helprilaw or RhodeIslandCourtRecords sites for a PDF. Ensure to list the correct division (Second, Third, Fourth, or Sixth) at the top and the county. After filing, the clerk signs/stamps the summons part.

- **Civil Summons (Small Claims)**: In many states, the small claims complaint itself acts as the summons. Rhode Island’s District Court usually prepares a **Summons** directed to the defendant once you file. It’s often printed on the same page or attached to your complaint. The summons will indicate the case name, number, the court address, and require the defendant to file an answer within 20 days ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/FAQs.aspx#:~:text=You%20must%20file%20your%20answer,judgment%20may%20enter%20against%20you)). It also sets a hearing date or indicates that after an answer, mediation/trial will be scheduled. The summons must be *served* on the defendant. If you are e-filing, the system may generate an “official summons” for you to download and have served. If filing paper, ask the clerk for the issued summons when you file. In small claims, the timeline is a bit different from other civil cases: The defendant has 20 days to answer, not to appear in court immediately (small claims in RI are not a preset hearing upon filing unlike some states, they wait for answer then schedule). The summons will reflect that. **Form**: The summons isn’t a form you fill; it’s issued by the clerk. But make sure you obtain it and attach a copy of the complaint to it for service.

- **Answer form (for defendant):** The defendant can respond in writing with an **Answer**. The court often provides a **Small Claims Answer form** on the reverse of the summons or as a separate form. For instance, there might be a standardized answer form where the defendant can tick a box denying the claim and provide any brief explanation or counterclaim. If you are the plaintiff, you don’t need this form, but it’s good to be aware it exists. (If you get a default, you might see that the defendant never returned this form). The Answer form usually includes a section for **Counterclaim** if the defendant has any, and a certification of mailing to plaintiff. **Where to get**: It’s usually mailed to the defendant with the summons (some courts include an answer form in the packet). It may also be on the RI Judiciary forms page (like “DC-53A Answer – Small Claims”). The defendant should file this within 20 days of service.

- **Military Service Affidavit:** If the defendant doesn’t answer and you seek a default, you must file an affidavit regarding defendant’s military status (Servicemembers Civil Relief Act requirement). The court likely has an **Affidavit of Non-Military Service** form. It typically says you have investigated and believe the defendant is not on active military duty. This is required before a default judgment will be entered ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/FAQs.aspx#:~:text=You%20must%20file%20your%20answer,judgment%20may%20enter%20against%20you)). (If defendant is in military, case may be stayed until they can appear). **Form**: Some state courts standardize this. The clerk might have a generic affidavit form or you can draft one referencing 50 U.S.C. § 3931. It’s not needed at filing, only when pursuing default.

- **Notice of Mediation or Trial:** After an Answer is filed, the court will send a **Notice of Mediation Date** (for self-represented parties) or directly a **trial date** if one or both sides have attorneys (since mediation is mandatory only when both sides are pro se per the statute ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE10/10-16/10-16-5.htm#:~:text=,of%20said%20mandatory%20mediation%20system)) ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE10/10-16/10-16-5.htm#:~:text=,of%20said%20mandatory%20mediation%20system)), and likely practice is to mediate all pro se cases first). This isn’t a form you fill, but a document you receive. Ensure you show up to that date. If mediation fails, they’ll set a trial date.

- **Subpoena form:** If you need to compel a witness to attend the hearing or bring documents, you can request the court issue a **Subpoena**. In District Court, the subpoena form can be obtained from the clerk (there might be a generic “District Court Subpoena” form). You fill in case info, witness name, what documents to bring (if any), and the clerk signs it. You must then have it served (usually by sheriff or constable) and pay witness fees (in RI, $10/day plus mileage). The subpoena form isn’t specific to small claims – it’s the same as in any civil case. Ask the clerk for a subpoena form well before the hearing if needed. **No fee** for issuing a subpoena in District Court ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-FAQ.aspx#:~:text=,a%20witness%20to%20court)), but you pay service.

- **Motion forms (if applicable):** If you need to file a motion (e.g. Motion to Continue hearing, Motion to Amend your claim, Motion to Vacate a default judgment), Rhode Island District Court often expects written motions. There isn’t a specialized small claims motion form; you typically would use a standard **District Court Motion form** or write your own motion in format (case caption, “Plaintiff’s Motion to ______”, grounds, relief sought, dated and signed). The court clerk might have a generic blank motion form. Alternatively, Rhode Island Legal Services might have templates. For a **continuance**, some courts allow an informal request by phone or letter if both parties agree, but formally one should file a motion to continue showing good cause. For **default judgment request**, some courts require a motion for default judgment after the answer deadline passes, along with the military affidavit and proof of service.

- **Execution form (post-judgment):** If you win and the defendant doesn’t pay voluntarily, after the applicable appeal period (typically 2 business days for small claims appeals ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-12/9-12-10.htm#:~:text=by%20claiming%20an%20appeal%20from,appeal%2C%20shall%20pay%20to%20the)) if not appealed) or after an appeal is over, you can get an **Execution** issued. The Execution is a document that authorizes you (through a sheriff) to enforce the judgment (seize assets, garnish wages, etc.). The **Execution form** is issued by the clerk upon request, not something you fill out originally (though you may need to apply or file a motion for execution if some time has passed). In District Court, you typically wait 30 days after judgment (since defendants have 20 days to appeal plus some processing) then request execution. The clerk will have an execution form with the case details and judgment amount, plus added interest and costs, for the sheriff. You might have to fill in some info like judgment amount, date, and if any payments made. There is a fee ($20) for the execution writ ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=Civil%20Filing%20Fee%20%20,%2480.00)). The sheriff then uses that to attempt collection.

- **Appearance form (for attorneys):** If either party is represented by a lawyer, the attorney will file an **Entry of Appearance** form or a notice. Pro se parties don’t file anything to appear (by filing the complaint you appear; defendants just file answer). But if down the road, say the plaintiff hires an attorney for collection or defendant gets counsel for an appeal, an appearance form is used. Usually a simple letter or notice on letterhead suffices in small claims.

- **Dismissal form (stipulation of dismissal or satisfaction):** If the case settles or you want to dismiss for any reason, you can submit a **Stipulation of Dismissal** (signed by both if it’s settled) or a **Notice of Voluntary Dismissal** (if you’re the plaintiff before answer). There isn’t a special small claims dismissal form; you’d use Rule 41 of civil procedure analog. The court may have a **“Disposition form”** or you just write a letter. If judgment was paid after entry, you might file a **Satisfaction of Judgment** form so the court records that it’s satisfied (the Judiciary might have a satisfaction form you sign and file).

- **Fee waiver application (IFP):** As noted earlier, if you cannot afford fees, you’d file a **Motion to Proceed in Forma Pauperis** along with an **Affidavit of Indigency**. The Judiciary likely has a form for this (common across courts). Ask the clerk for the “poverty affidavit” or check the court’s forms site for an Indigent Waiver form. Provide income info, etc.

**Where to find these forms:**
Rhode Island Judiciary’s website under District Court forms or the *HelpRILaw* site often provides PDF downloads. For example:
  - *Small Claims Complaint form:* Possibly on `courts.ri.gov` under District Court-> Forms-> Civil.
  - *Small Claims Answer form:* Possibly provided on service or available via clerk.
  - *Motion/affidavit forms:* in the “Miscellaneous” forms section perhaps.
If not easily found online, the District Court clerks are accustomed to giving out small claims packets with necessary forms at the counter. You can also call and ask if they can mail forms.

**Filling out forms tips:**
Fill them out completely and neatly. Use full legal names (no nicknames). For businesses, include the proper entity name (e.g. “ABC, Inc.” not just “ABC”). Provide addresses where defendants can be served (work or home). When stating your claim on the complaint, be succinct but clear (the statute only requires a simple statement; you don’t need legalese). For example: “Defendant failed to repay $2,000 loan made on 5/10/2025, due 8/10/2025.” Or “Defendant damaged Plaintiff’s property and agreed to pay $500, which remains unpaid.” Keep the story for court, just outline basis.

Sign the complaint. If filing by e-file, your typed name serves as signature under the rules (you register in the system). If multiple plaintiffs, each should sign or one can sign as “authorized agent” with explanation.

**No county-specific forms differences:** Rhode Island courts are unified; the forms are the same in every county’s District Court. Just ensure the correct division name is on the caption (e.g. Second Division for Newport, etc.). 

**Service forms:** After you get the summons and complaint, to serve via sheriff, you typically provide the sheriff with:
  - 1 copy of the summons & complaint for each defendant,
  - 1 additional copy for sheriff’s return perhaps,
  - A cover sheet or instruction to sheriff with addresses and case info. The sheriff will fill out the **Return of Service** on the original summons (or an attached form) and send it back to you or file with court. Confirm with the sheriff’s office their procedure.

All necessary forms to initiate (complaint & summons) will be given a case number by the clerk (e.g. “6SC-2026-XXXX”). Always put that on any subsequent forms you file.

**Summary of forms:**
1. **Complaint (Small Claims)** – to start the case ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=Small%20claims%20cases%20are%20handled,receive%20a%20judgment%20for%20money)).
2. **Summons** – issued by court, served on defendant.
3. **Answer (and Counterclaim) form** – defendant’s response form ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/FAQs.aspx#:~:text=,debt%2C%20what%20should%20I%20do)).
4. **Military Service Affidavit** – for default judgments.
5. **Motion forms** (optional, as needed for procedural requests).
6. **Subpoena** – to compel witnesses or documents.
7. **Execution** – post-judgment enforcement.
8. **Fee waiver affidavit** – if applicable to waive fees.
9. **Stipulation of dismissal/Satisfaction** – if case is settled or judgment paid.

Make sure to **keep copies** of everything you file for your own records. If filing in person, bring an extra copy for them to stamp “Filed” for you. If e-filing, you’ll have access to filed stamped copies in the system.

## 8. Service of process  
After filing your small claim, you must properly **serve** the defendant with the summons and complaint. Proper service is crucial – without it, the court won’t hear the case or issue a judgment. Rhode Island’s rules for serving small claims are similar to those for other civil cases, with some allowances since small claims are meant to be informal (but service requirements are basically formal). Here’s how service works in RI small claims:

- **Who can serve:** In Rhode Island, a small claims summons and complaint can be served by:
  - A **Rhode Island deputy sheriff** (each county has sheriffs who serve legal papers) ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=Service%20of%20a%20summons%20can,by%20using%20the%20following%20methods)).
  - A certified **constable** licensed by the RI Department of Business Regulation ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=Service%20of%20a%20summons%20can,by%20using%20the%20following%20methods)).
  - **Any person not a party and over 18** – actually, Rhode Island’s District Court Civil Rules (Rule 4(c)) allow service by any disinterested adult, but in practice, use of sheriffs or constables is strongly preferred and customary for small claims ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=Service%20of%20a%20summons%20can,by%20using%20the%20following%20methods)). The court’s small claims page explicitly lists only sheriff or constable as methods ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=Service%20of%20a%20summons%20can,by%20using%20the%20following%20methods)), suggesting they expect professional service. If you have a friend serve, it might be allowed legally but ensure that person can truthfully sign an affidavit of service and that it strictly follows the requirements. It’s safer to use a sheriff or constable who knows the rules. 
  - **Certified mail** by clerk: Rhode Island does *not* generally authorize service by certified mail in small claims, except possibly on out-of-state defendants with court permission (and even then might require a signed receipt). The small claims statute doesn’t mention mail service like some states do. The District Court FAQ or guidance isn’t explicit about mail – it likely requires personal or abode service. The small claims info page suggests if you need alternative ways to serve (like mail or publication), you should seek an attorney ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=2,the%20Department%20of%20Business%20Regulations)). So, plan on personal service by sheriff or constable.
  - **Note:** The plaintiff **cannot** serve the papers themselves (you are a party, so disqualified).

- **Allowed methods of service:** The basic methods (similar to Rule 4 of civil procedure) are:
  1. **Personal service:** Delivering a copy of the summons and complaint directly to the defendant in hand ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=Personal%20service%20of%20a%20summons,made%20in%20the%20following%20manner)). This is the best method – handing it to them.
  2. **Substituted service (abode):** If defendant is an individual, service can be made by leaving the papers at the defendant’s **dwelling house or usual place of abode** with a person of suitable age and discretion who resides there ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=1,at%20the%20office%20of%20the)) (usually an adult family member). E.g. leave with spouse or adult child at home.
  3. **Service on authorized agent:** If defendant has appointed an agent or by law has an agent (like a company’s registered agent), delivering there suffices ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=3,delivering%20a%20copy%20to%20any)).
  4. **Service on a corporation or LLC:** Deliver to an **officer, managing or general agent**, or leave at the corporation’s office with a person in charge ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=4,officer%2C%20director%2C%20or%20manager%20thereof)). Or deliver to its registered agent. Essentially, you can serve a company by giving it to a manager or someone apparently in charge at the business’s principal office, or to an executive. Sheriffs often go to the company’s registered agent (on file with Sec. of State). Rhode Island specifically: to serve a **private corporation**, deliver to any officer or to a resident agent or leave at the corporate office with someone employed there ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=4,officer%2C%20director%2C%20or%20manager%20thereof)).
  5. **Service on a public corporation/authority:** Deliver to any officer, director, or manager of the entity ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=officer%2C%20a%20managing%20or%20general,officer%2C%20director%2C%20or%20manager%20thereof)) (for a city, typically serve the city clerk or mayor; for state agency, often AG or agency head per state suit requirements).
  6. **Special cases:** If defendant is a minor or incapacitated, serve their guardian (if no guardian, possibly parent or someone, but likely you’d motion for appointment of guardian ad litem).
  7. **Service by publication:** This is a last resort if defendant cannot be located. You’d have to motion the court for permission to use publication (and show effort at finding them). Publication means publishing notice in a newspaper. It is rarely used in small claims because it’s expensive and often not effective for money collection. But it’s available if the court grants it. Typically, the court might require you to attempt certified mail first to last known address, and if returned or no good, then allow publication. Since small claims is money only, publication service can suffice for jurisdiction if approved.

- **Service by certified mail:** While not standard, if the defendant is out-of-state, Rhode Island’s civil rules (Rule 4(e)) allow service by **certified mail, return receipt** for out-of-state defendants, as long as it’s restricted delivery to them and they sign. In small claims, you’d likely ask the clerk if you can do that. Often, professional process service is still recommended even out-of-state via hiring a sheriff in that state or a certified mail. If you use certified mail to someone out-of-state, Rhode Island requires an **affidavit of service with the signed green card** attached to prove they received it. If they don’t sign or pick it up, you haven’t served them. So often, a personal service in the other state (via that state’s sheriff or process server) ends up needed, which is more complicated (and expensive). So consider whether chasing an out-of-state defendant in RI small claims is worthwhile – jurisdiction and enforcement can be hurdles.

- **Timing of service:** You must serve the summons and complaint to give defendant adequate notice **before** any court hearing or deadline. In Rhode Island, since an answer is due in 20 days ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/FAQs.aspx#:~:text=You%20must%20file%20your%20answer,judgment%20may%20enter%20against%20you)), you want to serve as soon as possible after filing. There isn’t a specific minimum number of days prior to hearing because the hearing isn’t scheduled until answer comes (except possibly a placeholder date on the summons). Generally, the rule for District Court (Rule 4(l) analogous) says if service is not made within 120 days after filing, the case can be dismissed ([studylib.net](https://studylib.net/doc/8235035/small-claims-court-procedures#:~:text=Small%20Claims%20Court%20Procedures%20Small,The%20small%20claims%20court)). So you *must* get them served within 120 days of filing (4 months) or show good cause or the claim could be thrown out for lack of timely service. So aim well within that – don’t delay. If you can’t find them, you might ask the court for an extension and permission for alternate service.

- **Proof of service:** After service, you need to provide proof to the court. If a **sheriff** serves, they will typically send the original summons back to the court (or to you) with a signed **Return of Service** indicating when, where, and how service was made (or attempted and not made). Sheriffs in RI often file the return directly with the clerk or hand it to you to file. Check with the clerk’s office if the return has been received before the answer deadline. If a **constable** serves, they will complete an **Affidavit of Service** (a notarized statement of who they served and how) and you must file that with the court. If you used **certified mail** (with court permission or out-of-state), you would file your own affidavit along with the signed return card as proof. Rhode Island small claims procedure requires that the fact of service be noted in the docket ([us.vlex.com](https://us.vlex.com/source/14841/chapter/chapter-10-16-small-claims-and-consumer-claims-425197767#:~:text=,10)). The return/affidavit does that. Make sure the proof is on file by the time of any default or hearing. Without it, the judge can’t proceed.

- **If defendant cannot be located:** If your first attempt at service fails (e.g. sheriff says “defendant moved” or “not found at address”), don’t give up. You may need to do some detective work:
  - Try to get a newer address (through neighbors, online search, their workplace, etc.).
  - You can request the clerk reissue alias summons to attempt again (sheriff might try multiple times within the same original summons validity, but after a while, you may get an alias).
  - If you truly can’t find them, you can motion the court for **alternative service**. Provide evidence of attempts and perhaps ask to serve by leaving with a relative, or by certified mail to last known plus posting at courthouse, or by publication. The court will consider if it’s a reasonable way to notify them given the circumstances ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2010/title34/chapter34-18/34-18-56.html#:~:text=Landlord%20and%20Tenant%20Act%20%3A%3A,CHECK)). Alternative service must be reasonably calculated to reach them ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2010/title34/chapter34-18/34-18-56.html#:~:text=Landlord%20and%20Tenant%20Act%20%3A%3A,CHECK)). For example, if you have an email known to belong to them, maybe court could allow service by email (not common, but possibly if person is evasive). Or if they left the state, the court might say publish in newspaper where last known or where they likely reside now. These are case-specific. You’d file a **Motion for Alternate Service** with details.
  - Without some form of court-approved alternate service, you cannot just proceed – actual service or court-approved constructive service is required.

- **Cost of service:** As mentioned, you (the plaintiff) initially pay the sheriff/constable. Sheriffs have set fees (like ~$45). For example, Providence County Sheriff’s Dept might charge $45 for in-county, plus a bit if mileage is significant. Constables set their own fees – always ask total cost upfront. If you have multiple defendants, each requires service (so double cost). If you have difficulty serving and need multiple attempts or addresses, additional fees may incur. Ultimately, if you win, you add these costs to your judgment ([us.vlex.com](https://us.vlex.com/source/14841/chapter/chapter-10-16-small-claims-and-consumer-claims-425197767#:~:text=,16)), so defendant reimburses. If you lose, you eat the cost.

- **Serving businesses:** If defendant is a company, find its RI **registered agent** (search RI Secretary of State business database). Sheriffs often prefer serving the agent because it’s clear. If it’s an out-of-state company not registered in RI, serving any officer or any place of business in RI works, or you might have to serve through RI’s Secretary of State (in some cases the Sec of State can accept if foreign company doing business without registering, as implied agent under law). That’s rare in small claims. Usually it’s local small businesses – find out if it’s an LLC so you list “XYZ LLC” and serve its registered agent (e.g. John Doe at 123 Main St).
  
- **Service on individuals with tricky situations:**
  - If the defendant is in jail or prison, you can have the sheriff serve them there (incarcerated persons can be served – coordinate with facility).
  - If in the military and stationed away, service can be done on base via certified mail or on the individual on leave, but note the complications under SCRA (if they are active duty, they can delay the case).
  - If defendant is a minor, you serve their parent/guardian.
  - If defendant has a guardian or is mentally incapacitated, serve the guardian or conservator.

- **When is service complete:** For personal service, it’s complete upon hand-off. For abode, when left at dwelling with appropriate person. For mail, when they sign the receipt (the date of receipt).
  
- **After service – next steps:** Once served, the clock ticks for answer (20 days). If no answer by then, you as plaintiff can move for default judgment (with affidavit of service and military status). If answer comes, case proceeds to mediation/trial.

- **If service is done but no proof on file by hearing:** If defendant doesn’t answer and you show up seeking default, the judge will ask for return of service. If it’s not in the file, they likely continue the case to allow time or deny default until proof is filed. Always bring a copy of the sheriff’s return or constable affidavit to court if you have it, to show the judge if it somehow didn’t reach the file yet.

To recap: **Use a sheriff or constable to serve the summons and complaint personally or at the defendant’s home or business** ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=Personal%20service%20of%20a%20summons,made%20in%20the%20following%20manner)) ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=4,officer%2C%20director%2C%20or%20manager%20thereof)). Do it promptly. File the proof of service. Without proper service, the court won’t proceed, so double-check that step. Improper service is a top reason small claims get delayed or dismissed. If the defendant was not correctly served according to these rules, any judgment could be void. So follow the letter of the rules for service to ensure your case can be heard on the merits.

## 9. Defendant’s response and default  
Once the defendant is served in a Rhode Island small claims case, they have an opportunity (and obligation) to respond. If they don’t, you can potentially get a default judgment. Here’s the timeline and process:

- **Time to respond:** The defendant must **file an Answer within 20 days** after being served ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/FAQs.aspx#:~:text=You%20must%20file%20your%20answer,judgment%20may%20enter%20against%20you)). This 20-day window is counted starting the day after service (excluding the day of service). If the 20th day falls on a weekend or holiday, it extends to the next business day. For example, if served on April 1, the answer is due April 21 (assuming that’s a business day). The summons explicitly states this deadline ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/FAQs.aspx#:~:text=,debt%2C%20what%20should%20I%20do)). *Note:* This is unlike some states where defendants just show up by a hearing date – Rhode Island expects a written answer in small claims, same as other civil cases.

- **Form of Answer:** The defendant can use the **Small Claims Answer form** provided with the summons (or get one from the clerk) to write their answer. On it, they will typically deny the claim or admit part of it, etc. They can also include any **Counterclaim** on that form (a section might say “Counterclaim: ___ amount, reason ___”). The answer should be signed by the defendant (or their attorney if they have one). The defendant should file the original with the District Court clerk (the division where the case is) and mail a copy to you (plaintiff) or your attorney ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-FAQ.aspx#:~:text=,file%20a%20small%20claims%20case)). The answer might simply say “I dispute the claim” or give a short reason (small claims allows informal answers, not lengthy pleadings).

- **No answer filed:** If the 20 days pass with no Answer filed, the defendant is technically “in default.” As the plaintiff, you can then move for a **default judgment**. The court will not automatically enter default; you have to request it (through a motion or at least appear at the scheduled date and ask). Rhode Island’s small claims statute § 10-16-7 says, *“At the time set for answering, if the defendant fails to file an answer, the court shall enter judgment for the plaintiff by default, with proof of claim”* ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2009/title-10/chapter-10-16/10-16-7/#:~:text=US%20Codes%20and%20Statutes%20%3A%3A,7%20Judgment%20on%20default%20by)). In practice, the “time set for answering” is 20 days after service. The court may set a status date just after that for a default hearing. If not, you should file a **Motion for Default Judgment** along with:
  - Proof of service (return/affidavit) demonstrating defendant was served and time elapsed.
  - **Affidavit of military status** (stating defendant is not in military) ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/FAQs.aspx#:~:text=You%20must%20file%20your%20answer,judgment%20may%20enter%20against%20you)), as required by federal law.
  - Some evidence of the claim’s amount if the claim isn’t a sum certain. If your claim amount is a fixed sum (like a unpaid note or specific invoice), you might file an **Affidavit of Amount Due** verifying the amount. If it’s not a sum certain, the judge may require a hearing in your presence to testify to damages before entering judgment (essentially a default hearing). Under § 10-16-7, it sounds like the court may want “proof of claim” even on default ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2009/title-10/chapter-10-16/10-16-7/#:~:text=US%20Codes%20and%20Statutes%20%3A%3A,7%20Judgment%20on%20default%20by)) (to prevent abuses). Often, small claims judges will still ask the plaintiff a few questions under oath to ensure the claim is valid and the amount is supported, then grant default.
  - If everything is in order, the court enters judgment in your favor for the amount claimed (plus costs, perhaps interest if applicable). 

- **Default hearing:** Rhode Island small claims does not necessarily have a separate hearing if default is straightforward and paperwork is in order. But some divisions schedule a short calendar where if the defendant hasn’t answered or appeared, they call the case, see only plaintiff present, swear you in, perhaps confirm details, then enter default. Make sure to attend any scheduled court date even if you expect a default. If no hearing was set yet, file for default in writing after the answer deadline. 

- **Setting aside default:** If a default judgment is entered, the defendant can later move to “vacate” or set it aside. They would need to file a **Motion to Vacate Default Judgment** under District Court Civil Rule 60(b) grounds (excusable neglect, lack of service, etc.). Rhode Island tends to allow liberal vacating of small claims defaults if done promptly and with some excuse (especially lack of notice or a good reason). For example, if the defendant wasn’t actually served or didn’t understand the need to answer (maybe they thought they just show up), the court might vacate to let them have their day, particularly if they file the motion within a short time (often within 1 year maximum for excusable neglect; but the sooner the better, ideally within 10 days or so of learning of judgment). Under § 10-16-11, small claims specifically provides for vacating decisions or orders for cause and ordering a new hearing ([us.vlex.com](https://us.vlex.com/source/14841/chapter/chapter-10-16-small-claims-and-consumer-claims-425197767#:~:text=,16)). So yes, there’s a procedure to reopen defaults. The defendant should explain why they missed the response (e.g., never got served, was out of state, illness, etc.) and also that they have a potentially valid defense. If the court is convinced, the default can be removed and case reinstated. If vacated, the case goes on to be heard on merits.

- **If Answer is filed (contested case):** Once the defendant files an answer, the case is considered “at issue.” The court will typically schedule a **mediation session** (if both parties self-represented) ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=)) or a trial date. The answer might also include a counterclaim. If so, as the plaintiff, you must *answer the counterclaim* within (presumably) 20 days as well (District Court rules require answer to counterclaim within 20 days). In small claims, even if you don’t formally answer a counterclaim, the court will likely proceed to hear it, but advisable to file a short reply denying it. The court will treat the counterclaim like a plaintif’s claim that the original plaintiff must defend against. The scheduling will cover both.

- **Scheduling after answer:** Typically, the clerk will mail both sides a **Notice of Mediation** date or a **Trial date**. If mediation is scheduled (common), both must appear. If one party fails to appear at mediation or trial, default (or dismissal) can occur then. But that’s later. 

- **What the Answer can contain:** The defendant in the answer can simply deny liability or raise defenses (like “the product was as-is, no warranty,” or “debt already paid,” etc.). They might also list any affirmative defenses (like statute of limitations: “the claim is time-barred”) – though small claims answers are informal, raising a clear defense like SOL or illegality in the answer is wise so the court knows the issue. They can also claim any offsets (like “plaintiff actually owes me $100 for damage, so net is less”). If they have a separate claim *exceeding* $5,000, they could assert it but as we discussed the case may move out of small claims or they waive the excess. Usually, they’ll keep it ≤ $5k to keep it in small claims.

- **Consequences of not responding:** If no answer, default as described. A default judgment means the defendant loses without contest and has to pay the judgment (subject to them later trying to vacate or appealing if they had excusable neglect – note: in RI small claims, only *defendants* have the right to appeal a judgment , which includes default judgments; but if they didn’t appear at all, they might be limited to a motion to vacate rather than appeal, since appeal is within 2 days of judgment entry which they might miss not knowing). A default judgment is enforceable like any other (see Post-judgment collection section). It also will show on records/credit potentially if not paid.

- **If defendant responds by paying before or at answer:** If the defendant, upon receiving the complaint, decides to pay in full to avoid court, that’s great. If they pay before actually filing an answer, the plaintiff should then file a notice of dismissal (since claim resolved). Or the defendant might file an answer saying “I have paid the debt on [date].” If you agree payment was received, you can stipulate to dismiss or just not pursue and let case be dismissed. If partial payment is made, you can either adjust the claim amount accordingly (maybe amend complaint or just request judgment for remaining). 

- **Communication with defendant after service:** Often, being served prompts defendants to contact the plaintiff to negotiate. That’s allowed (just ensure to still adhere to court deadlines – you can mutually agree to extend answer time by stipulation if near 20 days and you think resolution is close; otherwise, the defendant should file any answer to preserve rights). If you settle, put it in writing and both sign and then notify court.

- **If the defendant is the **plaintiff** in a counterclaim scenario (since roles flip for counterclaim):** As a plaintiff, if you are served with a counterclaim in their answer, treat it like you’re a defendant on that part: you have 20 days to reply (though in small claims, often not strictly enforced if you address it at hearing). If you do nothing and skip court, the defendant could potentially get a default on their counterclaim against you.

- **What if the defendant files bankruptcy after being served:** That automatically halts the small claims case (the “automatic stay”). If you learn they filed bankruptcy, you must stop pursuing default or judgment – the case will be stayed. You can file a suggestion of bankruptcy at state court. Eventually that claim might be discharged (if it’s an ordinary debt) and the small claim becomes moot. If you get a default and then find out they were in bankruptcy at time, that judgment can be voided.

In short, after service, **mark the 20-day deadline** on your calendar. If no answer by then, prepare your default request. If an answer arrives, get ready for mediation or trial. Always ensure proof of service is on record, since without it the clock hasn’t legally started. Rhode Island’s small claims process expects timely answer and will default if not – it’s not overly lenient (some states set a hearing regardless of answer, but RI requires the answer to avoid default). 

One more nuance:
- **Only defendant can appeal** a small claims judgment . So if the defendant defaults and then regrets it, their remedy is to either move to vacate in District Court or use the 2-day appeal window to claim an appeal to Superior (which would give a new trial). However, appeals must typically be within 2 days ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-12/9-12-10.htm#:~:text=by%20claiming%20an%20appeal%20from,attorney%E2%80%99s%20fee%20of%20fifty%20dollars)) of judgment and require paying costs (and possibly bond). A defaulted defendant likely won’t appear in 2 days to appeal (since they didn’t appear at all). So practically, their recourse is a motion to vacate default in District Court. The judge might set aside default if reason given. If they truly ignore it entirely, then the judgment sticks.

As a plaintiff, if you get default, wait the short appeal period (2 business days) before going to enforce, because the defendant might spring an appeal or motion quickly.

## 10. Hearing procedure  
If your case doesn’t end in default or settlement, it will proceed to a hearing (trial) in District Court. Here’s what to expect and how to prepare:

- **Setting of the hearing:** After an Answer is filed, the court typically refers the case to **mediation** first if both parties are self-represented ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=)). A mediation session will be scheduled (often on the same day set originally for trial). If mediation fails (no agreement), the case is then assigned a trial date, sometimes later that same day or on a future date ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=Cases%20filed%20by%20self,%E2%80%8B%E2%80%8B%E2%80%8B)). In some divisions, they might try immediate “hallway” mediation and if unsuccessful, go straight to the magistrate that day. In others, they mediate and if no success, schedule trial a few weeks out. If any party has an attorney, I believe they skip mandatory mediation (the statute authorizes mandatory mediation only for self-represented parties) ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE10/10-16/10-16-5.htm#:~:text=,of%20said%20mandatory%20mediation%20system)). In that scenario, you’d likely get a trial date set directly.

- **Representation by attorneys:** Rhode Island *allows* attorneys in small claims court – there is **no ban on lawyers** representing parties (some states prohibit lawyers in small claims, but not RI). However, note the special rule: a **corporation, LLC, or partnership with over $1 million in assets must be represented by an attorney** in small claims ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-FAQ.aspx#:~:text=,file%20a%20small%20claims%20case)). If a corporation has less than $1M assets and files an affidavit to that effect, it can be represented by an officer or employee (non-lawyer) ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-FAQ.aspx#:~:text=,file%20a%20small%20claims%20case)). So:
  - Individuals can represent themselves or hire an attorney.
  - Small businesses (with assets under $1M) can send an owner or authorized employee to court without a lawyer (but must file that affidavit of assets under $1M with the court) ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-FAQ.aspx#:~:text=,file%20a%20small%20claims%20case)).
  - Large companies (assets > $1M) if they are a plaintiff, they needed a lawyer to file in the first place (since RI requires that). If assets were misrepresented and discovered over $1M, the court could dismiss or require counsel.
  - If a defendant business fails to have counsel when required, the court could default them for not appearing through counsel.
  - The rule is basically to prevent big corporations from bullying in small claims without counsel while requiring small businesses to have flexibility. (This rule is per R.I. Gen. Laws § 10-16-3.1) ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-FAQ.aspx#:~:text=,file%20a%20small%20claims%20case)).
  - So you might see attorneys on either side, but often small claims litigants are pro se. The presence of attorneys can make it a bit more formal but still relatively informal environment.

- **Bench trial before a judge (or magistrate):** Small claims cases in Rhode Island are heard by a **District Court judge or a District Court small claims magistrate** (judicial officer). There is no jury trial at this stage. The trial is a bench trial – the judge is the fact-finder. (If a defendant wanted a jury trial, their remedy is to appeal a judgment to Superior Court for a de novo trial with jury – they can’t demand a jury in District small claims itself).

- **Informality of proceedings:** Rhode Island’s small claims statute emphasizes informality – strict rules of evidence do not necessarily apply ([us.vlex.com](https://us.vlex.com/source/14841/chapter/chapter-10-16-small-claims-and-consumer-claims-425197767#:~:text=Procedure%20By%20Claimants%20%20,Order%20of%20New)). This means:
  - Courtroom procedure is simplified. The judge may ask questions directly. They may not require strict authentication of documents – often if you have a relevant document, the judge will consider it, even if it’s hearsay like a receipt or letter, as long as it seems reliable.
  - That said, **bring all evidence** as if you needed to prove your case. Better to have too much than too little. Judges won't automatically do your work for you – you should present what you have, but the judge might guide the process a bit.
  - **No formal discovery** in small claims (no depositions or interrogatories pre-trial). However, if you needed info from the other side, small claims doesn’t really provide for that beyond what you learn in mediation or at trial by asking them.

## 1. Jurisdictional limits — MAXIMUM AMOUNT YOU CAN SUE FOR  
Rhode Island small claims court handles claims for money damages up to **$5,000** per case【1】. This $5,000 cap applies regardless of whether the plaintiff is an individual, a business (e.g. corporation, LLC, partnership), or a government entity – all are bound by the same monetary limit (no higher cap for businesses). The $5,000 limit is **exclusive of interest and court costs**, meaning you can still add applicable interest and allowed costs on top of the $5,000 principal claim【1】. There is no separate lower cap on small claims based on plaintiff type; Rhode Island does **not** impose (for example) a lower limit for a business suing a consumer – the limit is $5,000 across the board. 

Small claims in Rhode Island are authorized only for certain types of cases (covered in Section 2), but generally they include **“all civil actions at law wherein the amount in controversy is under $5,000”** (District Court has exclusive jurisdiction over such cases)【8】. You cannot split a larger claim into multiple small claims to evade the limit – if your total damages exceed $5,000, you must either **waive the excess** and sue for only $5,000 or file in the regular civil docket of District or Superior Court (over $5,000, the District Court and Superior Court have concurrent jurisdiction up to $10,000, and above $10,000 only Superior Court)【8】. Filing multiple suits for installments of a single claim would likely be dismissed as improper claim-splitting (and once a judgment on part of a claim is issued, you cannot later claim the rest). Rhode Island does not have a statutory limit on number of small claims cases you (as a plaintiff) can file per year – you can file as many as needed, as long as each is a bona fide separate claim. However, the court may sanction abuse (for example, if someone files dozens of frivolous suits). Unlike some states, there is no restriction like “no more than 2 claims over $2,500 in a year” – the only limitation is the $5,000 per-case cap.

**Plaintiff type considerations:** Individuals and small businesses are on equal footing regarding the $5,000 cap. One special rule: if a **business entity** is a plaintiff and has more than $1 million in assets, it must have an attorney represent it in small claims (see Section 13)【2】. But that doesn’t change the amount they can sue for (still $5,000 max). Government entities (e.g. a city suing for a small debt) also face the $5,000 cap in small claims. 

**Bottom line:** You can sue for up to $5,000 (not counting any interest/costs the law tacks on) in Rhode Island small claims court【1】. If you are owed more, you’ll have to pursue the case in a higher court or accept only $5,000. There is no provision to “increase” the small claims limit for any particular type of plaintiff or case – $5,000 is the hard limit for the claim amount【1】.

## 2. What types of cases qualify — EVERYTHING YOU CAN SUE FOR  
Rhode Island small claims court can only hear certain kinds of cases. By statute, it covers claims **“based on a contract, a retail sale, or services rendered”** (and certain tax disputes) where the amount sought is $5,000 or less【1】. In plainer terms, **small claims is for money that is owed under some kind of agreement or transaction**, not for personal injuries or other torts (wrongs) unrelated to a contract or sale. The goal is a quick, simple process for modest, straightforward disputes – typically debts, loans, failures to deliver goods or services, and the like. Importantly, you can only ask for **money** in small claims – the court **cannot** order someone to do something (no injunctions or specific performance) or grant you any other relief. If your situation doesn’t fit within these parameters, it won’t qualify for small claims. Let’s break down, by category, what **is** eligible in Rhode Island and what isn’t, with examples:

### Contract & money-owed disputes  
These are the bread-and-butter of small claims. If someone agreed to pay you or agreed to exchange something of value and didn’t fulfill that promise, it’s likely a contract claim. Rhode Island explicitly includes “**all ex contractu civil actions**” (meaning arising from a contract) under $5,000 in small claims jurisdiction【3】. Examples:

- **Breach of written contract:** *Eligible.* If you have a written agreement and the other side broke its terms, causing you financial loss (within $5k), you can sue. **Examples:** You hired a contractor under a written contract to renovate a room for $4,000, you paid a deposit of $2,000 and they never did the work – you can sue to get your $2,000 back. Or you bought equipment under a written sales contract, the seller failed to deliver, and you want a refund. All instances where terms in a signed contract weren’t honored (services not performed, goods not delivered or not as promised, etc.) are fine for small claims【1】. You should bring a copy of the written contract to court, but the small claims process is equipped to handle these. There is no special requirement before suing (though giving the other side a written demand or notice of breach is wise). The statute of limitations on written contract breach is 10 years in Rhode Island【11】 (so you have up to a decade from breach to file, though sooner is better).

- **Breach of oral/handshake agreement:** *Eligible.* Oral contracts are enforceable in Rhode Island (except certain types that must be written, like real estate sales, but those aside). If you had a clear verbal agreement and the other person didn’t follow through, you can sue for the damages, up to $5,000. **Examples:** You agreed verbally to lend your friend $1,000 and they’d repay in 3 months, but they haven’t paid a cent – that’s an oral contract (a loan agreement) and you can sue to recover the $1,000. Or you verbally hired someone to paint your house for $800, paid them $400 upfront and they never showed – you can sue for the $400 advance. Oral agreements for goods or services fall under “contract” actions allowed in small claims【1】. One caveat: the Uniform Commercial Code requires contracts for sale of goods over $500 to be in writing to be enforceable. So if an oral agreement was for a $2,000 purchase of goods, the defendant might raise the **Statute of Frauds** as a defense (no written contract). The court may still consider evidence of the deal (if e.g. goods were delivered or a deposit paid, it might infer a contract despite lack of full writing). The upshot is, small claims can entertain oral contract cases, but be prepared to prove the terms – bring texts, emails, witnesses that show the agreement existed. The limitation period for oral contracts is also effectively 10 years in RI【11】 (Rhode Island does not set a shorter limit for oral vs written – both fall under the 10-year catch-all for “civil actions not otherwise provided”) – but practically, evidence for an oral deal a decade ago may be scant, so don’t wait that long.

- **Unpaid personal loans (friends/family IOUs):** *Eligible.* This is one of the most common small claims scenarios: you lent someone money under the expectation of being paid back, and they haven’t paid. A loan creates either a contract (if terms like interest or deadline were specified) or at least an implied contract to repay. **Example:** You loaned an acquaintance $500 to help with rent, they promised verbally “I’ll pay you back next month,” next month came and went and no payment – you can sue for $500. Even without a formal promissory note, a loan is a contract. If you have anything in writing (even a text message “I’ll pay you back”), bring that. Absent writing, your testimony that it was a loan (not a gift) can suffice; the court will weigh credibility. There’s no pre-suit requirement, but a polite demand or reminder in writing is recommended before suing (and it serves as evidence the loan was acknowledged). Loans are subject to the 10-year limitation as a contract【11】.

- **Unpaid promissory notes:** *Eligible.* A promissory note is a written promise to pay (often with terms for installments or interest). If someone signed a promissory note to you and has defaulted on payments, you absolutely can use small claims to collect the balance, as long as it’s $5,000 or less. **Example:** You sold your car to a buyer who signed a note to pay $300 per month for 10 months ($3,000 total) but they stopped after 5 months – you can sue for the remaining $1,500 due under the note. Bring the original note to court. Notes are a subtype of contract, and Rhode Island considers actions “based upon a negotiable instrument” (like a check or promissory note) to be within small claims jurisdiction【3】. The statute of limitations for a promissory note (which is a negotiable instrument under UCC) in RI is generally 6 years from when the note was due or an installment was due【12】. (If it’s just one lump sum due on a date, 6 years from that date; if installments, arguably 6 years from each missed installment or from acceleration). But since the general contract limit is 10 years, even if you missed the 6-year window, some courts might still allow under common law contract theory – best not to risk it; file within 6 years of default.

- **Unpaid invoices/accounts receivable:** *Eligible.* If you are a business (or individual) owed money for goods or services you provided – e.g. an unpaid invoice or an open account – you can sue in small claims for the amount due (again, ≤$5,000). **Example:** You did freelance web design for a client and invoiced $2,000, but they never paid – that’s a straightforward claim for services rendered under either an express or implied contract. Or you delivered goods to a customer on credit (accounts receivable) totaling $4,500, and they haven’t paid – you can sue for the $4,500. These are contract-based claims (the client implicitly or explicitly agreed to pay for the goods/services). No special pre-suit actions are required by law, though sending the non-payer a final demand or reminder is standard business practice and helpful as evidence. If the sale of goods was involved, remember the 4-year UCC limit applies for breach of a sales contract【10】 (so don’t wait beyond 4 years to sue for payment for delivered goods). For services, 10-year limit【11】. In small claims, you can also claim any lawful finance charges that were agreed on (e.g. contract says 1.5% monthly interest on overdue accounts) – but **only up to $5,000 total** including those accrued charges up to filing. Interest continuing to accrue post-filing would be separate (the judgment can include pre-judgment interest as allowed – more on that in Damages section).

- **Unpaid rent (residential or commercial):** *Eligible.* Small claims can be used to recover overdue rent, as that arises from a lease contract. However – **not during an active tenancy if you are also seeking eviction.** Eviction (getting possession back) is a separate process handled on the landlord/tenant docket (Housing Court calendar of District Court), not in small claims. But if a tenant has moved out (or you’re only interested in the money, not eviction), you can sue for back rent or rent arrears in small claims. **Examples:** Your residential tenant moved out still owing you two months’ rent totaling $1,800 – you can file a small claim for $1,800. Or a commercial tenant let the rent slide for several months, left owing $5,000; you could claim $5,000 (the cap). Ensure you credit any security deposit appropriately (landlords must not double-dip – if you kept a deposit, subtract it from what’s owed unless it went to damages). For ongoing tenancies, landlords typically use the eviction court to get a judgment for possession and rent simultaneously. After tenancy, small claims is a convenient venue to just get the money judgment. Rhode Island’s statute of limitations for rent owed under a written lease is 10 years (written contract)【11】. (For an oral month-to-month, arguably 10 as well, but practically you’d act sooner). If you are a tenant who pre-paid last month’s rent or other fees and the landlord hasn’t returned what’s owed, that flips the scenario – the tenant can sue the landlord in small claims (see Landlord-Tenant section below for deposits, etc.). **Important:** If you want possession (eviction), *do not* file a small claim – file an eviction action. Small claims = money only.

- **Bounced or stopped checks:** Cashing someone’s check that then **bounces** (due to insufficient funds) or is later **stopped** by the issuer creates a cause of action. Rhode Island includes actions “based upon a negotiable instrument” in small claims【3】, which covers checks. *Eligible.* If someone paid you by check and it was dishonored by the bank, you can sue for the amount of the check plus any bank fees you incurred. **Example:** A customer wrote you a $300 check for an item, it bounced, and after being notified they still haven’t made the payment good – you can sue for $300 (and perhaps the $20 bank fee charged for the returned check). Some states have special penalties for writing bad checks (double or triple damages if not paid after a demand). Rhode Island’s law doesn’t provide a specific civil triple-damages penalty for bad checks; it mainly allows you to recover the face value plus protest fees or bank charges. (There is a criminal statute for writing bad checks over $100, but that’s separate – you could threaten to file a police report, but in civil small claims you just get the money owed.) To possibly get extra damages, you’d need to show something like common-law fraud, which is complex and unlikely in small claims. Stick to the basics: money owed on the check. It’s advisable (and courteous, and sometimes helps in court) to send the check writer a **demand letter** by certified mail, giving them e.g. 7–10 days to pay up, before suing. This creates a paper trail and might prompt payment. But legally, it’s not required in RI for small claims – you can sue as soon as the check bounces. Statute of limitations on a check: under UCC, 6 years from the date of dishonor or demand【12】. 

- **Open accounts/running tabs:** If you and someone had an ongoing financial relationship with multiple transactions and a balance due (like a **bar tab**, or you frequently spot a friend expenses and tally it up), you can sue for the final balance owed. *Eligible.* The law doesn’t use the term “open account” explicitly for small claims, but these function like implied contracts. **Example:** You and your roommate alternated paying utility bills and agreed to settle up later; at the end you paid $600 more than they did – that $600 can be claimed as money owed by agreement. Or a small business had an open account with a vendor where the vendor delivered goods repeatedly and kept a ledger – if the business owes $2,000 on that account, the vendor can small-claim it. Be sure to have an accounting of how you reached the amount (invoices, list of who paid what, etc.). Rhode Island doesn’t have a special SOL for open accounts; it would fall under the nature of the transactions – if it was sale of goods, 4 years from last purchase【10】; if services or just informal debts, 10 years【11】. 

- **Co-signer/guarantor claims:** If you co-signed a loan or guaranteed someone’s debt and ended up paying it, you have the right to seek reimbursement from the primary debtor. This is essentially an equitable or implied contract called “reimbursement” or “subrogation.” *Eligible.* **Example:** You co-signed an auto loan for a friend. The friend defaulted, and the bank went after you; you paid the remaining $4,000 to clear it. You can sue your friend in small claims for $4,000 because by co-signing you two had an understanding (even if not in writing) that they’re responsible for the debt and should repay you if you get stuck paying. Or if you guaranteed a lease and had to pay the rent, you can sue the tenant. Rhode Island small claims will hear these because they are fundamentally based on the debt contract that was guaranteed (thus rooted in contract). Bring proof you paid (receipts to the bank, etc.) and the original loan/guaranty documents if available. Sometimes the law might even *assign* you the rights of the creditor once you pay (subrogation), but even without that doctrine, it’s considered unjust enrichment of the debtor if they don’t pay you. Statute of limitations could be argued as 10 years (written guaranty contract)【11】 or perhaps 3 years if viewed as a contribution claim (but RI probably sees it as contract). Either way, don’t delay too long.

### Consumer disputes  
These are cases where you, as a consumer, paid for a product or service and something went wrong – defective goods, poor workmanship, not getting what was promised – and you want your money back or compensation. Rhode Island small claims can handle these **as long as the claim is ultimately about money owed due to a breach of the sale or service agreement**. Many consumer disputes also overlap with contract law or have specific consumer protection laws backing them. Key types:

- **Defective product or item not as described:** *Eligible.* If you bought a product (new or used) and it turned out to be defective or not as advertised, you can sue the seller for a refund or damages, up to $5,000. This falls under breach of warranty or misrepresentation in the context of a **retail sale of goods**, which is explicitly within small claims scope【1】. **Examples:** You purchased a used laptop for $800 from a local shop with the promise it was “fully functional,” but it keeps crashing – you could demand a refund or the cost of repairs. Or you bought new furniture for $2,000 and it arrived with significant damage and the store won’t replace it – you can sue for a refund or replacement value. Rhode Island, like all states, has **implied warranties** that goods are reasonably fit for use (unless sold “as is” or with warranty disclaimed). If a seller refuses to honor that, small claims is a remedy. Also, Rhode Island’s Unfair Trade Practices Act (UTPA) prohibits deceptive business practices – selling a known defective item might violate it. Under UTPA, a consumer can recover **actual damages or $200, whichever is greater, and possibly double or treble damages for willful violations**【6】. In small claims, you can invoke UTPA but remember the $5,000 cap. For instance, if actual damages are $1,000 and you prove the act was willful, the statute might allow up to $3,000, but the cap is $5,000 so you’re fine. You should also send the seller a notice or demand to fix the issue – not legally required in RI (unlike some states that require a 30-day notice under their consumer laws), but it’s persuasive to show the court you gave them a chance. If the purchase involved a **manufacturer’s warranty**, and they won’t honor it, you can also sue the manufacturer or the warrantor in small claims (federal Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act lets you use state small claims for warranty enforcement). 

- **Services not performed or performed poorly:** *Eligible.* If you paid for a service and the provider either didn’t do the work at all, or did a terrible job, you can seek compensation. This is a breach of the service contract. **Examples:** You hired a photographer for an event, paid a $500 deposit, and they never showed up – definitely you can claim the $500 back (and possibly any additional cost you incurred getting a last-minute replacement, to the extent within $5k). Or a cleaning company was paid $300 for a deep clean that they only half did – you might claim maybe $150 back for the portion not done or the cost to hire someone else to finish. Or you took your car to a mechanic, paid $600 for repairs, but the car is still not fixed due to shoddy work – you could sue either for a refund of what you paid, or the cost to redo the repair correctly (if you already went elsewhere and paid). In Rhode Island, service contracts are fully within small claims jurisdiction【1】. If a professional service was truly negligent (like say a $5,000 landscaping job that ruined your yard), you can treat it as breach of contract in small claims for the cost to fix. One thing: if the service is one regulated by a state board (e.g. contractor, auto repair), you might also file a complaint with that board, but that doesn’t preclude a small claim for your money. There’s no requirement to go through a licensing board first, though for contractors, Rhode Island’s Contractors’ Registration Board offers a dispute resolution process for homeowners (which can sometimes award up to $10,000 from a guaranty fund). But using the Board is optional; you can choose small claims for speed. As always, documenting dissatisfaction (photos of poor work, written complaints to the provider) helps your case. The limitation for suing on service contracts is 10 years in writing or (if not written) 10 under catch-all【11】, but practically, you’d act quickly while evidence and memory are fresh.

- **Cancelled events or no-show providers:** *Eligible.* If you booked and paid for a service tied to an event (caterer, DJ, venue, etc.) and they cancelled last-minute or just didn’t show, leaving you scrambling or causing loss, you can sue for what you paid and possibly consequential losses. **Example:** You rented a party hall for $1,000 for a wedding, but the hall double-booked and cancelled on you a week before, and they won’t refund your $1,000 deposit – you can absolutely small-claim that deposit. Or a DJ you paid $300 as a retainer never appeared and refuses refund – claim the $300 plus any extra you had to spend on emergency sound equipment, etc., as long as total is ≤$5k. These cases often involve breach of contract and sometimes emotional distress, but note: you generally cannot recover for stress or ruined expectations in small claims – only quantifiable monetary loss. But any extra cost you incurred due to the breach is fair game (keep receipts). If the no-show caused you to pay a premium for a replacement (e.g. $500 more), you might ask for that as damages. The court might award some or all of that if it’s a foreseeable result of their breach. Also, if you booked via a **written contract with cancellation terms**, bring that – if they violated those terms (like no refund when contract says you get refund on cancellation), it’s straightforward. If there’s a **force majeure** or similar clause they claim, that could be a defense (like if event was cancelled due to hurricane, contract might excuse them – but you might still get refund). Small claims will weigh fairness and contract language.

- **Auto repair disputes (overcharging or faulty repair):** *Eligible.* Car repair issues are very common. If a mechanic charged you for repairs that were not actually needed, or did not actually perform, or caused new damage, you can sue for the amount of money improperly charged or the cost to fix their mistakes. **Examples:** Mechanic charged $800 claiming to fix a brake issue, but the problem persisted and another mechanic found the first garage didn’t do much – you could sue the first mechanic for $800 (breach of contract – you paid for a service that wasn’t properly done). Or a body shop took your car after an accident, you paid a $1,500 job, but they used subpar parts or did incomplete work that violates your agreement – you can claim whatever portion of the $1,500 that represents the incomplete work, or the cost to correct it. If they caused additional damage (like scraped the paint), you could claim the cost to remedy that. Rhode Island has consumer protection regs for auto repairs (like requiring written estimates). If the shop violated those (e.g. charged way more than estimate without consent), that strengthens your case – you can invoke those rules as evidence of unfair practice. Possibly it could fall under UTPA as well for deceptive practice, allowing double/treble damages【6】, but again the small claims judge may or may not apply the multiplier. You should definitely *state* if something was done deceptively or willfully – it can’t hurt. No pre-suit demand needed by law, but practically, complaining to the shop’s manager or owner first is wise. Also, the RI Attorney General’s office takes auto repair complaints; while that won’t get you money directly, mentioning you’ll file a complaint might pressure the shop. If the dispute is simply over price (e.g. you think you were overcharged compared to the estimate or industry standard), the court will consider any signed estimate or whether the work justified the cost. Without a written estimate, by law the shop shouldn’t charge over $50 beyond a verbal quote unless authorized – that’s in RI’s Auto Repair Law. So that can be cited (e.g., “they initially said ~$500, but billed $900 without telling me – which is an unfair practice”).

- **Home improvement/contractor disputes:** *Eligible.* If you hired a home contractor for remodeling, repairs, etc., and they *did not complete the work or did it defectively*, you can sue for the money you lost. **Examples:** You gave a contractor a $2,000 deposit to remodel your bathroom, they did minimal work then disappeared – you can sue for the $2,000. Or the contractor did the job but with many defects and refuses to fix, and you have an estimate that it will cost $1,500 to correct their poor work – you can sue either for breach of contract to recover $1,500 (possibly to pay the new contractor). Generally, construction disputes often exceed $5k, but smaller jobs can fit here. Rhode Island’s Contractors’ Registration and Licensing Board (CRLB) can handle complaints and even award up to $10,000 from a guaranty fund, as noted. If your claim is, say, $7,000, you might consider going that route or suing in regular court; if it’s $5k or less, small claims is faster. No prerequisite to file with CRLB first (except if you want to claim against their fund, you must file CRLB complaint within 1 year). In small claims, present your contract (if written) and photos of poor work. Contractors in RI are required to be licensed – if yours wasn’t, mention that (that’s actually a violation that can help your credibility). The CRLB often encourages mediation; in small claims, a judge will cut to the chase and might ask “what portion of the money paid should be returned given the work quality?”. It often comes down to a reasonable value analysis if some work was done but flawed. **Special note:** If a contractor put a mechanic’s lien on your property but didn’t do the work, you would address that in a higher court (to discharge lien). But you can still small-claim the money.

- **Appliance/electronics repairs or warranty failures:** *Eligible.* If you paid for a repair service or you bought an appliance with a warranty and it wasn’t honored, you can use small claims. **Example:** You paid a tech $200 to fix your washing machine, it broke again two days later and the tech refuses to refund or redo under their “30-day guarantee” – you can sue for $200 (breach of contract and warranty). Or you bought a TV with an extended warranty, it broke, and the store/warranty provider is stalling or not honoring it – you can sue for the cost of repair or replacement up to $5k. Often, these warranty companies count on people giving up; a small claim gets their attention. The **Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act** (federal) allows you to recover attorneys fees in a breach of warranty suit – but since you likely won’t have an attorney in small claims, that’s moot, and small claims won’t grant attorney fees to you as a pro se. But mention the warranty terms and any breach. If it’s a manufacturer warranty, you might have to show you gave them chance to fix (most warranty procedures require you to go through authorized service, etc.). Document that you did so or they failed. Then sue either the retailer or manufacturer – small claims can handle either as long as jurisdiction (if manufacturer has presence or sufficient contacts in RI). The claim is still basically contract/warranty.

- **False advertising or deceptive practices (consumer fraud):** *Eligible,* in the sense that you can sue for the money you lost due to a deceptive act in a consumer transaction. Rhode Island’s Unfair Trade Practice Act (UTPA) gives consumers the right to sue for deceptive acts or misrepresentations in commerce【6】. So if a business tricked you into buying something under false pretenses, you can rescind the deal and get your money. **Examples:** A car dealer tampered with an odometer (rolled it back) on a used car you bought for $5,000 – that’s fraud. You could sue for damages (likely the difference in value or to undo the sale). Often these cases exceed $5k (because of the car’s value), but if it’s within $5k or you limit to $5k, small claims can be used. Another example: a gym advertised “Lifetime membership $1,000” and then shut down a year later without providing what was promised – you could claim the unused portion of your membership fee. Or a contractor used substandard materials after promising “top quality,” causing you loss – claim the cost difference. Under UTPA, if you prove the act was willful or reckless, the court *may* award up to three times your actual damages【6】. Small claims judges may hesitate to do punitive trebles formally, but they might award something in between actual and treble if fairness dictates. Keep in mind, **small claims is still limited to $5,000 total** – so even if the law might allow more, you can’t get above $5k here. If you want to pursue a large statutory punitive award, you’d need to go to Superior Court (which might be worthwhile if a clear-cut, egregious fraud occurred – plus attorneys’ fees can be awarded by UTPA in higher court). For many petty consumer frauds (under $5k damages), small claims is effective. No special notice is required under RI UTPA (contrast with Massachusetts which requires 30-day demand) – you can sue straightaway【6】. Still, sending a demand letter referencing UTPA and potential treble damages can prompt a settlement. For evidence, gather ads, written misrepresentations, witness statements, etc. If the case hinges on proving the act was deceptive, be prepared to articulate it clearly. Even if you can’t *prove* intentional fraud, you’ll at least get your actual loss if the judge agrees the result was unfair.

*(This category basically covers any scenario of a **consumer paying for something and not getting the benefit of the bargain** – small claims is designed for exactly that kind of dispute. Now, let’s clarify property damage and tort-type issues, which are more restricted in small claims.)*

### Property damage  
Small claims in Rhode Island is mostly for contract cases, but what about when someone damages your property? Generally, if the damage did **not** arise from any contractual relationship, it is a tort claim (negligence, etc.) – and Rhode Island’s small claims statute **does not include general tort (ex delicto) claims**. In fact, it specifies only ex contractu and certain consumer transactions【3】. That means if, say, your neighbor negligently drove into your fence causing $2,000 damage, you *cannot* use small claims for that – you’d have to sue in regular District Court (still $2,000 at issue, but under formal procedure). This is a key difference from some states where small claims can handle minor accidents or property damage; in RI, small claims is **not the forum for standard personal injury or property damage cases**. However, there are a few situations where property damage can be claimed in small claims **if it is tied to a contract or service**. For instance, if property was damaged by someone while performing a service for you (which is breach of their service contract), you can claim for that damage. We’ll differentiate: 

- **Vehicle collisions & traffic accidents:** *Not eligible in small claims.* A claim for damage from a car accident or any motor vehicle accident is a negligence tort. Even if the amount is small (e.g. $1,500), you must file as a regular civil case. District Court has jurisdiction for up to $5,000 in tort cases, but not via the small claims procedure – you’d file a civil complaint and possibly go through more formal process (which may involve lawyers and such, though you can self-represent there too). It may be tempting to try small claims because it’s simpler, but if the court sees it’s an auto accident, they will likely dismiss for lack of small-claims jurisdiction. (Also, note by RI law, if it’s only property damage to a vehicle, District Court hears it; if there’s personal injury, Superior Court hears it if over $5k. But anyway, not small claims.) If both parties *consent* to handle a fender-bender in small claims (no one objects), the court might possibly adjudicate it – but the defendant is likely to object or just remove it to the formal docket. So plan on the formal route for accidents. Also, small claims cannot handle claims for pain & suffering or medical injuries (which usually accompany accidents and easily exceed $5k). 

- **Intentional property damage or vandalism:** *Not eligible in small claims* (unless part of a contract relation). If someone purposely damaged your property (tore up your garden, broke your window in anger), that’s an intentional tort (and possibly a crime). Civilly, you’d sue for trespass or conversion, which are not covered by small claims jurisdiction. It must go to regular court. The only way it enters small claims is if, perhaps, the person who did it had some agreement with you that they violated (far-fetched for intentional acts). Essentially, no – small claims is not the venue for pure property damage claims.

- **Property damage caused by a service provider:** *Eligible, as part of a contract claim.* If damage occurred in the context of a contract, you can include that in your small claim as damages from breach. **Example:** You hired movers to move your piano for $300, they dropped it and caused $1,000 damage to the piano. You can sue the moving company in small claims for $1,000 (breach of the contract to perform services with reasonable care). Similarly, if a dry cleaner ruined your clothes (you paid for cleaning, got a shredded garment back), you can sue for the value of the garment – that’s within the service contract scenario. If a landlord’s contractor damaged your personal property while making repairs, and the landlord refuses to compensate (though this could also be framed as landlord’s breach of duty), you might try small claims if it’s essentially failure to fulfill a duty under the rental agreement (trickier, but possibly see Landlord section). Or a kennel (dog boarding service) lost or harmed your pet (pets are property in law) – you could sue for vet bills or the value under the service contract. These are all allowed because the duty arises from the contract for services or bailment.

- **Tenant-caused property damage (landlord suing tenant):** If during a tenancy, a tenant causes damage beyond normal wear and tear, the landlord may use small claims after the tenancy to recover repair costs (often this is just handled by deducting from the security deposit – but if damages exceed the deposit or no deposit, then a claim arises). This again is tied to the lease contract (implied agreement to not damage the premises). Many landlords handle it as part of deposit disputes (see Landlord–Tenant section on deposits). It’s eligible effectively as a contract claim.

- **Landlord-caused property damage (tenant suing landlord):** If a landlord negligently or intentionally damaged a tenant’s personal belongings (say during an illegal eviction they tossed your stuff), this is not clearly within small claims because it’s a tort or statutory violation (unlawful eviction). However, a tenant could frame it as breach of the rental contract’s covenant of quiet enjoyment or statutory duty – but realistically, those cases (wrongful eviction, property conversion) exceed small claims scope or belong in housing court. Likely not eligible. A tenant is better off suing in Superior Court for illegal eviction where punitive damages can be higher or contacting the police for conversion. Small claims could handle a straightforward claim like “landlord never returned my furniture they stored” as an implied contract/bailment if you spin it that way.

- **Neighbor disputes (trees, fences, water damage):** Not eligible in small claims. These are torts or nuisance issues, or seek injunctions (like to cut a tree). They belong in regular court (and often involve injunctive relief which small claims can’t do). So if your neighbor’s tree fell and broke your fence, that’s a negligence claim – not small claims in RI. If their dog chewed your $300 shoes, also a tort (property damage via animal). No contract between you and neighbor, so no small claim.

- **Pet injury or death caused by someone:** This is an emotional area – legally pets are property, so harm to a pet is property damage. As stated, if purely tort (neighbor’s dog attacked your dog), can’t do in small claims. If it happened in context of a service (groomer injured your pet), then it’s a contract claim (bailment – you entrusted pet, they had duty of care). That scenario can be in small claims (for vet bills, etc., not “emotional distress” value).

So the rule: **Property damage claims that arise from a contractual or service relationship are allowed; property damage claims arising from accidents or non-contractual incidents are not allowed in small claims.** For those not allowed, you must use the regular civil court process, even if the dollar amount is small. (This is a notable limitation of RI small claims as compared to many other states.)

### Landlord–tenant (financial only — possession/eviction is separate)  
Small claims can address **money disputes between landlords and tenants** that don’t involve physical possession of rental property. Eviction actions (to regain possession for nonpayment or breach) are handled on a different docket (District Court Housing Calendar) and follow summary eviction statutes – not small claims. But once the tenancy is over (or even during, if it’s strictly about money and not eviction), the parties can use small claims for certain issues. Common landlord-tenant money issues:

- **Unreturned security deposits:** *Eligible and very common.* Rhode Island law (Residential Landlord and Tenant Act) requires residential landlords to return a tenant’s security deposit (minus any legitimate deductions for damage or back rent) **within 20 days** after the tenant moves out and returns possession ([www.hemlane.com](https://www.hemlane.com/resources/rhode-island-security-deposit-laws#:~:text=20%20days%20after%20the%20end,address%20provided%20by%20the%20tenant)) ([www.hemlane.com](https://www.hemlane.com/resources/rhode-island-security-deposit-laws#:~:text=match%20at%20L124%2020%20days,address%20provided%20by%20the%20tenant)). If the landlord fails to return the deposit or wrongfully withholds any portion, the tenant can sue and, if the court finds the withholding was wrongful, the landlord is liable for **double the amount wrongfully withheld**【5】 (plus the tenant’s court costs and reasonable attorney’s fees)【5】. In practice, this means if your $1,000 deposit was unjustifiably kept, you can sue for $2,000. This penalty is meant to deter bad-faith retention of deposits. **Example:** You cleaned the apartment and fulfilled all obligations, but the landlord kept your $500 deposit without explanation or with pretextual reasons – you can sue for $500, and if the court agrees it was in bad faith, they’ll enter judgment for $1,000 (double) plus your filing fee and possibly statutory interest. Rhode Island’s deposit law (R.I. Gen. Laws § 34-18-19) supports the double-damages【5】. Usually, to get the double penalty, you must show the landlord acted **wrongfully or in bad faith** – e.g. they made up damages or missed the 20-day deadline with no excuse. If it’s a simple oversight and they return it late during the suit, the judge might just award the deposit itself. But often, failure to comply with the 20-day rule is presumptively wrongful unless the landlord proves some bona fide dispute. Note: The law also requires landlords to pay **annual interest of 5%** on security deposits held for over a year【7】. That interest (minus a 1% admin fee) is due to the tenant at tenancy’s end or annually. If the landlord didn’t pay that, you can claim the unpaid interest too (though it might be small). For deposit cases, **no formal demand is required** – the statute’s 20-day rule essentially acts as the demand. But it doesn’t hurt that you send a letter after move-out reminding landlord of address for return. If they ignored that, it bolsters bad faith. Statute of limitations: a security deposit essentially becomes due 20 days after termination, so you should sue within 10 years of that date (it’s a statutory obligation – treated like contract)【11】. In reality, most deposit suits happen within months.

- **Last month’s rent or prepaid rent refunds:** *Eligible.* If you gave a landlord prepaid rent (some landlords take first and last month upfront) and you didn’t end up using that last month (e.g. you moved out early or properly terminated the lease early), or the lease did not actually run that long, you might be owed a refund. Or if you overpaid rent (like landlord mistakenly charged extra month). These scenarios can be handled in small claims. They’re straightforward “money owed under the lease.” Example: You paid last month’s rent $1,200 at move-in, but ended up leaving prior to the last month and properly gave notice; the landlord re-rented to someone else immediately, effectively double-dipping that month – you can claim $1,200 back (since they incurred no loss for that period). There’s no special statute about last month’s rent like there is for deposits, but general contract principles and unjust enrichment apply. 

- **Damage to rental unit or cleaning fees (landlord claims against tenant):** After move-out, if a tenant caused damages beyond normal wear, or left the unit dirty requiring cleaning, the landlord might claim part of the deposit or send a bill. If the tenant disputes these or they exceed the deposit, the landlord can sue the tenant in small claims for the costs. *Eligible.* However, practically, landlords usually just subtract from deposit and then the fight happens when the tenant sues for deposit (and landlord counterclaims for any excess damage). In Rhode Island, if a landlord *wrongfully* withholds an amount, they suffer double penalty – but if there were legitimate damages equal to the deposit, no penalty. A landlord who believes damages exceed deposit can go for a small claims judgment for the rest. Example: Tenant’s pet caused $700 damage to carpets, deposit was $500, landlord can sue for the extra $200. These are breach of lease (tenant’s duty to maintain premises) claims. Documentation is critical – the landlord should have move-in/move-out condition evidence, repair bills, etc. A tenant could defend that the charges are excessive or items were normal wear. The small claims court will often try to be fair and might not give 100% of a dubious claim. Also note, if the landlord did not follow the *20-day rule* for returning deposit or itemized deductions, they risk the double damage penalty on deposit portion【5】 – meaning sometimes it’s safer for a landlord to not touch the deposit and instead sue directly (but that’s rare). Landlords should be mindful: failing to send an itemized list of damages within 20 days forfeits their right to keep any deposit and *could* forfeit the damages claim too (since that’s considered wrongful withholding). But they could still possibly sue for damages beyond the deposit – though the tenant will use the landlord’s procedural failure as a defense or to claim the double penalty. Small claims judges see a lot of deposit/damage fights and try to be equitable within the statutory framework.

- **Rent abatements or counterclaims for repair issues:** If a tenant had serious repair issues (landlord’s failure to maintain) and as a result withheld rent or moved out, they might claim they are owed money (like a rent abatement or reimbursement for repairs they paid for). These can be brought in small claims by the tenant as a **counterclaim** if the landlord sues for rent, or as a direct claim by the tenant if, for example, the tenant moved due to uninhabitable conditions and wants back some rent. Rhode Island law provides that if premises are not habitable and landlord is notified and fails to fix, a tenant can make repairs and deduct (up to certain amounts) or potentially recover damages. A tenant could, say, sue for the cost of a necessary repair the landlord refused to make (like you paid $200 to fix heat because landlord wouldn’t – arguably recoverable as breach of lease). Or if living conditions were awful due to landlord’s neglect, you might argue for a partial rent rebate (but usually that’s used as a defense in eviction, not an affirmative small claim). However, small claims could entertain it if framed as “landlord breached the lease by failing to provide X, and I suffered Y monetary loss.” It’s a bit complex to quantify. More often, these issues appear as defenses or set-offs in a landlord’s suit for rent. Note: Rhode Island allows tenants to use the Housing Court for code issues; but no requirement to do that first. So yes, such claims are *technically eligible*, but success may vary. If you go this route, have evidence of the condition, notices you gave the landlord, and perhaps an expert statement if needed.

- **Wrongful eviction or lock-out damages:** If a landlord performed a self-help eviction (locked you out without court order) – that’s illegal in RI. The tenant could sue for damages (e.g. cost of temporary housing, value of belongings if disposed, statutory damages). The Residential Landlord-Tenant Act allows tenant to recover possession or terminate and recover up to 3 months’ rent or treble damages, plus costs and fees, for an unlawful ouster (R.I. Gen. Laws § 34-18-36). That treble damages likely puts it out of small claims if rent was sizable, and it’s a serious matter – likely better in a higher court. If it’s small (your rent was $800, triple is $2,400), you could technically do it in small claims (under $5k). But since it involves equitable relief or complex statute, District Court might not handle it in small claims session. Possibly though – if you only seek the money, not reinstatement, a sympathetic small claims judge might. It’s a gray area. The safer path is in regular court, or call the police (since lock-out’s illegal). 

- **Commercial tenancy disputes:** A commercial landlord can sue a former tenant in small claims for unpaid rent or damages if under $5k, just like residential. No separate penalty statutes for deposits (since those laws apply to residential), so it’s straightforward contract. A commercial tenant could likewise sue for return of a security deposit or other overpayment. Those follow the lease terms strictly (if lease said deposit is refundable, etc.). Many commercial disputes exceed $5k, but small ones (like a small office deposit $1,000 not returned) fit fine in small claims.

**To summarize landlord-tenant in small claims:** security deposit return cases are the #1 scenario (tenant as plaintiff)【5】, and minor property damage or rent debt cases (landlord as plaintiff) are also common. Eviction, habitability, and illegal eviction issues usually go through specialized processes or exceed small claims, but portions of them can end up in small claims as money claims. Always bring the **lease agreement** or rental contract if written, and **photos, move-in/move-out checklists, correspondence** as evidence. Note the double-damage deposit law to leverage if you’re a tenant【5】, and the 20-day requirement ([www.hemlane.com](https://www.hemlane.com/resources/rhode-island-security-deposit-laws#:~:text=20%20days%20after%20the%20end,address%20provided%20by%20the%20tenant)). 

### Employment & wages  
If you worked and didn’t get paid, small claims can often help as a quick way to recover earnings. However, Rhode Island’s Department of Labor & Training (DLT) has an administrative process for wage complaints that can sometimes be used instead – but you are *not required* to go through DLT; you may directly sue in court (and for some claims, going to court might allow additional damages). Here are typical scenarios:

- **Unpaid wages or final paycheck:** *Eligible.* If an employer did not pay you for work you performed – whether it’s a last paycheck after quitting/firing, or any regular pay period – you can sue for those earned wages. Rhode Island law requires final wages to be paid by the next regular payday or within 24 hours of demand, depending on circumstances. If the employer’s failure is willful, the law (R.I. Gen. Laws § 28-14-19, -20) says they can be liable for an additional **double the amount in damages** (sometimes called a waiting time penalty)【5】. Also, Rhode Island law provides costs and **attorney’s fees** to a prevailing employee in a wage suit. So, example: your job ended and they owe you $800 for the last week, which they haven’t paid even after requests – you can sue for $800, and the court can award up to $1,600 (double) plus your court costs if it finds the nonpayment was wrongful【5】. In practice, small claims judges *do* often enforce the wage statutes. Even if not, you’ll get the $800 at least. No prerequisite to file a wage claim with DLT first – you have the option of going directly to court (DLT is optional for employee, mandatory for 3rd party complaints like AG). If you do file with DLT, they might investigate and even take the employer to court on your behalf, but that can be slow. Many employees use small claims for speed, especially for relatively small sums. Bring any evidence of hours worked (timesheets, pay stubs showing pattern, etc.) and evidence you weren’t paid (maybe a bounced paycheck or a letter from employer acknowledging nonpayment). The statute of limitations on wage claims is likely 3 years (wage claims are a statutory liability arguably within 3-year limit for statutes) – but some treat it as breach of oral contract (then 10 years catch-all). Federal FLSA is 2 (or 3 if willful). It’s best to act within 3 years to be safe ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=,2%20years)). Also note: if it’s a **minimum wage or overtime** violation, federal law (FLSA) allows double damages and is often handled in federal court, but you can also sue under RI state law which closely tracks FLSA – small claims might still award the due wages and possibly liquidated damages. But large overtime cases usually exceed $5k.

- **Unpaid overtime, commissions, or bonuses:** *Eligible.* If your employer promised overtime pay, or commissions, or a bonus you earned, and didn’t pay it, those fall under contract/wage law and can be claimed. **Example:** You’re in sales and earned $2,000 in commissions last quarter per company plan, but employer refuses to pay since you left the job – you can claim the $2,000. Or a bonus: if it was agreed (say in an offer letter: “$500 bonus if employed on Dec 31” and you were but they didn’t pay), you can claim that. Commissions and bonuses can be tricky – ensure you actually satisfied the conditions. If it was discretionary, you might not win unless you show it was essentially earned. Rhode Island’s wage law covers commissions in many cases (if it’s something that could be determined and due). They should be paid at termination if “definitely determined and due” under the agreement. If an employer willfully withholds a due commission or bonus, that same double-damages statute can apply【5】. Proof: bring the commission plan or emails promising the bonus and evidence of meeting targets.

- **Unreimbursed business expenses:** *Eligible.* If your employer owes you reimbursement for expenses (mileage, travel, supplies) by company policy or agreement, and hasn’t paid, you can claim those costs. It’s not exactly wages, but it’s money owed under employment. **Example:** Company policy says they’ll cover mileage for work trips, you drove 300 miles ($0.50/mi, $150) and they never paid you – you can small-claim the $150. Or you bought a work uniform for $100 after being told it’s reimbursable, but they didn’t pay – claim $100. These are straightforward contract issues. Keep receipts and any policy text or email approving it.

- **Freelancer or contractor not paid:** If you did work as an independent contractor (not an “employee”), you can still use small claims to get your payment – it’s just a breach of contract since labor laws might not apply. For example, you delivered consulting services with an invoice of $4,000 and client hasn’t paid – small claims as discussed under unpaid invoices (Section 2, Contracts) covers it. No wage laws needed; just enforce the contract.

- **Misclassification (employee vs contractor) claims:** If you contend you were really an employee and thus entitled to certain wages or overtime, that might be more complex than small claims can handle – usually handled by DLT or a larger suit. Small claims can’t give a ruling “you were an employee for 2 years” easily. They care about money owed. If, however, the core is simply “I worked X hours and was not paid minimum wage or overtime for those,” you can frame it as wages owed, and the misclassification is background. The employer might argue you’re not entitled because you were a contractor – so to win, you’d essentially have to persuade the judge you functioned as an employee (control, schedule, etc.). That’s a complicated legal test – likely not ideal for a small claims forum. That kind of dispute might be better at DLT or Superior Court if the amount is large. If small amount, you could try – but be prepared with evidence of the working relationship.

- **Better forum note:** Rhode Island’s DLT Wage Claim division will pursue claims (particularly straightforward ones like final paycheck) and can sometimes get penalties and ensure payment without court fees. However, DLT might take a while and they usually handle smaller employers or clear violations. If you want the double damage penalty or it’s urgent, court might be better. Also, if it’s a large company or you might get attorney’s fees, some attorneys take wage cases to Superior Court (if >$5k). But if it’s, say, $1,000 and you just want it quickly, small claims is great.

One caution: If you are a union employee with a collective bargaining agreement, wage disputes might have to go to arbitration per the contract, not court. Small claims would likely dismiss if there’s a binding grievance procedure. Similarly, certain types of claims (like ERISA-governed benefits or public employee pay disputes) might not be small-claimable. Most typical private employment wage issues, however, are fine.

### Personal services / professional disputes  
This category is similar to general consumer services but includes things like personal care services, lessons, etc., often provided by individuals or small businesses. You pay for a service concerning personal or family matters, and are dissatisfied or didn’t get what you paid for. These are handled as breach of contract or failure to perform cases in small claims:

- **Salon and spa services (hair, nail, etc.):** *Eligible.* If a hairdresser or cosmetologist seriously botched a service that you paid for, you can seek a refund or the cost to fix the damage. **Example:** You paid $200 for a hair coloring and the stylist’s error left your hair green and you had to get it corrected for $150 elsewhere – you could sue the original salon for $200 (maybe more if you had additional costs due to the mishap, like the $150). In reality it’s hard to put a dollar value on a bad haircut beyond what you paid, but if it caused extra expense or clearly didn’t meet what was promised, ask for at least a refund. Another example: A nail salon used improper technique and you got an infection requiring $75 in doctor visits – you could claim that $75 plus the service fee. These turn on whether the service was performed with reasonable care. In small claims, a judge won’t usually award for pain or embarrassment, just out-of-pocket losses. But if hair had to be re-dyed or took months to recover, you might claim the initial cost back. No special laws here except maybe consumer protection if they lied about qualifications. It’s mainly contract for services. Document with photos of the botch and any communications (salon acknowledging an issue or refusing to fix). The statute of limitations on these is 10 years (contract)【11】, but obviously you’d act quick.

- **Personal trainers, coaches, tutors (lessons not delivered):** *Eligible.* If you paid for lessons or coaching and the person didn’t deliver the sessions, or they did but with gross incompetence, you can claim what you lost. **Example:** You pre-paid a package of 10 piano lessons for $500, and the tutor gave only 3 lessons then vanished – you can sue for the value of the remaining 7 (e.g. $350). Or an SAT tutor guaranteed certain instruction which they failed to provide fully (or you found out they were scamming) – claim whatever portion of fees correspond to what wasn’t done. If services were done but poorly (like you or your child didn’t learn anything because the tutor was unprepared), it’s subjective but you could still attempt a refund citing breach of implied contract (service not rendered with reasonable skill). Small claims judges may or may not accept a “I didn’t learn anything” claim unless egregious (they might chalk it up to buyer’s remorse unless there’s clear non-performance). Stronger if specific promises were broken (e.g. “10 hours of instruction” but they only did 5; or “I will cover these topics” and didn’t). Provide any written agreement or advertisement to show what was expected. 

- **Catering and event services:** *Eligible (overlap with earlier event cancellations).* E.g. you paid a caterer deposit $1,000 and they delivered subpar or insufficient food – you could seek partial refund for the unsatisfactory portion. Or they no-showed (as earlier example) – refund deposit. These are service contracts. Possibly also misrepresentation if they claimed certain credentials or menu and didn’t deliver, which could fall under consumer protection for deceptive practice (maybe allowing extra damages, though in catering it usually is just breach). Photographs of what was provided vs promised can help.

- **Pet services (grooming, veterinary, boarding):** *Eligible for money aspects.* If a pet groomer or boarder fails to perform (e.g. you paid for a grooming that injured your pet or was not done), you can sue for a refund or vet bills from any injury (see earlier note: that’s a contract/bailment scenario). **Example:** Groomer was to groom your dog for $80, instead they nicked the dog badly requiring $200 vet care – you can sue the grooming salon for $280 (the $80 fee + $200 vet cost) for negligence in performing the service. Or if a kennel lost your pet or allowed it to be hurt, you can claim the vet costs or in extreme case the value of the pet (though assigning value to a pet’s life is limited to market value, which is tough – emotional value is not compensated). Usually, these businesses have you sign waivers limiting liability; those aren’t always enforceable if they were grossly negligent, but could reduce what you get. Small claims will weigh any signed waiver but might still award something if facts are bad. For vet clinics: if a vet truly commits malpractice, that’s generally beyond small claims (and typically more than $5k if serious). Minor veterinary overcharges or a simple error costing extra treatment might be handled if under $5k. But standard of care issues often need expert testimony, not suited for small claims. 

- **Medical or professional services:** *Not usually small claims.* For instance, you can’t sue your doctor in small claims for malpractice – those cases require expert witnesses and typically exceed $5k. Or your lawyer for messing up a case – also not small claims (and RI malpractice claims require certain procedures in higher court). So, small claims is **not** the venue for professional malpractice. If it’s a simpler matter like you paid a dentist $300 for a procedure that they didn’t actually do (e.g. paid for cleaning, they skipped half your teeth) – you could claim that as a contract issue perhaps. But serious claims – no.

- **Daycare or childcare issues:** If you paid a daycare and they didn’t provide care (e.g. facility closed and they didn’t refund pre-paid fees), you can claim unused fees. Or if a daycare lost some of your child’s property or caused minor property damage, you can claim for that. If any harm came to the child, that’s outside small claims (personal injury). But pure monetary disputes with daycare (refunds, deposit not returned, etc.) are fine. 

- **Memberships and subscriptions:** *Eligible.* Gym memberships, club memberships, etc., if you canceled properly and are owed a refund, or they overcharged you, you can claim that. (Rhode Island has a Health Club Act requiring pro-rata refunds if a gym closes, for example). If a company ignores that, you can small-claim the remaining value you paid for. Provide your contract and proof of cancellation. 

Basically, any personal service agreement where money changed hands and the expected service wasn’t delivered as agreed is a small-claimable contract issue. 

### Loans, debts, and financial  
This overlaps with contract and consumer categories but focusing on informal financial arrangements:

- **Money lent to friends/family:** *(Already covered above under oral contracts and personal loans.)* Yes, you can sue to recover loans you made【1】. Provide any proof such as an IOU note, Venmo records labeled “loan,” or witness to the agreement if possible. Even a text like “I’ll pay you back next week” works. Often these cases come down to credibility if no paperwork – the court will ask the defendant, “Did you borrow this money?” If they admit some or all, or if they come up with a shaky story, you likely win. People sometimes claim it was a gift; the burden can shift – but if it’s a sizable amount, courts often presume not a gift unless evidence suggests otherwise.

- **Shared expenses among roommates or partners:** *Eligible.* If you and a roommate or significant other pooled money or one paid more of joint expenses with the understanding of settling up later, that’s enforceable. **Example:** You and a roommate agreed to split utilities, but you ended up paying all of them for a year totaling $1,200, and roommate never paid you their share $600 – small claim that $600. Or an unmarried couple split, and one had paid the entire rent for last few months expecting half from the other ($900 each) – can claim the $900. These “implicit contract” cases might be a bit he-said/she-said, but if you have messages or it’s logical (why would you pay all if not expecting share?), judges often find in favor of equal sharing absent contrary evidence. If it’s an ex-partner situation involving lots of intertwined finances, small claims might only handle clear-cut pieces (like “I gave them $2,000 to buy a car in their name with promise of repayment” – yes claim that; but “I supported them for two years” – not likely recoverable because that’s seen as gratuitous unless you can pinpoint it as a loan).

- **Event cancellations (engagements, weddings):** If an engagement is broken, Rhode Island follows an approach (likely no-fault return of ring in many cases). But an engagement ring – the giver can sue to get it back or value if the other won’t return it. However, *giving* back a ring isn’t a money judgment – small claims can only do money. So the giver would ask for the **value** of the ring (say $3,000) if the ex-fiancée refuses to return it. That is allowed – it’s basically suing for property conversion or return under a conditional gift theory. This is not contract per se, but courts treat an engagement ring as a conditional gift – if condition (marriage) not met, ring should be returned. That’s a recognized principle in RI. Because it’s a property claim technically, one could argue it’s tort, but courts likely see it as a sui generis equitable claim and might allow it. There’s precedent in many states that engagement ring return can be handled in small claims as it’s straightforward. For other gifts in a broken relationship – most are not recoverable unless they were explicitly loans or conditional. If you can prove something was a **loan** (like “I’m giving you $5k for school, pay me back when you can”), then it’s a loan case. If it looks like a gift (birthday gift, etc.), you’re out of luck. 

- **Shared property after break-up:** If you bought something together with an ex and it’s in one person’s possession, small claims cannot decide property division (like who keeps the couch) – that’s not a money claim unless you frame it as “They have the couch we jointly paid $1,000 for, so I want $500 as my share.” That effectively seeks money for unjust enrichment. Small claims can do that – splitting value. But it can’t order the couch sold or given. The judge might say, “Either pay them $500 or relinquish the couch,” but formally they’ll only issue a money judgment. If the piece of property can be divided or valued easily, sometimes parties find it easier to just pay the half. If it’s something with sentimental value, this gets messy. But yes, you can attempt to recover the value of your portion of jointly owned property. Provide proof of purchase and contributions. This is essentially a **partition of personal property** idea done via money. Not guaranteed the court will entertain it, but it might, since ultimately it’s a debt claim (you unjustly have benefit of my share).

- **Credit card debts or bank debts:** *Note:* Creditors like banks or collection agencies can file small claims against consumers for debts (if under $5k) – Rhode Island does not bar assignees from small claims. However, often those agencies use District Court regular or Superior for larger, because attorneys are usually involved. In small claims in RI, a corporation >$1M assets must use an attorney【2】, and attorneys can participate anyway. So you might see collection companies suing there. As a defendant, you’d defend as in any case (if you owe it, you might just get a payment plan or default, if not, you can argue mistaken identity, etc.). The rules are similar except more informal evidence rules (they might not have to produce full chain of title of the debt – though a magistrate should still require some proof you owe the amount). If you’re a debtor being sued in small claims, be aware the plaintiff still has to prove you owe the money (they can’t just assert it without any documentation). If they have account statements or original contract, they present those. If you believe the debt is beyond the statute of limitations (credit card, typically 3 or 6 years depending on contract type), you must raise that as a defense. Rhode Island’s SOL for credit card (open accounts) is arguably 4 years (if viewed as sale of goods, some argue credit card is 10 as written contract – unclear in RI; 4 years for store credit vs 10 for generic credit is debated). Judges might lean toward 10 for a standard credit card contract because it’s a written contract not under UCC sale. It’s a defense to discuss with a lawyer if significant. But in a small claim, a pro se defendant can certainly assert “this debt is from 8 years ago, beyond statute.” The magistrate will apply the law if so. 

- **Bank account disputes:** If a friend promised to pay half of a joint account overdraft and didn’t, or you co-signed a loan and you paid it (we covered that), those are all fair game as contract or unjust enrichment. 

In summary, if money changed hands (or was supposed to) between private individuals and wasn’t returned or properly spent, small claims can usually sort it out, as long as you can frame it as “they agreed to pay me or share costs, and now owe me X dollars.” Rhode Island small claims is very much about these everyday personal debts.

### Vehicle-specific  
We addressed accidents (not in small claims) and mechanic disputes (yes in small claims under consumer services). A few more car-related issues:

- **Car purchase issues:** If you bought a used car and it was a lemon, typically the price is over $5k but if it was under, you might try to recover some damages in small claims. Rhode Island has a Used Car Lemon Law (for cars ≤ $3,000 or under 7 years old with certain mileage) that gives you rights to return the car within 30 days if major issues. If the dealer refuses, you could sue for a refund under that law. That might involve proving the defect and falls under consumer protection. If within small claims cap, could do. If it’s a private seller, “as is” rule likely applies (no recovery unless fraud can be shown). Fraud, as discussed, can be basis but tough to prove. For dealership disputes like missing title, improper registration fees, etc., small claims is fine to get fees back or force an issue via money.

- **Towing and impound fees:** If your car was towed illegally or without proper cause and you had to pay fines, you could sue the towing company or property owner for reimbursement – but this often involves statutory regs. Rhode Island requires certain notice for private property tows, etc. If they violated that, you can claim your $150 tow fee back as improper. That’s a small claim (breach of statutory duty scenario). It may be tricky to show it was illegal – sometimes best handled by a complaint to local police or city licensing board. But small claims is an option for the money part. If your car was damaged during a tow, and you can show the towing company’s fault, that is property damage – albeit it happened under a contract of some sort (either you contracted for a tow or if it was a mandated tow, the tow company had a duty). If it was police-ordered tow and car got damaged, that’s against the towing contractor maybe, but they might hide behind limited liability. Hard call, but you could attempt to sue the tow company for the damage as negligence (likely not small claims material because not contract with you, except maybe bailment once they had the car). The court might allow it as bailment (they had a duty to not damage your car once they towed it). Possibly yes – akin to dry cleaner scenario (you didn’t contract but property in their custody). Bailment claims (duty of care for item in custody) can be in small claims if value under $5k. That logic might allow you to sue a towing company for scratching your car while it was in their lot, etc. You’d need evidence they caused it (photos before/after). 

- **Parking lot damage:** If another known driver hit your parked car (not a contract scenario) – that’s an accident claim not in small claims. If an insured driver, you go through insurance. If unknown, you eat it or use your insurance. 

- **Traffic tickets or fines:** Not small claims – those go to traffic tribunal etc. (Some might think to sue city to refund a parking ticket, not happening; you must appeal through ticket system, not sue the city in small claims).

### Personal injury (small / minor)  
Most personal injury cases (slip & fall, car crash, dog bite, etc.) involve tort law and often damages beyond $5k, thus not suited for small claims. Rhode Island small claims courts do not handle typical personal injury lawsuits – those belong in regular court (District or Superior). **If** you have a very minor injury case (say you had a $1,000 medical bill and that’s it), could you use small claims? Technically, the statute doesn’t include “ex delicto” (tort) actions【3】, so a straight injury claim is outside jurisdiction. The defendant (or judge) could dismiss it as not allowed. So even a small medical bill from, say, slipping on someone’s icy step, cannot be pursued via small claims. You’d file a regular civil complaint in District Court (for $1k). It’s counterintuitive because many states allow it, but RI’s law is more restrictive. 

One workaround sometimes attempted: if there was *some* contract or transaction context. E.g., if you were a paying customer at a store and got hurt due to their negligence, you could sue for the cost of medical bills by framing it as breach of implied contract (they didn’t provide a safe environment implicit in allowing you as a customer). Not generally how it’s done – that’s clearly a tort claim at heart. I wouldn’t count on small claims accepting that. Maybe if both parties don’t raise an issue, the judge might just handle it to save everyone trouble, but by law, they could refuse. 

So, the bottom line: **personal injury claims do not go to RI small claims**. Use the proper civil court and possibly get a lawyer (especially if injury might be more than trivial). Even if small, small claims can’t award for pain & suffering or emotional distress effectively, and you can’t easily present medical expert evidence there. It’s not the forum.

The only personal injury-ish scenario that might appear is if an injury happened *in the context of a service contract*. For example, you paid for a guided tour and got slightly injured due to guide’s negligence – you could theoretically sue for the cost of the tour and maybe medical bills as part of breach of duty under the contract. But this is stretching – likely would be viewed as a tort. Another example: you hired a mover, and in moving a heavy item with you, the mover dropped it on your foot injuring you – you might try to claim your medical bills under the contract because he was contracted to move safely. Still, I suspect a RI small claims judge would say that’s beyond the scope (it’s an injury claim essentially). 

So unless it’s an extremely clear contractual duty that encompassed safety and a minor injury cost, personal injuries won’t be heard in small claims. 

### Specific statutory claims  
Certain Rhode Island statutes create causes of action with set damages or multipliers. Many we’ve mentioned in passing in relevant sections. To list a few that can come up:

- **Bad check statute:** Rhode Island doesn’t have a special civil penalty statute like some states (e.g., no triple check damages statute) – it criminalizes it and allows bank fee recovery, but no civil multiplier beyond perhaps using general UTPC if a business wrote a bad check (could argue deceptive). So nothing specific to invoke aside from demanding face value and any returned check fee.

- **Unfair Trade Practice Act (UTPA):** Already discussed for consumer fraud – allows *up to* 3× damages and attorney fees【6】. Only the Superior Court can award attorney fees (no attorneys in small claims typically), and trebling is discretionary. Small claims can apply it though. They likely cap at $5k total. If you want full treble beyond $5k, go to Superior. Use UTPA in small claims as leverage to at least get actual + maybe double if within cap.

- **Rhode Island Lemon Law (new cars):** If you have a lemon new car, it’s usually arbitrated or goes to Superior because the remedy is replacement or refund (often >$5k). Not a small claims matter.

- **Rhode Island Used Car Lemon Law:** This applies to used cars under certain conditions (price, etc.) – it gives a right to cancel sale if major defect in first 30 days or 1,000 miles. If dealer refuses, theoretically you could sue for the purchase price (if ≤ $5k) in small claims, basically enforcing the statute. That would be a straightforward claim: statute says if defect not fixed after 3 attempts, buyer can return car for refund. If dealer said no, buyer sues for refund. That could be done in small claims if price paid ≤ $5k. If price was more, out of small claims jurisdiction anyway.

- **Identity theft – civil:** Rhode Island has an Identity Theft Protection Act but enforcement is through AG or admin; individuals can’t easily get statutory damages under it except maybe via UTPC. If someone stole your identity and you had out-of-pocket costs, you typically can’t find that person or it’s part of criminal restitution. No direct small-claims scenario besides suing the person for fraud (which again is a tort not contract).

- **Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA):** Federal law - $1,000 statutory damages for harassment by a debt collector. You could sue a debt collector in state court. That can be done in small claims since $1,000 is under the limit. FDCPA allows attorney’s fees – but you likely won’t have an attorney in small claims, so you’d just aim for the $1k and any actual damages (like phone bills or emotional distress – though emotional distress might not be recognized in small claims). Honestly, plenty of pro se have used small claims for FDCPA because it’s simpler and some collectors don’t show up, leading to default $1k. It’s viable. Ensure you have evidence of the violation (letters, call logs). Some states might say federal FDCPA claim must be in federal court – not true, state courts have concurrent jurisdiction for FDCPA, so it’s allowed. If it’s a **Rhode Island** state analogue (Rhode Island has its own debt collection regulations through UTPA per se), you can also cite that. 

- **State wage laws:** Already noted, R.I. wage law allows double damages and fees【5】. Yes, small claims can and does enforce that (with cap caveat). Mention R.I. Gen. Laws § 28-14-19, § 28-14-20 when you sue an employer, to alert the judge that double is authorized for willful nonpayment. They often know it.

- **Rental housing laws:** Security deposit double (we covered)【5】, unlawful eviction (3× damages, but likely out of scope due to nature as explained). Also, if landlord knowingly uses unlawful lease provisions or fails to deliver essential services, the tenant can sue for actual damages (like cost of alternate housing if no heat for a week). That could be done in small claims (e.g. $300 for space heater and hotel during no heat). And Landlord-Tenant Act sometimes allows tenant to recover **3 months’ rent or 3× actual damages for certain violations** (like abuse of access). Those triple damages would again potentially exceed small claims if rent was big. But if rent was small and 3x is under $5k, one could attempt it. I haven’t seen that often – normally Housing Court injunctive relief is sought instead. But it’s possible.

- **HOA or condo statutory rights:** If an HOA violates some condo law giving owners rights to sue for fees or access to records, those typically involve injunctive relief or small statutory penalties – often not pursued in small claims but theoretically an owner could sue for, e.g., a $500 fine that HOA imposed contrary to law to get it back. That would be contract or statutory and fine for small claims. 

We can’t list every state statute, but the rule of thumb: if a statute gives a person the right to collect money (like a penalty or damages) and the amount is within $5k, and it doesn’t mandate a different forum, you can use small claims. E.g. if a law says “someone who suffers x can recover $500 from the offender,” you can sue for that $500 in small claims. 

**Other niche claim types in RI that one might bring in small claims**:  
- *Auto repossession deficiency*: If a car lender is chasing a small balance after auction, they might small-claim it. (Defendant can raise defenses like sale wasn’t commercially reasonable, but that’s technical). 
- *Bank overcharges*: If a bank wrongly charged fees, most people complain to the bank or CFPB, but you could sue for the $100 in fees. Banks might remove to federal court if they want, but likely they’d just pay or appear. 
- *Cell phone or cable billing errors*: If a company overbilled you and didn’t fix, you can sue for the amount. (Often they’ll fix on a regulatory complaint, but small claims is leverage).
- *Ticket or travel refunds*: E.g. airline lost luggage and legally owes you $600 under Montreal Convention – you might sue airline for $600. They might pay rather than send counsel for $600.
- *Airbnb/VRBO disputes*: If you rented a vacation home and it was misrepresented or unavailable and the platform or host refused refund, you can sue the host (if you have their identity) for the refund. Or Airbnb, but they’re out-of-state – might be hard to get jurisdiction unless contract says you can. Possibly small claims – some people have tried. It’s often arbitrated by their terms though. Host vs guest disputes can also end up in small claims for damages to property or deposit issues.

So small claims can be a forum for a variety of **statutory consumer rights** as long as the remedy sought is money ≤ $5k. If a law mandates something like “license revocation” or “injunction,” that’s not doable, but the money part is. 

### Counterclaims  
In Rhode Island small claims, if you are the **defendant**, you have the right to file a counterclaim against the plaintiff if you believe they owe you money or you have damages arising from the same matter. The counterclaim process is governed by the same small claims chapter. **Key points:**

- You must file the counterclaim in writing **with your Answer**, within the 20-day answer period ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/FAQs.aspx#:~:text=You%20must%20file%20your%20answer,judgment%20may%20enter%20against%20you)). The small claims answer form has a section for counterclaims – you’d write the amount and the reason. For example, landlord sues you for back rent $1,000, you counterclaim for $1,500 for property damage to your items due to landlord’s negligence (just an example). Or a contractor sues you for nonpayment, you counterclaim the work was defective and cost you money to fix. 

- The counterclaim, like the main claim, must independently qualify for small claims: it must be for $5,000 or less, and it must be a type of claim small claims hears. Typically, counterclaims are permissive in RI small claims – they don’t *have* to arise out of the same transaction (though usually they do). They can even exceed the original claim (and sometimes do if defendant’s losses are more than what plaintiff asked). However, **if a counterclaim exceeds $5,000, it will knock the case out of small claims**. The small claims statute says if a defendant’s counterclaim is above the small claims jurisdiction, the entire case should be transferred to the regular civil docket【3】. In practice, if you have a claim above $5k, you might choose to waive the amount above $5k and keep it in small claims by claiming “$5,000” only. Otherwise, the plaintiff (or you) may move it to District Court regular. Many times, defendants want it out of small claims if they have a large counterclaim or just to be in a formal court (maybe to allow discovery, etc.). The law (§ 10-16-9) basically allows a case to leave small claims if the counterclaim is beyond jurisdiction ([us.vlex.com](https://us.vlex.com/source/14841/chapter/chapter-10-16-small-claims-and-consumer-claims-425197767#:~:text=,Informality%20of)). So keep that in mind: if your damages are, say, $7,000, you can only counterclaim up to $5,000 in small claims (waiving the rest). Or file a separate suit in another court for full amount (but then possibly the small claim gets stayed or combined). The usual approach: **if counterclaim > $5k and defendant doesn’t waive excess, the case is transferred** to regular District Court (and then presumably defendant can seek full amount and plaintiff’s $ claim is still there too). 

- If you (defendant) file a counterclaim, the plaintiff must be notified. The clerk will typically send the plaintiff a copy of your answer/counterclaim. The plaintiff is allowed to file a reply, but in small claims that’s informal – they can simply come to court and defend. If the plaintiff wants to contest jurisdiction or something because of your counterclaim, they might raise it.

- The presence of a counterclaim does not by itself delay the hearing, unless it necessitates transfer or more preparation. Usually, the small claims hearing will handle both claim and counterclaim at once. The court will effectively treat it like a small two-way case and might offset judgments if both sides win something (or enter separate judgments – but often it’s netted out).

- **What if the counterclaim is unrelated?** In District Court ordinary, counterclaims can be compulsory (same transaction) or permissive (unrelated). The small claims statute doesn’t explicitly restrict counterclaims to the same transaction. So technically, a defendant could counterclaim any claim they have against the plaintiff, even unrelated. But if it’s unrelated and complex, the magistrate might ask why it’s being brought here. They could allow it if within $5k. Many times defendants use counterclaims tactically (like to discourage plaintiff or to force transfer). As long as it’s a valid claim and ≤ $5k, it can be heard. Example: someone sues you for a $500 loan, you counterclaim that a year ago they damaged your TV worth $400. Totally unrelated events, but small claims might let it ride to resolve all between parties. If it complicates things a lot, the court can separate them, but likely they’ll just adjudicate both.

- **If you don’t file a counterclaim now:** If your claim arises from the same transaction, you risk losing the chance to raise it later (res judicata could bar it as a compulsory counterclaim that wasn’t raised). If it’s completely separate, you can sue later or separately, but it might be efficient to do it now.

- **Exceeding jurisdiction scenario:** Suppose plaintiff sues for $4,000; you counterclaim for $7,000. Either party can ask to transfer the case out to regular court due to that (and the clerk might automatically flag it). In regular District Court, your case would now proceed under the normal civil rules (with case assignment etc.). You could also choose to **waive** the amount over $5,000 and just counterclaim for $5,000 to keep it in small claims. Often defendants do that if they prefer the simpler process.

- **Trial of counterclaims:** Both claim and counterclaim will be mediated (if applicable) and tried together. The judge will hear both sides of both claims. Potential outcomes: 
  - Plaintiff wins their claim, defendant loses counterclaim – judgment for plaintiff full amount.
  - Defendant wins counterclaim, plaintiff loses claim – judgment for defendant in the counterclaim amount (so effectively plaintiff owes defendant money; small claims judges can enter judgment for a defendant on a counterclaim).
  - Both win something – the court might offset. E.g. plaintiff proved $1,000 owed, defendant proved $600 on counterclaim, the court may net and give plaintiff $400 judgment. Or they might formally issue two judgments (one for plaintiff $1,000, one for defendant $600) and then parties could offset during collection. Usually easier to offset and give one net judgment.
  - Both lose (neither can prove their case) – then no one recovers anything, case dismissed on both ends.

- **Default scenario:** If a plaintiff doesn’t show up to contest your counterclaim, you could get a default on the counterclaim (and their claim might be dismissed). Vice versa, if defendant doesn’t show, plaintiff could get default on main claim and defendant’s counterclaim would be dismissed (assuming they didn’t show to pursue it).

- **Appeals and counterclaims:** Remember, only a defendant (or any party against whom a judgment is rendered) can appeal a small claims judgment . If both filed claims and each lost something, either could appeal aspects where they are “the losing party.” In practice, either can appeal a mixed judgment in District Court by claiming appeal to Superior within 2 days【4】. Then a whole new trial in Superior happens on all claims.

In short, **yes, defendants can counter-sue in Rhode Island small claims**, up to $5,000 ([us.vlex.com](https://us.vlex.com/source/14841/chapter/chapter-10-16-small-claims-and-consumer-claims-425197767#:~:text=,Informality%20of)). If the counterclaim exceeds the small claims limit and you insist on the full amount, the case won’t stay in small claims. It either gets removed to a higher court or you trim it to fit. And if you have a counterclaim, assert it now – small claims rules don’t explicitly force you, but general res judicata might prevent a separate lawsuit later if it’s same transaction.

### What you CANNOT sue for in small claims (specific exclusions)  
We’ve touched on many of these, but here’s a consolidated list of **non-eligible cases** for Rhode Island small claims court. If your case falls under these, you must use another court or process:

- **Evictions / Repossessions:** Small claims cannot handle eviction (landlord’s action to regain possession of property) or any action for possession of real property. Those go to the District Court Housing (for residential eviction) or Superior (for some commercial or foreclosures). So no eviction orders or orders ousting someone can be gotten in small claims – only monetary relief. Also, **replevin** (to recover possession of personal property, like you want your goods returned) is not available – small claims can’t order specific return of an item, only value of it. So if you want your property back specifically, small claims isn’t the right route (though you can sue for its value as substitute).

- **Family law matters:** Divorce, child support, alimony, child custody – none of that is in small claims (those are exclusive to Family Court). You can’t for example sue your ex-spouse in small claims for unpaid child support – that must go through Family Court enforcement (or R.I. Child Support Services). Likewise, any claims arising from marriage or cohabitation property division couldn’t be shoehorned into small claims (except as noted, discrete loans or items maybe). Also, **Paternity** or any family status issues – not small claims.

- **Defamation (libel/slander):** You cannot sue for defamation in small claims. Defamation usually seeks damages for reputation harm – inherently not suited to quick small claims, and it’s a tort. If you have a slam-dunk small defamation (say someone’s false statement cost you a $3,000 contract cancellation), theoretically damages = $3k, but small claims won’t handle it because it’s an intentional tort claim needing careful evidence of falsehood and damage. Superior Court is appropriate (and you’d likely need a lawyer). So no libel/slander suits in small claims in RI.

- **Real estate title or boundary disputes:** Not allowed. If you have a dispute over who owns land or location of boundary, only Superior Court (or Land Court if existed, which RI doesn’t separate) can decide that. Small claims cannot adjudicate property lines or enforce easements, etc. Similarly, **landlord-tenant disputes involving possession or status** – eviction as said, or whether a tenancy exists – not small claims. 

- **Malpractice (professional negligence):** Medical malpractice, legal malpractice, or any licensed professional’s negligence claims are beyond small claims. They require expert testimony and often high damages. Even if your damages are small (say a dentist cracked your tooth, $500 dentist bill to fix), you might think “I’ll sue in small claims for that $500.” However, to prove malpractice, you usually need an expert to show they breached standard of care – small claims doesn’t accommodate that well. The dentist will likely defend that it was a known risk or something. The court would probably find that too complex. Also, RI has a statute requiring a pre-suit inquiry for med mal in Superior Court. So no malpractice suits in small claims – those belong in Superior Court regardless of amount. 

- **Federal claims outside state jurisdiction:** 
  - Bankruptcy-related matters: If someone owes you but is in bankruptcy, small claims can’t override that – you’d file a proof of claim in bankruptcy court, not sue in state court (the debt is stayed/discharged).
  - Federal civil rights claims (e.g. suing police for brutality under 42 USC 1983) – definitely not small claims, those go to federal or superior.
  - ERISA (employee benefits), intellectual property, etc. – all federal domain typically, not small claims.

- **Class actions or multiple plaintiffs with aggregated claims:** You cannot turn small claims into a class action or group lawsuit on behalf of many. Each person’s claim must be filed separately (they might be consolidated by court if similar, but not a class). If many people have same issue (like a scheme affecting 50 consumers), each either files individually or a class action is filed in Superior Court. Small claims doesn’t have procedure for class certification. Likewise, if a couple of friends jointly own a claim (e.g. two co-signers paid a debt and both want reimbursement), they can both be named as co-plaintiffs in one small claim (that’s allowed – multiple plaintiffs can sue one defendant if it’s one common claim and under $5k collectively). That’s fine (like two tenants together sue one landlord for deposit return – yes, they can jointly sue, share the $5k cap). Just not classes beyond that.

- **Injunctive relief / restraining orders:** Small claims cannot issue injunctions (orders to do or not do something). If you need a restraining order or an order for a neighbor to stop a nuisance, you must go to Superior (or Family for personal protection orders). Small claims only deals in money judgments.

- **Suing the government (sovereign immunity issues):** You generally cannot sue the State of Rhode Island or its departments in small claims for money damages without following statutory claim procedures (which often direct to Superior Court if allowed at all). For example, if the state damaged your car with a snowplow, there’s a law requiring you file a notice with state within 90 days and then sue in Superior Court if over certain amount. Or if you want to sue a city for a slip and fall on a sidewalk, you must give notice in 60 days and sue in Superior Court (if >$1,500 usually). Also some claims against towns under $1,500 might go to District Court regular docket, not small claims (there is a statute letting District Court handle smaller municipal claims but not as small claims – they follow regular procedure). In any event, suing the government is tricky and not done via small claims typically. Example: you can’t sue the DMV in small claims because they suspended your license wrongly and cost you $500 – that’d be either an administrative appeal or a Superior Court claim (and likely sovereign immunity would bar money damages). 
  - Federal government absolutely can’t be sued in state small claims – if you had a claim under Federal Tort Claims Act (like USPS truck hit your fence $800 damage), you must file an FTCA claim with the federal agency and possibly sue in federal court if denied. Not state small claims.
  - One minor exception: If a municipality or state has explicitly consented to small claims for some reason (none known; they usually don’t appear as defendants there). So practically, don’t sue government in small claims; use the proper channels.

- **Workers’ compensation:** If you’re injured at work, you cannot bypass the workers’ comp system by suing in small claims for your medical co-pays or lost wages – comp is the exclusive remedy. So no work injury suits in small claims (even if employer didn’t have comp insurance, you’d then sue in regular court, not small claims, likely bigger issue).

- **Tax disputes and fines:** If you think your property tax is too high or you got a fine from a regulatory agency, you cannot use small claims to contest it. Taxes have specific appeal processes (to tax assessor, then courts). Fines by agencies must be appealed within that agency or proper court (like traffic tribunal for traffic fines). You can’t just file a small claim “City of Providence owes me $300 because my property assessment was unfair” – it will be dismissed (also likely sovereign immunity and failure to follow statutory appeal route).

- **Admiralty or specialized matters:** If you had, say, a small maritime salvage claim or a dispute about a probate estate, those are specialized (admiralty goes to federal, probate issues to Probate Court or Superior). Small claims would not entertain, e.g., “the executor of an estate owes me $1,000 from inheritance” – you’d go to Probate Court claim process, not small claims.

- **Criminal fines or victim restitution:** If you’re owed restitution ordered by a criminal court, you enforce it through that court, not small claims (though sometimes converting it to a civil judgment in Superior if needed). But you wouldn’t start a duplicate small claim – the law gives you other ways.

- **Suing someone who is bankrupt or protected by law:** If the defendant is in active bankruptcy, as mentioned, the case cannot proceed – automatic stay halts it. If they got a discharge of that debt in bankruptcy, small claims will dismiss as it’s uncollectable (they have immunity from that debt). Also, if someone is on active duty military and you try to sue them, the court will not enter default without a military affidavit and likely stay proceedings if they can’t appear ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/FAQs.aspx#:~:text=You%20must%20file%20your%20answer,judgment%20may%20enter%20against%20you)) (SCRA protection). That doesn’t bar the claim, it just delays if they’re deployed etc.

That covers most exclusions. Essentially, **small claims is only for money-only civil disputes that are not specifically excluded by law**. If it smells like a tort (injury, property damage not from a contract) or requires non-monetary orders, or involves governmental or family matters, it’s excluded. 

### Damages — what you can and cannot recover in small claims  
In Rhode Island small claims, the court can award **compensatory monetary damages** to make the winning party whole. It generally cannot award things like punitive damages (except as allowed by statutes like we discussed), nor can it award non-monetary relief. Here’s what you should (and shouldn’t) expect to recover:

- **Actual/Compensatory damages:** *Yes.* This is the core of every small claims case – the out-of-pocket loss or contract amount you are owed. This includes money you paid and deserve back, the value of property that was not returned or was damaged, the cost to repair a harm, wages you are owed, etc. The court will typically award the exact proven amount of actual loss (or a portion if you only prove part). Bring documentation (receipts, bills, estimates) to substantiate the amount. You cannot just speculate or ask for an arbitrary amount – it should tie to real economic loss.

- **Consequential damages:** *Often, yes,* if they are directly related to the breach and reasonably foreseeable. For example, if a photographer breached and as a consequence you had to hire another last-minute at higher cost, that extra cost is consequential damage – small claims can include that. Or if a contractor’s breach caused you to have to stay in a hotel for 2 nights ($200) due to no heat, you can claim that. The general contract rule of foreseeability applies, but small claims judges use common sense. They likely won’t award remote or highly speculative damages (e.g. “because my car repair was delayed, I missed a job interview and lost potential income” – too speculative, they’d deny that). But out-of-pocket expenses that naturally flow from the breach (like extra interest you had to pay due to someone’s breach) can be recovered. Keep receipts for these.

- **Incidental costs:** *Yes.* This is similar to consequential – any small costs incurred in dealing with the breach. e.g. postage for sending demand letters, travel costs to meet or remedy something. These are minor and often lumped into actual damages. The court might or might not specifically add them unless you highlight them. (Some courts might just say “that’s part of your costs of suing,” which you might get as court costs anyway if you win, but postage etc. is not a taxable cost normally – that’s overhead. They might not itemize little things like $10 of gas driving to fix an issue, unless it’s significant relative to claim).

- **Punitive or exemplary damages:** *Generally, no.* Rhode Island courts do not award punitive damages (damages meant to punish beyond actual loss) in small claims. Punitive damages in RI require showing of willfulness or malice and are typically decided in Superior Court by a judge or jury with specific instructions. A small claims magistrate will stick to compensation, except where a statute effectively authorizes a multiple of damages (which is somewhat punitive in nature). But those are specific (like wage double, deposit double, UTPC up to triple). Outside those, you can’t ask for “$5,000 to punish them for being fraudulent” if your actual loss was $1,000 – the court won’t do that on its own. Even with those statutes, the extra is more considered “liquidated” or “penalty” damages authorized by law rather than open-ended punitive. So unless a statute applies, no punitive. 

- **Statutory multiple damages or penalties:** *Yes, if applicable,* but within the $5k cap. As detailed, RI has a few: double damages for withheld wages【5】, double for wrongful deposit withholding【5】, up to treble for consumer protection willful violations【6】, etc. Small claims judges can award those statutory extras if you prove the conditions. They will not exceed $5k total though. For example, if double damages total to $5,400, they’ll likely just award $5,000 (since they lack jurisdiction above that). Usually, though, these come into play for smaller amounts (like deposit $500 becomes $1,000, fine). If a statute provides a fixed penalty per violation (like some consumer statutes might say $50 per day of violation), they can calculate that and award it if it’s sum-certain and still under cap. You should cite the statute to the judge so they know they have authority to do it. They generally want to follow the law. 

- **Attorney’s fees:** *Not usually, unless a specific statute or contract provides and it’s appropriate.* In small claims, most people don’t have attorneys, so attorney fees aren’t an issue. If you did hire one (rare, and might not even be allowed to fully participate for >$1M corp rule reasons), typically each side bears their own fees. But if there’s a law that says “prevailing party gets attorney’s fees” – e.g. wage claims【5】 or UTPC【6】 – the small claims court in principle could award a reasonable amount for any legal work. If you didn’t actually pay an attorney (you were pro se), you can’t get “fees” for your own time – that’s not allowed. If you had limited help (like you consulted a lawyer who charged you $200 to prepare some documents), you could attempt to claim that as a cost or damage, but the court might not grant it (they usually stick to statutory costs like filing fee, service). For example, deposit law allows attorney’s fee to tenant【5】 – if the tenant had one, they’d get it, but in small claims tenants usually don’t so that part of statute just isn’t utilized. So practically, attorney fee shifting isn’t seen in small claims except maybe in the rare scenario a business used an attorney and contract had fee clause – the court might incorporate that into judgment. They’d scrutinize though; some small claims judges are reluctant to add fees because it complicates things.

- **Court costs:** *Yes.* The prevailing party in small claims is entitled to certain court costs – specifically the filing fee and service of process fee. Rhode Island law says the court “shall allow or disallow costs accruing” as in other courts ([us.vlex.com](https://us.vlex.com/source/14841/chapter/chapter-10-16-small-claims-and-consumer-claims-425197767#:~:text=,16)). Usually, if you win, the judgment will include your filing fee and any sheriff/constable fee as **“costs”**. They might not mention it explicitly at hearing, but when the clerk enters judgment, they add them. For example, if you won $1,000 claim, plus you paid $55 filing and $45 service, the final judgment may be $1,100 with costs. Check the judgment paperwork to ensure costs were included. If not, you can motion to add them (likely a clerical oversight). If you lose, you might have to pay the opponent’s costs – but in practice, if a defendant wins (case dismissed), courts don’t often stick the individual plaintiff with the defendant’s costs, especially if defendant is a business that would have paid an attorney regardless. They have discretion. But statute says at least an attorney’s fee of $50 if removed to Superior on appeal, etc. (Those are details for appeals; not needed here). In any event, counts on paying your own costs if you lose.

- **Interest:** *Yes.* Rhode Island allows **pre-judgment interest** on civil money judgments – it’s 12% per annum from date of injury or breach for tort, and from date of filing for contract (generally) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2009/title-9/chapter-9-1/9-1-13#:~:text=%C2%A7%209,shall%20accrue%2C%20and%20not%20after)). However, I’m not 100% sure if small claims magistrates automatically add interest. In District Court (regular) and Superior, the clerk adds statutory interest to judgments. If your claim is clearly a liquidated debt from a past date (like an unpaid invoice from Jan 1, 2025), you can request interest from that date. Rhode Island General Laws § 9-21-10 provides interest on money judgments in any court ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2009/title-9/chapter-9-1/9-1-13#:~:text=%C2%A7%209,shall%20accrue%2C%20and%20not%20after)). Courts have interpreted that to apply even if not specifically pleaded. It’s 12% simple interest. So yes, you can get **pre-judgment interest** added in small claims. Many times, small claims decisions will just say “Judgment for $X plus interest and costs.” If not mentioned, you should politely ask, “Your Honor, I believe I’m entitled to statutory interest.” They will usually agree (it’s the law). Post-judgment interest (after judgment until paid) also accrues at 12% ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2009/title-9/chapter-9-1/9-1-13#:~:text=%C2%A7%209,shall%20accrue%2C%20and%20not%20after)). You don’t need to ask for that explicitly; it’s by law on any judgment. The court clerk or when you go to enforce, interest will be calculated from judgment date. So short answer: **yes, interest** (pre-judgment at 12% from the appropriate date, and post-judgment at 12% until paid) is recoverable ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2009/title-9/chapter-9-1/9-1-13#:~:text=%C2%A7%209,shall%20accrue%2C%20and%20not%20after)). It can be significant if the dispute has dragged on a long time. (For example, if someone didn’t pay a $5,000 debt from 5 years ago, roughly $3,000 interest has accrued – though note, in contract cases interest by law usually only from date of lawsuit filing for contract breaches, but some debate on that in RI; tort is from date of injury. If you had an invoice due 3 years ago and you sue now, you might argue interest from due date – some judges might give from filing only. It’s technical. But if you explicitly ask from due date citing equity, they might grant it since in Superior contract cases, interest is typically from date of breach at court’s discretion. Either way, you’ll get at least from filing to judgment.)

- **Pain and suffering:** *No.* Small claims will not award money for pain, suffering, humiliation, emotional distress, etc. Those are non-economic damages that are part of personal injury or intentional tort cases, which we’ve said aren’t in small claims. Even if you tried to attach it to something (like “I had so much stress from this breach, give me $500 for that”), the answer is generally no. An exception might be if some statute allows it (not really, or if a contract had a specified penalty for inconvenience – rare). They stick to quantifiable losses. They understand stress happens, but they can’t monetize it under small claims scope.

- **Punitive exemplary (common law):** *No.* Already covered. Unless you can fit it as statutory multiple, not happening.

- **Specific performance or replacement:** *No (only as money equivalent).* If you want the court to force the seller to give you the item you paid for, small claims can’t order that – they’ll instead order a refund. If you want your job back, small claims can’t do that – only money for lost wages. If you want the contractor to actually finish the work, small claims can’t compel them – you get money to hire someone else. So any non-monetary remedies are off-limits.

- **Rescission of contract:** Indirectly, yes in that they can effectuate rescission by awarding your money back in exchange for you returning the goods (the court might condition it, but usually not explicitly, it just gives money judgment and the parties sort out returning property). But small claims won’t formally void a contract or transfer title. They just issue monetary compensation. Practically, if you want to return a defective product and get a refund, that’s accomplished by suing for the price you paid (and presumably you’d give the item back after judgment or as part of a settlement – the court might not orchestrate that, but it’s implied you can’t keep the goods *and* get full refund). Some judges will explicitly say: “Judgment for $500 upon plaintiff returning the item to defendant within 10 days.” They can encroach a bit to ensure fairness (though strictly, they can’t enforce the return, they condition judgment on it).

- **File a lien or seize property before judgment:** No, small claims doesn’t issue attachments or preliminary injunctions. You get relief only at judgment, then enforce via normal collection means (see Post-judgment collection).

- **Collections costs post-judgment:** When you’re collecting, some costs can be added (like sheriff’s fee for execution, etc.). But in terms of judgment, the court at trial doesn’t pre-emptively give you future collection expenses. You handle that post-judgment if needed (e.g. exam fees, those can be tacked on by order of court during enforcement).

- **“Court costs” detail:** Court costs include filing fee $55, e-filing fees $17.50+3.25, perhaps service fee ~$45, maybe subpoena service costs if any, witness fee if you paid a subpoenaed witness $10. These can be awarded to winner ([us.vlex.com](https://us.vlex.com/source/14841/chapter/chapter-10-16-small-claims-and-consumer-claims-425197767#:~:text=,16)). They do not include travel, lost wages for attending court, child care, etc. – those are not reimbursed. Each party bears their own incidental costs of coming to court.

To be sure of getting costs and interest, it’s good practice in your complaint or at trial to say, “I also seek statutory interest and costs.” The judge/clerk likely will add them regardless because it’s standard, but stating it ensures it’s on record.

So, **in summary:** You can get **money** for the **actual losses** you suffered, including any legally provided multipliers or interest ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2009/title-9/chapter-9-1/9-1-13#:~:text=%C2%A7%209,shall%20accrue%2C%20and%20not%20after)). You cannot get non-monetary relief, nor compensation for subjective non-economic harm (pain, suffering, etc.), nor typical punitive damages, in Rhode Island small claims. Keep your claim to dollars-and-cents amounts that you can justify with evidence【1】. 

## 3. Where to file  
All small claims cases in Rhode Island are handled by the state **District Court** system. There is not a separate “Small Claims Court” building – you file in the District Court for the division (county) that has venue, and the case will be assigned to the small claims calendar there. 

- **Court name and divisions:** Rhode Island is divided into a few **Divisions of District Court** (by county or group of counties). Small claims are heard in the District Court of the division where you file. Specifically:
  - **Sixth Division District Court** – covers Providence County (and also Bristol County). This court is in Providence (Garrahy Judicial Complex). Many small claims fall here due to population.
  - **Third Division District Court** – covers Kent County (court in Warwick).
  - **Fourth Division District Court** – covers Washington County (court in Wakefield).
  - **Second Division District Court** – covers Newport County (court in Newport).
  
  You should file in the appropriate division based on the proper venue rules (see below). The official court name on your paperwork will be “District Court of Rhode Island, [Third] Division” etc. There is a small claims clerk or section within each District Court clerk’s office that processes these.

- **Venue (which location is proper):** Rhode Island’s small claims statute § 10-16-3 sets venue rules similar to normal civil cases ([us.vlex.com](https://us.vlex.com/source/14841/chapter/chapter-10-16-small-claims-and-consumer-claims-425197767#:~:text=,Order%20of%20New)). Generally, you should file in the division where:
  - The **defendant lives or has their principal place of business**, or
  - Where the **cause of action arose** (like where the contract was signed or to be performed, or where the transaction occurred).

  Practically, this often means:
  - If suing an individual: file in the division for the county they reside in. E.g. you live in Providence but the person who owes you money lives in South Kingstown – you should file in Fourth Division (Washington County) because the defendant resides there. (You being elsewhere doesn’t anchor venue; defendant’s residence does).
  - If suing a business: file where the business operates or is headquartered. If it’s an in-state business, that’s likely the division of its main office. If it’s an out-of-state company but has a local branch or did business in a certain county, you might use where the transaction occurred.
  - If the incident or contract happened in a certain county: you can choose that as venue even if parties live elsewhere. For example, you purchased a defective item in Kent County from a traveling vendor who lives out-of-state – you could file in Third Division (Kent) since that’s where the sale (cause of action) occurred. (There’s some flexibility, but it’s safer to stick to defendant’s locale if possible).
  - For **landlord-tenant deposits or rent**: Typically venue is where the rental property is (which is where cause arose and often where defendant landlord or tenant is). That tends to be the same division as property’s county.
  - If you file in the “wrong” division by mistake: The law allows a case filed in the wrong location to be transferred to a proper one at judge’s discretion rather than dismissed ([answers.justia.com](https://answers.justia.com/questions/answered/small-claims?page=157#:~:text=Small%20Claims%20Questions%20%26%20Answers,still%20be%20reported%20to%20credit)). Rhode Island statute § 9-4-4 basically says if filed in wrong county, the court may transfer it to correct one rather than throw it out ([answers.justia.com](https://answers.justia.com/questions/answered/small-claims?page=157#:~:text=Claims%20for%20Virginia%20on%20Feb,still%20be%20reported%20to%20credit)). So, worst-case, your case might get moved. But that’s a delay and hassle. So best to get it right initially.

  If you’re unsure of the defendant’s county, you can use things like addresses from bills, the Secretary of State’s business lookup (for a company’s registered agent location), etc. You can also call the court clerk’s office and describe the situation; they might guide you (“Oh, that town is in Fourth Division.” They won’t give legal advice on venue, but can confirm county divisions).

- **Filing in the wrong division:** If you do and the defendant objects in their answer or at hearing, the case could be transferred. If no one objects, sometimes it just stays (especially if it’s convenient or if defendant doesn’t mind). But since each division is a separate venue, a defendant could argue improper venue and likely get a transfer. Always better to file correctly to avoid that.

- **Electronic filing (eFiling):** Rhode Island has a mandatory **electronic filing system (Odyssey File & Serve)** for attorneys. For **self-represented litigants**, e-filing is *optional*. You are allowed to file paper at the clerk’s office if you prefer, or you can register and use the e-filing portal. The Rhode Island Judiciary website has an E-Filing user guide. If you e-file, you’ll upload your small claims complaint as a PDF and pay the fees online. The system will then generate a case number and electronic summons. **Note:** E-filing adds a $17.50 “case processing fee” and $3.25 tech surcharge to your filing fee ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=Civil%20Case%20Processing%20Fee%20to,%E2%80%8B)), as discussed earlier. If you file in person on paper, the clerk will still scan it into the system and those fees still apply ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=shall%20be%20assessed%20on%20a,%E2%80%8B)) (so either way it’s assessed). The advantage of e-filing is you can monitor your case online (through the Public Portal or File & Serve), receive notifications, and it’s quicker. If you’re not comfortable online, you can physically file. 

- **How to file (in person):** Go to the clerk’s office of the appropriate District Court division during business hours. Request a **Small Claims Complaint form** (some forms might be on a stand or you ask the counter clerk). Fill it out – including names, addresses, amount, and brief statement of claim (one or two sentences). The clerk can help ensure you have filled in required parts, but they won’t advise what to write substantively. Then you pay the filing fee ($55 + surcharges) by cash, check, or card. The clerk will stamp your complaint, assign a case number (e.g., “6SC-2026-000123”). They will then either give you a hearing date immediately or (more common) they will issue a **summons** (which will instruct the defendant to answer within 20 days and then likely set mediation after answer). In some divisions, they might schedule an initial court date ~6–8 weeks out and include that on the summons as “Answer by X date; Mediation on Y date.” It varies. The clerk will give you the original summons and complaint to serve on the defendant, and keep copies for the court file. Some courts mail the summons for you by certified mail (like small claims in some states do), but in Rhode Island, **you (the plaintiff) are responsible for arranging service** ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Small%20Claims.aspx#:~:text=Service%20of%20a%20summons%20can,by%20using%20the%20following%20methods)) – typically by sheriff or constable. The clerk will usually say, “Now you need to have this served on the defendant. Here’s the sheriff’s office info...” etc. (More on service in Section 8). 

- **If defendant lives out-of-state:** You can still file in Rhode Island if jurisdiction is proper (e.g., the contract was in RI or defendant has business ties here). But serving them out of state will be a bit more effort (possibly via certified mail or hiring a process server in that state). Also, an out-of-state defendant could later challenge RI’s jurisdiction if they have no substantial connection (lack of personal jurisdiction). That’s complex. If it’s borderline (like an online transaction, person lives far away), consider filing in their state’s small claims instead (if feasible and you’re willing to travel). Or see if they’ll just settle.

- **Filing multiple cases:** If you have multiple unrelated claims, you may file them separately in appropriate divisions. Rhode Island doesn’t have a limit per year or anything for number of filings, unlike some states. If you become a frequent filer (like a debt collector filing hundreds of small claims), the court might treat you normally but note if you’re an LLC with >$1M assets you need an attorney【2】. But an individual or small biz can file many, that’s fine.

- **What if you file in the wrong forum entirely (like Superior Court by mistake)?** If you filed a $3,000 claim in Superior Court, the attorney for defendant might move to dismiss or transfer, since Superior lacks jurisdiction under $5k (District has exclusive up to $5k) ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil.aspx#:~:text=The%20District%20Court%20has%20exclusive,of%20the%20defendants%20in%20the)). The likely outcome: the Superior Court justice would transfer it to District Court (Rule of Civil Procedure allows transfer when court lacks jurisdiction but another does). That wastes time. So correct is starting in District (small claims or regular docket). If you accidentally filed as a regular civil case in District (with a formal complaint) but it could have been small claims, the court might ask if you want to transfer it to small claims (especially if pro se). Possibly they’ll just treat it under formal track though. Better to choose clearly at start: if it’s ≤$5k and qualifies, file as small claim to keep it simple.

**Court addresses and contact:** The Rhode Island Judiciary website lists addresses and phone numbers for each District Court division. For example:
  - 6th Division: 1 Dorrance Plaza, Providence, RI.
  - 3rd Division: 222 Quaker Ln, Warwick, RI.
  - 4th Division: 4800 Tower Hill Rd, (Route 1), Wakefield, RI.
  - 2nd Division: 45 Washington Sq, Newport, RI.

Check which one fits your case’s venue. You can also find the **Small Claims filing instructions** on the court’s site or Rhode Island Legal Services which explains where to file【1】. 

- **Online hearing options:** Post-2020, courts sometimes hold certain hearings via Webex or phone. Generally, small claims mediations or trials are back in person. But check any current pandemic-era rules if applicable – at current date (2026) likely in-person. 

- **If you file in wrong venue and case transferred:** You will typically not have to pay a new fee; the file is just moved. But it causes delay and you’ll have to re-serve summons issued by new division perhaps. So try to avoid that.

To summarize: *File your case in the appropriate **District Court division** for the area connected to the defendant or dispute.* Use e-filing or paper filing as you prefer. The case will then be managed by that court’s small claims calendar. If unsure, calling the clerk to confirm venue is wise (they can at least confirm which division covers a certain city/town). Filing in the right place ensures your case isn’t thrown out or moved unnecessarily.

## 4. Filing fees  
When you initiate a small claims case in Rhode Island, you’ll need to pay the required filing fees. The fee structure is statewide (not county-specific), set by statute or court rules. Here’s the breakdown:

- **Small claims filing fee:** **$55.00** – This is the base fee to file a small claims case in District Court ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=Civil%20Filing%20Fee%20%20,%2480.00)). It covers filing the complaint and issuance of the summons. This fee is the same regardless of the amount of the claim (anywhere from $0.01 up to $5,000 is $55). It’s also the same for individuals and businesses. (This $55 is significantly lower than the fees for formal civil cases in higher courts, which is part of making small claims accessible).

- **Case processing and technology surcharge:** **$17.50** (processing) + **$3.25** (tech) – These are additional one-time fees assessed on any new civil case filed through Rhode Island’s electronic case management system ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=Civil%20Case%20Processing%20Fee%20to,%E2%80%8B)). Even if you file in person on paper, the clerk will enter the case into the e-system, so these fees apply in all cases. So effectively, a small claim costs **$55 + $17.50 + $3.25 = $75.75** total to file (plus a possible credit card fee if paying by card). These extra fees are authorized by R.I. Gen. Laws § 8-15-11 and court rules to fund the electronic filing system (Tyler Technologies) and other tech improvements. The “processing fee” goes to the vendor and the tech surcharge to the court’s tech fund ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=shall%20be%20assessed%20on%20a,%E2%80%8B)) ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=Civil%20Case%20Processing%20Fee%20to,%E2%80%8B)). Note that if you are **approved for a fee waiver** (in forma pauperis), these surcharges would be waived along with the base $55.

- **Service of process fees:** *Separate from court filing fees.* After filing, you need to have the defendant served with the summons/complaint. If you use a **sheriff** or **constable**, they charge a fee typically around **$45–$75** depending on mileage and number of parties (standard in-state service on one defendant often ~$45). For example, Providence County Sheriff’s Department had a statutory fee of $45 plus mileage (mileage is minimal for local addresses). Constables set their own rates but are similar. This cost is not paid to the court – you pay the sheriff/constable directly. If you win, you can add that amount to your judgment as a recoverable cost ([us.vlex.com](https://us.vlex.com/source/14841/chapter/chapter-10-16-small-claims-and-consumer-claims-425197767#:~:text=,16)). If you choose **certified mail** with court permission for out-of-state service, you’d pay postal fees (about $7–$10) for that. Typically, Rhode Island expects personal service by sheriff/constable (certified mail is not the norm except possibly for out-of-state – see Service section). Also note: if you need to serve subpoenas on witnesses, each service might incur a similar fee (usually ~$40–$50 if a sheriff serves a subpoena).

- **Motion fees:** In small claims, filing motions (e.g. a Motion to Vacate a default) usually does not require a separate fee. In Superior Court, certain motions (like summary judgment) have fees, but District Court small claims doesn’t charge for motions. (One exception: if you file an **appeal** to Superior Court after a small claims judgment, you must pay an appeal filing fee of $100 plus a $50 attorney fee to the other side as required by law ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-12/9-12-10.htm#:~:text=by%20claiming%20an%20appeal%20from,attorney%E2%80%99s%20fee%20of%20fifty%20dollars)). But that’s post-judgment, not part of initial filing.)

- **Fee waiver (in forma pauperis):** If you cannot afford the filing fee, you can apply for a fee waiver. You’d file a motion or affidavit of indigency with details about income, expenses, etc. The court may require a hearing or just review the affidavit. If granted, the court can waive the $55 and the $17.50/$3.25 surcharges (and often sheriff service fees can be waived or the sheriff will still attempt service and not require prepayment – they might get paid by state funds). Rhode Island law allows waiver of fees for indigent persons so that inability to pay doesn’t prevent access ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-FAQ.aspx#:~:text=attorney%20if%20you%20have%20assets,%E2%80%8B)) ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-FAQ.aspx#:~:text=attorney%20if%20you%20have%20assets,%E2%80%8B)). The forms for this are typically available (ask clerk for “Form # PC-8.1” or similar – they sometimes use generic forms). If you qualify (usually if you receive public assistance or have income below ~125% poverty, etc.), the judge will sign an order waiving fees. This should be done at the time of filing – bring your fee waiver application when you go to file (or file it electronically). Note: Even if fees are waived, if you win, the other side may still be ordered to pay the normal fees (the state doesn’t need reimbursement, but it’s fair the loser pays the fees you would have had to). In practice in small claims, if an indigent plaintiff wins, they often still get the “costs” awarded (since they had a fee waiver, there’s no out-of-pocket, but the statute says prevailing party gets costs – the judge might just not list costs in that case because none were paid).

- **Payment methods:** At the clerk’s office, they accept cash, money order, or check (to “Rhode Island District Court”). Many accept credit/debit cards but note: if paying by credit card, a **3.25% convenience fee** is applied (the same percentage as e-filing) ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=,%E2%80%8B)). So for $75.75, 3.25% is about $2.46 extra. E-filing online requires card or electronic bank payment and will also include that percentage on top of the $75.75. If you want to avoid the card fee, pay by check or cash in person. 

- **Are fees recoverable from the defendant if you win?** Yes, basic filing and service fees are awarded as part of “costs” ([us.vlex.com](https://us.vlex.com/source/14841/chapter/chapter-10-16-small-claims-and-consumer-claims-425197767#:~:text=,16)). The technology surcharge and e-filing fee likely count as part of filing fee (courts treat it as one cost). So if you win, your judgment should include $75.75 in costs (plus your service fee) on top of damages. If you lose, typically each party bears their own costs (though the judge could order the losing party to pay the winner’s costs; in practice, if a plaintiff loses, the court often does not make the defendant pay any costs because defendant likely didn’t incur any besides maybe service for a counterclaim or subpoena). If a defendant had to pay a filing fee for a counterclaim and they prevail, they could get that fee back from plaintiff as costs too. Costs are at court’s discretion but usually follow the outcome.

- **Fee for judgment enforcement:** Not a filing fee, but note: if you have to get an **Execution** (the document to have sheriff levy assets), the court charges $20 for issuing an execution writ ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=Civil%20Filing%20Fee%20%20,%2480.00)). Also, if you request a **garnishment or attachment** post-judgment, the sheriff may have fees (sheriff charges a fee per wage garnishment served, etc.). Those enforcement costs can generally be added to the amount the debtor owes (the execution form will allow adding sheriff’s fee and interest). But initially, you might have to pay them. There’s also a fee if you move the case up (e.g. appeals we mentioned $100 + $50). But that’s later stage.

For quick reference: 

- Filing a small claim: **$75.75** (plus card fee if applicable). 
- Typical Service by sheriff: ~$45. 
- Total upfront outlay: around **$120** (give or take, depending on service).
- If you subpoena a witness, subpoena forms are free but serving it and witness fee: maybe another $50 or so.
- These sums can be recovered if you win.

If you truly cannot pay, use the fee waiver option ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-FAQ.aspx#:~:text=attorney%20if%20you%20have%20assets,%E2%80%8B))— the court has forms and it’s not overly stringent if you show need (e.g. on SSI, or income near minimum wage and family to support, etc.).

**Always get a receipt** for any fee you pay. If filing in person, the clerk will give a receipt for the $75.75. If e-filing, you can print the confirmation. Keep it in case of any discrepancy.

In sum, expect to pay around **$76 to the court** to start your small claim ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=Civil%20Filing%20Fee%20%20,%2480.00)) ([www.courts.ri.gov](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/districtcourt/Pages/Civil-Fees-and-Costs.aspx#:~:text=Civil%20Case%20Processing%20Fee%20to,%E2%80%8B)), and about **$45–$65 to serve** the defendant (unless they will voluntarily accept service or you have alternate arrangement). These fees are then typically added to the judgment for reimbursement ([us.vlex.com](https://us.vlex.com/source/14841/chapter/chapter-10-16-small-claims-and-consumer-claims-425197767#:~:text=,16)) if you win, so the losing party ultimately pays them. Small price to pay (relative to higher courts) for pursuing justice on your claim. 

## 5. Statute of limitations  
A statute of limitations (SOL) is the deadline by which you must file your lawsuit, measured from the date the claim “accrued” (when the injury happened or contract was breached, etc.). If you file too late, the defendant can use the statute of limitations as a defense to get the case dismissed. It’s critical to know the SOL for your type of claim to ensure you are within time. In Rhode Island, the limitations periods are mostly set by Title 9, Chapter 1 of the General Laws. I will outline the common ones relevant to small claims:

- **Written contracts:** **10 years** – Rhode Island has a long limitations period for contract claims. The general catch-all provision says “all civil actions not otherwise limited by statute” must be brought within 10 years ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2009/title-9/chapter-9-1/9-1-13#:~:text=%C2%A7%209,shall%20accrue%2C%20and%20not%20after)). Since contract breaches aren’t given a special shorter limit (except sales of goods, see below), a breach of a written contract can be sued up to 10 years from the date of breach. For example, if someone signed a promissory note or written agreement and defaulted in 2016, you theoretically have until 2026 to sue ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2009/title-9/chapter-9-1/9-1-13#:~:text=%C2%A7%209,shall%20accrue%2C%20and%20not%20after)). (It’s always better to sue sooner – evidence and memory fade. But legally, 10 years is the window). **Citation:** R.I. Gen. Laws § 9-1-13(a) – “all civil actions...except as otherwise provided, shall be commenced within ten (10) years next after the cause of action shall accrue” ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2009/title-9/chapter-9-1/9-1-13#:~:text=%C2%A7%209,shall%20accrue%2C%20and%20not%20after)). 

- **Oral contracts:** **10 years** – Rhode Island does not differentiate a shorter SOL for oral vs written contracts in the statutes. Many states have, say, 6 for written, 3 for oral. Rhode Island’s general 10-year catch-all covers oral contract claims too ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2009/title-9/chapter-9-1/9-1-13#:~:text=%C2%A7%209,shall%20accrue%2C%20and%20not%20after)). So an unwritten agreement breach also gets 10 years. (But proving an oral contract after a decade is tough – still, legally you could). There is an arguable nuance: Under the Uniform Commercial Code for sales of goods (even if oral, see next), but for services or loans that were just oral, 10 years. **Example:** You lent someone money on a verbal promise in 2015 – you could sue as late as 2025 (though maybe not wise to wait). The cause accrues when they breached (if no set time, from date demand for repayment was made or a reasonable time passed).

- **Sale of goods (under U.C.C. Article 2):** **4 years** – This is a special statute: R.I. Gen. Laws § 6A-2-725(1) sets 4 years for breach of contracts for sale of goods ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=6,10%20years)). So if your claim is that a seller breached a contract to deliver goods or sold defective goods breaching warranty, you have 4 years from when the breach occurred. Usually, for warranty, breach is upon delivery of defective goods. For a contract to deliver goods by a date, it’s from that date if not delivered. **Example:** You bought a TV in 2020 that was warranted for 1 year and it failed in 2020 but you didn’t sue the store until 2025 – that’s 5 years, likely barred by this 4-year rule ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=6,10%20years)). However, note: if the warranty explicitly extended to future performance (rare, except some warranties promise future performance), sometimes the start date can extend. But keep it simple: 4 years from sale/delivery for product issues. *Caution:* If your purchase was also a consumer transaction, you might have separate limitations under consumer laws, but generally 4 is the rule. **Citation:** R.I. Gen. Laws § 6A-2-725(1) – “An action for breach of any contract for sale must be commenced within four (4) years after the cause of action has accrued” ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=6,10%20years)). (And accrual is when breach occurs or when default discovered for warranty issues ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=6A,1%20year))).

- **Negotiable instruments (checks, promissory notes):** **6 years** – Rhode Island’s U.C.C. Article 3 (Negotiable Instruments) gives specific limits:
  - For a check or draft, typically 6 years from when it is dishonored or 10 years from issue whichever earlier (UCC 3-118). 
  - For a promissory note payable on demand or at a definite time, 6 years after the due date or demand . 
  Essentially, an action on a negotiable instrument (like suing the maker of a promissory note or a bounced check) is **6 years**. **Example:** You have a promissory note due in full on Jan 1, 2018 – you must sue by Jan 1, 2024 . Or a check bounced on July 1, 2020 – sue by July 1, 2026. **Citation:** R.I. Gen. Laws § 6A-3-118(a) – (though not excerpted above, it aligns with general UCC rule: note payable at definite time = 6 years from due; if demand instrument, 6 years from demand).

- **Open accounts (running charges):** If it’s a store account or credit account for goods, arguably the U.C.C. 4-year applies per transaction. If it’s an account for services, default 10-year. But often for revolving credit like credit cards, courts often apply the written contract limit (10 years) because a credit card agreement is a written contract. (Some defense attorneys argue it’s an “open account” or “implied” and should be shorter, but RI doesn’t have a statute specifically for open accounts. Some states do (e.g. 6 years). Rhode Island’s compiled list in § 9-1-36 doesn’t list a special one for open account aside from the UCC for goods). So likely 10 if not goods. Still, many collection suits are filed well before 10 years anyway.

- **Personal injury (injury to person):** **3 years** – R.I. Gen. Laws § 9-1-14(b) says actions for injuries to the person must be filed within 3 years ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=9,3%20years)). This covers physical personal injuries (like slip and fall, car accident injuries) and also likely some intentional torts (assault, etc.). It also covers **injury to reputation** – slander/libel is under § 9-1-14(a) if spoken (1 year) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/title-9/chapter-9-1/section-9-1-14/#:~:text=,words%20spoken%2C%20and%20not%20after)), but personal injuries are 3 years. In small claims context, you wouldn’t typically have these, but for completeness: if someone had a small personal injury claim under $5k (which as we noted can’t go via small claims anyway), it’d be 3 years to sue in regular court ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=9,3%20years)). **Wrongful death** is similarly 3 years.

- **Property damage (to personal property):** Rhode Island doesn’t have a separate statute for damage to property (personal property). Some states pair it with personal injury at 3. Rhode Island’s compiled list (§ 9-1-36) lists “injuries to the person, generally – 3 years” ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=9,3%20years)), but does not mention property. Therefore, property damage likely falls under the 10-year catch-all ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2009/title-9/chapter-9-1/9-1-13#:~:text=%C2%A7%209,shall%20accrue%2C%20and%20not%20after)). That said, a property damage claim from a car accident often would be considered “injury to personal property” and many would assume 3 years (some case law in RI might apply 3 by analogy). But absent explicit statute, arguably 10. To be safe, assume **3 years** for any tort claim to be safe, but the law on property damage length is a bit unclear due to omission (some lawyers might still treat it as 3 as a practical rule). Rhode Island’s Supreme Court has implied property damage from negligence is also 3 years (because they often treat property damage analogous to personal) – but I don’t have a direct citation here. Let’s lean on caution: treat it as 3. For example, if someone negligently damaged your property in 2019, file by 2022 to be sure. If you filed after 3 but before 10, you could argue no specific statute for property so 10, but a judge might not buy that. Best not to risk it. (Given small claims doesn’t accept tort anyway, moot for small claims, but if it did in contract contexts, contract timeline would apply if it's from breach of contract causing property damage.)

- **Fraud (common law):** If someone defrauded you (a tort), the SOL is 10 years catch-all unless considered personal injury. However, Rhode Island has a **“discovery rule”** for fraud: the clock starts when fraud is discovered or should have been discovered (by case law, not statute). Also, there’s a trick: § 9-1-20 says if a person fraudulently conceals the cause of action, that tolls the statute until discovery. So effectively, for fraud, you get more time if you didn’t know of it. But if you discovered it late, you must sue within a reasonable time of discovery (the fraud statute doesn’t specify, but courts might say promptly or at most within 10 from discovery, but I think it’s just equitable). In practice, try to sue within 10 of the actual act or within 3 of discovery, whichever is later, to be safe.

- **Statutory penalties or claims:** Many statutory causes borrow the catch-all 10 if not specified. For example, a claim under the Unfair Trade Practices Act – the Act doesn’t specify a period, so 10 years likely applies (though one could argue it’s like a tort of deceit which is 3, but UTPC claims in RI have been allowed beyond 3 when done as contract/warranty breaches, as far as I know). Usually, you wouldn’t wait that long for a consumer claim, but legally probably 10.

- **Judgments:** A Rhode Island judgment (if you win one) is valid for **20 years** before you’d have to renew it ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=9,3%20years)). That’s not a statute of limitations for filing, but an enforcement limit. Mentioning: once you have a small claims judgment, you can enforce it for up to 20 years (and can renew if needed). 

- **Municipal claims notice:** Not a limitation but a condition: if suing a city/town for something allowed, you must send a notice of claim within 60 days (e.g. injury from a defect in street) ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=39%20%20,3%20months%20from%20last%20day)), and then overall SOL is 3 years or so for the suit. But since small claims can’t do personal injury, a common scenario might be damage to car from pothole – that requires 60-day notice to city, and suit in Superior Court if at all. 

- **Out-of-state or after defendant leaves state:** R.I. Gen. Laws § 9-1-18 tolls (pauses) the SOL if the defendant is out of state and not amenable to service for a period of time. So if someone who owes you money fled RI for 2 years, that 2 years might not count in the limitation period. This prevents defendants from running out the clock by hiding out. So you may have longer if they were absent. (This can be complex if they could be served by other means anyway, but that’s detail).

- **Tolling for minors/incompetents:** If the person entitled to sue is a minor or of unsound mind at the time of accrual, the statute typically is tolled until they turn 18 or regain competence (with a cap of certain years). Specifically, for personal injury, § 9-1-14.1 says minors can sue until age 21 for injuries (3 years after turning 18) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/title-9/chapter-9-1/section-9-1-14-1/#:~:text=,one%20%2821%29%20years%20of%20age)). Also for others, RI common law tolls until disability removed. Not too relevant for small claims as minors rarely bring small claims (except maybe a 17-year-old turning 18 can then sue within the period – but if an infant had property damage, weird scenario). 

Now, since small claims deals mostly with contracts and small debts, the key numbers to remember:
- **Contracts/debts:** 10 years ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2009/title-9/chapter-9-1/9-1-13#:~:text=%C2%A7%209,shall%20accrue%2C%20and%20not%20after)) (except sale of goods 4 years ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=6,10%20years))).
- **Wages:** Wage statutes are silent on SOL, likely 3 or 6 depending if considered liability imposed by statute (if so, RI has a general 3-year for actions “given by statute” not including penalty to state ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=,2%20years)) or arguably the catch-all). Actually, RI enumeration in § 9-1-13(b) says actions under Fair Debt Collection etc. – not directly wages. Many treat wage claims like written contract (10) or like penalty (2-3). Federal FLSA is 2 (3 if willful). But since you can choose forum, to be safe assume 3 for wages too (though some wage claims are 6 under some reading, but the DLT suggests 3). If you’re near a borderline, just file; let the judge decide if it’s timely. 
- **Security deposit:** considered a statutory penalty (double damages) – arguably subject to the 3-year “penalty” SOL because it’s a penalty given by statute. However, the tenant’s base claim is contractual (return of deposit) – that portion might be 10. The double might be considered a penalty requiring suit within a shorter time. It’s not explicitly addressed. I would not wait beyond 3 years to sue for a deposit, out of caution. Most sue within months anyway. 
- **Consumer protection (UTPA):** likely 10, but better not to test it beyond a few years.
- **Judgments:** If you have an old small claims judgment, you have 20 years to enforce (then renew) ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=9,3%20years)).

**Important:** The statute of limitations is an affirmative defense – if you file late, the court won’t automatically dismiss; the defendant must raise it. If they don’t raise it, they waive it and the case can proceed. But don’t rely on that – most will raise it if obvious. 

In small claims, a defendant might not be legally savvy to assert SOL, but many will if they see it’s clearly old (“I owe money from 8 years ago? That’s old!”). The magistrate could even prompt, “This claim is from 2014, counsel/defendant, do you assert statute of limitations?” If they say yes, case dismissed. So file within the time windows above to avoid that defense altogether.

## 6. Pre-filing requirements  
Rhode Island does not impose any blanket pre-filing requirements like mandatory demand letters or mediation for small claims cases in general. Unlike in some states (Massachusetts for consumer protection demands, for example), there’s no across-the-board rule that you must contact the defendant first or attempt ADR before suing. **However**, as a practical matter and in some specific types of disputes, it is wise or required to take certain steps before heading to court:

- **Demand letters (strongly recommended):** While not legally required for most claims, sending a **demand letter** or otherwise requesting payment before suing is a good idea in almost every case. A simple written demand (sent by certified mail or at least email/letter) serves multiple purposes:
  1. **It may resolve the issue** without court – sometimes people pay up when formally asked.
  2. **It documents your attempt** to resolve it, which looks good to the court. If you go straight to suing without any prior notice, the defendant might appear shocked and the judge might ask “Did you try to work this out?” It’s better to be able to show you did. 
  3. For certain statutory claims, a demand letter might be necessary to preserve certain damages (in RI, it’s not common except maybe under a commercial code for buyer’s remedies needing notice to seller of defect within a reasonable time ([webserver.rilegislature.gov](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE9/9-1/9-1-36.htm#:~:text=6A,1%20year)), or under security deposit law, tenant should give forwarding address and request – not strictly required but prudent).
  
  **What to include:** Identify the debt or issue, the amount due, a time frame to respond (e.g. 10 or 14 days), and state that if not resolved you intend to pursue legal action. Example: “Dear John, On June 1 you borrowed $500 from me, which you promised to repay by August 1. It is now overdue. Please pay $500 by September 15 or I will take appropriate legal action to recover it.” Send this by certified mail, return receipt or at least get proof of mailing. If it’s an email or text, save a screenshot. This becomes evidence: it proves the due amount and date, and the fact that you gave them a chance. Some defendants might pay up to avoid court once they see you mean business. Others may ignore it – but then ignoring a reasonable demand can sway a judge in your favor.
  
  **Not legally mandated except in a few cases:** Rhode Island doesn’t have an all-case requirement, but:
   - For **bad checks**, some states require a 30-day demand to get treble damages. Rhode Island’s bad check statute (civil) doesn’t have a treble provision, so no formal demand needed beyond the bank’s bounce notice. Still, it’s polite to give the issuer a chance (“Your check bounced, please make it right in 7 days or I pursue legal and maybe criminal remedies”).
   - For **security deposits**, Rhode Island law doesn’t require the tenant to request return; it puts the onus on landlord to return within 20 days ([www.hemlane.com](https://www.hemlane.com/resources/rhode-island-security-deposit-laws#:~:text=20%20days%20after%20the%20end,address%20provided%20by%20the%20tenant)). But it’s smart for the tenant to give a forwarding address and maybe a written inquiry after 20 days “I have not received my deposit...” to further establish bad faith if ignored. Not required by statute though.
   - For **wage claims**, no demand needed by statute (some states require employee to demand final paycheck, RI says it’s due within 24 hours upon demand if discharged, but that’s about when it’s due, not a pre-suit requirement exactly – though to trigger waiting time penalty, often a demand is involved). Again, practically, many employees do ask HR or boss first before running to court or Dept of Labor. It’s just logical.
   - For **auto repairs**, there’s no law requiring you to let the mechanic try to fix again. But many shops have a policy to attempt a remedy if you complain – if you skip complaining and sue, the court might wonder if you gave them a chance. So it’s prudent to notify the shop of the problem and request a refund or fix, and note their refusal in your case.
   - For **consumer protection (UTPA)**, Rhode Island does *not* have a mandatory 30-day demand like Massachusetts’ law does【6】. You can sue without a prior letter. But sending one can’t hurt and might lead to settlement or at least show willfulness if they ignore it. If
