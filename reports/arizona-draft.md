## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $5,000 for individuals; $5,000 for businesses [S6].
- **Splitting a claim:** If a claim exceeds $5,000, you cannot split it into multiple claims to fit within the small claims limit. Instead, you must file in a higher court. [S6]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S6].

## 2. What you can sue for

### Contracts and money owed

Arizona allows you 6 years to file a claim for breach of a written contract and 3 years for an oral contract.

- **Breach of a written contract** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A contractor signed a $4,000 contract to remodel your kitchen, took the deposit, and never finished.
  - **Statute:** Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 12-548. 6-year deadline from breach. Bring the signed contract, payment proof, and any correspondence showing the breach. [S5]

- **Breach of an oral contract** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You agreed verbally to lend a friend $2,000, which they promised to repay in 6 months, but they haven't.
  - **Statute:** Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 12-543. 3-year deadline from breach. Provide evidence of the agreement, such as emails or witness statements. [S5]

  - **Example:** You lent $3,500 to a colleague who agreed to repay in installments but has defaulted.

  - **Statute:** Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 12-548. 6-year deadline from breach. Present the original promissory note and any records of payments made. [S5]

- **Unpaid invoice for services rendered** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You provided $4,500 worth of consulting services, but the client hasn't paid the invoice.

## 3. What you CANNOT sue for

- **Eviction (unlawful detainer)** — Small claims courts in Arizona do not have jurisdiction over eviction cases. Where to go instead: Justice Court's regular civil division. [S6]
- **Defamation (libel or slander)** — Claims involving defamation are excluded from small claims court jurisdiction. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S6]
- **Malpractice** — Professional malpractice claims exceed the small claims jurisdictional limit. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [unverified]
- **Family law matters** — Divorce, child custody, and related matters are not handled in small claims court. Where to go instead: Family Court. [unverified]
- **Claims against the state** — Small claims courts do not have jurisdiction over claims against the state or its subdivisions. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S6]

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of the debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File an application for a writ of garnishment, serve the employer, and the employer will withhold a portion of the debtor's wages.

**Estimated cost:** $30-$50 plus service fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor is employed and earns above the exemption threshold.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings or 30 times the federal minimum wage, whichever is greater.

### Bank levy

Seize funds from the debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of garnishment, serve the bank, and the bank will freeze and remit funds from the debtor's account.

**Estimated cost:** $30-$50 plus service fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor has sufficient funds in the account.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $300 is exempt from levy.

### Property lien

Place a lien on the debtor's real property to secure the judgment.

Record the judgment with the county recorder to create a lien on the debtor's property.

**Estimated cost:** $10-$30 recording fee
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns real property; lien must be satisfied before property can be sold or refinanced.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $150,000.

- **Trade-name (DBA) rule:** Businesses operating under a fictitious name must file a statement with the court; form: SC-14 (Fictitious Name Statement). [S6]
- **Licensing notes:** Unlicensed contractors may be barred from recovering fees for unlicensed work. [S6]

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Exceeding the monetary limit

**What goes wrong:** If your claim exceeds $5,000, the court will dismiss the case or require you to waive the excess amount.

**How to avoid it:** Ensure your claim, excluding interest and costs, does not exceed the $5,000 limit. [S6]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** Failure to properly serve the defendant can result in delays or dismissal of your case.

**How to avoid it:** Follow Arizona's rules for service of process carefully, using authorized methods. [S12]

### Missing the statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations has expired will lead to dismissal of your claim.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type before filing. [S5]

### Not bringing necessary evidence

**What goes wrong:** Lack of evidence can result in losing your case, even if your claim is valid.

**How to avoid it:** Gather all relevant documents, photos, and witness statements before the hearing. [S8]

### Failing to appear at the hearing

**What goes wrong:** If you don't show up, the court may dismiss your case or enter a default judgment against you.

**How to avoid it:** Mark your calendar and ensure you attend the scheduled hearing. [S8]

### Ignoring post-judgment procedures

**What goes wrong:** Winning a judgment doesn't guarantee payment; you must take steps to collect.

**How to avoid it:** Familiarize yourself with collection methods like garnishment and liens. [S14]

## 18. Recent changes

- **2025-01-01** — **Increase in small claims monetary limit**. The jurisdictional limit for small claims cases increased from $3,500 to $5,000, allowing more disputes to be resolved in small claims court. Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 22-503. [S6]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, attorneys are not allowed in Arizona small claims court unless both parties agree in writing. [S8]

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: Small claims cases are typically resolved within 30 to 90 days from filing to judgment. [S8]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: The maximum amount you can sue for in Arizona small claims court is $5,000, excluding interest and costs. [S6]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear, the court may enter a default judgment in favor of the plaintiff. [S8]

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: No, decisions in Arizona small claims court are final and cannot be appealed. [S8]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: A judgment in Arizona is valid for 5 years and can be renewed before it expires. [S5]
