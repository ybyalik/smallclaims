## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $25,000 for individuals; $25,000 for businesses [S4].
- **Splitting a claim:** If a claim exceeds the $25,000 cap, you must waive the excess to file in General Sessions Court; splitting one dispute into multiple claims is not allowed. [S4]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S4].

## 14. Post-judgment collection

- **Payment deadline expectation:** 10 days.
- **Post-judgment interest rate:** 5.5% — Tenn. Code Ann. § 47-14-121. Interest accrues on the judgment amount until paid in full.

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File a writ of garnishment with the court, serve the employer, and the employer withholds a portion of the debtor's wages to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees  
**Effectiveness:** Most effective when debtor is W-2 employed at a stable job.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings (federal CCPA floor)

### Bank levy

Seize funds from debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution, serve the bank, and the bank freezes and transfers funds to the court.

**Estimated cost:** $50-100 plus sheriff fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor maintains significant bank balances.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Social Security and federal benefits

### Property lien

Place a lien on debtor's real property to secure the judgment.

File a lien with the county recorder's office; the lien must be satisfied before the property can be sold or refinanced.

**Estimated cost:** $25-50 filing fee  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor owns real property with equity.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $5,000

### Writ of execution

Seize and sell debtor's non-exempt personal property.

Obtain a writ of execution, sheriff seizes property, and it is sold at public auction to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $100-200 plus sheriff fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor owns valuable non-exempt personal property.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Personal property up to $10,000

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Wrong defendant naming

**What goes wrong:** If you sue 'John's Garage' but the legal entity is 'JG Auto Repair LLC', the judgment may be unenforceable against the LLC's assets.

**How to avoid it:** Search the Secretary of State business records before filing and use the exact registered name; for sole proprietors use the individual's full legal name. [unverified]

### Missed statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing one day after the deadline gets the case dismissed with prejudice.

**How to avoid it:** Check the SOL for your specific claim type before filing; for fraud, the discovery rule applies but be prepared to prove when you reasonably could have discovered it. [unverified]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant isn't properly served, the case may be dismissed or delayed.

**How to avoid it:** Follow Tennessee's rules for service carefully; consider using the sheriff's office or a professional process server. [unverified]

### Failing to appear in court

**What goes wrong:** If you don't show up, the court may dismiss your case or enter a default judgment against you.

**How to avoid it:** Mark your calendar and set reminders; if you can't attend, request a continuance in advance. [unverified]

### Insufficient evidence

**What goes wrong:** Without enough evidence, the judge may rule against you.

**How to avoid it:** Gather all relevant documents, photos, and witness statements before the hearing. [unverified]

### Ignoring court orders

**What goes wrong:** Failing to comply with court orders can result in contempt charges or dismissal of your case.

**How to avoid it:** Read and follow all court orders carefully; if unsure, seek legal advice. [unverified]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in General Sessions Court; individuals and businesses can represent themselves.

**Q: How long does it take?**
A: The timeline varies by county and case complexity, but most cases are resolved within 30 to 90 days.

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**
A: The maximum amount you can sue for in General Sessions Court is $25,000. [S4]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**
A: If the defendant fails to appear, the court may enter a default judgment in your favor.

**Q: Can I appeal?**
A: Yes, either party can appeal a General Sessions Court decision to Circuit Court within 10 days. [S11]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**
A: A judgment is valid for 10 years and can be renewed before it expires.
