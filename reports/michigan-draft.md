## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $7,000 for individuals; $7,000 for businesses [S4].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds $7,000, you must either waive the excess amount to file in small claims court or file in a higher court. Splitting one dispute into multiple claims to fit under the cap is not permitted. [S4]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S4].

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File a writ of garnishment with the court, serve the employer, and the employer withholds a portion of the debtor's wages to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $15 plus service fees

### Bank levy

Seize funds from debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of garnishment, serve the bank, and the bank freezes and transfers funds to the court.

**Estimated cost:** $15 plus service fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor has sufficient funds in the account.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Social Security and federal benefits

### Property lien

Place a lien on debtor's real property to secure the judgment.

File a notice of judgment lien with the county recorder's office, which encumbers the property until the judgment is satisfied.

**Estimated cost:** $25 plus recording fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor owns real property; may delay satisfaction until property is sold.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $40,000

### Writ of execution

Seize and sell debtor's non-exempt personal property to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, and the sheriff seizes and sells the debtor's property at auction.

**Estimated cost:** $15 plus sheriff's fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor owns valuable non-exempt property.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Personal property up to $3,000

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Wrong defendant naming

**What goes wrong:** If you sue 'John's Garage' but the legal entity is 'JG Auto Repair LLC', the judgment may be unenforceable against the LLC's assets.

**How to avoid it:** Search the Secretary of State business records before filing and use the exact registered name; for sole proprietors, use the individual's full legal name. [S4]

### Missed statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing one day after the deadline gets the case dismissed with prejudice.

**How to avoid it:** Check the statute of limitations for your specific claim type before filing; for fraud, the discovery rule applies but be prepared to prove when you reasonably could have discovered it. [S4]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant isn't properly served, the case may be delayed or dismissed.

**How to avoid it:** Follow Michigan's service rules carefully; consider using a professional process server if unsure. [S4]

### Filing in the wrong venue

**What goes wrong:** The case may be transferred or dismissed, causing delays and additional costs.

**How to avoid it:** File in the correct county based on where the defendant lives or where the cause of action arose. [S4]

### Failing to collect judgment

**What goes wrong:** Winning a judgment doesn't guarantee payment; you may need to take additional steps to collect.

**How to avoid it:** Familiarize yourself with collection methods like garnishment and liens; act promptly to enforce the judgment. [S4]

### Overlooking exemptions in collection

**What goes wrong:** Certain debtor assets are protected from collection efforts, limiting recovery options.

**How to avoid it:** Understand Michigan's exemption laws to assess the feasibility of collecting on a judgment. [S4]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, attorneys are not permitted to represent parties in Michigan's small claims court; you must represent yourself.

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: Most small claims cases are resolved within 30 to 90 days from filing to judgment.

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: The maximum amount you can sue for in Michigan's small claims court is $7,000, excluding interest and costs.

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear, the court may enter a default judgment in your favor, but you must still provide evidence to support your claim.

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: You can appeal a magistrate's decision within 7 days; if a judge heard the case, the decision is final with no right to appeal.

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: A judgment in Michigan is valid for 10 years and can be renewed before it expires.
