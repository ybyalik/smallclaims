## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $12,500 for individuals; $6,250 for businesses [S1, S12].
- **Annual filing cap:** Individuals can file more than two claims over $2,500 in a calendar year; however, they are limited to two claims exceeding $2,500 per year. Statute: Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 116.221 [S5].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds the small claims limit, you cannot split it into multiple claims to fit within the cap. You may choose to waive the excess amount and file in small claims, or file in a higher court to seek the full amount. [S5].
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S5].

## 10. Service of process

- **Who can serve:**
  - Sheriff
  - Certified mail by clerk
  - Private process server

- **Methods accepted:**

  ### Certified mail by clerk
  The court clerk sends the documents to the defendant via certified mail.
  - Pros: Convenient; Handled by the court
  - Cons: Less reliable; Defendant may refuse delivery

  ### Personal service by sheriff or process server
  A sheriff or registered process server personally delivers the documents to the defendant.
  - Pros: More reliable; Proof of service provided
  - Cons: Additional cost; May require multiple attempts

  ### Substituted service
  If the defendant cannot be personally served, documents are left with a responsible adult at the defendant's residence or business, and a copy is mailed.
  - Pros: Alternative when personal service fails
  - Cons: Additional steps required; May be contested

- **Timing:** 15 days (in county); 20 days (out of county). [S1]
- **Proof of service form:** SC-104 (Proof of Service); file at least 5 days before the hearing. [S11]
- **Business service:** Serve the agent for service of process listed with the California Secretary of State; for sole proprietors, serve the owner personally. [S1]
- **Out-of-state defendant:** Service must comply with the laws of the state where the defendant is served. [S1]
- **If you can't locate the defendant:** If the defendant cannot be located, you may request the court's permission for service by publication. [S1]
- **If the defendant is dodging service:** If the defendant is evading service, document your attempts and consider alternative methods like substituted service or service by publication. [S1]

- **Default judgment appealable?** true — A defendant who failed to appear may appeal a default judgment within 30 days. [S14]
- **Notice of appeal form:** SC-140 (Notice of Appeal (Small Claims)).
- **Frivolous appeal penalty:** $1,000 — Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 116.780. If the court finds the appeal to be frivolous or intended to cause delay, it may award the respondent up to $1,000 in attorney's fees and costs. [S14]

## 14. Post-judgment collection

- **Payment deadline expectation:** 30 days.
- **Post-judgment interest rate:** 10% — Cal. Civ. Code § 685.010. Interest accrues at 10% per annum on the unpaid principal amount of the judgment. [S15]

### Wage garnishment

Obtain a portion of the debtor's wages until the judgment is satisfied.

File a writ of execution with the court, serve it to the debtor's employer, who will then withhold a portion of the debtor's wages and remit it to the court.

**Estimated cost:** $40 for writ issuance plus service fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor is employed and earns above the exemption threshold.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings or 40 times the state minimum wage, whichever is greater

### Bank levy

Seize funds from the debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution, serve it to the debtor's bank, which will freeze the account and remit funds to the court.

**Estimated cost:** $40 for writ issuance plus service fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor has sufficient funds in the account.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Social Security benefits
- Supplemental Security Income (SSI)
- Public assistance benefits

### Property lien

Place a lien on the debtor's real property to secure the judgment amount.

Record an abstract of judgment with the county recorder's office where the debtor owns property; the lien must be satisfied before the property can be sold or refinanced.

**Estimated cost:** $25-$50 recording fee
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns real property and intends to sell or refinance.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $600,000, depending on circumstances

### Till tap

Seize cash directly from the debtor's business register.

Obtain a writ of execution, instruct the sheriff to visit the debtor's business and collect cash from the register.

**Estimated cost:** $40 for writ issuance plus sheriff's fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective for businesses with significant cash transactions.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- None

### Vehicle levy

Seize and sell the debtor's vehicle to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution, instruct the sheriff to seize the vehicle, which is then sold at auction; proceeds are applied to the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $40 for writ issuance plus towing and storage fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns a vehicle with sufficient equity.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Vehicle exemption up to $3,325

- **Wage garnishment cap:** 25% of disposable earnings.
- **Judgment lifespan:** 10 years; renewal: File an application for renewal of judgment before the 10-year period expires; the renewed judgment is valid for another 10 years.
- **Debtor's exam form:** SC-134 (Application and Order to Produce Statement of Assets and to Appear for Examination).
- **Exempt assets:**
  - Homestead—up to $600,000, depending on circumstances
  - Vehicle—up to $3,325
  - Social Security and public assistance benefits
  - Retirement accounts
- **Bankruptcy effect:** Filing for bankruptcy may discharge the judgment debt; however, certain debts are non-dischargeable.
- **Satisfaction of judgment form:** EJ-100 (Acknowledgment of Satisfaction of Judgment).
- **Multiple creditors priority:** Judgment liens are prioritized based on the date of recording; earlier liens have higher priority. [S15]

## 15. Special rules for businesses

- **Self-representation allowed?** Yes — Businesses may be represented by a regular employee, officer, or director; attorneys are not permitted. [S1]
- **Can a business send an employee instead of an attorney?** Yes [S1].
- **Can a business send an attorney?** No [S1].
- **Form for non-attorney representation:** SC-109 (Authorization to Appear)
- **Insurance adjuster representation:** Not allowed [S1].
- **Sole proprietor employee exception:** A sole proprietor may be represented by a regular employee if the claim is related to the business. [S1]
- **Out-of-state business plaintiffs:** must register with Secretary of State first: Yes — Cal. Corp. Code § 2105. [S1]
- **Trade-name (DBA) rule:** If the business operates under a fictitious name, it must file a declaration with the court.; form: SC-103 (Fictitious Business Name Declaration).
- **Licensing notes:** Unlicensed contractors may be barred from recovering fees for work requiring a license. [S1]

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Incorrect defendant information

**What goes wrong:** Suing the wrong party or using incorrect names can result in dismissal or unenforceable judgments.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the exact legal name and address of the defendant before filing; for businesses, check with the California Secretary of State. [S1]

---

### Missing statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations has expired will lead to dismissal of the case.

**How to avoid it:** Determine the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type and ensure you file within that period. [S5]

---

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** Failure to serve the defendant correctly can delay the case or result in dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Follow the court's rules for service of process carefully, and use a reliable method to ensure proper service. [S1]

---

### Insufficient evidence

**What goes wrong:** Lack of adequate evidence can lead to losing the case, even if your claim is valid.

**How to avoid it:** Gather all relevant documents, photos, and witness statements to support your claim. [S1]

---

### Ignoring court deadlines

**What goes wrong:** Missing filing deadlines or court dates can result in dismissal or default judgments.

**How to avoid it:** Keep track of all deadlines and court dates, and ensure timely compliance with court requirements. [S1]

---

### Overlooking post-judgment procedures

**What goes wrong:** Winning a judgment does not guarantee payment; failure to follow up can result in non-collection.

**How to avoid it:** Familiarize yourself with collection methods and take proactive steps to enforce the judgment. [S15]

## 18. Recent changes

- **2024-01-01** — **Increase in small claims monetary limits**. The jurisdictional limit for individuals in small claims court increased from $10,000 to $12,500, and for businesses from $5,000 to $6,250. Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 116.221. [S5]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, attorneys are not permitted to represent parties in California small claims court; you must represent yourself. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: Typically, the hearing is scheduled 30 to 70 days after filing, and the entire process can take a few months. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: Individuals can sue for up to $12,500, while businesses are limited to $6,250. [S1]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear, the court may enter a default judgment in your favor, but you must still present evidence to support your claim. [S1]

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: Only the defendant can appeal a small claims judgment, and they must do so within 30 days by requesting a new trial. [S14]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: A small claims judgment is valid for 10 years and can be renewed for an additional 10 years if necessary. [S15]
