## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $5,000 for individuals; $5,000 for businesses [S1].
- **Splitting a claim:** If a claim exceeds the $5,000 cap, you cannot split it into multiple claims to fit within the limit. Instead, you must file in the regular civil division of the District Court. [S4]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S4].

## 10. Service of process

- **Who can serve:**
  - Sheriff
  - Certified mail by clerk
  - Private process server

- **Methods accepted:**

  ### Certified or registered mail

  The court clerk sends the summons and complaint to the defendant via certified or registered mail with return receipt requested.
  - Pros: Cost-effective, Official record of delivery
  - Cons: Defendant may refuse to accept mail, Delivery can be delayed

  ### Personal service by sheriff or process server

  A sheriff or licensed process server personally delivers the summons and complaint to the defendant.
  - Pros: High likelihood of successful service, Proof of service provided
  - Cons: More expensive than mail service, Scheduling can be challenging

- **Timing:** 5 days (in county); 10 days (out of county).
- **Proof of service form:** Form 3 (Proof of Service); file at least 3 days before the hearing.
- **Business service:** Serve the registered agent of the corporation or, if none, an officer or managing agent. For sole proprietors, serve the individual owner.
- **Out-of-state defendant:** Service must comply with Hawaii rules and the laws of the state where the defendant is served.
- **If you can't locate the defendant:** If the defendant cannot be located after diligent efforts, the court may allow service by publication.
- **If the defendant is dodging service:** If the defendant is evading service, document all attempts and consider requesting alternative service methods from the court.

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of the debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File a writ of garnishment with the court, serve the employer, and the employer withholds a portion of the debtor's wages to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $30 plus service fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor is employed and earns sufficient wages.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings or 30 times the federal minimum wage, whichever is greater

### Bank levy

Seize funds from the debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, serve it on the debtor's bank, and the bank freezes and transfers funds to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $30 plus service fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor has sufficient funds in the bank account.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $1,000 in the debtor's account is exempt from levy

### Property lien

Place a lien on the debtor's real property to secure the judgment amount.

Record the judgment with the Bureau of Conveyances to create a lien on the debtor's real property, which must be satisfied upon sale or refinancing.

**Estimated cost:** $25 recording fee  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns real property and intends to sell or refinance.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $30,000

### Writ of execution

Seize and sell the debtor's non-exempt personal property to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, have the sheriff seize non-exempt property, and sell it at public auction to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $30 plus sheriff's fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns valuable non-exempt personal property.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Personal property up to $1,000 in value

- **Wage garnishment cap:** 25% of disposable earnings.
- **Judgment lifespan:** 10 years; renewal: File a motion to renew the judgment before the 10-year period expires; if granted, the judgment is extended for another 10 years.
- **Debtor's exam form:** Form 12 (Motion for Examination of Judgment Debtor).
- **Exempt assets:**
  - Homestead — $30,000
  - Vehicle — $2,575
  - Retirement accounts (state and federally protected)
  - Social Security and federal benefits
- **Bankruptcy effect:** Filing for bankruptcy imposes an automatic stay on collection efforts; certain judgments may be discharged in bankruptcy.
- **Satisfaction of judgment form:** Form 5 (Satisfaction of Judgment).
- **Multiple creditors priority:** Judgment liens are prioritized based on the date of recording; earlier liens have higher priority.

## 15. Special rules for businesses

- **Self-representation allowed?** Yes — Businesses can represent themselves in small claims court without an attorney. [S1]
- **Can a business send an employee instead of an attorney?** Yes [S1].
- **Can a business send an attorney?** Yes [S1].
- **Form for non-attorney representation:** Form 13 (Authorization to Appear on Behalf of Business Entity) [S1].
- **Insurance adjuster representation:** Not allowed [S1].
- **Sole proprietor employee exception:** Sole proprietors can authorize an employee to represent them in court. [S1]
- **Out-of-state business plaintiffs:** must register with Secretary of State first: Yes — Haw. Rev. Stat. § 414-431 [S1].
- **Trade-name (DBA) rule:** Businesses operating under a trade name must file a Certificate of Assumed Name with the Department of Commerce and Consumer Affairs.; form: Form 14 (Certificate of Assumed Name) [S1].
- **Licensing notes:** Unlicensed contractors may be barred from suing to recover fees for work requiring a license. [S1]

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Incorrect defendant information

**What goes wrong:** Suing the wrong party or using incorrect names can result in dismissal or unenforceable judgments.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the exact legal name and address of the defendant before filing; for businesses, check the Department of Commerce and Consumer Affairs records. [S1]

### Missing statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations has expired will lead to dismissal of the case.

**How to avoid it:** Confirm the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type and file within the allowed time frame. [S4]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** Failure to properly serve the defendant can delay the case or result in dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Follow Hawaii's rules for service of process carefully, using certified mail or a qualified process server. [S1]

### Insufficient evidence

**What goes wrong:** Lack of evidence can lead to losing the case, even if your claim is valid.

**How to avoid it:** Gather and organize all relevant documents, photos, and witness information before the hearing. [S1]

### Ignoring court deadlines

**What goes wrong:** Missing deadlines for filings or appearances can result in default judgments or case dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Keep track of all court dates and deadlines, and ensure timely submissions and appearances. [S1]

### Overlooking collection procedures

**What goes wrong:** Winning a judgment doesn't guarantee payment; failure to follow proper collection procedures can leave you unpaid.

**How to avoid it:** Familiarize yourself with Hawaii's judgment collection methods and take appropriate steps to enforce the judgment. [S1]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in Hawaii's small claims court. The process is designed to be accessible for individuals representing themselves. However, you may choose to hire an attorney if you prefer. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: Small claims cases in Hawaii are typically scheduled for a hearing within 30 days of filing. The duration of the case can vary depending on factors such as service of process and court schedules. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: In Hawaii's small claims court, you can sue for up to $5,000, exclusive of interest and costs. There is no monetary limit for cases involving the return of a residential security deposit. [S1]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear at the hearing after being properly served, the court may enter a default judgment in your favor. You will still need to provide evidence to support your claim. [S1]

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: No, decisions made in Hawaii's small claims court are final and cannot be appealed. However, the court may alter or set aside a judgment under certain circumstances. [S1]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: In Hawaii, a judgment is valid for 10 years from the date it is entered. You can renew the judgment before it expires to extend its enforceability. [S4]
