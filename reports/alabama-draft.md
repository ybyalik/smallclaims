## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

- **wrong defendant naming** — If you name the wrong defendant, your case may be dismissed. How to avoid: Double-check the legal name and status of the person or business you are suing.
- **wrong venue** — Filing in the wrong county can lead to dismissal. How to avoid: File in the county where the defendant lives or where the dispute occurred.
- **statute of limitations expired** — Filing after the deadline means your case will be dismissed. How to avoid: Check the statute of limitations for your claim type before filing.
- **bad service** — Improper service means the defendant is not legally notified and your case may be delayed or dismissed. How to avoid: Use an approved method and file proof of service on time.
- **asking for non-monetary relief** — Small Claims Court cannot grant non-monetary remedies, so your case will be dismissed if you ask for them. How to avoid: Only request money judgments.
- **no-show by plaintiff** — If you do not appear at the hearing, your case will be dismissed. How to avoid: Attend your hearing or request a continuance in advance if needed.
- **default + appeal mistake** — Failing to respond to a default judgment or missing the appeal deadline means you lose your right to challenge the judgment. How to avoid: Respond promptly and file any appeal within the required timeframe.
- **judgment-proof debtor** — If the defendant has no assets or income, you may win but be unable to collect. How to avoid: Consider the defendant’s ability to pay before filing.

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer to file or defend a small claims case in Alabama?**  
A: No, you do not need a lawyer. Individuals and businesses may represent themselves in Alabama Small Claims Court, though you may choose to hire an attorney if you wish [S4].

**Q: How long does a typical small claims case take in Alabama?**  
A: Most cases are resolved within a few months. Hearings are usually scheduled soon after filing, and decisions are often made on the spot at the hearing [S10].

**Q: What is the maximum amount I can sue for in Alabama Small Claims Court?**  
A: The maximum you can sue for is $6,000, not including interest and court costs [S4].

**Q: What happens if the defendant does not show up to the hearing?**  
A: If the defendant does not respond or appear, the plaintiff may request a default judgment, but must still provide evidence to support the claim [S10].

**Q: Can I appeal a Small Claims Court decision in Alabama?**  
A: Yes, either party can appeal within 14 days. The appeal is a new trial (trial de novo) in the Circuit Court, and a bond is required [S14].

**Q: How long is a judgment good for in Alabama?**  
A: A judgment is valid for 10 years and can be renewed by filing a motion before it expires [S15].
