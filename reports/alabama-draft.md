## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $6,000 for individuals; $6,000 for businesses [S1, S13].
- **Splitting a claim:** If a claim exceeds the $6,000 cap, you must waive the excess to file in small claims court or file in a higher court. Splitting one dispute into multiple claims to fit under the cap is not allowed. [S5]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S5].

## 10. Service of process

- **Methods accepted:**

  ### Sheriff personal service
  Sheriff or constable personally hands the papers to the defendant.
  - Pros: Most reliable, Official
  - Cons: May be slower, Additional cost

  ### Certified mail by clerk
  Clerk sends the papers via certified mail to the defendant.
  - Pros: Less expensive, Convenient
  - Cons: Defendant may refuse delivery, Less reliable

  ### Private process server
  A private individual authorized to serve legal documents delivers the papers to the defendant.
  - Pros: Flexible scheduling, Can be faster
  - Cons: Additional cost, Must ensure proper authorization

- **If the defendant is dodging service:** If the defendant is evading service, document all attempts and seek court permission for alternative service methods.

## 14. Post-judgment collection

- **Payment deadline expectation:** 30 days.
- **Post-judgment interest rate:** 7.5% — Ala. Code § 8-8-10. Interest accrues on the judgment amount until paid in full. [S16]

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File a writ of garnishment with the court; serve the employer; employer withholds and remits to the court.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees  
**Effectiveness:** Most effective when debtor is W-2 employed at a stable job.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings (federal CCPA floor)

### Bank levy

Seize funds from debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution; serve the bank; bank freezes and remits funds to the court.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor maintains significant funds in bank accounts.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Social Security and federal benefits

### Property lien

Place a lien on debtor's real property to secure the judgment.

Record the judgment with the county; lien attaches to debtor's property; must be satisfied before sale.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus recording fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor owns real property; may take time to realize payment.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $15,000

### Writ of execution

Seize and sell debtor's non-exempt property to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain writ from court; sheriff seizes property; property sold at auction; proceeds applied to judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $50-100 plus sheriff fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor owns valuable non-exempt property.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Personal property up to $7,500

- **Wage garnishment cap:** 25% of disposable earnings.
- **Judgment lifespan:** 10 years; renewal: File a motion to renew the judgment before expiration; pay applicable fees.
- **Debtor's exam form:** SM-4 (Notice of Trial).
- **Exempt assets:**
  - Homestead — $15,000
  - Vehicle — $7,500
  - Retirement accounts (federally and/or state-protected)
  - Social Security and federal benefits
- **Bankruptcy effect:** Filing for bankruptcy may discharge the judgment; certain debts are non-dischargeable.
- **Satisfaction of judgment form:** SM-4 (Notice of Trial).
- **Multiple creditors priority:** Priority among creditors is generally determined by the order of filing or recording the judgment. [S16]

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Wrong defendant naming

**What goes wrong:** If you sue 'John's Garage' but the legal entity is 'JG Auto Repair LLC', the judgment may be unenforceable against the LLC's assets.

**How to avoid it:** Search the Secretary of State business records before filing and use the exact registered name; for sole proprietors, use the individual's full legal name. [S1]

### Missed statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing one day after the deadline gets the case dismissed with prejudice.

**How to avoid it:** Check the SOL for your specific claim type before filing; for fraud, the discovery rule applies but be prepared to prove when you reasonably could have discovered it. [S1]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant isn't properly served, the case may be delayed or dismissed.

**How to avoid it:** Follow Alabama's rules for service of process carefully; consider using the sheriff's office or a professional process server. [S1]

### Failing to appear in court

**What goes wrong:** If you don't show up, the court may dismiss your case or enter a default judgment against you.

**How to avoid it:** Mark your calendar and set reminders; if an emergency arises, notify the court as soon as possible. [S1]

### Not bringing necessary evidence

**What goes wrong:** Without proper evidence, you may lose your case even if you're in the right.

**How to avoid it:** Gather all relevant documents, photos, and witness statements well before your court date. [S1]

### Ignoring post-judgment procedures

**What goes wrong:** Winning a judgment doesn't guarantee payment; you must take steps to collect.

**How to avoid it:** Learn about collection methods like garnishment and liens; the court clerk can provide information on post-judgment procedures. [S1]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in small claims court. The process is designed to be accessible for individuals representing themselves. However, you may choose to hire an attorney if you prefer. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: The timeline varies, but most small claims cases are resolved within 30 to 90 days from filing to judgment. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: In Alabama, the maximum amount you can sue for in small claims court is $6,000. [S1]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear, the court may enter a default judgment in your favor. You may still need to provide evidence to support your claim before the judgment is finalized. [S1]

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: Yes, either party can appeal a small claims judgment within 14 days. The appeal will result in a new trial (trial de novo) in the circuit court. [S6]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: In Alabama, a judgment is valid for 10 years and can be renewed for an additional 10 years if necessary. [S4]
