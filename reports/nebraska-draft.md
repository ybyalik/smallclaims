## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $7,500 for individuals; $7,500 for businesses [S1, S12].
- **Annual filing cap:** No more than 2 cases per week or 10 per calendar year. Statute: Neb. Rev. Stat. § 25-2802 [S4].
- **Splitting a claim:** If a claim exceeds $7,500, you cannot split it into multiple small claims cases; you must file in a higher court. [S4].
- **What's excluded from the cap:** Interest and court costs are excluded [S4].

## 3. What you CANNOT sue for

- **Eviction (unlawful detainer)** — Small claims court cannot handle eviction proceedings; these must be filed in county court. Where to go instead: County Court. [S1]
- **Title to real estate** — Disputes involving ownership or title to real property are beyond the jurisdiction of small claims court. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]
- **Defamation** — Claims involving defamation, such as libel or slander, are not suitable for small claims court. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]
- **Malpractice** — Professional malpractice claims require complex evidence and are not handled in small claims court. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]
- **Family law matters** — Issues like divorce, child custody, and support are outside the scope of small claims court. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]
- **Probate matters** — Disputes involving wills, estates, and trusts are not handled in small claims court. Where to go instead: County Court. [S1]
- **Claims against the federal government** — Small claims court does not have jurisdiction over cases against federal entities. Where to go instead: Federal Court. [S1]
- **Workers' compensation claims** — These claims are handled by the Nebraska Workers' Compensation Court, not small claims court. Where to go instead: Nebraska Workers' Compensation Court. [S1]
- **Class actions** — Small claims court is not equipped to handle class action lawsuits. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]
- **Injunctions and specific performance** — Small claims court cannot issue orders requiring someone to do or stop doing something. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Exceeding the jurisdictional limit

**What goes wrong:** Filing a claim over $7,500 in small claims court will result in dismissal or transfer to a higher court.

**How to avoid it:** Ensure your claim does not exceed the $7,500 limit; if it does, file in county or district court. [S4]

### Suing the wrong party

**What goes wrong:** Naming the incorrect defendant can lead to dismissal and wasted time.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the legal name and address of the party you're suing before filing. [S1]

### Missing the statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the deadline results in automatic dismissal of your case.

**How to avoid it:** Check the statute of limitations for your specific claim type before filing. [S4]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** Failure to serve the defendant correctly can delay or dismiss your case.

**How to avoid it:** Follow Nebraska's rules for serving process carefully, using authorized methods. [S13]

### Not preparing evidence

**What goes wrong:** Lack of evidence can result in losing your case, even if you're in the right.

**How to avoid it:** Gather all relevant documents, photos, and witness statements before the hearing. [S1]

### Failing to collect judgment

**What goes wrong:** Winning a case doesn't guarantee payment; you may need to take further steps.

**How to avoid it:** Learn about post-judgment collection methods like garnishment and liens. [S8]

## 18. Recent changes

- **2025-07-01** — **Increase in small claims jurisdictional limit**. The maximum amount for small claims cases increased from $6,000 to $7,500. LB139 (2024). [S4]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**
A: No, attorneys are not permitted to represent parties in Nebraska small claims court; you must represent yourself. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**
A: Small claims cases typically resolve within 30 to 90 days from filing to judgment. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**
A: As of July 1, 2025, the maximum amount you can sue for in Nebraska small claims court is $7,500. [S4]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**
A: If the defendant fails to appear, the court may enter a default judgment in your favor. [S1]

**Q: Can I appeal?**
A: Yes, either party can appeal a small claims judgment to the district court within 10 days. [S7]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**
A: A judgment in Nebraska is valid for 5 years and can be renewed before it expires. [S4]
