## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $5,000 for individuals; $5,000 for businesses [S1, S12, S4].
- **Annual filing cap:** A party may file no more than 12 claims in small claims court during a calendar year. RSMo § 482.305 [S4].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds $5,000, you may waive the excess and file in small claims court, but you cannot split one claim into multiple cases to stay within the limit. [S4]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S4].

## 2. What you can sue for

### Contracts and money owed

Missouri allows you to file claims for money owed under contracts, with a statute of limitations of 5 years for written contracts and 3 years for oral contracts.

- **Breach of a written contract** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A contractor signed a $4,000 contract to remodel your kitchen, took the deposit, and never finished.
  - **Statute:** RSMo § 516.120. 5-year deadline from breach. Bring the signed contract, payment proof, and any correspondence showing the breach. [S4]
- **Breach of an oral contract** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You agreed verbally to lend a friend $2,000, which they promised to repay in 6 months, but they haven't.
  - **Statute:** RSMo § 516.120. 3-year deadline from breach. Provide evidence of the agreement, such as emails or witness statements. [S4]
- **Unpaid personal loan** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You loaned a colleague $1,500 with the understanding they'd repay in 3 months, but they haven't.
  - **Statute:** RSMo § 516.120. 5-year deadline for written agreements, 3 years for oral. Gather any written agreement or communication about the loan. [S4]
- **Default on a promissory note** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A borrower signed a promissory note to repay $4,500 over a year but stopped making payments after 3 months.
  - **Statute:** RSMo § 516.110. 10-year deadline from default. Present the original promissory note and records of payments made. [S4]
- **Unpaid invoice for services rendered** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You provided graphic design services worth $3,000 to a client who hasn't paid the invoice issued 6 months ago.
  - **Statute:** RSMo § 516.120. 5-year deadline from when payment was due. Include the invoice, service agreement, and any communication regarding payment. [S4]
- **Unpaid balance on an open account** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A customer has an outstanding balance of $2,500 on their store credit account and hasn't made a payment in 8 months.
  - **Statute:** RSMo § 516.120. 5-year deadline from the last payment or charge. Provide account statements and records of transactions. [S4]
- **Unpaid rent by tenant** — eligible.
  - **Example:** Your tenant owes $4,000 in unpaid rent over the past 4 months and has vacated the property.
  - **Statute:** RSMo § 516.120. 5-year deadline from when rent was due. Bring the lease agreement and records of missed payments. [S4]
- **Payment with a bad check** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A customer paid for a $1,200 service with a check that bounced due to insufficient funds.
  - **Statute:** RSMo § 570.120. Send a statutory notice demanding payment within 30 days before filing. Keep a copy of the notice and proof of mailing. [S18]
  - **Special remedy:** Check amount + service charge up to $25 + civil penalty up to $100 OR 2x check amount, whichever is greater.
- **Co-signer seeking repayment** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You co-signed a $5,000 loan for a friend who defaulted, and now you're being held responsible.
  - **Statute:** RSMo § 516.120. 5-year deadline from default. Provide the loan agreement and evidence of payments you've made on behalf of the borrower. [S4]

## 3. What you CANNOT sue for

- **Eviction (unlawful detainer)** — Small claims court cannot handle eviction proceedings; these must be filed in the associate circuit court. Where to go instead: Associate Circuit Court. [S4]
- **Title to real estate** — Disputes involving the title to real estate are beyond the jurisdiction of small claims court. Where to go instead: Circuit Court. [S4]
- **Defamation** — Claims for defamation, including libel and slander, are not permitted in small claims court. Where to go instead: Circuit Court. [S4]
- **Malpractice** — Professional malpractice claims exceed the jurisdictional limits and complexity suitable for small claims court. Where to go instead: Circuit Court. [S4]
- **Family law matters** — Issues such as divorce, child custody, and support are not handled in small claims court. Where to go instead: Family Court. [S4]
- **Probate matters** — Disputes involving wills, estates, and trusts are outside the jurisdiction of small claims court. Where to go instead: Probate Court. [S4]
- **Claims against the federal government** — Small claims court cannot hear cases against federal agencies or the U.S. government. Where to go instead: Federal Court. [S4]
- **Class actions** — Class action lawsuits are beyond the scope and procedures of small claims court. Where to go instead: Circuit Court. [S4]
- **Injunctions and specific performance** — Small claims court cannot issue orders requiring a party to do or refrain from doing something. Where to go instead: Circuit Court. [S4]
- **Workers' compensation claims** — Claims for workplace injuries are handled by the Division of Workers' Compensation, not small claims court. Where to go instead: Division of Workers' Compensation. [S4]
- **Bankruptcy matters** — Bankruptcy cases are under federal jurisdiction and cannot be filed in small claims court. Where to go instead: Federal Bankruptcy Court. [S4]
- **Mandatory arbitration disputes** — Disputes subject to mandatory arbitration clauses are not suitable for small claims court. Where to go instead: Arbitration Panel. [S4]
- **Suing a deceased person** — Claims against deceased individuals must be filed against their estate in probate court. Where to go instead: Probate Court. [S4]

## 10. Service of process

- **Who can serve:**
  - Sheriff
  - Certified mail by clerk
  - Private process server

- **Methods accepted:**

  ### Certified mail with return receipt
  The court clerk sends the summons and petition to the defendant via certified mail, requiring the defendant's signature upon delivery.
  - Pros: Cost-effective, Handled by the court
  - Cons: Defendant may refuse delivery, No personal contact

  ### Personal service by sheriff
  A sheriff or deputy personally delivers the summons and petition to the defendant.
  - Pros: Official and reliable, Proof of service provided
  - Cons: Additional cost, May take longer

  ### Personal service by private process server
  A private individual authorized to serve legal documents personally delivers the summons and petition to the defendant.
  - Pros: Flexible scheduling, Can be faster
  - Cons: Higher cost, Must ensure proper documentation

- **Timing:** 10 days (in county); 10 days (out of county).
- **Proof of service form:** SC-6 (Affidavit of Service); file at least 10 days before the hearing.
- **Business service:** Serve the registered agent or an officer of the corporation; for sole proprietors, serve the individual owner.
- **Out-of-state defendant:** Service must comply with Missouri rules and the laws of the state where the defendant is located.
- **If you can't locate the defendant:** If the defendant cannot be located, service by publication may be permitted with court approval.
- **If the defendant is dodging service:** If the defendant is evading service, document attempts and seek court guidance for alternative methods.

## 14. Post-judgment collection

- **Payment deadline expectation:** 30 days. [S4]
- **Post-judgment interest rate:** 9% — RSMo § 408.040. Interest accrues on unpaid judgments at the statutory rate until paid. [S4]

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of the debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File a garnishment application with the court, serve the employer, and the employer withholds a portion of the debtor's wages to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees  
**Effectiveness:** Most effective when the debtor is employed and earns above the exemption threshold.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings (federal CCPA floor)

### Bank levy

Seize funds from the debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, serve it on the debtor's bank, and the bank freezes and turns over funds to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $50-100 plus sheriff fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor has sufficient funds in the account; may require knowledge of the debtor's banking information.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Social Security and federal benefits
- Certain retirement accounts

### Property lien

Place a lien on the debtor's real property, which must be satisfied upon sale or refinancing.

File the judgment with the county recorder to place a lien on the debtor's property; the lien must be satisfied before the property can be sold or refinanced.

**Estimated cost:** $25-50  
**Effectiveness:** Effective for securing payment over time; may not result in immediate payment.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $15,000

### Writ of execution

Authorize the sheriff to seize and sell the debtor's non-exempt property to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ from the court, and the sheriff seizes and sells the debtor's non-exempt property at auction to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $50-100 plus sheriff fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns valuable non-exempt property; may be time-consuming.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Personal property up to $3,000
- Tools of trade up to $3,000

- **Wage garnishment cap:** 25% of disposable earnings. [S4]
- **Judgment lifespan:** 10 years; renewal: File a motion to revive the judgment before the 10-year period expires to extend its enforceability. [S4]
- **Debtor's exam form:** SC-12 (Motion for Debtor Examination).
- **Exempt assets:**
  - Homestead — $15,000
  - Vehicle — $3,000
  - Retirement accounts (federally and state-protected)
  - Social Security and federal benefits
- **Bankruptcy effect:** Filing for bankruptcy may discharge certain judgments; consult legal advice for specifics. [S4]
- **Satisfaction of judgment form:** SC-4 (Satisfaction of Judgment).
- **Multiple creditors priority:** Judgment liens are prioritized by the date of recording; earlier liens have higher priority. [S4]

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Exceeding the claim limit

**What goes wrong:** If your claim exceeds $5,000 and you file in small claims court, you must waive the excess amount, or the case may be dismissed.

**How to avoid it:** Ensure your claim is within the $5,000 limit or file in the appropriate higher court. [S4]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** Failing to serve the defendant correctly can result in delays or dismissal of your case.

**How to avoid it:** Follow Missouri's rules for service of process carefully, using certified mail or personal service as required. [S4]

### Missing the statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations has expired will lead to dismissal of your claim.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type and file within the allowed time frame. [S4]

### Insufficient evidence

**What goes wrong:** Without adequate evidence, the judge may rule against you, even if your claim is valid.

**How to avoid it:** Gather all relevant documents, receipts, and witness statements to support your case. [S4]

### Ignoring counterclaims

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant files a counterclaim and you are unprepared, you could lose more than your original claim.

**How to avoid it:** Be prepared to address potential counterclaims by understanding the defendant's possible arguments. [S4]

### Failing to collect judgment

**What goes wrong:** Winning a judgment does not guarantee payment; you may need to take additional steps to collect.

**How to avoid it:** Familiarize yourself with collection methods like garnishment and liens, and be prepared to implement them if necessary. [S4]

## 18. Recent changes

- **2025-08-28** — **Increase in small claims court limit**. The maximum claim amount in Missouri small claims court increased from $3,000 to $5,000, allowing more disputes to be resolved in this forum. RSMo § 482.305. [S4]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in Missouri small claims court. The process is designed to be accessible for individuals representing themselves, though you may choose to hire an attorney if you prefer. [S4]

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: The timeline varies by case and county, but most small claims cases in Missouri are resolved within 30 to 90 days from filing to judgment. [S4]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: In Missouri small claims court, you can sue for up to $5,000, excluding interest and court costs. [S4]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear at the hearing, the court may enter a default judgment in your favor, provided you present sufficient evidence to support your claim. [S4]

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: Yes, either party can appeal a small claims judgment by requesting a trial de novo in the circuit court within 10 days of the judgment. [S4]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: A judgment in Missouri is valid for 10 years and can be renewed before it expires to extend its enforceability. [S4]
