## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $10,000 for individuals; $10,000 for businesses [S1, S11].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds $10,000, you cannot split it into multiple small claims cases. You must either reduce your claim to fit within the limit or file in a higher court. [S4]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S4].

## 2. What you can sue for

### Contracts and money owed

North Carolina allows you to file claims related to breaches of contract and money owed up to $10,000. The statute of limitations is generally 3 years for such claims.

- **Breach of a written contract** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A contractor signed a $4,000 contract to remodel your kitchen, took the deposit, and never finished.
  - **Statute:** N.C. Gen. Stat. § 1-52(1). You have 3 years from the date of breach to file. Bring the signed contract, payment proof, and any correspondence showing the breach. [S4]
- **Breach of an oral contract** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You agreed verbally to lend someone $2,000, and they failed to repay as promised.
  - **Statute:** N.C. Gen. Stat. § 1-52(1). The statute of limitations is 3 years. Provide evidence of the agreement and any partial payments. [S4]
- **Unpaid personal loan** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You loaned a friend $5,000 with the understanding it would be repaid in a year, but they haven't paid.
  - **Statute:** N.C. Gen. Stat. § 1-52(1). File within 3 years of the agreed repayment date. Documentation of the loan and any communications are helpful. [S4]
- **Default on a promissory note** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A borrower signed a promissory note for $8,000 and failed to make the agreed payments.
  - **Statute:** N.C. Gen. Stat. § 1-52(1). You have 3 years from the date of default to file. Present the original note and records of missed payments. [S4]

  - **Example:** You provided $3,000 worth of consulting services, invoiced the client, but haven't received payment.

## 10. Service of process

- **Who can serve:**
  - Sheriff
  - Certified mail by clerk
  - Private process server

- **Methods accepted:**

  ### Sheriff personal service
  Sheriff personally delivers the summons and complaint to the defendant.
  - Pros: Reliable, Official
  - Cons: Additional fee, May take longer

  ### Certified mail
  Clerk sends the summons and complaint via certified mail to the defendant.
  - Pros: Less expensive, Proof of delivery
  - Cons: Defendant may refuse delivery

  ### Private process server
  A private individual authorized to serve legal documents delivers the summons and complaint.
  - Pros: Flexible timing, Can be faster
  - Cons: Additional cost, Must ensure proper service

- **Timing:** 5 days (in county); 10 days (out of county).
- **Proof of service form:** AOC-CV-317 (Affidavit of Service); file at least 3 days before the hearing.
- **Business service:** Serve the registered agent or an officer of the corporation; for sole proprietors, serve the individual directly.
- **Out-of-state defendant:** Service must comply with North Carolina rules and the rules of the state where the defendant is located.
- **If you can't locate the defendant:** If the defendant cannot be located, service by publication may be an option, but it requires court approval.
- **If the defendant is dodging service:** If the defendant is evading service, you may request the court to allow alternative methods of service.

Wage garnishment is not a viable collection method for most civil judgments in North Carolina.

### Bank levy

Seizing funds from the debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

After obtaining a writ of execution, the sheriff can levy the debtor's bank account. The debtor must be notified and has the right to claim exemptions.

**Estimated cost:** $25 plus sheriff's fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor has sufficient funds in the account; debtor may claim exemptions.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $500 in bank account
- Social Security and other federal benefits

### Property lien

Placing a lien on the debtor's real property to secure the judgment.

By docketing the judgment with the clerk of court, a lien is placed on the debtor's real property in that county. The lien must be renewed every 10 years.

**Estimated cost:** $25  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns real property; may not result in immediate payment but secures the debt.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $35,000

### Writ of execution

Authorizes the sheriff to seize and sell the debtor's non-exempt property to satisfy the judgment.

After the 10-day appeal period, the creditor can request a writ of execution. The sheriff will attempt to locate and seize non-exempt property for sale.

**Estimated cost:** $25 plus sheriff's fees  
**Effectiveness:** Depends on the debtor's assets; debtor may claim exemptions.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $3,500 in personal property
- One motor vehicle up to $3,500
- Tools of trade up to $2,000

- **Wage garnishment cap:** 0% of disposable earnings.
- **Judgment lifespan:** 10 years; renewal: File a motion to renew the judgment before the 10-year period expires; the judgment can be renewed once for an additional 10 years. [S1]
- **Debtor's exam form:** AOC-CV-506 (Notice of Rights to Have Exemptions Designated).
- **Exempt assets:**
  - Homestead — $35,000
  - Vehicle — $3,500
  - Retirement accounts (federally and state-protected)
  - Social Security and federal benefits
- **Bankruptcy effect:** Filing for bankruptcy imposes an automatic stay on collection efforts; certain judgments may be discharged in bankruptcy. [S1]
- **Satisfaction of judgment form:** AOC-CV-413 (Satisfaction of Judgment).
- **Multiple creditors priority:** Judgment liens are prioritized based on the date of docketing; earlier liens have higher priority. [S1]

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Incorrect defendant information

**What goes wrong:** Suing the wrong party or using incorrect names can result in dismissal or unenforceable judgments.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the exact legal name and address of the defendant before filing; for businesses, check the Secretary of State's records. [S1]

### Missing statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations expires leads to automatic dismissal of the case.

**How to avoid it:** Confirm the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type and file within the allowed time frame. [S4]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** Failure to serve the defendant correctly can delay the case or result in dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Follow the prescribed methods of service and ensure proof of service is filed with the court. [S1]

### Ignoring counterclaims

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant files a counterclaim and you don't respond, you could lose by default.

**How to avoid it:** Address any counterclaims promptly and prepare to defend against them in court. [S1]

### Failing to collect judgment

**What goes wrong:** Winning a judgment doesn't guarantee payment; you must take steps to collect.

**How to avoid it:** Familiarize yourself with collection methods and act promptly to enforce the judgment. [S1]

### Overlooking exemptions

**What goes wrong:** Debtors can claim certain property as exempt from collection, limiting recovery options.

**How to avoid it:** Understand the debtor's exemption rights and plan collection efforts accordingly. [S1]

## 18. Recent changes

- **2025-01-01** — **Increase in small claims monetary limit**. The jurisdictional limit for small claims court increased from $5,000 to $10,000, allowing more cases to be heard in this forum. N.C. Gen. Stat. § 7A-210. [S4]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in small claims court. The process is designed to be accessible for individuals representing themselves. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: Most small claims cases are scheduled for a hearing within 30 days of filing and are typically resolved at the hearing. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: The maximum amount you can sue for in North Carolina's small claims court is $10,000. [S1]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear, the magistrate may enter a default judgment in favor of the plaintiff, provided the plaintiff presents sufficient evidence. [S1]

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: Yes, either party can appeal the magistrate's decision to district court within 10 days for a new trial. [S1]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: A judgment is valid for 10 years and can be renewed once for an additional 10 years. [S1]
