## 1. Jurisdictional limits — MAXIMUM AMOUNT YOU CAN SUE FOR  
**Dollar caps:** In California small claims court, a natural person (individual) can sue for up to **$12,500** per case ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-or-limited-civil#:~:text=If%20you%20are%20an%20individual,there%20may%20be%20an%20advantage)) ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/ca/code-of-civil-procedure/ccp-sect-116-221/#:~:text=In%20addition%20to%20the%20jurisdiction,231)). However, businesses and other entities (corporations, LLCs, partnerships, government bodies) have a lower cap of **$6,250** ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-or-limited-civil#:~:text=If%20you%20are%20an%20individual,there%20may%20be%20an%20advantage)) ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.220.#:~:text=,6%2C250)). These limits are per case – you cannot demand more than the cap in your claim (any excess must be waived). 

**Per-year filing limit:** There is also a yearly limit on the number of large claims. No plaintiff may file more than **two small claims actions in a calendar year** where the amount demanded exceeds $2,500 ([www.sucorte.ca.gov](https://www.sucorte.ca.gov/small-claims/before-you-start#:~:text=,calendar%20year%20for%20over%20%242%2C500)). In other words, you can only bring at most two claims above $2,500 each year (there is no limit on the number of claims for $2,500 or less). This applies to both individuals and businesses. 

**Sole proprietors:** If you are a sole proprietor suing on behalf of your business (e.g. you operate under a fictitious business name), you are treated as a natural person – your limit is $12,500 (assuming you’ve met any fictitious business name registration requirements – see Section 13) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/california/code-ccp/part-1/title-1/chapter-5-5/article-5/section-116-540/#:~:text=,corporation%20in%20small%20claims%20court)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/california/code-ccp/part-1/title-1/chapter-5-5/article-5/section-116-540/#:~:text=,the%20following%20conditions%20are%20met)). But if your business is a separate legal entity (corporation, LLC, etc.), the $6,250 entity cap applies.  

**No claim-splitting:** You cannot skirt the limits by “splitting” a larger claim into multiple smaller lawsuits. California does not allow dividing one cause of action into separate suits to get under the small claims cap. Instead, you must either **waive** any amount above the small-claims limit or file in a higher court ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/california/code-ccp/part-1/title-1/chapter-5-5/article-2/section-116-220/#:~:text=,is%20not%20operative%20until%20judgment)). For example, if someone owes you $15,000 on a single contract, you could sue for $12,500 in small claims and **waive** the extra $2,500[^1], but you cannot file two claims for $7,500 each. If you try to split a single claim, the court will likely dismiss the case for violating the *one cause of action* rule. 

**Important:** These dollar limits were raised in 2024 (the individual limit increased from $10,000 to $12,500) ([www.rygardnerlaw.com](https://www.rygardnerlaw.com/consumers-rejoice-small-claims-court-limits-are-raised#:~:text=As%20of%20January%201%2C%202024%2C,220)). Make sure you are using the current numbers. Also, note that these caps are for the **claim amount** not including allowable court costs. If you win, you can often add court fees and certain costs on top of the judgment without worrying about the cap. 

[^1]: Cal. Code of Civ. Procedure § 116.220(d) – If a claim exceeds the small claims jurisdictional amount, the excess may be waived (giving up the right to recover that portion) to proceed in small claims ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/california/code-ccp/part-1/title-1/chapter-5-5/article-2/section-116-220/#:~:text=,is%20not%20operative%20until%20judgment)). 

## 2. What types of cases qualify  
**Allowed in small claims:** Generally, small claims court can handle any **civil dispute for money** (or recovery of specific property) within the jurisdictional limit. Typical cases include: 

- **Contract disputes** – e.g. unpaid bills, loans, or invoices; security deposit refund claims; contractor or repair disputes; breaches of a service agreement, etc. ([abcdocz.com](https://abcdocz.com/doc/123939/small-claims-court-the#:~:text=Examples%20of%20other%20disputes%20that,%E2%80%A2%20YournewTVwillnotwork%2Candthestorerefuses)). For example, a tenant suing a landlord for return of a security deposit, or a freelancer suing a client for unpaid fees, would be classic small claims matters.  
- **Property damage or personal injury** – e.g. damage to your car from a minor traffic accident, damage to your property by a neighbor, or injuries from a minor incident (note the $12,500 limit) ([abcdocz.com](https://abcdocz.com/doc/123939/small-claims-court-the#:~:text=security%20deposit%20you%20paid,%E2%80%A2%20Yourtenantcauseddamagetotheapartmentin)). Many small claims arise from car accidents (so long as injuries and damages are within the small claims limit) and minor personal injury claims such as a dog bite or slip-and-fall with relatively low medical costs.  
- **Landlord-tenant disputes (money only)** – e.g. tenant suing over an unreturned security deposit or a landlord suing a former tenant for property damage (not for eviction – evictions are not allowed in small claims). You can sue for unpaid rent as a money claim (including COVID-19 rental debt within certain limits), or for property damage beyond normal wear-and-tear ([abcdocz.com](https://abcdocz.com/doc/123939/small-claims-court-the#:~:text=security%20deposit%20you%20paid,%E2%80%A2%20Yourtenantcauseddamagetotheapartmentin)). (However, see below regarding recent COVID-19 rental debt rules.)  
- **Consumer purchase disputes** – e.g. a person suing a mechanic for a botched car repair, or a customer suing a contractor for unfinished work, or suing a store for failing to honor a refund. If you bought goods or services and something went wrong (defective product, poor workmanship, etc.), you can seek compensation up to the limit.  
- **Loan and debt collection** – if someone borrowed money or goods and didn’t repay, you can sue to recover the debt (plus allowable interest, if any). Businesses or collection agencies can also use small claims to collect debts (subject to the filing limits noted).  

In short, any case that is essentially “I am owed money (or the return of specific personal property) up to $12,500” is a potential small claim. The court can even award **equitable relief** in a limited sense – for instance, **rescinding a contract** or **ordering restitution** – but only as a complement to a money judgment and where authorized by statute ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.220.#:~:text=,court%20to%20award%20that%20relief)). The primary relief must be monetary damages or recovery of specific personal property (worth <= $6,250 for entities or $12,500 for individuals) ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.220.#:~:text=,6%2C250)) ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.220.#:~:text=,6%2C250)). 

**Explicitly NOT allowed:** Small claims **cannot** be used for cases that seek primarily non-monetary relief or that fall under special court procedures: 

- **No evictions (unlawful detainer)** – You *cannot* use small claims to evict a tenant or otherwise recover possession of real property ([abcdocz.com](https://abcdocz.com/doc/123939/small-claims-court-the#:~:text=,%E2%80%A2%20Youweredefraudedinthepurchaseofacar%2Cand)). Eviction cases must be filed as formal unlawful detainer actions in regular superior court. (A small claims court can *order* someone to return a specific item of personal property – e.g. a piece of equipment – but not real property like land or a rental unit ([abcdocz.com](https://abcdocz.com/doc/123939/small-claims-court-the#:~:text=court%20can%20order%20the%20return,small%20claims%20court%20are%3A%20%E2%80%A2)).)  
- **No family law or probate matters** – Small claims does not handle divorce, child support, custody, alimony, wills, estates, or similar family/probate issues. Those must go to the family or probate divisions of the court. For example, you cannot sue someone in small claims to enforce a child support order or to determine inheritance rights.  
- **No defamation or harassment injunctions** – Claims for defamation (libel/slander) or requests for restraining orders/injunctions are generally not suitable for small claims. Small claims judges can’t issue injunctions except where a law specifically allows it ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.220.#:~:text=,court%20to%20award%20that%20relief)). (You *could* theoretically sue for money damages from defamation, but such cases are complex and better suited for regular court. Small claims has no power to order someone to stop saying or publishing something – only for money damages.)  
- **No class actions or representative suits** – Each small claims case is an individual claim. You cannot bring a class action in small claims, and generally you can’t represent anyone else’s claim (every plaintiff must be present or properly represented as allowed; see Section 13).  
- **Title to real estate** – Small claims can’t determine ownership of real property or issues like quiet title or foreclosure. If your dispute is fundamentally about who owns land or a house, that’s outside small claims jurisdiction.  
- **Tax, bankruptcy, or federal law issues** – Small claims is a division of the state superior court, so it cannot decide federal claims that belong in federal court (e.g. patent or bankruptcy matters). It also does not handle disputes with the IRS or California tax agencies.  

Additionally, the small claims court cannot order specific performance or other purely equitable remedies *unless* a statute explicitly allows it ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.220.#:~:text=,court%20to%20award%20that%20relief)). It deals mainly in **money judgments**. If you need a court order making someone do or stop doing something (like an injunction), small claims is not the venue. 

**Counterclaims:** If you are sued in small claims, you *may file a counterclaim* (called a Defendant’s Claim, form SC-120) against the plaintiff if you also have a dispute with them. The counterclaim is basically your own small claim and is subject to the **same monetary limits** – e.g. an individual can counter-sue for up to $12,500, a business up to $6,250 ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-or-limited-civil#:~:text=If%20you%20are%20an%20individual,there%20may%20be%20an%20advantage)). If your claim against the plaintiff **exceeds** the small claims limit, you have the option to **ask the court to transfer the case** to the regular superior court ([www.legalfina.com](https://www.legalfina.com/en/laws/ca/civil_procedure/of_courts_of_justice/organization_and_jurisdiction/small_claims_court/actions#:~:text=,the%20plaintiff%E2%80%99s%20claim%2C%20the%20defendant)). In fact, California law says that if a defendant’s claim is beyond small claims jurisdiction and arises out of the same transaction, the defendant can file a motion to move the entire case to a higher court ([www.legalfina.com](https://www.legalfina.com/en/laws/ca/civil_procedure/of_courts_of_justice/organization_and_jurisdiction/small_claims_court/actions#:~:text=,the%20plaintiff%E2%80%99s%20claim%2C%20the%20defendant)). This is done by filing a *motion to transfer under CCP §116.390* with a declaration explaining the large counterclaim. If granted, the case will be heard as a regular civil (usually limited civil) case, where both sides can then have attorneys. (If the motion to transfer is denied by the small claims judge, the defendant could choose to waive the excess and stay in small claims, or not file the counterclaim in small claims at all.) 

**Important:** If you file a Defendant’s Claim, it should be filed **before or at the time of the hearing** on the plaintiff’s claim, and you must properly serve the plaintiff with your claim. The small claims court often hears both the claim and counterclaim together at the same hearing (if the timing of service allows). The counterclaim filing fee is the same schedule as a normal claim (and if you already filed two claims over $2,500 this year, you might be barred from filing a third such counterclaim). If you have a potential counterclaim, do not delay – file it early enough so you can serve it on the plaintiff at least 15 days before the trial (or 20 days if the plaintiff is out of county) ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.340#:~:text=,which%20the%20action%20is%20filed)). Otherwise the plaintiff can ask for a postponement to have time to prepare a defense. 

## 3. Where to file  
**Court name:** Small claims cases in California are heard in the **Superior Court** for the county. Each county’s superior court has a Small Claims division (sometimes informally called “Small Claims Court”). For example, if your dispute is in Los Angeles County, you file in the Los Angeles County Superior Court – Small Claims. In larger counties, the Superior Court may designate specific branch courthouses to handle small claims cases (e.g. LA County has several district courthouses for small claims, San Diego County directs cases by geographic area, etc.). The key is that you must file in the proper county *and* the proper courthouse location as required by venue rules. 

**Venue (proper county):** As a general rule, you should file in the county (and court district) where the **defendant** lives or does business ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims/start-case/where-to-file#:~:text=Figure%20out%20which%20court%20to,file%20in)). This is the usual venue for small claims. However, there are several venue provisions that give you options depending on the case: 

- **For injury or property damage cases:** You may file in the county where the injury or damage occurred *in addition* to where the defendant resides ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims/start-case/where-to-file#:~:text=,or%20car%20accident)). For example, if you were in a car accident while traveling, you can sue in the county where the crash happened or where the defendant driver lives. 
- **For contract cases:** You can file in the county where the contract was **entered** (made), where it was to be **performed** (or where performance was due), or where it was **breached**, as well as where the defendant lives ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims/start-case/where-to-file#:~:text=,contract)). So if you’re suing over an agreement, venue is proper either in the defendant’s home county or in the county where the events of the contract took place (e.g. where it was signed or supposed to happen). 
- **For sales of goods:** If you’re suing regarding an item you sold, you can sue in the county where the item was **sold or delivered**, or where the buyer lives now or lived at the time of sale ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims/start-case/where-to-file#:~:text=,you%20sold)). Similarly, if it’s about something you *bought* or services you paid for, you can also file where you (the purchaser) live or lived when the purchase was made, in addition to where the seller is located ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims/start-case/where-to-file#:~:text=,or%20work%20you%20paid%20for)). 
- **Other special venue rules:** There are specialized venue statutes for certain cases. For example, if you’re suing a landlord for return of a security deposit, venue is proper where the property is located *and* where the landlord lives. If you’re suing a contractor who performed work on your property, you might file where the work was done. When in doubt, consult the California Code of Civil Procedure venue provisions (CCP §§ 392–395) or ask the small claims clerk or advisor. 

**Consequences of wrong venue:** Filing in the wrong county (or wrong district within a county) can result in your case being dismissed. The court may not automatically transfer it – often, the case will simply be **thrown out** if venue is improper ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims/start-case/where-to-file#:~:text=If%20you%20file%20in%20the,help%20from%20your%C2%A0small%20claims%20advisor)) ([dcba.lacounty.gov](https://dcba.lacounty.gov/portfolio/venue-filing-in-the-right-court-2/#:~:text=There%20are%20twelve%20Small%20Claims,This%20is%20called%20the%20venue)). This can be a costly mistake because you’ll have to re-file in the correct court and pay the filing fee again – and if the statute of limitations has run in the meantime, you could lose your claim entirely. Therefore, ensure the county you choose is allowed by one of the venue rules above. If you genuinely cannot determine the correct venue (for example, you don’t know the defendant’s current address), the small claims advisor might be able to help, or you may need to hire a professional process server or investigator to locate the defendant. 

**Finding the right courthouse:** Within a county, there may be multiple small claims courthouses. For instance, San Diego County has small claims hearings in a few regional courthouses (Central, North County, East County, South County), and Los Angeles County has about a dozen courthouses that handle small claims, each serving certain ZIP codes or cities. Check the Superior Court’s website for your county under “Locations” or “Small Claims” to find which courthouse covers your area or the area of your dispute. Many courts provide a **venue lookup tool** (e.g., Los Angeles has a Filing Court Locator where you input a ZIP code to find the proper small claims court location ([dcba.lacounty.gov](https://dcba.lacounty.gov/portfolio/venue-filing-in-the-right-court-2/#:~:text=Based%20on%20these%20choices%2C%20there,you%20find%20the%20right%20court))). You can also ask the court clerk. Filing at the wrong courthouse (even if it’s the right county) might delay your case since it will need to be transferred or re-filed. 

**Electronic filing (e-filing):** Whether you can file your small claim online depends on the county. California is gradually rolling out e-filing, but it’s not yet statewide for small claims. Some counties **permit or even require e-filing** for small claims: for example, **Orange County** allows electronic filing of small claims through court-approved service providers ([www.occourts.org](https://www.occourts.org/online-services/efiling/efiling-small-claims#:~:text=Select%20one%20of%20the%20following,your%20small%20claims%20documents)), and **Sacramento County** expanded permissive e-filing to small claims as of June 2024 ([saccourt.ca.gov](https://saccourt.ca.gov/small-claims/e-filing.aspx#:~:text=As%20of%20June%2017%2C%202024%2C,file%20whenever%20possible)). On the other hand, **Los Angeles County** currently does **not** offer e-filing for small claims (LA had a pilot e-file system that was **decommissioned** – as of now, small claims filings in LA must be done by paper, either in person, by mail, or by fax filing) ([ww2.lacourt.org](https://ww2.lacourt.org/eFiling/eFilingNotice.aspx#:~:text=You%20may%20submit%20your%20Small,Claims%20filings%20via)). Always check your local court’s website for up-to-date e-filing information. If e-filing is available, the court will list approved Electronic Filing Service Providers (EFSPs) on its site and you’ll need to file through one of those portals. If e-filing is not available, you must prepare paper forms and submit them to the clerk. 

**Filing by mail or drop-box:** In all counties, you have the option to file in person at the courthouse clerk’s office. Many courts also accept filing by **mail** – you send your completed forms and fees to the court’s mailing address (allow extra time and include a self-addressed stamped envelope for return of your filed copies). Some courts (like LA) allow **fax filing** for small claims for a small additional fee ([ww2.lacourt.org](https://ww2.lacourt.org/eFiling/eFilingNotice.aspx#:~:text=You%20may%20submit%20your%20Small,Claims%20filings%20via)) – you fax your documents to a dedicated number and the clerk files them. And nearly all courthouses have a **drop-box** where you can drop off filings after hours. Be mindful that if you mail or drop-box your claim, the hearing date will be set and mailed to you, which might delay things slightly compared to filing in person where the clerk can often give you a trial date on the spot. 

**Venue vs. jurisdiction:** Remember, “venue” is about the correct location; “jurisdiction” is about the authority to hear the case. In small claims, jurisdiction is mostly about the claim amount and subject matter (as covered in Section 1 and 2). Make sure you meet the jurisdictional requirements (e.g. not asking for more than $12,500, and a valid cause of action for money) *and* file in a proper venue. 

**If the defendant moves or is out of state:** If your defendant has moved out of California, small claims may not be practical – the court might not have personal jurisdiction unless the dispute is connected to California. If the defendant only moved to a different county in CA, you generally still sue where the dispute arose or the contract was made, etc., as allowed, but serving them may require extra steps (see Service of Process in Section 8). 

In summary, choose your filing location carefully. Filing in the wrong place can lead to dismissal ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims/start-case/where-to-file#:~:text=If%20you%20file%20in%20the,help%20from%20your%C2%A0small%20claims%20advisor)). Use the venue rules to determine the correct county and courthouse. When in doubt, call the small claims clerk or consult the court’s self-help materials for guidance on venue. 

## 4. Filing fees  
Filing a small claim requires paying a **court filing fee** (unless you qualify for a fee waiver – see below). The filing fee amount in California is **tiered based on the amount you’re suing for**, and also on how many small claims you have filed in the past year. The fee schedule (current as of 2026) is: 

| **Amount of your claim**                 | **Filing Fee**        |  
| ---------------------------------------- | --------------------- |  
| $0 to $1,500                              | $30 **fee** ([www.lacourt.org](https://www.lacourt.org/page/SC0024#:~:text=Your%20claim%20amount%20is%3A%20,%2475.00))  |  
| $1,500.01 to $5,000                       | $50 **fee** ([www.lacourt.org](https://www.lacourt.org/page/SC0024#:~:text=Your%20claim%20amount%20is%3A%20,%2475.00))  |  
| $5,000.01 to $12,500 (individuals only[^2]) | $75 **fee** ([www.lacourt.org](https://www.lacourt.org/page/SC0024#:~:text=%240.01%20to%20%241%2C500.00%20%20,%2475.00)) ([dcba.lacounty.gov](https://dcba.lacounty.gov/portfolio/small-claims-court-fees-3/#:~:text=Amount%20of%20Your%20Claim%20,you%20must%20pay%20a%20%24100)) |  
| More than 12 claims filed in last 12 months (any amount) | $100 **fee** ([www.lacourt.org](https://www.lacourt.org/page/SC0024#:~:text=Your%20claim%20amount%20is%3A%20,%24100.00)) ([dcba.lacounty.gov](https://dcba.lacounty.gov/portfolio/small-claims-court-fees-3/#:~:text=Amount%20of%20Your%20Claim%20,you%20must%20pay%20a%20%24100)) |  

- **Frequent filer fee:** If you (as an individual or business) have filed more than 12 small claims in California within the previous 12 months, the filing fee for each new case is **$100** ([www.lacourt.org](https://www.lacourt.org/page/SC0024#:~:text=Your%20claim%20amount%20is%3A%20,%24100.00)). This rule is designed to discourage bulk use of small claims by high-volume filers (like collection agencies). The $100 fee applies regardless of the claim amount once you exceed 12 filings in a year. 

[^2]: Only natural persons (individuals) can sue for more than $5,000 in small claims. Businesses and organizations are capped at $6,250 ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-or-limited-civil#:~:text=If%20you%20are%20an%20individual,there%20may%20be%20an%20advantage)), so the $75 fee bracket effectively only applies to individuals. A business suing for, say, $6,000 would pay $75 (since $6,000 > $5k) – but they can’t go above $6,250 by law.  

**Payment methods:** Filing fees are paid to the clerk at the time of filing your Plaintiff’s Claim (form SC-100). Courts accept cash, check, money order, and often credit/debit cards (in person). If mailing your filing, you can include a check or money order payable to “Clerk of the Superior Court.” Some courts have an online payment system if e-filing. Always verify the payee name and acceptable payment forms on your court’s website. 

**Fee waivers:** If you cannot afford the filing fee (or other court fees such as service costs), you can request a **fee waiver**. To do this, you fill out form FW-001 (Request to Waive Court Fees) and FW-003 (Order on Court Fee Waiver) and submit them with your claim. If the court finds you meet the income guidelines or public assistance criteria, your filing fee can be waived. This means you pay $0 now, though if you win and collect money, the court may order the fees repaid out of the judgment. The fee waiver covers not only the filing fee but also other court fees like service by a sheriff (if you use the sheriff) and hearing fees. You must disclose financial information (income, expenses, etc.) on the FW-001. The clerk can provide these forms, and there is no fee to apply for a waiver ([dcba.lacounty.gov](https://dcba.lacounty.gov/portfolio/small-claims-court-fees-3/#:~:text=If%20you%20cannot%20afford%20to,court%20to%20consider%20your%20request)). It’s important to **apply for the waiver before or at the same time as filing** your claim; if you’ve already paid, it’s often too late to get a refund. If your waiver request is denied, you have the option to pay the fees or ask for a hearing. 

**Serving the defendant – costs:** In addition to the filing fee, budget for the cost of serving your claim on the defendant (see Section 8 on Service of Process). If you choose the **Sheriff or Marshal** to serve the papers, the fee is typically around **$40–$50 per defendant** in California (varies by county – for example, Los Angeles Sheriff charges $50 for serving a claim) ([dcba.lacounty.gov](https://dcba.lacounty.gov/portfolio/small-claims-court-fees-3/#:~:text=Service%20of%20Plaintiff%E2%80%99s%20Claim%20,%242%2C000)). This fee can be added to the judgment as recoverable cost if you win. If you use a **registered process server**, fees usually range from ~$75 to $150 depending on distance and number of attempts (this is not set by law – it’s a private service fee, but likewise recoverable in the judgment). If you opt for the court to serve by **certified mail** (available in some courts), the court charges a small fee (around **$15**) for this service ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims/start-case/serve#:~:text=,only%20offered%20in%20some%20courts)). Certified mail service has a lower success rate (the defendant must sign the receipt), so many people prefer personal or substitute service despite the higher cost. 

**Other fees:** Here are some other fees that might come up in a small claims case: 

- **Motion to postpone (continue) the hearing:** $10 (if you request a postponement of the trial, using form SC-150, there’s a $10 fee unless you have a fee waiver).  
- **Requesting an interpreter:** No fee for a court-provided interpreter. California courts provide interpreters in small claims free of charge for languages commonly requested, but you usually need to notify the court in advance. (If you bring your own private interpreter, the court doesn’t charge any fee.)  
- **Parking and convenience fees:** Not court fees, but note that some courthouses charge parking fees. Also, if you file by fax, the provider may charge a small fax filing fee in addition to the court fee (e.g., $2–$3 per page).  

All standard fees in civil cases (including small claims) are set by state law. You can find the official **Statewide Civil Fee Schedule** on the California Courts website, which confirms the amounts above. If you win your case, the court will usually award your filing fee and process server fee as part of the judgment, meaning the defendant should reimburse those costs ([dcba.lacounty.gov](https://dcba.lacounty.gov/portfolio/small-claims-court-fees-3/#:~:text=You%20must%20pay%20the%20court%2C,a%20service%20provided%20by%20the)). Conversely, if you lose, you generally lose your own fees (but you would not be paying the opponent’s fees, since attorney fees aren’t allowed except in exceptional cases in small claims). 

## 5. Statute of limitations  
Every legal claim has a *statute of limitations* – a deadline by which you must file the lawsuit. Filing even one day late can result in your case being dismissed as time-barred. The clock for the statute of limitations (SOL) usually starts when your claim “accrues,” typically the date of the injury, breach, or loss (or the date you discovered the problem, in some cases). Below is a summary of California’s statutes of limitations for common claim types relevant to small claims: 

| **Type of Claim**                     | **Limitation Period**                                          | **Governing Law**                   |
|---------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| **Written contract** (breach of a written agreement, promissory note, etc.) | 4 years from the date the contract was broken (breach) ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/civil-lawsuit/statute-limitations#:~:text=,the%20injury)). If the breach was ongoing, from the date of the last breach. | CCP § 337(1) ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/civil-lawsuit/statute-limitations#:~:text=,the%20injury)) |
| **Oral contract** (verbal agreements, unwritten promises) | 2 years from the date the contract was broken ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/civil-lawsuit/statute-limitations#:~:text=,the%20injury)). (Many informal agreements fall in this category if not made in writing.) | CCP § 339(1) ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/civil-lawsuit/statute-limitations#:~:text=,the%20injury)) |
| **Property damage** (damage to personal or real property, e.g. car damage, vandalism) | 3 years from the date the damage occurred ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/civil-lawsuit/statute-limitations#:~:text=,date%20the%20contract%20was%20broken)). (For ongoing damage, possibly from the last day of damage.) | CCP § 338(c) ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/civil-lawsuit/statute-limitations#:~:text=,date%20the%20contract%20was%20broken)) |
| **Personal injury** (injury to a person, such as in a car accident, slip-and-fall, etc.) | 2 years from the date of injury (or from the date you discovered the injury) ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/civil-lawsuit/statute-limitations#:~:text=Common%20statutes%20of%20limitations%3A)) ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/civil-lawsuit/statute-limitations#:~:text=Sometimes%2C%20if%20the%20problem%20,this%20situation%20applies%20to%20you)). (If the injury wasn’t immediately apparent, the “discovery rule” may apply – see below.) | CCP § 335.1 ([www.frahmanlaw.com](https://www.frahmanlaw.com/civil-law-time-limits/#:~:text=Firm%20www.frahmanlaw.com%20%20,or%20from%20the%20date%20on)) |
| **Fraud or deceit** (misrepresentation causing loss) | 3 years from when the fraud was discovered (or should have been discovered with reasonable diligence). <br>*(Example:* You learn in 2023 that a used car sold to you in 2020 had its odometer rolled back. The SOL would likely run 3 years from discovery in 2023.)* | CCP § 338(d) (fraud discovery rule) |
| **Unpaid wages** (claims for earned wages, overtime, commissions, etc.) | 3 years from date the wages were due. <br>*(Note:* Some wage claims could be 4 years if based on a written contract, or have other deadlines under labor laws. The Labor Commissioner’s jurisdiction has a three-year SOL for most wage claims, so 3 years is a safe general rule in court as well.)* | CCP § 338(a) (liability created by statute), Labor Code § 1194 (wage claims) |
| **Breach of warranty (sales of goods)** | 4 years from delivery of the goods for breach of a **written warranty** on a sale of goods. <br>Implied warranties: 4 years from delivery as well (under UCC). | Com. Code § 2725(1) (UCC sales contracts) |
| **Open book account** (e.g. credit card debt, revolving account) | 4 years from the date of the last entry (last payment or charge) on the account. <br>*Note:* Credit card debts are often pleaded as “open book accounts” to get a 4-year SOL (if not based on a signed agreement). | CCP § 337(2) (book accounts) |
| **Judgment enforcement** (to enforce a prior court judgment) | **10 years** from entry of the judgment (to file for renewal or enforcement) ([ryancwoodlaw.com](https://ryancwoodlaw.com/judgement-enforcement-renewal-of-judgment-liens/#:~:text=683,pursuant%20to%20the%20judgment%20is)). *This is relevant if you are suing on an older judgment from another state or a past small claims case.* | CCP § 683.020 ([ryancwoodlaw.com](https://ryancwoodlaw.com/judgement-enforcement-renewal-of-judgment-liens/#:~:text=683,pursuant%20to%20the%20judgment%20is)) (judgment expires after 10 years unless renewed) |

These are general statutes. **Important:** Determining the correct statute of limitations can be tricky. Some situations have special rules or exceptions, and if your case type isn’t listed above, you’ll need to find the applicable law. For instance, claims for professional malpractice, certain government claims, or specific statutory causes of action may have different time limits. If you’re unsure, **consult an attorney** or do further research – filing after the limitations period means the defendant can get the case dismissed immediately by raising a statute of limitations defense. 

**When the clock starts:** In many cases the clock starts at the moment the wrongful act occurred or the contract was breached. For example, if a written contract was breached on March 1, 2022, you generally have until March 1, 2026 to sue for that breach. However, there’s a key exception called the **discovery rule**: if you **did not discover the problem right away**, the SOL may start running from the date you discovered (or reasonably should have discovered) the injury or issue ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/civil-lawsuit/statute-limitations#:~:text=Sometimes%2C%20if%20the%20problem%20,this%20situation%20applies%20to%20you)). This often arises in cases of fraud or hidden defects. For instance, if a contractor concealed a construction defect and you discover it 2 years later, the SOL for fraud would run from the discovery date (but you must show you couldn’t have found it sooner). The discovery rule is fact-specific and not automatic; you might have to convince the judge that delayed discovery was justified. 

**Tolling:** Certain circumstances can “toll” (pause) the statute of limitations. Examples: if the defendant is a minor, the clock might not run until they turn 18; if the defendant is out of state or in prison for a long period, tolling might apply; or if the plaintiff is mentally incapacitated or bankrupt proceedings are involved, tolling could pause the countdown. These situations are governed by specific statutes (see CCP §§ 351 et seq. for tolling provisions). Generally, assume the clock is ticking unless you have a clear basis for tolling. 

**Don’t wait too long:** It’s risky to file a claim that is very old. Even if you calculate that you are just within the deadline, evidence and witnesses might be harder to obtain as time passes. It’s best to act promptly. If your claim is nearing the deadline (or worse, past it), that’s an issue you must seriously consider – the court will expect you to know the limitation period. If you file after the SOL, the defendant can move to dismiss. The court will not do this automatically; the defendant must raise it as an affirmative defense. But rest assured, they almost always will, because it’s a quick way to win. 

**Practical tip:** When you fill out your Plaintiff’s Claim, there’s a section that asks, “When did this happen?” or “Date of occurrence.” If that date is outside the normal statute of limitations, expect the judge to ask why your case should not be time-barred. Be prepared to explain any discovery-rule or tolling argument (with evidence). Otherwise, the judge may dismiss the case on their own motion or upon the defendant’s explanation. 

In summary, check the statute of limitations for your type of case **before** filing. Common deadlines: 2 years for personal injuries and oral contracts ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/civil-lawsuit/statute-limitations#:~:text=,the%20injury)), 4 years for written contracts ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/civil-lawsuit/statute-limitations#:~:text=,the%20injury)), 3 years for property damage ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/civil-lawsuit/statute-limitations#:~:text=,date%20the%20contract%20was%20broken)) or fraud, etc. If in doubt, it’s safer to file sooner and let the court sort it out, rather than miss the deadline. 

## 6. Pre-filing requirements  
One hallmark of small claims court is its informality – there is generally no requirement for formal demand letters, mediation, or other pre-filing actions under California law **except in specific types of disputes**. However, it is **strongly recommended** (and sometimes required by good sense or courtesy) to attempt certain steps before suing: 

- **Demand letter:** While not strictly required by statute in most cases, it’s wise to send the potential defendant a **demand letter** asking for payment before you sue. In fact, the small claims court information booklet advises doing so, and some judges will ask if you gave the defendant an opportunity to resolve the issue. A concise letter (or email) that states what is owed and a deadline to pay can sometimes resolve the matter without litigation. If you do go to court, bringing a copy of that demand letter shows the judge you tried to settle reasonably. *(Exception:* Certain statutes, like the federal Fair Debt Collection Practices Act, require a notice before suing for some claims – but that’s outside small claims scope generally.)* 

- **California “Delayed Discovery” disputes:** If your claim involves something like a defective product or poor workmanship, it’s often useful (though not mandated) to notify the seller or contractor and give them a chance to fix the problem. For example, in a home improvement dispute, sending a list of defects and requesting repairs *before* suing might be looked upon favorably by the court. Again, not a formal requirement, but it can bolster your case if the other side ignored you. 

- **Mandatory mediation or ADR:** California small claims courts do **not** require mediation or alternative dispute resolution before filing. **However,** many courts offer **free or low-cost mediation** on the day of the small claims hearing (or even before). For instance, in some counties the judge will ask parties on the hearing day if they want to step outside and mediate with a trained mediator. This is voluntary but highly encouraged. In a few places (like some Northern California courts), there are programs where if both parties agree, they can mediate *instead* of going to hearing, and if an agreement is reached, the case is taken off calendar. Check your local court’s small claims web page for any pre-filing mediation programs. Participation is typically optional – you can always choose to have your day in court. 

- **Special notice for government claims:** If your claim is against a government agency or municipality, you usually must file a **Government Claim** first *before* suing (even in small claims). California law requires that you submit a claim to the agency (often within 6 months for injury or property claims) and have it denied or ignored, before you can file a court action. For example, if you’re suing a city for damage to your car caused by a poorly maintained road, you must follow the Government Claims Act procedures (Gov. Code § 910 et seq.). Only after the government entity denies your claim (or 45 days pass without response) can you file in small claims. If you sue a government entity without doing this, the case will be dismissed for lack of jurisdiction. So, for any government-related case, research the “claim filing” requirement and deadlines. 

- **Landlord-tenant specific:** For certain landlord-tenant issues, state or local law may require a notice before action. For instance, a landlord demanding payment for property damage might have needed to provide a security deposit accounting within 21 days after move-out (Civ. Code § 1950.5); a tenant seeking a deposit return should ideally demand it in writing after that 21-day period. If you’re a landlord suing a former tenant, make sure you complied with any legal prerequisites like providing a disposition of deposit or notice of damages – failing to do so can hurt your case (the tenant could even countersue for deposit penalties). 

- **Consumer protection statutes:** Some consumer laws ask for notice. E.g., California’s Lemon Law (Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act) requires that the manufacturer be given a reasonable number of repair attempts – essentially an opportunity to fix the issue – before the buyer can seek a refund or replacement. If you’re using Lemon Law rights in small claims (you can, if the amount is within $12,500), be sure you followed those steps (notify manufacturer or dealer of the issue and allow repairs). Similarly, for claims under the Consumers Legal Remedies Act (CLRA) for certain unfair practices, a 30-day demand letter is required (Civil Code § 1782). While CLRA claims for damages under $12,500 could theoretically be in small claims, they’re rare. Just remember: if a specific law underpinning your claim has a pre-suit notice requirement, you must honor it even in small claims. 

In **summary**, California doesn’t impose universal pre-filing requirements for small claims – you generally can go ahead and file once you decide to. But practically: **always attempt to resolve** the dispute first. Not only might you save time and the filing fee, but if it ends up in court, you can show you made good-faith efforts. Keep copies of any letters, emails, or texts where you requested payment or resolution. The court likes to see that the lawsuit wasn’t your first resort. 

**Note:** When you fill out your Plaintiff’s Claim, there’s a section asking what you asked for and how you calculated the amount. This is a good place to mention “I asked the defendant on [date] to pay $X or return my property and they refused” – showing that a demand was made. 

Finally, **no arbitration clause issues** – In small claims, arbitration clauses in contracts (which usually compel disputes to private arbitration) are **not enforceable against the plaintiff.** If you’re the one suing in small claims, you can ignore an arbitration clause (CCP § 1284.3(b) actually prohibits companies from forcing consumers into arbitration in small claims; also, by choosing small claims, you waive over-$7,500 amounts, etc.). The defendant, however, *does* give up the right to arbitrate by participating (and cannot remove the case to arbitration). So don’t worry if your contract had an arbitration clause – you are still free to file in small claims; the opposing side can’t compel arbitration once you’ve chosen this forum. 

## 7. Forms required  
To start a small claims case in California, you will need to fill out and file certain **Judicial Council forms**. All counties use the same standard forms for small claims (with some courts adding local forms for particular needs). Here are the primary forms and their purposes: 

- **Plaintiff’s Claim and ORDER to Go to Small Claims Court (Form **SC-100**):** This is the main **complaint** form for small claims. It tells the court and the defendant who you are, who you are suing, how much you are suing for, and why ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-forms#:~:text=SC,Plaintiff%27s%20Claim%20and%20ORDER%20to)). It also includes a court order setting the hearing (the clerk will fill in the date, time, and location of your trial after you file). Essentially, SC-100 is your lawsuit – you must complete it with details of your claim (there are sections to explain the dispute). The form also asks for the defendant’s address for service, and about venue (why this court is the right place). **Every small claims case begins with an SC-100 form.** (If you have multiple plaintiffs or defendants, see SC-100A below.)  

- **Other Plaintiffs or Defendants (Attachment to Plaintiff’s Claim) (Form **SC-100A**):** If you have more than one plaintiff or more than one defendant in your case, list the extra parties on this attachment ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-forms#:~:text=Fill%20out%20forms%20to%20start,Plaintiff%27s%20Claim%20and%20ORDER%20to)). SC-100 only has space for one plaintiff and one defendant; SC-100A can be attached to add additional names/addresses and their roles. For example, if you and your spouse are suing two defendants, you would file SC-100 plus an SC-100A listing the second plaintiff and second defendant. (Each SC-100A has room for two additional parties.) 

- **Fictitious Business Name (Declaration) (Form **SC-103**):** If you (the plaintiff) are suing under a **fictitious business name** (i.e., you’re doing business as “XYZ Auto Repair” and that’s the name you’re suing under), you must file a declaration confirming that you have complied with the fictitious business name laws ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-forms#:~:text=Go%20to%20Small%20Claims%20Court,103)). California Code of Civil Procedure § 116.430 requires that if a business (not a corporation or LLC) is the plaintiff and operates under a fictitious name, the business must have a current fictitious business name statement on file, and an SC-103 form must be filed with the court stating the real names of the owners and the county of registration. In practice: if you’re a sole proprietor or partnership using a DBA, fill out SC-103 and file it alongside your SC-100. (If you forget, the judge may ask you to complete it at the hearing or could dismiss the case until it’s done.) **Note:** This form is only for the *plaintiff’s* fictitious name. If the defendant is a business with a fictitious name, you do **not** need an SC-103 – instead, you should be sure to sue them under their true legal name, as discussed in Section 15. 

- **Proof of Service (Small Claims) (Form **SC-104**):** After filing, you must serve the defendant. Whoever serves the papers will need to sign a **Proof of Service** form (SC-104) to be filed with the court. SC-104 has check boxes for personal service or substituted service, etc., and is used to prove to the judge that the defendant was properly served. Each defendant needs a separate Proof of Service form. (There is also a **SC-104A** which is simply a second page if you need to list more service details or additional defendants served by one server, but usually SC-104 is enough). The proof must be filed at least 5 days before the trial ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.340#:~:text=,five%20days%20before%20the%20hearing)), or earlier if the court’s local rules require. The person who served (whether a friend, process server, or sheriff) fills out and signs the SC-104. If you used **certified mail service by the clerk**, the clerk will fill out a different form (often a **Clerk’s Certificate of Mailing** or SC-113) to file as proof ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/jcc-form/SC-113A#:~:text=Guide%20selfhelp,Go%20to)). Proper service and filing of the proof is crucial – without it, the judge cannot proceed with the case (see Section 8). 

- **Defendant’s Claim and ORDER to Go to Small Claims Court (Form **SC-120**):** If you are the defendant and wish to file a counterclaim (your own small claims suit against the plaintiff), this is the form to use ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/jcc-form/SC-120#:~:text=,Go)). It is essentially the same format as SC-100, but for the defendant to initiate their claim. The SC-120 has to be served on the plaintiff. For multiple parties, a **SC-120A** attachment (similar to SC-100A) is used. The defendant’s claim form must be filed before the hearing (ideally well in advance so it can be served on the plaintiff with the required notice). The clerk also issues an order on this form setting the hearing (usually the same date/time as the plaintiff’s claim if filed before the hearing). 

- **Notice of Authorization (Optional) (Form **SC-109**):** If a party (individual or business) wants to have a non-lawyer representative appear for them (allowed in certain situations – see Section 13 on who can appear), that representative should have a signed **Authorization to Appear** (SC-109) form ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/jcc-form/SC-109#:~:text=Authorization%20to%20Appear%20,Get%20form)). For example, a corporation sending a regular employee, or an individual who cannot attend sending their spouse, would use SC-109. The form identifies the representative and the capacity (e.g. an employee, a family member) and must be presented to the judge at the hearing. You typically do **not** file SC-109 with the clerk in advance; instead, the representative brings it on the hearing day. It’s a good idea for any business entity appearing through a non-lawyer agent to have SC-109 completed. If a party is an attorney representing themselves (or an attorney who is a partner/officer of a business party), SC-109 isn’t needed – that person is essentially the party. SC-109 is mostly to document lay representatives, since lawyers (except self-represented litigants) aren’t allowed in small claims. 

- **Request to Postpone Trial (Form **SC-150**):** If either party cannot attend on the scheduled court date, they may request a one-time postponement (continuance) of the trial. This is done with SC-150, which you file with the clerk (and mail a copy to the other side) no less than 10 days before the trial (or explain why you need a shorter notice). There’s a $10 fee for a postponement request unless you have a fee waiver. The form asks for the new date and reasons. The court will notify if the postponement is granted. If you absolutely cannot attend and it’s last minute, you might instead send a representative (with Authorization form) or as a very last resort, send a letter to the court explaining your side (although that is not as effective – if you don’t appear, you usually lose). Thus, SC-150 is your formal way to reschedule the hearing if needed. 

- **Request to Waive Court Fees (Form **FW-001**) and Order on Court Fee Waiver (Form **FW-003**):** As mentioned above, these are needed if you seek a fee waiver for filing fees or sheriff fees. You would fill out FW-001 (financial info) and submit it *with* your SC-100 at time of filing, and the court will issue FW-003 to grant or deny the waiver. These are standard Judicial Council forms used in all civil cases, including small claims. 

After judgment, if you win, there are additional forms for enforcement: for example, **Writ of Execution (Form EJ-130)** to garnish wages or levy accounts ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-forms#:~:text=court%20clerk%20court%20to%20,wages%2C%20or%20take%20possession%20of)), **Abstract of Judgment (Form EJ-001)** to place a lien on real property, **Notice of Judgment Lien (Form JL-1)** for liens on personal property, etc. If the debtor doesn’t pay, you might also use **SC-134 (Application and Order to Produce Statement of Assets)** or directly serve **SC-133 (Judgment Debtor’s Statement of Assets)** which the court clerk usually hands to the debtor if they appeared at trial ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-forms#:~:text=match%20at%20L314%20Judgment%20Debtor%27s,220)). Those enforcement forms come into play in Section 12 (Post-judgment collection). 

One form worth noting is **SC-133 (Judgment Debtor’s Statement of Assets)** – if you win and the debtor doesn’t pay within 30 days, they are supposed to complete SC-133 and mail it to you, listing their assets, employer, bank, etc. ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-forms#:~:text=match%20at%20L314%20Judgment%20Debtor%27s,220)). The court clerk often provides this form to the debtor with the judgment. If they don’t comply (and many don’t), you can then motion for a debtor’s examination. 

**Local forms:** Some counties have a few local forms for small claims. For example, a county might have a local form for **stipulations to Judge Pro Tem** (if a temporary judge will hear the case, you might sign a stipulation form on the day of trial). Another common local form is an **interpreter request** or **mailing instruction form**. These vary by court. Check your county court’s website “Small Claims Forms” section – for instance, San Diego provides a local form packet PKT-019 with instructions and any needed local forms for filing ([sdcourt.ca.gov](https://sdcourt.ca.gov/sdcourt/smallclaims2/smallclaimsforms#:~:text=Small%20Claims%20Forms%20,Small%20Claims)). But in general, the Judicial Council forms (SC-100, SC-104, etc.) are all you need to initiate and conclude a case. 

**Where to get forms:** All these forms are available for free on the California Courts website (courts.ca.gov > Forms > Small Claims). The self-help center also provides links and sometimes form fillable tools. Many court clerk offices will give you a small claims starter packet with SC-100 and related forms if you request it in person or by mail. Make sure you use the latest versions (Judicial Council forms get updated periodically – the form number stays the same but revision dates change). As of 2026, the versions should be current if obtained from the official site. 

**Filling them out:** The forms come with instructions (for example, form SC-100 has an information sheet SC-100-INFO with guidance). Fill them out neatly, preferably typed or online-filled then printed, because illegible forms can delay processing. Be very careful to list correct names for plaintiff(s) and defendant(s) (see Section 15 on naming). If you’re suing a business, include its exact legal name, and if suing an individual include any aliases (“aka”). Double-check addresses – the court will use these for notices and you’ll use the defendant’s address for service. 

Finally, after you fill out the forms, you will **file** them with the court and pay the fee (or submit the waiver). The clerk will stamp your forms, assign a case number, and write the trial date on the SC-100 (and SC-120 if any). You will get a **filed copy** back for each defendant to serve, and a copy for yourself. Make sure to keep your copy and bring it on the hearing day. 

## 8. Service of process  
After filing your claim, you must “serve” the defendant – meaning you must formally deliver a copy of the filed lawsuit paperwork to them – **within California’s time deadlines** and by approved methods. Proper service is crucial: without it, the court cannot proceed to hear your case. Here’s what you need to know about serving a small claims defendant in California: 

**Who can serve:** *You, as a party, **cannot** serve the papers yourself.* Service must be done by someone who is at least **18 years old and not a party to the case** ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims/start-case/serve#:~:text=Your%20server%20must%20be%3A)). This can be: 

- A friend, relative, or any other adult who is not involved in the lawsuit (e.g. a coworker). Many people ask a neutral friend to hand-deliver the papers.  
- A professional **process server** (you can hire one for a fee). They are experienced in finding and serving defendants.  
- The county **Sheriff’s Department** – most Sheriff or Marshal offices will serve small claims papers for a fee (around $40–$50) ([dcba.lacounty.gov](https://dcba.lacounty.gov/portfolio/small-claims-court-fees-3/#:~:text=Service%20of%20Plaintiff%E2%80%99s%20Claim%20,%242%2C000)). Not all counties’ sheriffs serve small claims, but many do – check with the clerk. In some counties, the sheriff only serves if the address is within that county.  
- In limited cases, **certified mail by the court clerk** – some courts offer service by certified mail (for a $15 fee) where the clerk sends the claim to the defendant’s address **certified mail, return receipt requested** ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims/start-case/serve#:~:text=,only%20offered%20in%20some%20courts)). This is considered service **only if the defendant personally signs the receipt**. **Warning:** Certified mail service has a low success rate – defendants often don’t sign or pick up the mail. Use it only as a last resort or for out-of-state defendants where personal service is impractical (and note: if the defendant is out of state, you normally cannot use small claims effectively unless they have sufficient contacts in California).  

**Allowed methods:** California law allows a few **methods of service** for small claims: 

1. **Personal service** – The server **physically hands** the court papers to the defendant (or to someone authorized to accept service on their behalf, like an officer for a corporation). This is the best form of service. The server should identify the defendant (name and describe the documents as the court papers). Personal service can be at the defendant’s home, work, or even in public – as long as the right person gets handed the papers. 

2. **Substituted service** – If the defendant cannot be found in person after reasonable attempts, the server can leave the papers with a **responsible adult at the defendant’s home or workplace** *and* also mail copies to the defendant’s address ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims/start-case/serve#:~:text=,substituted%20service)). Specific rules: the papers must be left with someone at least 18 years old *who resides at the defendant’s home* (e.g. a family member or roommate) or *who is in charge at the defendant’s workplace* (e.g. a receptionist or manager) ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims/start-case/serve#:~:text=,substituted%20service)). The server must inform that person that the documents are court papers for the defendant. Then the server must **mail** another copy to the defendant at the same address where the papers were left (regular first-class mail is fine) ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims/start-case/serve#:~:text=The%20server%20can%20hand%20the,side%20at%20that%20same%20address)). Substituted service is deemed complete **10 days after mailing**. This method is useful if the defendant is avoiding you but there’s someone at their residence or job who will accept papers. You should try personal service first; the law technically requires “reasonable diligence” (usually 2-3 attempts at personal service on different days/times) before using substitute service. 

3. **Service by certified mail (clerk)** – As noted, the court clerk can serve by certified mail *if* the court offers it. This method is complete **only if the defendant signs the green card (return receipt)**. If someone else signs or it’s unclaimed, service is not valid. This is why many small claims advisors caution against relying on certified mail – if the defendant ignores the mail, you’ll have to re-serve by another method and possibly postpone your hearing. 

4. **Posting and mailing or publication** – In very rare cases, if you absolutely cannot find the defendant to serve them, you can ask the court for permission to serve by **posting or publication**. Posting (sometimes used in landlord/tenant cases) would mean getting a court order to post the summons on the property and mail it. Publication means publishing the notice in a newspaper. These are generally last resorts and require a showing that you made exhaustive attempts to locate and serve the defendant. Small claims courts do have discretion to authorize such alternate service, but it’s uncommon. If you truly can’t find the defendant, discuss with the clerk or small claims advisor about options. The court may require you to file a declaration of due diligence (efforts made) before granting such an order. 

**Timing requirements:** California has strict timelines for service before the hearing: The **defendant must be served a certain minimum number of days before the court date**. Specifically, the *Proof of Service* (SC-104) itself states the deadlines: 

- If the defendant is being served **within the county** where the case is filed, service must be completed at least **15 days before the hearing** ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.340#:~:text=,which%20the%20action%20is%20filed)).  
- If the defendant is being served **outside the county** (but within California), service must be at least **20 days before the hearing** ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.340#:~:text=,which%20the%20action%20is%20filed)). 

These are minimums. Serving earlier is always better (to ensure the defendant gets notice and to avoid any challenge). The “count” of days does not include the day of service or the day of the hearing – it’s a clear 15 (or 20) full days in between. For example, if your trial is on May 30 in the same county, you must serve by end-of-day May 14 or earlier. If serving an out-of-county defendant for a May 30 hearing, serve by May 9 or earlier. 

**Filing the proof:** After service, the **Proof of Service (SC-104)** must be filed with the court **at least 5 days before the hearing** ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.340#:~:text=,five%20days%20before%20the%20hearing)). This lets the court know service was done. If you miss that, bring the proof to the hearing (the judge may allow it if the defendant appears and doesn’t object), but it’s risky – better to file it on time. If you fail to file proof and the defendant doesn’t show up, the judge cannot legally enter a default judgment because there’s no proof of service in the file. That could result in a continuance or dismissal. 

**Who to serve for businesses:** If you are suing a business entity, you need to serve the **correct agent**:  
- For a **corporation or LLC** – serve an officer of the company or the corporation’s **Registered Agent for Service of Process** (you can find the agent’s name and address on the California Secretary of State’s business search website). Serving the receptionist at the corporation’s main office can also work via substitute service if followed by mailing ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.340#:~:text=,one%20of%20the%20following%20methods)) ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.340#:~:text=by%20substituted%20service%20as%20provided,order%20to%20the%20defendant%E2%80%99s%20usual)). But the safest is to serve the designated agent.  
- For a **partnership** – serve a general partner. If a general partner is served, that binds the partnership. You can also serve the partnership’s business address via substitute service (leaving with person in charge + mailing).  
- For a **sole proprietorship** – serve the owner themselves. If the owner is absent, you can sub-serve an adult at their home or business as with any individual.  
- For a **public entity (city, county, state)** – you must serve the appropriate official (e.g. city clerk or county counsel’s office) *and* only after filing a government claim as described in Section 6. Check Government Code § 53051 for local agencies – typically serving the “clerk of the board” or “city clerk” is required.  

**Serving by certified mail (clerk):** If you choose this option (where available), note that **only a court clerk** may send the certified mail ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims/start-case/serve#:~:text=,only%20offered%20in%20some%20courts)). You cannot send it certified mail yourself and call it good – service by mail is only valid if done by a neutral adult after first getting permission of the court for substituted service *or* if acknowledging receipt. In small claims, the one exception is the clerk’s certified mail. You pay the fee, the clerk sends it, and if the green card comes back signed by the defendant, the clerk will file the proof (SC-113). If it comes back unsigned, you must re-serve by another method. 

**If the defendant avoids service:** The law doesn’t require the defendant to accept papers in hand or sign anything (except certified mail case). If your process server attempts service and the defendant runs away or refuses to open the door, generally the server can *leave* the papers in the defendant’s presence (e.g. drop at their feet or in the vicinity) – that often counts as valid personal service if the person clearly refused after being identified. Process servers are trained in these tactics (“drop service”). The server would then describe the scenario on the proof of service. If a defendant is truly evading, you might try stake-outs at different times, or use substitute service on a household member. You can also hire a professional who has database access to find alternate addresses or workplaces. 

**Serving out-of-state defendants:** You generally must personally serve an out-of-state defendant (substitute service out of state is a gray area – some judges accept it, some don’t, because CCP § 415.20 is silent on out-of-state sub-service). You cannot use the clerk’s certified mail for out-of-state (they only mail within CA). You might need to hire a process server in that state. Notably, if the defendant has no ties to California, the court might lack personal jurisdiction – so think carefully if suing someone out of state; they could ignore the case, and you’d have trouble enforcing the judgment across state lines unless you domesticate it. But if, say, they caused an accident in California then moved, you can sue here and serve them out of state (California allows out-of-state service by personal delivery or by certified mail with return receipt if signed by defendant, per CCP § 415.40). The 20-day rule still applies (actually 20 days if out-of-county, which covers out-of-state too). Also, if time is short, you can ask the court to continue the hearing to allow out-of-state service, since that often takes longer. 

**Proof of Service form:** On form SC-104, the server will fill in how service was done (personal or substitute), the date, time, address, name of person served (or description if unknown). If substituted, they’ll also fill when they mailed the copies. The server signs it under penalty of perjury. If the server is a registered process server or sheriff, they often will file it for you (especially the Sheriff does this as part of their fee). If a friend serves, you are responsible for making sure the proof gets filed. Always **bring a copy of the proof of service to the hearing**, just in case the court’s file is missing it or if there’s any question. 

**Service fees and recovery:** Keep your receipt for any service costs (process server invoice or Sheriff fee). If you win the case, the judge will typically add the cost of service to your judgment award ([dcba.lacounty.gov](https://dcba.lacounty.gov/portfolio/small-claims-court-fees-3/#:~:text=You%20must%20pay%20the%20court%2C,a%20service%20provided%20by%20the)), so the defendant reimburses you. However, this is only true for one reasonable method – if you, say, tried certified mail and it failed, then paid a process server, you can usually recover the successful service cost, not necessarily every failed attempt. But judges often award all reasonable costs. If you had a fee waiver, the court covers the sheriff fee, but if you win, the waived fees can be added to judgment for reimbursement to the state. 

**What if you cannot serve in time:** If the hearing is approaching and despite diligent efforts you haven’t served the defendant, you have a couple options. You can **ask the court to postpone the hearing** (use form SC-150 or write a letter to the court) explaining you need more time to effect service ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims/start-case/serve#:~:text=What%20if%20I%20can%27t%20serve,by%20the%20deadline)). Courts usually grant at least one continuance for difficulty in service – it’s a common issue. You must do this *before* the hearing date (at least a few days prior, ideally). If you simply show up without service, the judge will not hear the case; they might continue it if you appear and ask, or they might dismiss “without prejudice” so you can re-file. It’s better to get ahead of it and request a new date. Each local court has slightly different procedures: some want a written request 5 days in advance ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims/start-case/serve#:~:text=What%20if%20I%20can%27t%20serve,by%20the%20deadline)), others allow a oral request on the hearing date (but that wastes a trip). Always try to serve as early as possible to avoid this. 

**If defendant actively avoids or moves:** As mentioned, consider alternate addresses (work, etc.). You can also use **subpoena** techniques pre-judgment in some cases – though in small claims, formal discovery isn’t allowed, you might pay for a DMV search if it’s a vehicle-related case to get an updated address, or use internet people-finders. If the defendant has moved *and you know the new address*, you can serve there (no matter that the court is in another county, as long as venue was proper when filed). If they moved out of state, refer to out-of-state service above. 

In summary, **service of process** in small claims must follow the same fundamental rules as in other civil cases, but with a simpler approach (no summons needed – the SC-100 itself acts as the summons). Ensure someone 18+ and not you serves the defendant in one of the allowed ways, with enough lead time (15 or 20 days) ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.340#:~:text=,which%20the%20action%20is%20filed)). File the proof ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.340#:~:text=,five%20days%20before%20the%20hearing)). Without proper service, you won’t get a judgment. California self-help centers and the SC-104 information sheet (SC-104B and SC-104C) provide detailed instructions – it’s worth reading those if you have any doubt. 

## 9. Defendant’s response and default  
Unlike in formal civil cases, a small claims **defendant does not file a written answer** to the claim. The defendant’s “response” is simply to **appear at the hearing** and present their side. When the defendant is served with the Plaintiff’s Claim (SC-100), it includes the hearing date – the onus is on them to show up in court. Here’s what can happen: 

- **If the defendant timely appears on the hearing date:** The case will be heard by the judge or commissioner, and both sides can explain their story, show evidence, and call witnesses (see Section 10 for hearing procedure). After both sides are heard, the judge will decide the case (often the decision is mailed later as a judgment notice). 

- **If the defendant **fails to appear** at the hearing:** This is when a “default” comes into play. If you (the plaintiff) have properly served the defendant and **filed the proof of service** ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.340#:~:text=,five%20days%20before%20the%20hearing)), and the defendant then does not show up in court, the judge may enter a **default judgment** in your favor. However, it’s not automatic – you still have to prove your case briefly to the judge’s satisfaction. The judge will typically have you testify under oath as to why the defendant owes you money and confirm that they were served and didn’t respond. You should still bring your evidence to substantiate the claim (especially for damages). If everything is in order, the court can award you a default judgment up to the amount you sued for (plus costs). This judgment is just as enforceable as any other – you can proceed with collection (Section 12). 

    - If you did **not file the proof of service** or service was not done correctly, and the defendant doesn’t show, the judge will **not** issue a judgment. Instead, the case might be continued to give you another chance to serve, or dismissed without prejudice. This underscores the importance of proper service. 

    - If the defendant wasn’t served at least 15 (or 20) days before the hearing, they can appear solely to ask for a postponement due to improper notice. Judges usually grant that – which is effectively not a default but a rescheduling. 

- **If the plaintiff (you) fails to appear:** (Worth noting since “default” can cut both ways.) If you, as the plaintiff, don’t show up, and the defendant does appear, the court will typically dismiss your claim. This dismissal could be “without prejudice” (meaning you could re-file) or, if you’ve previously not appeared or the judge feels it appropriate, it can even be “with prejudice” (ending the case permanently). Often it’s without prejudice for a first non-appearance, so you can refile (after paying a new fee). If neither party shows up, the case is usually dismissed without prejudice. 

**No formal answer required:** As noted, the defendant doesn’t need to file anything in advance to dispute the claim – their appearance is their opportunity. The small claims process assumes defendants will either show up to defend or not. **However,** a defendant **may** file a **Defendant’s Claim (SC-120)** if they have a counterclaim against you (as covered in Section 2). This isn’t really an “answer” – it’s a separate claim which might be heard at the same time. If a defendant files an SC-120, they must serve you (the plaintiff) with it before the hearing (at least 5 days before trial if you reside in the same county, or 15 days if you reside outside the county ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.340#:~:text=,which%20the%20action%20is%20filed)) ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.340#:~:text=,five%20days%20before%20the%20hearing))). If they do so, be prepared to address their claim at the hearing. 

**Default judgment process:** If the defendant doesn’t come to court, judges will still require some evidence or testimony from you to ensure the claim is valid and the amount is proven. This is called a “prove-up.” Small claims judges are often somewhat lenient in prove-up (no adversary to object), but they might ask you questions and require you to swear that the allegations are true. For example, if you’re claiming $5,000 for a loan that wasn’t repaid, the judge may ask for any proof of the loan (promissory note, texts, etc.) and that no payments were made. If you’re well-prepared, this usually is straightforward and a judgment for the full amount (plus costs) will be entered. If you lack proof or the claim seems questionable, a judge could enter judgment for less than you asked or possibly dismiss if truly unproven – but that’s rare when the defendant didn’t appear. 

After a default judgment, the court clerk will mail a **Notice of Entry of Judgment (SC-130)** to both parties (to the defendant’s last known address). This usually happens the same day or within a few days of the hearing. That notice triggers important timelines for the defendant (see next point). 

**Motion to vacate (set aside) default:** A defendant who misses the hearing **can** ask the court to set aside a default judgment, but only on certain grounds and within strict time limits. Under CCP § 116.730, a defendant who didn’t appear can file a **Motion to Vacate** within **30 days after the clerk mailed the notice of judgment** ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/ca/code-of-civil-procedure/ccp-sect-116-730/#:~:text=,the%20judgment%20to%20the%20parties)). In the motion, the defendant should explain a good reason for not appearing (e.g. they never received the papers, or had an emergency or confusion about the date). The court will schedule a hearing on the motion (usually in front of the same judge). If the defendant had a valid excuse (legally called “good cause,” such as illness, lack of notice, etc.), the judge may grant the motion and re-open the case ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/ca/code-of-civil-procedure/ccp-sect-116-730/#:~:text=,motion%20in%20the%20plaintiff%27s%20absence)). The case would then be re-set for a new hearing on the merits (sometimes even immediately that day if both parties are present and ready ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/ca/code-of-civil-procedure/ccp-sect-116-730/#:~:text=,330))). If the plaintiff doesn’t show up at the motion hearing, the court often grants the motion by default and schedules a new trial date ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/ca/code-of-civil-procedure/ccp-sect-116-730/#:~:text=,motion%20in%20the%20plaintiff%27s%20absence)).

If the 30-day deadline is missed (for example, the defendant never got the mailed notice and finds out after two months), there’s a outside limit: a defendant who didn’t get actual notice of the lawsuit in time to attend can move to vacate **within 180 days of entry of judgment** on the grounds of lack of notice (this is per CCP § 116.740, often invoked when service was improper) ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/ca/code-of-civil-procedure/ccp-sect-116-730/#:~:text=01%2C%202025%20,claims%20court%20may%20file%20a)). In practice, if a defendant truly wasn’t served, they may file a set-aside motion up to 6 months after judgment. But if they were served properly and just forgot the court date or ignored it, they are stuck with the 30-day limit. 

**Appeal vs. vacating:** In small claims, only the *defendant* (or a plaintiff on a defendant’s counterclaim) can appeal the judgment (see Section 11). **However, you cannot appeal a default judgment** because you didn’t participate in a trial – instead, your remedy is the motion to vacate. If a defendant timely files a motion to vacate and the court **denies** it, *then* the defendant can appeal that denial to the appellate division of the superior court ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/ca/code-of-civil-procedure/ccp-sect-116-730/#:~:text=01%2C%202025%20,claims%20court%20may%20file%20a)). That’s a complicated scenario: essentially, the defendant gets one chance to convince the small claims judge to reopen, and if refused, then they have 10 days to file a notice of appeal of the denial (not of the judgment itself). On such an appeal, the appellate division would decide if the denial was an abuse of discretion. If they find in the defendant’s favor, they send it back for a new small claims trial. If they uphold the denial, the default stands and is final. 

For most laypeople: if you’re a defendant who missed court, act quickly – file the motion to vacate **within 30 days of notice** ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/ca/code-of-civil-procedure/ccp-sect-116-730/#:~:text=,the%20judgment%20to%20the%20parties)). There’s a one-page form (SC-135) for this motion available in some courts (or you can write your own). Explain why you missed the hearing and that you have a defense to the claim. The court will notify you of the new hearing on the motion (often, the plaintiff is also notified to attend). If you had a good reason (didn’t get served, had a medical emergency, etc.), judges often give you that second chance. But simply saying “I forgot” or “I was too busy” may not satisfy the judge. Still, small claims judges sometimes lean toward giving a day in court if the request is timely. 

**If default is set aside:** then the case is back on – a new hearing will be set (or heard immediately if parties are there and ready and both agree to proceed ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/ca/code-of-civil-procedure/ccp-sect-116-730/#:~:text=,330))). The previous judgment is nullified. If you’re the plaintiff, you’ll need to present your case anew (and possibly re-serve if there’s any doubt about service). If the defendant again fails to show at the new hearing, the court will enter judgment again, likely final this time (the defendant will not get an indefinite number of bites at the apple). 

**Enforcement during the 30-day window:** If you have a default judgment, note that the court clerk will typically not issue a writ of execution or allow enforcement until **30 days after judgment** (this mirrors the appeal/vacate period). Actually, in small claims, a defaulted defendant can file a motion to vacate within 30 days, and if they do, enforcement may be stayed. In practice, many creditors wait until the appeal period (30 days) expires before actively garnishing or levying, to avoid complications. Also, by law, the judgment debtor (even by default) has 30 days to voluntarily pay before you can add certain fees or start collection – interest accrues immediately though. So while you can prepare enforcement paperwork, it’s often strategic to see if the defendant will file a motion or appeal by Day 30. If not, on Day 31 you can proceed full steam (see Section 12). If they do file a motion, hold off on enforcement until that’s resolved (the court usually stays enforcement when a motion to vacate is pending, especially if they set a hearing). And if they appeal (in cases where appeal is allowed), an appeal in small claims **stays the judgment entirely** – the judgment cannot be enforced during the appeal process ([abcdocz.com](https://abcdocz.com/doc/123939/small-claims-court-the#:~:text=%2475,judgment%20debtor%29%2036)) ([abcdocz.com](https://abcdocz.com/doc/123939/small-claims-court-the#:~:text=%2475,has%20the%20right%20to%20invite)). 

**Plaintiff’s default rights:** As the plaintiff, you have no right to appeal an unfavorable judgment on your claim (including a dismissal for your non-appearance), but you can file a motion to vacate if *you* didn’t attend the hearing for a good reason – similar timeline (30 days). For example, if you missed court due to a car accident or not receiving notice of a rescheduled date, you can motion to vacate the judgment of dismissal within 30 days of mailing of that notice. The court may grant a new hearing. If they deny, a plaintiff actually cannot appeal that denial (since plaintiffs have no appeal rights in small claims). That’s why it’s critical for plaintiffs to attend or timely request postponements. 

In short, the defendant’s “response” in small claims is to show up and argue their case. If they don’t, you can win by default – but be sure all procedural steps (service, proof, timing) are correct. If you win by default, the defendant has limited avenues to undo it, and if they miss those, the judgment is final and you can proceed to collection. 

## 10. Hearing procedure  
Small claims hearings are designed to be relatively quick and informal, but it’s still a court proceeding and you should come prepared. Here’s what to expect at the hearing and how to present your case: 

**No attorneys (generally):** In California small claims, **attorneys are *not* allowed to represent parties** during the hearing ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/california/code-ccp/part-1/title-1/chapter-5-5/article-5/section-116-540/#:~:text=116,of%20a%20small%20claims%20action)). You must represent yourself (unless you fall under a special category like a representative of a business or you’re a lawyer representing your own claim). The prohibition on lawyers means that the judge will hear directly from the actual people involved. There are a few exceptions: a business entity can send a regular employee or officer (but not an attorney hired solely to appear) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/california/code-ccp/part-1/title-1/chapter-5-5/article-5/section-116-540/#:~:text=,corporation%20in%20small%20claims%20court)), and an individual who is unable to appear (e.g., deployed military, or has a language barrier in some debt cases) might get court permission for someone else to assist. However, **as a rule, do not bring a lawyer** – they’ll be turned away. (Attorneys can sit in the audience, but cannot address the court. The only time you’ll see attorneys in small claims is if a party is suing their attorney for something like fees – then the attorney-defendant can defend themselves, of course, since they are the party.) If you are an attorney yourself and you are a plaintiff or defendant, you are allowed to appear on your own behalf (you just can’t represent someone else). 

**Who presides:** Your case may be heard by a **judge**, a **commissioner**, or a temporary judge (judge pro tem). Commissioners and pro tem judges are often experienced attorneys who handle small claims calendars. You have the right to refuse a temporary judge if you prefer a real judge – in practice, most people accept the pro tem to avoid delay, since often the pro tem is the only one hearing small claims that day. The atmosphere is less formal than higher courts – the judge may be sitting at a table or bench and often calls cases from a calendar list. There is no jury in small claims; all trials are **bench trials**. The courtroom might have 10–20 cases on calendar that session, set for the same time. 

**Before the hearing:** Arrive early. Check in with the courtroom clerk or bailiff when you get there. Many courts have **mediation programs** – you might be asked if you want to mediate. If both sides agree, a mediator might sit with you in the hallway to attempt a settlement. If you settle, you can often put the agreement in writing and have it become the judgment (or dismiss the case). If not, you go before the judge as planned. Mediation is free and often available right then; it can be a good way to reach a compromise on your own terms. It’s voluntary, though strongly encouraged. 

**When your case is called:** The judge or clerk will call your case name (often something like “Smith vs. Jones”). Both parties come forward to the tables or podium. The judge will ask each of you to swear or affirm to tell the truth (this may be done for each individual case, or once for all cases at the start – practices vary). Because it’s informal, you might not see a court reporter – small claims proceedings are **usually not recorded** (no transcript). Some courts have electronic recording, but many do not. This means if you later appeal (only defendants can, in small claims), the new trial is de novo anyway, but just note that what you say isn’t being transcribed verbatim. 

**Presenting your case:** As the plaintiff, you have the burden of proof (generally “preponderance of evidence” – more likely than not). You will speak first. The judge will typically say, “Tell me why you’re here” or ask you to state your case. You should concisely explain **what happened, why the defendant owes you, and the amount of damage**. Be sure to hit the key points: the judge may steer you with questions. It’s important to be **organized**: have a brief timeline or notes, and present any **evidence** in a logical manner. Small claims rules of evidence are relaxed – but basic relevance and reliability still matter. You can **show documents or photos** to the judge. When you want to present a piece of evidence, say “Your Honor, I have [describe item, e.g. 3 photos of the damage, a contract, text messages, receipts]. May I present these?” The clerk or bailiff will usually take them to the judge. If you have multiple exhibits, bring **copies** for the judge and the other side (and yourself). Some courts prefer you pre-mark them (Plaintiff’s Exhibit 1, 2, etc.), but in informal small claims, you can also just hand them over and refer to them by description. 

    - **Visual evidence:** Photos are extremely helpful in many cases (damage, location of incident, etc.). Bring printed copies (not just on your phone – though some judges will look at a phone, it’s better to have printouts). If it’s a video or audio recording, bring a device to play it (with sound if needed) – not all courtrooms have DVD players nowadays, so a smartphone or tablet with the clip cued up is useful, but check court’s policy on electronics. Keep it short; if you have a 5-minute video, perhaps cue a relevant 30-second segment. 

    - **Documents:** Contracts, estimates, bills, canceled checks, emails, texts – these can all be evidence. Print texts or emails out. It’s a good idea to highlight the crucial parts (but don’t overly mark up the originals, use highlighting on copies if needed). The judge may only skim them, so be prepared to summarize what a document is and what it proves. For example: “This is the repair estimate for my car – $2,300 – after the defendant backed into me. And here is the photo of the damage.” Or “Here is the contract we signed on June 1; as you can see in paragraph 5, the defendant agreed to pay me \$500 per week, and here are bank statements showing no payments received for the last 4 weeks.” 

- **Witnesses:** You have the right to bring **witnesses** to support your story. In small claims, witnesses can be very informal – a friend who saw the incident, an expert who gave you an estimate, etc. Because you can’t subpoena someone to *testify* in small claims (except through the judge’s discretion with a small claims subpoena if absolutely needed), most witnesses come voluntarily. If a crucial witness is reluctant, you can request the court issue a **Small Claims Subpoena** (Form SC-107) to compel them to attend ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-forms#:~:text=Small%20Claims%20Subpoena%20for%20Personal,Before%20filling%20out%2C%20bring%20the)), but you must serve that subpoena in advance and pay witness fees (currently $35 per day plus mileage) ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-forms#:~:text=Small%20Claims%20Subpoena%20for%20Personal,Before%20filling%20out%2C%20bring%20the)). Many people don’t go that far in small claims; instead, they bring a signed **declaration or letter** from a witness. Technically, that’s hearsay and the court *could* ignore it, but often judges will consider written statements if they seem credible and the other side doesn’t object strongly. However, a live witness is better – the judge can ask them questions. If you have a witness, when it’s your turn you can say, “I have a witness here who saw the incident – may he testify?” The judge will usually allow it briefly. The witness will be sworn in too and can tell what they observed. Keep witnesses on point – no long-winded tangents. If time is short, judges might also skip live testimony and instead accept a written statement. 

- **The defendant’s turn:** After you (and your witnesses) speak and show your evidence, the judge will turn to the defendant to hear their side. The defendant can question the evidence you presented, explain why they think they don’t owe what you claim, and present their own evidence or witnesses. As the defendant speaks, the judge may ask you some follow-up questions or allow you a brief rebuttal. Keep it civil – do not interrupt the other party (except if the judge invites you to respond). If the defendant presents new evidence that you haven’t seen, you have a right to look at it and comment. The judge might ask you, “What do you say about this?” at the end.  

- **Conduct:** Address the judge as “Your Honor.” Remain polite and focused. Small claims hearings are informal, but this is still a courtroom – do not argue directly with the other side; always direct your remarks to the judge. If the other party says something false, note it down and respond when it’s your turn: “Your Honor, I need to clarify a point – the defendant said I received a refund, but I have my bank statement here showing no refund was received.” The judge will often let each side speak in turn, and then there might be a bit of back-and-forth moderated by the judge for clarification. Keep your voice calm. Emotion is understandable in some disputes, but try not to let it derail your presentation. Judges appreciate when you stick to facts and proof. 

- **Burden of proof:** As plaintiff, you must prove your claim by a **preponderance of the evidence** (more likely than not). In numeric terms, you need to convince the judge at least 51% in your favor. If it’s a tie or too close to call, the plaintiff loses. So make sure to bring sufficient evidence. If it’s just “he said, she said,” the one with the burden (plaintiff) can lose if the stories are equally plausible. Corroborating evidence (documents, photos, independent witness) often tips the balance. For example, in a security deposit dispute: if it’s just tenant’s word vs landlord’s word on the apartment condition, that’s tough – but if the tenant has move-out photos showing it was clean, that strongly supports their claim. Always ask yourself, “How do I prove this point?” for each element of your case. 

- **Judge’s questions:** The small claims judge might interrupt either of you to ask questions. This is normal – don’t be thrown off. The judge might say, “I’ve read your papers” (if a statement was filed) or “I see the evidence, but I have a question about X.” Answer directly and honestly. The judge may also ask the defendant a question and then look to you for your response to the answer. The process is more conversational than a formal trial. 

- **Time limits:** Because there are often many cases on calendar, each case might only get a very short time (sometimes 10–15 minutes total). Some courts explicitly set time limits (e.g. each side 5 minutes to speak). Be prepared to **summarize your case succinctly**. Lead with your strongest points. If you have lots of evidence, pick the most compelling pieces – you might not have time to cover every minor detail. Organize your paperwork in a binder or folder so you can quickly pull out what you need. If the judge needs more detail, they’ll ask. It’s wise to practice a 2–3 minute narrative of your case beforehand so you can hit the key facts quickly. 

**Witness fees and subpoenas:** If you did subpoena someone (for instance an expert mechanic to testify on car damage value), you need to have given them the statutory **witness fee** ($35 plus mileage) in advance, or at least have it ready at court. If not, they could refuse to testify. Usually in small claims, formal subpoenas are rare. But another scenario: if you need documents from a third party (like phone records or a contract held by someone), you can use a **Small Claims Subpoena Duces Tecum** (SC-107 with the appropriate attachment) to demand those papers. You’d serve it and they can mail the records or bring them. The SC-107 form covers both commanding appearance and production of documents ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-forms#:~:text=Small%20Claims%20Subpoena%20for%20Personal,Before%20filling%20out%2C%20bring%20the)). If you served any subpoenas for documents, confirm if those docs were received by the court or bring proof that you served it. A common use might be subpoenaing a repair shop for records or an employer for proof of something. But again, weigh necessity – most small claims cases rely on evidence the parties themselves bring. Remember you can also **ask the judge to order the defendant** to show evidence. For example, if you believe the defendant has crucial evidence at home (like the faulty part they replaced, or records of payments), you could say during the hearing, “Your Honor, the defendant likely has XYZ – I request they be ordered to bring it.” Under CCP § 116.510, the judge can indeed grant a **continuance** and order a party to produce additional evidence or witnesses at the next hearing if it’s important. That is discretionary. 

**Language interpreters:** If you or your witness isn’t comfortable in English, you should request an **interpreter** *in advance* from the court (most courts have a form or online request). California courts now provide free language interpreters for small claims litigants who need them. On the day, the interpreter (for Spanish, Vietnamese, etc.) will translate proceedings for you. If an official interpreter isn’t available, you’re allowed to bring an adult friend or relative to interpret (but they must only interpret, not act as your advocate). They’ll be sworn to accurately translate. Under no circumstances should the interpreter also be a witness in the case – that’s a conflict. If you have any language issue, do not wait – request an interpreter well before the court date. 

**No jury, no formal rules of evidence:** There’s no jury – the judge decides both facts and law. The **rules of evidence** are relaxed: hearsay, for example, might be allowed if it seems reliable. Judges often allow written statements or second-hand info if it’s the best the party could do (though if the other side objects, the judge will weigh that objection in terms of reliability, not technical admissibility). Nonetheless, **first-hand evidence** is always more persuasive. The judge may ignore evidence they find untrustworthy or irrelevant. There’s no formal discovery (you can’t demand documents from the other side before trial as you would in regular civil litigation). That means sometimes you see the opponent’s evidence for the first time at the hearing. If that prejudices you, you can ask for a continuance to review it – but judges rarely continue cases just because of surprise evidence, unless it’s truly extensive and you couldn’t anticipate it. 

**Courtroom etiquette:** Dress neatly (no need for a suit, but avoid shorts, tank tops, hats). Address the judge respectfully. Don’t interrupt the judge or the other party. Keep your cool even if the other side lies or provokes you – the judge is observing demeanor. Losing your temper can undermine your credibility. On the flip side, don’t be overly rigid or legalistic – speak plainly. This court is meant for ordinary people, not lawyers, so legal jargon isn’t necessary (though if certain laws support you, you can mention them briefly). 

**After both sides present:** In many cases, the judge will not announce a decision immediately. They often take the case “under submission” and you’ll get the judgment by mail a week or two later. Sometimes, however, especially if the case is straightforward, the judge might tell you the decision on the spot. For instance, “I find in favor of the plaintiff for $2,100 plus costs.” Or “Judgment for defendant – plaintiff did not meet burden of proof.” Don’t argue after a judgment is announced – there is no further debate. If it’s under submission, just thank the judge and leave. You’ll have to wait for the mailed Notice of Entry of Judgment (Form SC-130) to see the result. It usually arrives within a few weeks. If more than 30 days pass, you can contact the clerk. 

**If one party doesn’t show:** We covered default in Section 9, but during the hearing: if the defendant isn’t there, you still present your case (briefly) to the judge. The judge will ask you to swear that everything is true and may verify service details, then likely award default judgment if all is in order. If the plaintiff isn’t there but the defendant is, the judge might dismiss the case (often without prejudice). If neither shows, dismissal. Some courts call the calendar twice (once at start, once after other cases) to give late parties a chance. 

**Subsequent procedures:** If the judgment is in your favor, the defendant is supposed to pay promptly (or as the judgment dictates). The court’s judgment order will often say something like “Judgment for plaintiff in the amount of $X principal, $Y costs.” Payment arrangements can be made between parties, or the defendant can file a **Request to Pay Judgment in Installments (Form SC-220)** if they need a payment plan and you don’t agree (the court can approve a payment schedule). If you’re the losing party and you’re the defendant, you have the right to appeal within 30 days (see Section 11). If you’re the losing plaintiff, you cannot appeal, except possibly to challenge a denial of a motion to vacate if you didn’t appear. 

**Documenting the hearing:** Remember, since often there’s no transcript, you might want to take notes of what the judge orders if it’s announced orally. However, the formal record is the mailed Notice of Entry of Judgment. 

In summary, the hearing is informal but you should prepare like you would for any trial: know your key points, have your evidence ready and organized, and be prepared to tell your story clearly and truthfully. The judge will guide the process, but you are responsible for proving your claim or defense. 

## 11. Appeals  
California’s small claims appeal process is unique. **Only the defendant** (or a plaintiff who was a defendant on a counterclaim) can appeal a small claims judgment – the original plaintiff has no right to appeal on their own claim ([findhoalaw.com](https://findhoalaw.com/code-of-civil-procedure-section-116-710-small-claims-appeal/#:~:text=FindHOALaw%20findhoalaw,not%20appear%20at%20the%20hearing)). Here are the key points about appeals: 

**Who can appeal:** If you (the defendant in the small claims case) lose (either by default or after a trial), you have the right to appeal the judgment, *except* in certain scenarios like if you already used a motion to vacate (more on that below). The **plaintiff generally cannot appeal** ([findhoalaw.com](https://findhoalaw.com/code-of-civil-procedure-section-116-710-small-claims-appeal/#:~:text=FindHOALaw%20findhoalaw,not%20appear%20at%20the%20hearing)). This one-sided appeal right is by design: the idea is that as a plaintiff, you chose small claims and its limits, so you’re bound by the outcome. The defendant, however, is given one opportunity to have the case heard “again” in a higher court (with more formal proceedings). There is one wrinkle: if there was a **Defendant’s Claim (counterclaim)** and the defendant (as counter-plaintiff) loses on that, then *that* defendant (now in role of plaintiff on the counterclaim) cannot appeal their loss on the counterclaim; however the original plaintiff, who was the *defendant* on that counterclaim, **can appeal the counterclaim judgment**. In short, whichever side was in the **defendant posture in the small claims case may appeal** an adverse judgment on that claim ([findhoalaw.com](https://findhoalaw.com/code-of-civil-procedure-section-116-710-small-claims-appeal/#:~:text=FindHOALaw%20findhoalaw,not%20appear%20at%20the%20hearing)). This means if both parties had claims against each other and both lost, both could appeal those respective parts. 

**Deadline to appeal:** An appeal must be filed **within 30 days** after the court mails or delivers the Notice of Entry of Judgment (SC-130) ([abcdocz.com](https://abcdocz.com/doc/123939/small-claims-court-the#:~:text=Judgment%20%28Form%20SC,the%20appeal%20is%20dismissed%20or)). This is a hard deadline – 30 days exactly, not court days. If you miss it, the judgment is final. There is no extension. The 30-day clock typically starts from the mailing date of the notice (which is printed on the form). For example, if judgment is mailed on July 1, the appeal deadline is July 31. If judgment is given to you in court (some judges hand out the judgment immediately), it’s 30 days from that date of hand delivery ([abcdocz.com](https://abcdocz.com/doc/123939/small-claims-court-the#:~:text=Judgment%20%28Form%20SC,the%20appeal%20is%20dismissed%20or)). The clerk’s mailing is more common, so usually you go by the mail date. 

To appeal, you file a **Notice of Appeal (Form SC-140)** with the small claims court ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-forms#:~:text=,Appeal%20the%20judge%27s%20decision)). This form is very short – you just fill in identifying information and sign. There is an appeal filing fee of **$75** in most cases ([abcdocz.com](https://abcdocz.com/doc/123939/small-claims-court-the#:~:text=%2475,judgment%20debtor%29%2036)) ([abcdocz.com](https://abcdocz.com/doc/123939/small-claims-court-the#:~:text=appears%20on%20the%20form,loses%20the%20claim%20on%20appeal)) (the fee might be slightly different if multiple defendants appeal, etc., but $75 is standard as of 2026). If you have a fee waiver, it can cover the appeal fee as well. The clerk will then set a new hearing date in the **Superior Court (limited civil department)** for the appeal (usually within 60–90 days). The small claims court will forward the case file to the higher court. 

**Nature of the appeal (“Trial de Novo”):** A small claims appeal is a brand-new trial (**“de novo”** – literally “from the beginning”) ([abcdocz.com](https://abcdocz.com/doc/123939/small-claims-court-the#:~:text=%2475,judgment%20debtor%29%2036)). This means the case is heard all over again in the superior court, as if the small claims trial hadn’t happened. **New evidence** can be presented, new witnesses can be called – you are not bound by what happened in the first trial. In fact, the original small claims judgment is not given any weight (the higher court judge or commissioner won’t even read the small claims trial’s outcome except to know what was decided; they certainly won’t defer to it). It’s a fresh second chance. The one big difference: on appeal, **both sides are allowed to have attorneys** if they wish. The appealing party (usually defendant) might hire a lawyer for the appeal, and if so, the respondent (original plaintiff) can also hire one to defend. Many times, neither side hires counsel, but the right is there. The appeal is heard in the superior court’s civil division (often by a different judge, not a temporary judge). Procedurally, it’s actually considered a **new limited civil case**, but with some relaxed rules – specifically, **no new discovery** is permitted unless ordered by the court (CCP § 116.770(b)). The appeal trial is somewhat informal but often a bit more strict than small claims – some judges follow rules of evidence more closely, especially if lawyers are present. However, there is still no jury; the appeal is also a bench trial. 

**What can be appealed:** The entire small claims judgment on that claim is at issue. For example, if the small claims court ordered the defendant to pay $5,000, the defendant appeals – the superior court on appeal can uphold that, reduce it, raise it (up to the small claims limit), or reverse it entirely. They essentially re-decide the case. Note: the limit on recovery remains what it was in small claims (the plaintiff cannot suddenly ask for more money than originally claimed). You can’t increase your claim on appeal, because it’s the same case being retried. If the defendant had a counterclaim that they lost and they appeal, that counterclaim is tried de novo too. Often the superior court will hear both the original claim and counterclaim together if both were part of the notice of appeal. 

Importantly, **default judgments can’t be appealed** by the defaulting defendant – if you didn’t show up, your remedy was a motion to vacate, not an appeal. Only a defendant who appeared (or had a trial) can directly appeal the judgment. (If a motion to vacate was denied, *that denial* can be appealed within 10 days, but that’s a separate matter.) 

**Stay of enforcement:** Once a Notice of Appeal is filed, the small claims judgment is automatically **stayed (paused)** ([abcdocz.com](https://abcdocz.com/doc/123939/small-claims-court-the#:~:text=%2475,judgment%20debtor%29%2036)). The judgment cannot be enforced during the appeal process. The defendant does **not** have to pay the judgment to file an appeal; nor do they have to post a bond for the amount (unlike other civil cases). The filing of the appeal itself halts enforcement ([abcdocz.com](https://abcdocz.com/doc/123939/small-claims-court-the#:~:text=appears%20on%20the%20form,loses%20the%20claim%20on%20appeal)). For example, if a wage garnishment or lien was started, it must stop pending the appeal outcome. The idea is that the appellate trial will decide the rights anew. If the defendant (appellant) ultimately loses again, then the judgment becomes enforceable (including interest from the original judgment date, usually). If the defendant wins on appeal (meaning the judgment is reversed or reduced), that new judgment takes effect. 

**Appeal hearing scheduling:** The appeal trial is usually heard in the **“Limited Civil”** department of the superior court (since small claims is technically under that $25k limited jurisdiction). Some counties have specific times or courtrooms for small claims appeals. It might be a more formal courtroom setting. The clerk will notify both parties of the appeal trial date. It’s often 1 to 3 months after you file the appeal. If you need to postpone an appeal hearing, you may request it, but do so with a good reason. 

**Preparation for appeal:** Both sides should prepare as if going to a regular trial. If attorneys are involved, expect more structured presentation. Even if no attorneys, since this is in front of a superior court judge, you should be ready to present witnesses, evidence, and follow more standard court procedures (stand when speaking, etc., if that court expects it). However, many judges still keep it somewhat informal if parties are self-represented. They will again hear testimony and review evidence. You can introduce evidence that you *didn’t* show in the first trial – maybe you gathered more documentation or found a key witness. That is allowed. One limitation: you cannot appeal a part of the judgment in order to increase an award. For instance, if you’re the plaintiff and you won but you think you should have gotten more money, you cannot appeal to get more because you, as plaintiff, have no appeal right. Similarly, a defendant who partially prevailed cannot cross-appeal to reduce something further if they weren’t tagged with liability as a defendant (complex scenarios aside). 

**Outcome of appeal:** The superior court on appeal will issue a **new judgment**. This supersedes the small claims judgment. It could be the same result, or different. This **judgment on appeal is final** – there is no further appeal from it. (Technically, if some legal error occurred, one could try to petition for an extraordinary writ, but that is extremely rare and not a normal “appeal”). The decision on appeal is binding like any civil judgment. If the appellate judgment is in favor of the original plaintiff, they can enforce it (and it will include costs from both the small claims and appeal, possibly). If the appellate judgment is for the defendant (meaning the defendant wins the appeal and owes nothing or less), the original plaintiff cannot appeal that. 

**Costs and fees on appeal:** The winner on appeal (which could be either party) is typically entitled to recover certain costs – e.g., the $75 appeal filing fee, costs of subpoenaing witnesses for the appeal, etc. If the appellant (defendant) loses again, sometimes the court may even award the respondent (plaintiff) up to $150 in attorney’s fees – *but only if* the respondent had a lawyer on the appeal and the court finds the appeal was without substantial merit (CCP § 116.790). Generally, attorney’s fees are not awardable in small claims, *except* that specific scenario as a penalty for a bad-faith appeal. This is to discourage defendants from appealing just to drag it out. In practice, such awards are rare – most small claims appeals do not involve any attorney or, if they do, the court usually won’t say the appeal was meritless unless it clearly was a delay tactic. 

If you are the **respondent** (the original plaintiff who won in small claims and now must defend the appeal), you cannot recover “attorney fees” for hiring a lawyer to handle the appeal, except possibly that $150 cap if the court finds the appeal frivolous (and only if you win again). You can recover ordinary costs like service of subpoenas, witness fees, etc., if you win. If you lose the appeal (i.e., the judgment is reversed or reduced), you might have to pay the appellant’s costs. 

**Appeal of motion to vacate denial:** Just for completeness – if a defendant lost by default and filed a motion to vacate within 30 days and the small claims judge denied it, *that* denial can be appealed to the appellate division (not as a trial de novo, but as a normal appeal on whether the denial was proper) ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/ca/code-of-civil-procedure/ccp-sect-116-730/#:~:text=01%2C%202025%20,claims%20court%20may%20file%20a)). The deadline for that is very short: 10 days from the notice of denial. If successful, the appellate division would likely send it back with instruction to grant a new trial. This is an uncommon scenario and typically requires showing the small claims judge abused discretion in denying the motion (the bar is high). 

**No appeal by plaintiff:** To reiterate, as the plaintiff, you generally cannot appeal if you lose. If you think a legal error occurred or you feel it’s unjust, your recourse is extremely limited. You might file a motion to correct a clerical error or ask the judge to reconsider (though there’s no formal provision for reconsideration in small claims). But you can’t appeal to the appellate division. This asymmetry is intentional. The only slight exception is if you were also a defendant on a counterclaim that you lost – then in that capacity you can appeal. 

**Practical considerations:** Most small claims appeals are filed by defendants who either truly feel the decision was wrong or sometimes to get the “bigger” court where lawyers are allowed. Often, the mere filing of an appeal opens a window for settlement discussions between the parties – now the plaintiff has to go through another round, and the defendant faces potentially more judgment if costs add up. It’s common for cases to settle in between the small claims judgment and the appeal trial. If you reach a settlement, the appellant should formally dismiss the appeal (by filing a dismissal of appeal), and then the small claims judgment can be adjusted per the settlement (often the settlement involves agreeing to vacate the judgment once payment is made, etc.). If the appeal is dismissed, the original judgment becomes final immediately. 

**Attending appellate hearing:** If you’re the respondent (plaintiff) and the defendant appeals, *you must attend the appeal trial.* If you don’t show up, the appeal judge may hear only the appellant’s side and could rule against you up to the amount claimed (though not more than original judgment). It would be essentially a default of the appeal, and they could dismiss your claim. So do not ignore an appeal thinking “I already won, I don’t need to fight again.” You do. The case is effectively reopened. On the flip side, if the **appellant** (defendant who appealed) fails to show up at the appeal hearing, the court will likely dismiss the appeal and the original judgment will stand (and can be enforced). 

In sum, a small claims appeal gives the losing defendant one more chance in a higher court with more formalities. The deadline is 30 days ([abcdocz.com](https://abcdocz.com/doc/123939/small-claims-court-the#:~:text=Judgment%20%28Form%20SC,the%20appeal%20is%20dismissed%20or)) – it’s short, so act fast if you intend to appeal. The appeal is a completely new trial (trial **de novo**) ([abcdocz.com](https://abcdocz.com/doc/123939/small-claims-court-the#:~:text=%2475,judgment%20debtor%29%2036)), after which the resulting judgment is final and binding on both parties. 

## 12. Post-judgment collection  
Winning a judgment is one thing – **collecting** it is another. Statistics show many small claims winners never fully recover their money. California provides various tools to enforce a judgment, but it often requires persistence and knowledge of the debtor’s assets. Here’s a comprehensive overview of collection methods and rules for California small claims judgments: 

**When judgment is payable:** In California, a small claims judgment debtor (the losing party) is supposed to pay the judgment **immediately or within 30 days** of the notice of entry of judgment, unless an appeal or motion to vacate is filed. The judgment creditor (winner) should ideally send a polite demand for payment once the judgment is final. If the debtor doesn’t pay within 30 days and no appeal is pending, you can proceed with enforcement measures. Interest accrues on judgments from the date of entry at an annual rate of **10%** ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/california/2007/ccp/685.010-685.110.html#:~:text=2007%20California%20Code%20of%20Civil,the%20principal%20amount%20of%20a)) (simple interest, not compounded, except interest on accrued interest after renewal). This interest rate is set by law (for most civil judgments) at 10% per annum ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/california/2007/ccp/685.010-685.110.html#:~:text=2007%20California%20Code%20of%20Civil,the%20principal%20amount%20of%20a)) – which can be significant over time. It acts as a incentive for the debtor to pay sooner rather than later. *(Note:* Certain consumer and medical debt judgments have a lower 5% rate and shorter renewal period per recent law – see below – but most small claims judgments will be at 10% unless they specifically fall into that new category ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/judgment-renewals-and-interest-rates#:~:text=Typically%2C%20a%20judgment%20can%20be,interest%20rate)).) 

**Voluntary payment and info exchange:** Ideally, the debtor will pay voluntarily. The court form **SC-133 (Judgment Debtor’s Statement of Assets)**, which the debtor is ordered to fill out and send to you if the judgment isn’t paid within 30 days ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-forms#:~:text=match%20at%20L314%20Judgment%20Debtor%27s,220)), can provide information on their income, assets, bank accounts, etc. In practice, many debtors ignore this requirement. If the debtor does return SC-133, use that info to decide which collection method is best. If they don’t, you may need to compel information (more on the **debtor’s exam** below). 

**Enforcement methods:** California law (the Enforcement of Judgments Act, CCP § 680.010 et seq.) provides several powerful tools once you have a judgment. The primary methods are: 

- **Judgment lien on real property (Abstract of Judgment):** If the debtor owns any real estate (house, land) in California, you can record an **Abstract of Judgment** (Form EJ-001) with the County Recorder to create a **judgment lien** on their property. The abstract is obtained from the court clerk (there’s a small fee, around $40, to issue one). Once recorded in a county, it becomes a lien on any real property the debtor currently owns or later acquires in that county ([ryancwoodlaw.com](https://ryancwoodlaw.com/judgement-enforcement-renewal-of-judgment-liens/#:~:text=683,pursuant%20to%20the%20judgment%20is)). The lien lasts 10 years (and can be renewed if judgment is renewed). This means if the debtor tries to sell or refinance that property, they’ll have to pay off the lien (your judgment plus interest) to clear title. It’s a passive but effective tactic – you might not get paid immediately, but you secure your judgment. If the judgment is big enough and there’s equity, you could potentially force a sale via foreclosure on the lien, but that’s rare for small claims (and homestead exemptions often prevent it). Usually, you just wait until the debtor eventually sells or dies, and then you get paid from escrow. Nevertheless, record an abstract in every county where the debtor owns (or might own) property. Cost of issuing/recording an abstract is recoverable as an added cost on the judgment once paid ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-forms#:~:text=court%20clerk%20court%20to%20,wages%2C%20or%20take%20possession%20of)). 

- **Wage garnishment (Earnings Withholding Order):** If the debtor is employed, you can garnishee a portion of their wages. Procedure: first, obtain a **Writ of Execution (Form EJ-130)** from the court clerk (fee ~$40) – this writ is essentially the court’s order authorizing enforcement actions by the sheriff ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-forms#:~:text=court%20clerk%20court%20to%20,wages%2C%20or%20take%20possession%20of)). The writ will list the amount owed (judgment + interest + allowable costs). You then prepare an **Application for Earnings Withholding Order** (Judicial Council Form WG-001) and give it to the sheriff or marshal in the county where the debtor’s employer is located. The sheriff will serve the employer with an **Earnings Withholding Order**. By law, the employer must then divert a portion of the debtor’s paycheck to the sheriff, who will then send it to you. How much? Generally up to **20% of the debtor’s disposable earnings** or the formula amount that leaves them with a protected portion (the debtor must be left with at least 48 x state minimum wage per week, as of recent law) ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/ca/code-of-civil-procedure/ccp-sect-706-050/#:~:text=subject%20to%20levy%20under%20an,the%20lesser%20of%20the%20following)) ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/ca/code-of-civil-procedure/ccp-sect-706-050/#:~:text=,disposable%20earnings%20for%20that%20week)). Under the new California formula (effective 2023), the garnishment amount is the lesser of 20% of disposable income or 40% of disposable income over 48x min wage ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/ca/code-of-civil-procedure/ccp-sect-706-050/#:~:text=,disposable%20earnings%20for%20that%20week)). In simpler terms, that means low-income debtors might have nothing garnishable (if they earn near minimum wage, most or all may be exempt), whereas higher earners will have 20% taken. The employer will continue to withhold each pay period until the judgment (plus accruing interest) is paid in full, or the debtor leaves that job. There are some restrictions: if the debtor has a very low income, the calculation could yield $0. Also, if another garnishment is already in effect (like for child support or another judgment), only one garnishment order runs at a time and certain priority rules apply (family support takes priority, etc.). If multiple creditors line up, they queue and take turns (first come, first served). As the judgment creditor, you should include the accrued interest and any enforcement costs on the **Memorandum of Costs (Form MC-012)** and perhaps an updated interest calculation when asking for the writ, so that the garnishment covers those as well. Wage garnishment is often one of the most effective means of getting paid, assuming the debtor has a stable job. 

- **Bank account levy:** If you know where the debtor banks, you can levy their bank accounts. Process: with the same **Writ of Execution**, you instruct the sheriff to execute a **Bank Levy**. You typically use a form called **Instructions to Sheriff** where you specify the bank name and address and the account number if known (not required but helpful). The sheriff then serves a **levy** on the bank – which freezes the funds in the debtor’s account up to the amount of the writ. The bank will hold the funds for a short period (roughly 10 days) and then send them to the sheriff, who after another brief period releases them to you, unless the debtor files a claim of exemption or the funds are exempt. It’s important to note that certain types of funds in bank accounts can be **exempt** from levy: for example, **Social Security**, unemployment, disability benefits, and other public benefits are exempt from collection if the debtor claims them. There are automatic protections too: banks must automatically protect Social Security deposits from levy (they look back 2 months and shield that amount if identifiable). Nonetheless, a levy might catch some non-exempt money. If the account is joint with someone else, that other person can file a third-party claim asserting their portion. The debtor can also file a **Claim of Exemption** if the money in the account is needed for support or is from exempt sources. You as creditor can oppose that, and the court will have a hearing to decide. If no claim of exemption, after the holding period the money goes to you. Levying a bank account often works best if you time it around when the debtor typically has funds (e.g., right after payday or other influx). The **sheriff’s fees** and sometimes bank’s levy fees (banks can charge the debtor like $10 for processing a levy) can be added to the debt. One challenge: you must know where the debtor banks. Sometimes you see a check they wrote or a past payment or their paycheck’s bank. If not, you might need to do a **debtor’s exam** to find out (see below). Note: California law (as of 2020s) also provides some automatic exemption for minimal balances – a judgment debtor can keep $1,788 (if single) or $2,378 (if married) in a deposit account exempt, if they claim it, under CCP § 704.220 (these amounts adjust – these figures might change with inflation). But the debtor must affirmatively claim that exemption.  

- **Keeper or Till Tap (business assets):** If the debtor is a business (or an individual who runs a business with a cash register), you can use the sheriff to collect from the business’s cash on hand. A **“Till Tap”** is when a sheriff’s deputy goes to the business unannounced and takes the money out of the cash register at that moment, up to the amount of the judgment ([dcba.lacounty.gov](https://dcba.lacounty.gov/portfolio/small-claims-court-fees-3/#:~:text=Earnings%20Withholding%20%20,%242%2C000)). This is a one-time seizure of cash. In LA County, for example, the sheriff fee for a till tap is $100-$175 (the fee can be added to the judgment) ([dcba.lacounty.gov](https://dcba.lacounty.gov/portfolio/small-claims-court-fees-3/#:~:text=Earnings%20Withholding%20%20,%24350)). This is effective if the business does a lot of cash sales. A **“Keeper”** is more intensive: the sheriff installs a deputy (the “keeper”) at the business for a set period (like 8 hours or multiple days) to collect all money coming in (sales, etc.) ([dcba.lacounty.gov](https://dcba.lacounty.gov/portfolio/small-claims-court-fees-3/#:~:text=Earnings%20Withholding%20%20,%242%2C000)). The keeper essentially runs the register or observes sales and takes proceeds to apply to the judgment. This is more expensive – you pay in advance for the keeper’s time (e.g. $350 for an 8-hour keeper in LA) ([dcba.lacounty.gov](https://dcba.lacounty.gov/portfolio/small-claims-court-fees-3/#:~:text=Earnings%20Withholding%20%20,%242%2C000)). But if the business has high traffic, it can result in a substantial collection. If one day isn’t enough, you can request multiple days (each day you pay). Keepers are used in scenarios like restaurants, convenience stores, etc., where a lot of cash transactions happen. The presence of a sheriff’s officer can sometimes prompt the debtor to quickly negotiate or pay to get them out of there, as it’s embarrassing and disruptive. Both till tap and keeper require a writ of execution and coordination with the sheriff’s civil division. You need to give the sheriff the business address and usually best hours to go. 

- **Levy on personal property:** You can also direct the sheriff to seize certain non-exempt personal property of the debtor – like valuable equipment, vehicles, etc. This is more complicated and costly, and rarely done for small claims due to cost-benefit. For example, you could levy a **vehicle**: the sheriff would impound the debtor’s car and eventually auction it. But if the car has a loan or low value, it might not net much. Also, vehicle exemptions apply: a debtor can keep a car with equity up to a certain amount (currently $3,325 is exempt equity for one vehicle, CCP § 704.010). If the car’s equity over any loan is more than that, you can consider a levy. But you’d have to pay towing and storage fees upfront (recoverable later if sale yields enough). Similarly, you could levy other property (like expensive machinery, or even funds a third party owes to the debtor – called a “Third Party Levy” or attachment of accounts receivable). These are complex and usually require attorney help even though in small claims you don’t have one – but after judgment, you can actually hire an attorney or collection agency to assist with enforcement if you want. They just couldn’t represent you at trial, but they can in post-judgment proceedings. 

- **Judgment Debtor Examination (Order of Examination):** If the debtor isn’t paying and you don’t know what assets or income they have, you can apply for a court order to force them to come to court and answer questions about their finances. This is the **Judgment Debtor Exam**. Procedure: you file an **Application and Order for Appearance of Judgment Debtor (Form SC-134 for small claims or formal EJ-125)**. In small claims, you often use form SC-134 which the clerk can issue. The court will set a hearing date and issue an order (sometimes called an “ORAP” for Order of Examination). You must then **personally serve** that order on the debtor (at least 10 days before the hearing). This can be done by a process server or sheriff. At the hearing, the debtor is placed under oath and you (or a judge, depending on county practice) can ask them about assets: “Where do you work? How much do you earn? Where are your bank accounts? What property do you own? Do you have any cars? Any real estate? What’s the balance? Account numbers? Do you expect any inheritance?,” etc. It’s a fact-finding mission to identify collectible assets. You can subpoena documents for this exam (using a subpoena duces tecum SC-107) to have them bring bank statements, pay stubs, etc. ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-forms#:~:text=,trial)). If the debtor doesn’t show up to the exam, the judge can issue a **bench warrant** for their arrest for contempt of court (failing to comply with the court order). Often, the threat of that is enough to get them to cooperate. If a warrant is issued, the debtor can technically be arrested and brought to court to answer – though in small claims, real arrests are rare unless it’s a egregious repeat avoidance. The exam is a very useful tool to gather information. After hearing the info, you can immediately ask the judge for relief – e.g., “Your Honor, the debtor just testified he has a bank account at Wells Fargo with roughly $2,000. May I have an order directing turnover of funds or a levy?” Judges might not directly order a turnover at the exam (some do if say the debtor brought cash or a checkbook – they can order the debtor to pay on the spot). More typically, you use the info to then do a wage garnishment or bank levy as described. Notably, if the debtor fails to appear or doesn’t satisfy the judgment, the judge at the exam might also order **installment payments** if both sides agree (or sometimes even if not, under CCP § 116.620 the court can approve a payment schedule on motion). But installment orders in small claims are less common unless the debtor proactively asked for one via SC-220. 

- **Installment payment plan via court:** If the debtor can’t pay in lump sum, they might file a **Request to Pay Judgment in Installments (Form SC-220)**. The court will send you a copy and allow you to oppose if you want. If you agree (or don’t oppose), the court may approve a payment schedule. If the debtor then makes payments as ordered, you cannot enforce by other means as long as they’re current. If they default on payments, you can then enforce the remainder immediately. Sometimes working out a reasonable payment plan is more fruitful than aggressive enforcement, especially if the debtor is willing but broke – they might not have wages to garnish or assets to levy, so a voluntary payment plan (maybe $100/month) at least yields something. And remember, interest accrues at 10%, so if payments are slow, interest will add up (though on a small principal, it’s not huge monthly, but still). 

**Exemptions:** California law protects certain income and property of the debtor from being taken to satisfy a judgment. Major exemptions include: Social Security benefits, SSI, unemployment, worker’s comp, most public benefits, and a portion of wages as described (75-80% or more of wages are exempt due to formula) ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/ca/code-of-civil-procedure/ccp-sect-706-050/#:~:text=,disposable%20earnings%20for%20that%20week)). Additionally, basic personal property like necessary clothing, household furnishings, tools of trade (up to certain value), one vehicle up to ~$3,300 equity, etc., are exempt under CCP § 704.010 et seq. If you try to levy something that is exempt, the debtor can file a **Claim of Exemption** (for a bank levy or wage garnishment, etc.) and they usually will win on that point. For example, if their bank account only has SSI funds, those are exempt by law (and banks by law automatically protect SSI and veterans direct deposits in certain amounts). You cannot seize certain property like qualified retirement accounts either – those are exempt. The homestead exemption for a primary residence is quite high in CA (hundreds of thousands), so forcing a sale of a house is generally not practical unless the house is very valuable with no mortgage and the judgment is relatively large. For small claims, that’s generally off the table. So, focus on *non-exempt assets*: wages (above a threshold), bank accounts (with non-exempt money), rent income the debtor might receive, business revenue, vehicles (if not needed for work or if luxury), possibly tax refunds (California Franchise Tax Board has a program where they intercept state tax refunds for certain debts including court judgments if you apply, but that’s often used for gov’t debts, not as common for private small claims but check the local FTB Court-Ordered Debt program). 

**Judgment liens on personal property:** Apart from real estate, you can also place a lien on a debtor’s personal property (business equipment, etc.) by filing a **Notice of Judgment Lien (Form JL-1)** with the California Secretary of State. This creates a lien on personal property assets like accounts receivable, equipment, and vehicles (it’s similar to a UCC filing). This is more applicable if the debtor is a business. It puts the world on notice that you have a claim on their assets. If they try to sell the business or collateral that’s under lien, you might get paid. 

**Renewal of judgment:** A California judgment is enforceable for **10 years** and can be renewed for additional 10-year periods ([ryancwoodlaw.com](https://ryancwoodlaw.com/judgement-enforcement-renewal-of-judgment-liens/#:~:text=683,pursuant%20to%20the%20judgment%20is)). If your judgment isn’t fully paid within 10 years, you must renew it before the 10 years run out or you lose the ability to enforce (it becomes dormant/uncollectible) ([ryancwoodlaw.com](https://ryancwoodlaw.com/judgement-enforcement-renewal-of-judgment-liens/#:~:text=683,pursuant%20to%20the%20judgment%20is)). To renew, you file an **Application for Renewal of Judgment (Form EJ-190)** and have the clerk issue a **Notice of Renewal (Form EJ-195)**, which you must serve on the debtor (by mail is fine) ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-forms#:~:text=create%20a%20lien%20against%20the,190%C2%A0tells)). Renewal can be done at any time during the 10th year (or earlier, though if you renew early you forfeit some post-judgment interest). When renewed, added accrued interest becomes part of the new principal, and the judgment life extends 10 years from the renewal. You can renew indefinitely every 10 years until it’s paid ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/judgment-renewals-and-interest-rates#:~:text=Generally%2C%20renewals%20last%2010%20years,but%20there%20are%20some%20exceptions)) – except note, *new law from 2020:* for judgments on **certain consumer debts** (like personal loans under certain amounts) and **medical debts**, the law now limits those to one renewal and a total of 5% interest ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/judgment-renewals-and-interest-rates#:~:text=Typically%2C%20a%20judgment%20can%20be,interest%20rate)). Specifically, if your case was primarily for personal debt under $50,000 or for medical expenses, there’s a chance it falls under CCP § 683.110 et seq as amended by SB 1200 (2020) – which allows only one 5-year renewal and interest at 5% (the self-help blurb we saw mentions that ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/judgment-renewals-and-interest-rates#:~:text=Typically%2C%20a%20judgment%20can%20be,interest%20rate))). But for typical small claims like contract breaches, property damage, etc., the standard 10 year/10% rule applies ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/judgment-renewals-and-interest-rates#:~:text=Generally%2C%20renewals%20last%2010%20years,but%20there%20are%20some%20exceptions)). Most small claims creditors won’t need renewal if the debtor pays or can be found within 10 years, but it’s good to diarize the deadline. If time is almost up and the debtor still hasn’t paid in full, renew the judgment to preserve it. 

**Judgment interest:** We covered it, but to restate: a money judgment accrues **10% annual interest** (simple) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/california/2007/ccp/685.010-685.110.html#:~:text=2007%20California%20Code%20of%20Civil,the%20principal%20amount%20of%20a)). For example, a $5,000 judgment gains $500 per year in interest (approx $41.67 per month) until paid. If the debtor eventually pays years later, they must pay the accrued interest too. You, as the creditor, should keep track of payments and interest. The interest rate is statutory (not variable), except as noted for some consumer/medical judgments post-2023 which are 5%. If the debtor is a government entity, the interest is only 7% by law ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/judgment-renewals-and-interest-rates#:~:text=The%20general%20rule%20is%20that,debtor%20is%20a%20government%20agency)) (not common in small claims as you usually can’t sue government for money in small claims due to claim limits, but if you did and won, it’s 7%). You can use a **Memorandum of Costs After Judgment (MC-012)** to add things like additional costs (sheriff fees, etc.) and accrued interest to the judgment formally. You typically file that when you have some enforcement activity or when you’re seeking a writ or renewal. The debtor has a window to oppose if they think your math is wrong. 

**Priority of liens and multiple creditors:** If the debtor owes multiple judgments, priority often goes by who put liens or levies first. For wage garnishments, only one can run at a time (the others wait their turn; support orders have priority). For real estate, if multiple abstracts are recorded, it’s first in time, first in right (the earliest recorded lien gets paid first from any sale). For bank levies, if two creditors levy the same account around the same time, usually the one that hit the bank first (the bank will use the date/time of service) gets the money (if enough, the second may get any remainder up to theirs). 

**Satisfaction of Judgment:** Once the debtor pays in full, you (the creditor) are obligated to file a **Satisfaction of Judgment (Form EJ-100)** with the court, and also, if any liens were recorded, to record a Release of Lien (often done by recording the signed satisfaction). This clears the record that the judgment is paid. You should do this within 14 days of demand or risk penalties. If the debtor pays you directly, definitely file the satisfaction so it doesn’t linger on their credit or property. If payment came through garnishment fully, the court might automatically enter satisfaction, but don’t assume – it’s your duty. 

**Credit reports:** Judgments can appear on credit reports (though recent changes have de-emphasized civil judgments on credit reports). Regardless, a satisfied judgment is better for the debtor’s credit than unsatisfied. So debtors have incentive to pay and get it cleared. 

**Special cases – enforcement against businesses:** If you have a judgment against a **corporation or LLC** and they don’t pay, all the above applies to the company’s assets. One thing to note: you cannot go after the personal assets of the owners/shareholders unless you got a judgment against them too (which generally you didn’t unless you proved an alter ego scenario). So for a company, you might levy bank accounts in the company’s name, or place liens on company property, but not the owner’s personal accounts. If the business has closed, collecting can be hard unless they still had assets. In some cases, if you suspect fraud (they shut down one company and opened another to avoid paying), you might need legal help to enforce (like file a separate action to add the new company as a successor or do a creditor’s suit). This is beyond small claims scope typically. 

**If the debtor files bankruptcy:** If the judgment debtor declares **bankruptcy**, that usually halts all collection (automatic stay) and may discharge the debt (unless it’s a type not dischargeable, like certain fraud judgments). If you receive a bankruptcy notice, consult an attorney. Often, small claims judgments get wiped out in bankruptcy, meaning you have to stop all collection. A recorded lien from before bankruptcy might survive if it attached to property (secured claim), but that’s a complex area. 

**Final caveat – cost vs benefit:** Enforcing a judgment can cost money (sheriff fees, etc.). You can add those to the judgment, but you still front them. If the debtor is truly “judgment proof” (no job, no assets), you might spend time and money and get nothing. Sometimes the best approach is to periodically ask the debtor if they can pay or wait until their situation improves (judgments last a long time). However, be proactive within your rights: record the abstract, it’s cheap and could pay off later; try a debtor exam if you have no info; and use garnishments/levies if you know where to hit. Also, small claims advisors in many counties can help strategize on collection – don’t hesitate to reach out to them. 

This is indeed where many plaintiffs fail – they win the case but then do nothing to collect, or don’t know how. By understanding and using the above tools, you greatly increase your chances of getting paid. It may take persistence over months or even years. Keep earning that 10% interest in the meantime. And always follow legal procedures to the letter when executing these remedies, because improper execution (like a wrongful levy on exempt funds) can be challenged and set you back. Execution and attachment laws (CCP 699.010 et seq. and 706.010 et seq.) outline these steps in detail if you need to reference them. 

## 13. Special rules for businesses  
Businesses in small claims court have a few different rules to be aware of, regarding representation and filing limits: 

- **Representation (who appears):** A business entity (like a corporation, LLC, or partnership) **cannot be represented by an attorney** in small claims (just like individuals can’t) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/california/code-ccp/part-1/title-1/chapter-5-5/article-5/section-116-540/#:~:text=116,of%20a%20small%20claims%20action)). Instead, the business must appear through a **non-lawyer agent**. Specifically, a **corporation** or **LLC** can send a **director, officer, or regular employee** to represent it ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/california/code-ccp/part-1/title-1/chapter-5-5/article-5/section-116-540/#:~:text=,corporation%20in%20small%20claims%20court)). The person must be employed for purposes other than just making court appearances ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/california/code-ccp/part-1/title-1/chapter-5-5/article-5/section-116-540/#:~:text=,corporation%20in%20small%20claims%20court)) (so you can’t hire an “employee” solely to do the small claims – in practice, courts allow an owner or manager or any bona fide employee). They should have sufficient knowledge of the facts to discuss the case. This representative needs to be identified on a form **Authorization to Appear (SC-109)**, signed by a corporate officer or LLC member, which they present to the court ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/jcc-form/SC-109#:~:text=Authorization%20to%20Appear%20,Get%20form)). For a **partnership**, one of the partners (who is not a lawyer) can appear, or a regular employee of the partnership ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/california/code-ccp/part-1/title-1/chapter-5-5/article-5/section-116-540/#:~:text=,party%20in%20small%20claims%20court)). For a **sole proprietorship** (where the owner is the plaintiff or defendant), the owner themselves should appear – but there is a provision: if the owner is **unable to attend** (e.g., out-of-area or health issues) and the claim is on an account, they can authorize a regular employee to appear ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/california/code-ccp/part-1/title-1/chapter-5-5/article-5/section-116-540/#:~:text=,the%20following%20conditions%20are%20met)) (like a bookkeeper who has knowledge of the accounts) under certain conditions. That employee would also use SC-109 to show authorization. If a business tries to send an attorney, the court will disallow it; the attorney could only sit in the audience as an advisor, not speak. If a business sends no one, they default. If they send a person who is not properly authorized or knowledgeable, the judge might not allow them to proceed (or might proceed but it will hurt their case). So, businesses should pick a representative wisely – often it’s an owner (for small LLCs or closely-held corporations, an owner/officer usually comes) or a manager. Note: **Insurance companies** (if a business is a defendant and has liability insurance) sometimes send a claims adjuster to small claims for the insured – that’s allowed under CCP § 116.540(i) for an insured defendant. E.g., you sue Bob’s Trucking Co. for an accident, their insurer can send an employee (insurance adjuster) to defend in the name of the insured ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/california/code-ccp/part-1/title-1/chapter-5-5/article-5/section-116-540/#:~:text=,corporation%20in%20small%20claims%20court)). Similarly, a defendant who is an individual and has insurance can have a representative from the insurer (often a lawyer or adjuster) – but careful: attorneys are barred, yet there’s an exception for insurance lawyers to represent their insured in small claims *defense* (CCP 116.530 allows a lawyer to assist in certain cases like if the defendant is on active duty military or a jail inmate; for insurance, typically they send an adjuster or house counsel not as “attorney” but as rep – that’s a nuanced area, but commonly adjusters come). 

- **Filing limits for businesses:** As mentioned in Section 1, a business (corporation, LLC, partnership, etc.) is limited to **$6,250 per claim** as the maximum claim amount ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-or-limited-civil#:~:text=If%20you%20are%20an%20individual,there%20may%20be%20an%20advantage)). Additionally, **no party (including businesses)** may file more than **two claims over $2,500 in a year** ([www.sucorte.ca.gov](https://www.sucorte.ca.gov/small-claims/before-you-start#:~:text=,calendar%20year%20for%20over%20%242%2C500)). This rule hits frequent-filer businesses like collection agencies or landlords with multiple large claims: you can’t circumvent the $2,500 two-claim limit by using different agents; it’s per entity. However, a business can file an **unlimited number of claims for $2,500 or less**. So a company could file 100 small claims for $2,000 each in a year, but only two claims for, say, $5,000 each in that year. This often affects large apartment management companies or lenders – they might have to keep most claims at $2,500 or below after using their two “big” claims annually. If a business tries to file a third claim in a year over $2,500, the court will likely reject it (or the defendant can point out the violation and get it dismissed). 

- **Fictitious Business Name (DBA):** If a business is suing *under* a fictitious name (doing business as XYZ), or if you are an individual with a DBA, California law (§ 116.430) requires that you have a current fictitious business name statement on file with the county and that you **attach a Fictitious Business Name declaration (SC-103)** to your claim ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-forms#:~:text=Go%20to%20Small%20Claims%20Court,103)). Essentially, the court wants to know the real legal entity behind the business name. For example, Joe Smith files as “Acme Services, a fictitious business name of Joe Smith.” Joe should have filed a DBA with the county and then sign SC-103 saying “Joe Smith is doing business as Acme Services, filed in Orange County on [date].” If a business plaintiff fails to comply, the case can be put on hold or dismissed until they fix it ([california.public.law](https://california.public.law/codes/code_of_civil_procedure_section_116.430#:~:text=California%20Code%20of%20Civil%20Procedure,filing%20of%20a%20declaration%20stating)). For **defendants,** if you’re suing a business and you list only the DBA, that’s technically incorrect – you should sue the actual owner(s). For instance, if you want to sue “Acme Services” and find out it’s a sole prop of Joe Smith, you should sue “Joe Smith, dba Acme Services.” If you only put Acme Services and it’s not a corporation/LLC, you haven’t sued a legal person. The court might allow an amendment if the mistake is realized. Similarly, if it’s “Acme Services, LLC” you should sue under the LLC’s exact registered name (including LLC). Mistakes in naming a business defendant are common pitfalls (see Section 15). 

- **Out-of-state businesses:** If a business from out of state wants to file a claim in California small claims, or appear as a defendant, one issue is **registration with the Secretary of State**. Under California Corporations Code § 2105, a foreign (out-of-state) corporation or LLC **transacting intrastate business** in California cannot maintain a lawsuit in California courts until it obtains a Certificate of Qualification to do business in CA. If an out-of-state business is just suing based on an isolated transaction, often courts don’t scrutinize this. But if the business does substantial business in CA and never registered, the defendant can raise lack of capacity. A quick way is to get registered (though that takes time and fees). For small claims, this is seldom raised, but be aware: if you’re an out-of-state company regularly doing business in CA (thus required to register with CA), failing to register could jeopardize your case. As a practical matter, most small claims judges might not enforce this strictly unless obvious. For defendants, if you’re suing an out-of-state company that hasn’t registered, you can still sue them (registration is plaintiff’s requirement to sue, not a barrier to being sued). You *can* still sue foreign entities; service might have to be done through Secretary of State if they have no agent here (California law allows serving the CA Secretary of State on behalf of corporations/LLCs that haven’t designated an agent or failed to register, see CCP § 416.10 and Corp Code § 2111). 

- **Attorneys within a business:** What if a corporation’s regular employee who appears happens to be an attorney? CCP § 116.540(b) says a corporation can appear through a regular employee *whether or not that employee is an attorney* ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/california/code-ccp/part-1/title-1/chapter-5-5/article-5/section-116-540/#:~:text=,corporation%20in%20small%20claims%20court)). So if the general counsel of a company (who is a full-time employee) comes to small claims, that’s allowed. They must be appearing as an employee, not as outside counsel. So theoretically, a business’s in-house lawyer can represent the business. But a hired outside lawyer cannot. Likewise, a partner in a partnership who happens to be a lawyer can appear for the partnership as the party – that’s allowed since they are a principal. 

- **Multiple cases limit:** Another rule: no party may file more than **two small claims cases statewide for more than $2,500 each** per year ([www.sucorte.ca.gov](https://www.sucorte.ca.gov/small-claims/before-you-start#:~:text=,calendar%20year%20for%20over%20%242%2C500)). This was discussed. For businesses, it prevents say a collection agency from flooding the court with 50 claims of $5,000. They’d be limited to two >$2,500, and all others must be $2,500 or less. The penalty for violating this (if they file a third) is that the court can dismiss the case, or if somehow it goes through, the judgment may be void. The small claims filing form (SC-100) actually asks if you’ve filed more than 2 over $2,500 in the last 12 months. Lying on that could be perjury. So businesses must abide. 

- **Transfer to regular court if counterclaim exceeds limit:** If a business is sued in small claims and they (being the defendant) have a counterclaim that exceeds small claims limit ($6,250 for them), they can utilize CCP § 116.390 to request a **transfer** to superior court (as discussed in Section 2) ([www.legalfina.com](https://www.legalfina.com/en/laws/ca/civil_procedure/of_courts_of_justice/organization_and_jurisdiction/small_claims_court/actions#:~:text=,the%20plaintiff%E2%80%99s%20claim%2C%20the%20defendant)). For example, you sue a company for $5,000 in small claims, and they believe you actually owe them $20,000 – the company can’t counterclaim in small claims for $20k (over the limit), so they can file a motion to transfer the case to the civil division so both claims can be resolved at once. A business might do this if the stakes justify hiring lawyers and going to regular court. (They could also just file a separate lawsuit in superior court and then likely get the small claims case consolidated or dismissed). This is a strategic decision. Companies more often than individuals might seek to transfer a case out of small claims to avoid the informal setting or having a non-lawyer appear – especially if they prefer an attorney handle it. But note, small claims was designed to prevent that for claims within the limit. The company can only force a transfer if *they* assert a larger claim. They can’t just veto small claims because they don’t like it – if they have no large counterclaim, they must stay in small claims without counsel. 

- **Compliance with licensing:** One more nuance: if a business is required to have a license (say a contractor’s license) and they don’t, that can affect their ability to sue for fees (per the Contractor’s State License Law, an unlicensed contractor cannot use the court to collect for unlicensed work). So, if you are a business suing someone, ensure you complied with any licensing laws for your trade, because the defendant can use that as a defense (e.g., “they did work requiring a license and didn’t have one, so under B&P Code § 7031 they can’t collect a penny”). This is not a small-claims-specific rule, but a general legal principle that often comes up with business plaintiffs. 

In summary, businesses in small claims must navigate representation rules (use a non-lawyer agent with authorization) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/california/code-ccp/part-1/title-1/chapter-5-5/article-5/section-116-540/#:~:text=,corporation%20in%20small%20claims%20court)), lower claim limits ($6,250 max) ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-or-limited-civil#:~:text=If%20you%20are%20an%20individual,there%20may%20be%20an%20advantage)), and filing frequency limits ([www.sucorte.ca.gov](https://www.sucorte.ca.gov/small-claims/before-you-start#:~:text=,calendar%20year%20for%20over%20%242%2C500)). They also need to keep their business paperwork in order (DBA filings, etc.) to avoid technical issues ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-forms#:~:text=Go%20to%20Small%20Claims%20Court,103)). But small claims can be very useful for businesses especially to collect on smaller debts without hiring an attorney. Many property management companies, for instance, regularly use small claims for past-due rent or damage (often keeping claims at $2,500 to avoid the 2-per-year cap). As a business, treat the process professionally: send a knowledgeable employee, have your documentation (invoices, contracts) well-organized, and you’ll be taken seriously by the court. 

## 14. County-level differences  
The core small claims laws are the same statewide (California Code of Civil Procedure § 116.110 et seq. and California Rules of Court). However, there are **some local differences** in how small claims are administered. Below are a few notable county-specific practices or variations, especially focusing on some of the largest counties: 

- **Los Angeles County:** *Venue and filing:* LA is so large that it’s divided into multiple court districts. You must file your small claim in the proper **District Courthouse** based on where the defendant lives or where the dispute arose. The LA Superior Court provides a **Filing Court Locator** tool by ZIP code ([dcba.lacounty.gov](https://dcba.lacounty.gov/portfolio/venue-filing-in-the-right-court-2/#:~:text=Based%20on%20these%20choices%2C%20there,you%20find%20the%20right%20court)). If you file in the wrong district, the case may be transferred or dismissed. LA used to allow e-filing for small claims, but as of recent years, the **small claims e-filing system was decommissioned** ([ww2.lacourt.org](https://ww2.lacourt.org/eFiling/eFilingNotice.aspx#:~:text=You%20may%20submit%20your%20Small,Claims%20filings%20via)). Now filings can be done by walk-in, mail, or fax filing (LA has a fax filing service for a fee) ([ww2.lacourt.org](https://ww2.lacourt.org/eFiling/eFilingNotice.aspx#:~:text=You%20may%20submit%20your%20Small,Claims%20filings%20via)). LA has a robust **Small Claims Advisor** program through the Department of Consumer and Business Affairs – free advice by phone or in-person workshops. On hearing day, LA small claims cases are often heard by volunteer temporary judges (often attorneys sitting pro tem). In LA, if either party doesn’t want a temporary judge, they must inform the clerk; otherwise you’ll likely be heard by one. LA also encourages **mediation**; in many LA courthouses, mediators from organizations like the DRPA (Dispute Resolution Program) are available on the hearing day – the judge will often send parties to mediate before hearing the case. Another small LA detail: if you’re suing for property damage from a vehicle accident in LA and the defendant has insurance that paid you part (like partial insurance payment), LA local rules require that the insurance info be listed (there’s a local form or requirement about indicating insurance payments to avoid double recovery). Also, LA local rule 3.37 dictates that a defendant must be served at least 15 days before the hearing if within county (20 if out of county) which is just restating state law. *Courthouse differences:* LA has some courts that run evening or weekend small claims sessions for convenience (e.g., some districts had night court for small claims). Check the LA Court website for the latest – these can change with budget.  

- **San Diego County:** San Diego’s Superior Court handles small claims at several locations (Central (Downtown), North County (Vista), East County (El Cajon), South County (Chula Vista)). Venue is by where the defendant lives or where the contract/incident happened, similar to LA’s multi-district approach. San Diego offers e-filing for many civil case types but **currently does not allow e-filing for small claims** (as of 2026, small claims still must be filed on paper in SD). They do provide a **Small Claims Packet** (PKT-019) with forms and instructions on their website ([www.sdcourt.ca.gov](https://www.sdcourt.ca.gov/sdcourt/smallclaims2/smallclaimsforms#:~:text=Small%20Claims%20Forms%20,19%20Rental)). San Diego has a **Small Claims Legal Advisor** program (joint with the San Diego Law Library) that gives free help by phone or email. On hearing day in SD, volunteer mediators from the National Conflict Resolution Center are often present to facilitate settlements (especially in the downtown court). Some San Diego small claims are heard by attorneys serving as temporary judges, others by commissioners. One local twist: San Diego Local Rule states you should bring enough copies of exhibits for each party and the judge (they strongly encourage pre-marking exhibits and exchanging with the other side before the hearing to save time). Another difference: In San Diego, if your case involves a traffic accident, they may combine multiple related small claims to be heard by one judge for consistency. *Languages:* San Diego has a high volume of Spanish speakers; the court does provide interpreters (if requested) for small claims, but because of demand, they encourage parties to bring a friend to interpret if possible. However, legally you have a right to a provided interpreter.  

- **Orange County:** Orange strongly encourages use of their **online SmartForms and e-filing** system – indeed, Orange County Superior Court has **mandatory e-filing** for small claims as of recent years ([www.occourts.org](https://www.occourts.org/online-services/efiling/efiling-small-claims#:~:text=Orange%20www,eFiling%20for%20Small)). Plaintiffs file electronically via the court’s approved EFSPs ([www.occourts.org](https://www.occourts.org/online-services/efiling/efiling-small-claims#:~:text=Select%20one%20of%20the%20following,your%20small%20claims%20documents)). This is a big difference from many other counties. So in Orange, you likely will be filing your SC-100 online (through a vendor like OneLegal or others) and paying electronically. Self-represented litigants can opt out of e-filing, but it’s available and generally expected. Orange’s small claims hearings take place at the Civil Complex Center in Santa Ana for most cases. Orange County also offers a free **mediation program** on the day of hearing (through Community Service Programs). If both parties, say, appear and check in, they might be offered mediation before seeing the judge. Another note: Orange County Local Rule 370 deals with small claims – requiring for example that if a party needs an interpreter, they should notify the court 5 days in advance; if an ADA accommodation is needed, notify 5 days ahead. *Advisors:* Orange didn’t have a county-funded small claims advisor historically, but legal aid organizations like Legal Aid Society of Orange County provide some guidance resources. Orange also issues **night court** sessions occasionally due to volume. They also allow telephonic appearance for judgment debtor exams in some cases if arranged (especially during/post pandemic), but normally you appear in person.  

- **San Francisco County:** SF has one central courthouse (400 McAllister St.) where small claims are heard. **E-Filing:** As of mid-2024, San Francisco Superior Court expanded e-filing, but notably, they list small claims as *exempt from mandatory e-filing*. So small claims can be filed on paper or possibly e-filed voluntarily depending on local policy, but it’s not mandatory. Check SF court’s e-filing portal ([sf.courts.ca.gov](https://sf.courts.ca.gov/online-services/efiling#:~:text=Francisco%20sf.courts.ca.gov%20%20...e,filing)) – it mainly covers civil limited/unlimited. SF does require an **initial hearing mediation session** for many small claims. Pre-pandemic, SF had a program where you might first meet with a volunteer mediator on your hearing date, and only if no agreement, then go before the judge. SF’s small claims judges often schedule *night court* (5:30 pm calendars) for convenience. SF also has a rule that if either party doesn’t speak English well, they *will* provide an interpreter at no cost (as part of statewide mandate). *Local forms:* San Francisco sometimes asks plaintiffs in automobile accident cases to bring 3 copies of everything (one for judge, one for other side, one for themselves – which is just good practice anywhere). SF’s **Small Claims Advisor** service is run by the Bar Association of SF – they have a hotline and clinic hours to help folks prepare. Another SF specific: If suing on a COVID-19 rental debt, as of the time of this writing, due to state law AB 832, small claims had a special jurisdiction for such claims but only within specific periods, and SF local rules may have processes for those (like requiring a cover sheet that it’s a COVID rent case). That expired in 2025 as mentioned ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-or-limited-civil#:~:text=)).  

- **Alameda County:** Small claims in Alameda (Oakland, etc.) are heard in dedicated sessions (often by commissioners or judges pro tem). Alameda strongly encourages **mediation** the day of – the Alameda County Superior Court partners with SEEDS Community Resolution Center to provide mediators on-site. Alameda’s small claims can be filed by mail or in person; at this time Alameda does *not* have e-filing for small claims (they barely have it for other case types). *Local quirk:* Alameda County Local Rule 3.11 requires the plaintiff to provide a stamped envelope addressed to each defendant for mailing the judgment (some courts do this – they make you bring envelopes for the court’s use to mail out notices). Check if your court does; many just use court postage. Alameda’s advisor is through the Superior Court Self-Help Center which gives materials and can do limited consultation – often they refer to online info.  

- **Riverside & San Bernardino Counties:** These two large counties also have multiple districts (e.g., Riverside has small claims in Riverside, Murrieta, etc., San Bernardino has it in San Bernardino central and other local courthouses). Both have embraced e-filing for many cases. As of 2026, **Riverside County** allows e-filing for small claims through a portal; **San Bernardino County** similarly encourages e-filing. If not mandatory, it’s at least an option. They too provide small claims advisors (often via the Legal Aid or County services). Because these counties cover many cities, check their venue rules – typically you file in the court nearest to where the defendant lives or contract signed, etc.  

- **Santa Clara County (Silicon Valley):** They have e-filing for limited civil but not for small claims (as of now). Small claims is heard in Downtown SJ courthouse. They have a small claims advisor at the courthouse self-help center. They often set small claims at 8:30 am calendars with many cases, so they heavily use mediation and staggering to manage volume.  

**Local fees and costs:** Filing fees and costs are set by state law, so they’re uniform (as covered in Section 4). One difference is service fees: serving by sheriff might cost slightly different amounts in different counties (e.g., LA Sheriff $40-$50 ([dcba.lacounty.gov](https://dcba.lacounty.gov/portfolio/small-claims-court-fees-3/#:~:text=Service%20of%20Plaintiff%E2%80%99s%20Claim%20,%242%2C000)), some counties $35). Also, some counties charge for parking at courthouse (LA does, Orange downtown does, etc.), some don’t – trivial but notable if you drive.  

**Local forms differences:** Generally, Judicial Council forms are used statewide. But certain counties have **local forms** for convenience: e.g., some have a local **Request for Reset** form if you need to postpone (rather than SC-150). Some have local **Proof of Service by Mail** forms for mailing forms like a demand for payment on a judgment. LA has a local form for **attorney fee waiver** for attorneys who are parties (obscure). These are minor. The most important local form is for LA or other counties with **multiple venues** – often a required local form is a **Certification of Venue** or Cover Sheet where you state why that district is correct. For example, LA used to require a local form LACIV 109, where you certify you filed in proper district. Always check the court’s website “Forms -> Local Forms -> Small Claims” to see if they list any. If none, you’re good with state forms.  

**Scheduling and continuances:** How easily you can postpone a hearing can vary. Some courts (like those in small rural counties) will just grant one continuance if you ask. Others, like LA, require a written request 10 days prior and typically only one postponement is allowed. Some counties may require you to appear to request it if short notice. Always follow local instructions (often in the flyer with your trial date).  

**COVID-era changes:** As of 2026, most courts have ended remote appearances for small claims, but a few might still allow **video appearances** if both sides agree or for certain hearings like a motion to vacate. For instance, during 2020-2022, many counties instituted Zoom small claims trials. Some might keep that for convenience (e.g., if someone living far away, judge might allow phone testimony). Check local policy; some courts have technology to do hybrid hearings, others returned fully in-person.  

**Language assistance differences:** All courts provide interpreters, but the availability may differ. LA, SF, etc., have staff interpreters in many languages ready because of larger populations. In a smaller county, you might need to request earlier so they can arrange one. But law says the court should provide one if requested.  

**Advisors:** The quality and availability of small claims advisor services vary: 
  - LA’s is well-funded via DCBA with multiple advisors available by phone or even in-person at some courts. 
  - SF’s is via Bar Association – limited hours. 
  - Some counties like Ventura or Fresno have an online “SmartAdvisor” Q&A or scheduled callback because less staffing. 
Check your court’s self-help page. They might have a specific number or times to call for small claims help.  

**Local mediators:** As repeated above, nearly all populous counties have free mediation at the courthouse on hearing day through non-profits. E.g., LA (DCBA), Orange (OC Human Relations or CSP), SD (NCRC), Bay Area (SEEDS, Peninsula Conflict Center, etc.). Smaller counties often have DRPA-funded community mediation you can contact ahead or at trial. Taking advantage of mediation can save you time; plus, if you reach an agreement, they often can put it in writing and have the judge make it a binding stipulated judgment or dismissal per terms. If the other side is open to settling, definitely consider it.  

In sum, while the *laws* are uniform, the *logistics* of small claims can differ by county: **where to file, how to file (electronic or not) ([www.occourts.org](https://www.occourts.org/online-services/efiling/efiling-small-claims#:~:text=Select%20one%20of%20the%20following,your%20small%20claims%20documents)), availability of mediation, and scheduling practices**. Always check your county court’s website for a “Small Claims” section – they usually have local instructions and FAQs that highlight any special rules. When in doubt, call the small claims clerk. Doing so can reveal practical tips like “In our county, bring three copies of everything” or “We only accept filings via drop-box after 3pm”, etc. Being attuned to these local practices will make your small claims experience smoother. 

## 15. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons  
Even with small claims’ simplified process, there are common mistakes that can cause your case to be dismissed or for you to lose. Here are pitfalls to avoid: 

- **Suing the wrong party or name:** Perhaps the most frequent error is not correctly naming the defendant. For example, suing “John’s Plumbing” when that’s just a business name, not a legal entity, or suing an individual when the contract was actually with their LLC. If the defendant’s name is wrong, any judgment might be unenforceable (you can’t collect from someone not actually named). *Solution:* Do your homework on who is legally responsible. If it’s a business, look up its official name and owner. For a sole proprietorship or partnership, list the owners’ names (e.g. “John Smith dba John’s Plumbing”). For a corporation/LLC, use the exact registered name (you can search the Secretary of State’s online business registry). If you’re unsure, you can sue multiple alternatives (for instance, “Acme, Inc., and John Doe (owner of Acme if not a corporation)”). The court allows amendments if you discover the right name later, but ideally get it right upfront. *Pro tip:* If you get a judgment and then realize the name was slightly off (like missing “Inc.” or a misspelling), you can ask the court to correct it as a clerical error – but if you named a completely wrong entity, you might have to start over. So double-check names on contracts, checks, etc. Similarly, plaintiffs sometimes forget to include “AKA” aliases or married names of defendants – include those if relevant. E.g. “Jane Smith aka Jane Doe” if recently married/divorced. 

- **Improper service of process:** Failing to serve the defendant correctly or within the required time is a sure way to get a case dismissed or delayed. Examples: having a friend mail the claim to the defendant (not allowed – service must be by *personal delivery* or *substituted delivery + mail* by a non-party ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.340#:~:text=,one%20of%20the%20following%20methods)) ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.340#:~:text=by%20substituted%20service%20as%20provided,order%20to%20the%20defendant%E2%80%99s%20usual)), or by clerk’s certified mail in some cases). Or serving the defendant too late (less than 15 days before hearing in-county ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.340#:~:text=,which%20the%20action%20is%20filed))). Or not filing the proof of service. If the court has no proof of service on file by the hearing, the judge will likely dismiss the case without prejudice (meaning you can re-file) or continue it. *Solution:* Use a qualified process server or someone reliable who will properly execute service and complete the Proof of Service form. Ensure service is done well before the deadline (and file the proof). Double-check that the address used is valid – serving an old address where defendant no longer lives = no service. If you only have an old address, try to find a current one (through online search, utility bills, etc.) *Note:* If you realize at the hearing that service was bad (say the defendant wasn’t served), ask for a continuance to re-serve rather than pressing for default (the judge won’t give default if service is clearly bad). Common scenario: someone serves the defendant’s 16-year-old child at home (not allowed; substitute service requires person 18+ ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims/start-case/serve#:~:text=,substituted%20service))). That could quash service. Avoid shortcuts – follow the service rules strictly. 

- **Statute of limitations expired:** As covered in Section 5, suing after the legal deadline will likely result in dismissal if the defendant raises it (and judges can raise it on their own as well). Many self-represented litigants aren’t aware of SOL and file very stale claims. For instance, someone tries to sue in 2026 for a security deposit from 2018 – that’s beyond the 4-year written contract limit. If the defendant asserts “Your Honor, the statute of limitations has run,” the case will be dismissed with prejudice. *Solution:* Always consider the date of the incident or breach and check the applicable limitation. If it’s borderline, be prepared to explain a discovery rule argument or tolling (like “the defendant was out of state for two years, tolling the period”). But if it’s truly expired, don’t waste time filing – or if you do, know the defendant just has to bring it up to win. (Some might not know to, but many do). Judges often ask upfront: “When did this happen?” If you respond with a date beyond the SOL, the judge might prompt the defendant: “Do you have any comment about the timeliness?” This signals dismissal coming. 

- **Suing for the wrong type of relief:** People sometimes ask for remedies small claims can’t give. Common error: asking for **injunctive relief** (“I want the neighbor to stop noisy parties” or “I want the contractor to finish the job”). Small claims cannot order someone to do or not do something (with rare statutory exceptions) ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.220.#:~:text=,court%20to%20award%20that%20relief)). They only give money or return of property. If you only want non-monetary relief, small claims will dismiss because they can’t grant that. *Solution:* Convert what happened into a monetary equivalent. Instead of “finish the job,” sue for the cost to hire someone else to finish (money damages). Instead of “stop the nuisance,” perhaps sue for monetary compensation for the harm the nuisance caused thus far (though ongoing nuisance injunctions must go to a higher court if that’s truly the goal). Another example: someone sues for “pain and suffering” in an emotional sense like defamation or harassment. Small claims can award money for defamation if proven, but not an order to retract a statement or apologize. So tailor the claim to money. Also, you generally cannot sue for **punitive damages** in small claims – some might try to ask for punitive on top of actual damages in, say, a fraud case. While in theory a judge could award punitive damages (there’s no explicit prohibition if it’s within the cap, though it’s unusual without formal litigation), most small claims judges won’t entertain punitive damages *explicitly*. They’ll award a reasonable amount and that’s it. So asking for a large punitive sum is a red flag and likely won’t be awarded. 

- **Insufficient evidence:** Small claims litigants sometimes show up with basically no evidence besides their story. If the case boils down to one person’s word against the other and you have the burden of proof (plaintiff) and nothing to back it up, you risk losing. For example, suing a mechanic for $1,000 of poor repairs but bringing no receipts, no photos of the alleged poor work, no expert statement that it was faulty – just “it didn’t seem right.” The judge might find you didn’t meet your burden. Or claiming someone damaged your property but not having any proof they did it (no witness, video, etc.). *Solution:* Anticipate what evidence is needed to convince a neutral third party (the judge). Bring contracts, emails, texts, photos, witness statements – whatever makes your case credible. If it’s just a verbal deal, at least provide context or proof of payments or something. If you lack evidence on key elements (like you claim $5,000 in damages but have no estimates or bills to justify that amount), the judge may award much less or nothing. Remember, the judge doesn’t know either of you and wasn’t there – you must paint the picture effectively. Another evidence pitfall: not bringing the item in question when it’s feasible. Example: suing over a defective small appliance – bring the appliance if possible to show what’s wrong. Or at least photos of the defect. Don’t just rely on your description if physical evidence could be shown. That said, in small claims, judges try to get to the truth even with minimal evidence, but you severely handicap yourself without supporting documentation. 

- **Lack of preparation/organization:** Some cases get dismissed or delayed simply because the plaintiff is not organized or prepared. E.g., not knowing the legal basis for their claim, or not having an articulated amount. Sometimes plaintiffs sue and when the judge asks “How did you calculate this amount?”, they have no clear answer or they start changing the amount on the spot (which can’t exceed what they claimed in the filing). Or they forget crucial pieces of evidence at home. Or can’t find a document in the stack when asked and waste time shuffling. This makes a poor impression and can undermine your case. *Solution:* Prepare a brief outline or checklist of points you need to prove and evidence for each. Organize your exhibits in a folder or binder, labeled. Practice a short opening statement. Being prepared also means having copies of everything for the other side – failing to share your evidence can annoy the judge and opposing party; some judges might exclude evidence not shown to the other side when fairness demands (though small claims is informal). 

- **Venue issues:** Filing in the wrong county or court is a pitfall (discussed in Section 3). If you sue in a county with no connection to the defendant or dispute, the judge can dismiss for improper venue (or transfer if the defendant asks, but small claims usually dismisses and you re-file). *Example:* You live in County A, defendant in County B; you sue in A just for convenience even though nothing happened there. The defendant shows up and says “I don’t live here and the contract wasn’t signed here.” That likely gets your case tossed. *Solution:* Always file in a proper venue as per CCP § 116.370 (generally where defendant resides or where contract/accident occurred). 

- **Failure to appear or lateness:** If you as plaintiff don’t show up to court (or arrive very late), your case will be dismissed. Judges often call the calendar and if plaintiff isn’t present, they may dismiss (sometimes with a 10 minute grace or second call later). If you’re the defendant and don’t show, you risk default (as discussed). So not showing is a huge pitfall. *Solution:* Mark the date, time, address. Plan to arrive at least 30 minutes early (to park, go through security, find the room). If an emergency arises, notify the court *before* the hearing if possible and request a postponement. Simply not showing not only could lose the case but also forfeits your filing fee and time. 

- **Default judgment mistakes:** If you win by default, ensure you don’t leave any loose ends. Sometimes plaintiffs forget to file the proof of service or miscalculate costs, and the default judgment might not capture everything, or worse, might not be entered because of a clerical omission. Also, if the defendant had a change of address and didn’t actually get notice (but you thought they did), they can later vacate the default. Not exactly a “pitfall” on plaintiff’s side, but it can undo a win. *Solution:* Dot your i’s in default cases – make sure the service was solid, proof filed, and prepare a concise statement of claim at the default hearing so the judge has enough to award your claim. 

- **Collectability issues:** Winning a case that is uncollectable can feel like a pitfall. People sometimes pour effort into winning against essentially judgment-proof defendants (no money, no assets). That’s not a reason a judge will dismiss (the judge doesn’t consider collectability in ruling), but from a practical perspective it’s a pitfall for the plaintiff – you “win” but never see a dime, essentially wasting time. *Solution:* Before suing, try to gauge if the defendant has a job or property. If not, maybe a compromise or alternate approach is better, or at least be mentally prepared that you might have a hollow victory. 

- **Ignoring court instructions or deadlines:** If the court orders you to do something (like provide copies of evidence to the other side or file a brief by a date), do it. In some instances (though rare in small claims), a judge might ask parties to submit something after the hearing. Failing to do so can cause you to lose. 

- **Counter-suits and confusion:** If both parties sue each other (plaintiff’s claim and a defendant’s claim), sometimes plaintiffs get confused and fail to respond to the counterclaim. Remember, if you are sued back, you must defend against it or risk losing that portion by default. A plaintiff might show up and only talk about their claim, forgetting the defendant also filed one, and then present no defense to it. The judge could end up giving both money, or something odd. *Solution:* Treat a Defendant’s Claim as seriously as you expect them to treat yours. Prepare a defense, bring evidence to refute their allegations. 

- **Premature filing (not meeting demand or notice requirements):** While small claims doesn’t usually require a formal demand letter or waiting period (except government claims or special COVID rent cases etc.), if you file too hastily, it could reflect poorly. For example, suing the day after a breach without even asking the other side to fix the issue can make the judge wonder if you gave a fair chance. Also, some statutes have specific pre-filing steps (like CLRA 30-day notice in consumer fraud, etc. – though such complex statutory claims rarely come to small claims). *Solution:* Generally send a written demand and wait a reasonable short time (say 10-15 days) before suing, so you can honestly tell the judge you tried. If required (like government claim), obviously do that or your case gets tossed. 

- **Failing to bring the contract or law that applies:** If your case hinges on a written contract, not bringing it is a huge mistake. Or if a specific law is crucial (say you’re suing under a warranty law or a statute that provides damages), at least bring a printout of that statute or be able to state it. Judges appreciate when litigants are prepared with the relevant paperwork. If you’re claiming something like “I’m entitled to 2x my deposit because landlord acted in bad faith,” that’s from Civil Code 1950.5. If you know that, mention it and maybe have a copy. Many pro ses don’t do this, but it can help. Not having the lease or promissory note, etc., is a glaring omission. 

In summary: **Know your defendant, serve them properly, be timely, come prepared with evidence, and follow the law.** Common reasons small claims cases get dismissed are avoidable with a bit of diligence. Pay attention to detail – small claims might be informal, but it’s still law. One good practice is to observe a small claims calendar in your county before your hearing (some courts now stream them or you can sit in the courtroom) – you’ll quickly notice lots of these pitfalls happening (“case dismissed, no proof of service; next!”, “Sir, you sued the wrong company, case dismissed.”, etc.). Learn from others’ mistakes so you don’t repeat them. 

## 16. Recent changes  
Small claims procedures are largely stable, but there have been a few **important changes in the last 3-4 years** in California: 

- **Increased claim limits (2020s):** Effective **January 1, 2024**, the maximum claim amount for an individual in small claims raised from $10,000 to **$12,500** ([www.rygardnerlaw.com](https://www.rygardnerlaw.com/consumers-rejoice-small-claims-court-limits-are-raised#:~:text=As%20of%20January%201%2C%202024%2C,220)). This was a significant change brought by California Senate Bill 71 (2023), reflecting inflation adjustments. It’s the first increase in over a decade. Likewise, the cap for a business (entity) plaintiff rose from $5,000 to **$6,250** ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-or-limited-civil#:~:text=If%20you%20are%20an%20individual,there%20may%20be%20an%20advantage)). If you had outdated information, you might think the limits are $10k/$5k – but as of 2024 it’s $12.5k/$6.25k. Note that the rule that a business can’t sue a guarantor for more than $6,250 if the guarantor charges a fee was also likely adjusted (guarantor limits in CCP §116.220(c) went up proportionally; e.g., fee-charging guarantor defendant limit became $8,125 for individuals ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CCP&sectionNum=116.220.#:~:text=,five%20dollars%20%28%248%2C125)), etc.). For most users, the key is: individuals can now claim more. However, the rule that individuals **cannot file more than two claims above $2,500 per year** remains in place ([www.sucorte.ca.gov](https://www.sucorte.ca.gov/small-claims/before-you-start#:~:text=,calendar%20year%20for%20over%20%242%2C500)) (that threshold was not raised to match inflation, interestingly). 

- **COVID-19 rental debt and small claims:** During the COVID-19 pandemic, the Legislature temporarily allowed landlords to use small claims court to recover unpaid residential rent that accrued due to COVID hardship, **regardless of amount** (normally eviction-related rent debt over the limit wouldn’t be in small claims). This was authorized by emergency legislation (like AB 832). There were also **earlier deadlines**: landlords had to wait until November 1, 2021, to file such cases, and small claims had jurisdiction even if the amount exceeded $10k. This was an extraordinary measure to address the wave of back rent. However, that provision **sunset on October 1, 2025** ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-or-limited-civil#:~:text=)). As of Oct 2025, a landlord can no longer sue for COVID rental debt above the normal limits in small claims. If they still have such debt, they must go to regular civil court (or keep it within $12,500). So small claims is back to normal for rent cases. But during 2022-2024, many small claims courts saw very large rent cases (e.g., $20k, $30k) under those special rules. Now, post-2025, that’s done. In fact, many courts, like the CA Courts Self-Help, explicitly note: “IMPORTANT: As of 10/1/2025, a landlord can no longer sue in Small Claims court for unpaid COVID-19 rental debt above $12,500” ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-or-limited-civil#:~:text=for%20unpaid%20COVID,19)). So that was a recent change/temporary rule that has ended. 

- **Wage garnishment protections (2023):** A major change effective **September 1, 2023**, via SB 1477 (2022), altered how much of a debtor’s wages can be garnished for a judgment. The old federal baseline (25% or amount over 40x fed min wage) was replaced in CA with a more debtor-friendly formula: now only **20% of disposable earnings** can be taken, or the amount above **48 times the state (or local) minimum wage** per week, whichever is less ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/ca/code-of-civil-procedure/ccp-sect-706-050/#:~:text=,disposable%20earnings%20for%20that%20week)). This effectively means judgment debtors keep more of their paycheck. For example, at $18/hr min wage, 48x = $864/week protected; only 40% of earnings above that can be garnished, capped at 20% overall. This new law reduces garnishment amounts especially for lower and middle-income earners. It applies to Earnings Withholding Orders issued on or after Sep 1, 2023 ([www.ftb.ca.gov](https://www.ftb.ca.gov/about-ftb/newsroom/public-service-bulletins/2023-17.html#:~:text=subject%20to%20levy%20shall%20not,just%20at%20a%20reduced%20percentage)), so it affects small claims judgment enforcement now. As a creditor, you might collect slower; as a debtor, you keep more income. Additionally, California’s consumer debt exemption statutes now automatically protect more money in bank accounts – e.g., about $1,788 is automatically exempt for a single debtor’s deposit account (as of 2023; this adjusts). These were attempts to prevent pushing debtors into poverty. So if you’re enforcing a judgment, be aware of these numbers (the court or sheriff will apply them, but it’s good to know why you might get less from a garnishment than expected). 

- **Interest on judgments for consumer/medical debt (2023):** Assembly Bill 177 (2020) and related legislation reduced the interest rate on some judgments: If the judgment is on a claim “based on consumer debt” (essentially personal, family, or household purpose debt, like credit card or medical bills) under $50,000, the interest rate on that judgment is now **5%** per year, not 10%. Also, those judgments can only be renewed once (so max 15 years enforceability: original 10 + one 5-year renewal) ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/judgment-renewals-and-interest-rates#:~:text=Typically%2C%20a%20judgment%20can%20be,interest%20rate)). This took effect for judgments entered from Jan 1, 2023 onward. Many small claims involve consumer debt (e.g., a bank suing for credit card debt or a hospital for medical bills). Creditors in those cases have to live with 5% interest. The court self-help site explicitly notes: some judgments for medical expenses or personal debt have only 5% interest and one renewal ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/judgment-renewals-and-interest-rates#:~:text=Typically%2C%20a%20judgment%20can%20be,interest%20rate)). Thus, if you have, say, a small claims judgment for an unpaid hospital bill, expect 5% interest (the court may automatically designate it if applicable). For typical tort or contract disputes not primarily consumer debt, the 10% and unlimited renewals still apply ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/judgment-renewals-and-interest-rates#:~:text=Generally%2C%20renewals%20last%2010%20years,but%20there%20are%20some%20exceptions)). But distinguishing can be tricky; “consumer debt” is defined in Civil Code § 1788.2. If you’re a judgment creditor, check if this applies. This change aims to prevent interest from doubling consumer debts over time. 

- **Remote hearings and court operations post-COVID:** During 2020-2021, courts switched to remote hearings for small claims (via Zoom or phone). As of 2022-2023, most have returned to in-person hearings by default. However, **some courts now offer a hybrid option** or easier process for requesting remote appearance. There’s actually a California Rule of Court (Emergency Rule 11, later standard rules) permitting remote appearances in small claims upon request. The law AB 716 (2021) is bringing more remote access. So, recent change: you might be able to appear by videoconference if you have hardship getting to court, if your court has the setup and grants it. Each county differs: e.g., Santa Clara still has many remote small claims calendars; LA mostly in-person now but might allow by request; Orange uses in-person trials. Check local court announcements. So, the “recent change” is fluid: the pandemic forced modernization, and some of those practices will remain. For example, e-filing expanded (as mentioned, Sac, Orange, etc.). Courts also often post hearing calendars online—some now livestream small claims (LA’s are not usually streamed, but some smaller counties might stream YouTube for public access). So expect continued tech upgrades. 

- **Filing fee adjustments:** Fees generally change on a uniform schedule (some increase every few years slightly). For example, the “more than 12 filings” fee became $100 in 2010 and remains so. The standard $30/$50/$75 tiers have been stable for a while; they did not increase with the 2024 jurisdictional limit bump (the law did not raise the fee for the new max tier, it stays $75 for claims above $5,000 up to $12,500 ([www.lacourt.org](https://www.lacourt.org/page/SC0024#:~:text=,%2475.00))). However, be aware of **fee waivers** expansions: recent legislative changes made it easier for low-income litigants to get fee waivers, and most courts now have an online process to apply. Not exactly a “change” in law, but a change in practice: courts are strongly encouraged to grant fee waivers if the person receives needs-based benefits or falls under income guidelines. 

- **Interpreter provision:** Since 2015, but ramping up in later years, California has moved toward providing free interpreters in all civil cases (including small claims). By 2021, virtually all courts had funding to cover interpreters in small claims for all languages requested. Historically (pre-2014), small claims litigants had to bring their own interpreter (except for Spanish in some courts that provided it). Now it’s firmly a right: if you request an interpreter, the court will provide one at no cost. This was a phased change (mandated by AB 1657 and others). If someone was told years ago “small claims doesn’t provide interpreters, bring a friend,” that’s outdated. The recent reality: **free language access** is provided (though you should request in advance to ensure availability). 

- **Advisory services and online tools:** In the last few years, courts and legal aid have released more online “Smart FAQs,” chatbots, or self-help modules for small claims. For instance, some counties have an online interview that helps fill out forms (San Diego has one for the Plaintiff’s Claim). The state also created an online dispute resolution pilot in a few courts (e.g., Fresno and Butte counties had an ODR platform for small claims where parties could negotiate online and possibly settle before hearing). These pilots might expand, enabling litigants to resolve or at least exchange evidence electronically pre-trial. While not a legal change, it’s a procedural innovation that’s new. 

- **Legislation watch:** There aren’t many new bills affecting small claims jurisdiction currently pending (as of 2026). One could speculate future raises of limits to keep up with inflation (if 2024 was $12,500, perhaps in a decade they’ll bump to $15k etc.). Also, during housing crises, the legislature might again tweak rules about rental disputes in small claims (like they did for COVID). Another area: the limit on number of filings (2 over $2,500/year) has been criticized by serial litigants – no change yet, but one could watch if any bill tries to adjust that. So far, none has succeeded; the policy is to prevent professional litigants from abusing small claims. 

- **Small claims appeals of trial de novo by video:** The higher courts recently (2021) allowed small claims appeals to potentially be heard by remote means under certain local rules. So some appellate divisions might let an appellant or witness appear via Webex etc. This is part of broader remote integration. Not a huge change, but it’s making appeals slightly more accessible. 

To summarize the big recent changes: **higher claim limits (2024)** ([www.rygardnerlaw.com](https://www.rygardnerlaw.com/consumers-rejoice-small-claims-court-limits-are-raised#:~:text=As%20of%20January%201%2C%202024%2C,220)), **COVID rent debt allowances (2020-2025)** ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/small-claims-or-limited-civil#:~:text=)) (now expired), **greater protections for judgment debtors (2023)** in garnishments ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/ca/code-of-civil-procedure/ccp-sect-706-050/#:~:text=,disposable%20earnings%20for%20that%20week)) and interest ([selfhelp.courts.ca.gov](https://selfhelp.courts.ca.gov/judgment-renewals-and-interest-rates#:~:text=Typically%2C%20a%20judgment%20can%20be,interest%20rate)), and **technological shifts** (e-filing, remote hearings) accelerated by the pandemic. It’s always wise to check current statutes (Code of Civil Procedure § 116.210 et seq. for any amendments) and your local court’s website for “News” or “Changes in small claims procedure” to catch any new developments. The Judicial Council may also update forms to reflect changes (e.g., SC-104 info form updated with new service rules or SC-140 with updated appeal instructions). Keep an eye on revision dates on forms (the bottom left of forms shows revision; many were revised 1/1/2024 for the new limits). Using the latest forms ensures you get the benefit of recent changes.
