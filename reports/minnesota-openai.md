## 1. Jurisdictional Limits – Maximum Amount You Can Sue For

Minnesota’s small claims division (called **Conciliation Court**) can hear cases up to **$20,000** in amount (not including court fees) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,involves%20a%20consumer%20credit%20transaction)) ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=You%20can%20file%20a%20claim,file%20in%20Conciliation%20Court%3F%E2%80%9D%20below)). This $20,000 cap applies to *most* claims and most types of plaintiffs (individuals, sole proprietors, partnerships, companies, etc.). **Exception:** if the claim involves a “**consumer credit transaction**” – essentially a business suing a consumer over a personal debt – the maximum is **$4,000** ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,involves%20a%20consumer%20credit%20transaction)) ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=You%20can%20file%20a%20claim,file%20in%20Conciliation%20Court%3F%E2%80%9D%20below)). (A *consumer credit transaction* means a sale on credit or a loan for personal/household use, where the plaintiff is the original creditor ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=%28b%29%20,of%20personal%20property%2C%20in%20which)). This prevents lenders or retailers from using Conciliation Court for more than $4,000 on consumer debts. Notably, debt collection agencies (assignees) may not fall under that definition, but they still must abide by the $20,000 general limit and other rules.)

All types of plaintiffs – individuals, corporations, LLCs, sole proprietors, partnerships, and government entities – can use Conciliation Court up to the applicable dollar limit. **Minnesota does not impose a lower cap based on plaintiff type** (unlike some states). The key factor is the nature of the claim (consumer debt or not) rather than who is suing. Government agencies can sue in Conciliation Court for money owed to them (e.g. welfare overpayments) subject to the same limits ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,extent%20authorized%20in%20subdivision%205)).

Minnesota also does **not** limit the number of small claims cases you can file per year. You may file as many claims as you have, as long as each claim is within the dollar limit and not splitting one larger claim. **However, you cannot split a single claim into multiple cases** to evade the limit. For example, you cannot sue for $20,000 now and reserve $5,000 for later – if your total loss is $25,000, you’d have to either waive the excess and sue for $20,000, or file in regular District Court. Once you choose to reduce your claim to the conciliation limit, you **cannot later sue for the remainder** arising from the same events ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=fee%20to%20file%20in%20Conciliation,Court%3F%E2%80%9D%20below)) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=County%2C%20with%20some%20exceptions%20,to%20reach%20a%20higher%20amount)). The court will reject filings over $20,000 and will dismiss cases that appear to be improper claim-splitting.

> **Bottom line:** In Minnesota Conciliation Court, you can sue for up to $20,000 (raised from $15,000 as of August 1, 2024 ([zmattorneys.com](https://zmattorneys.com/small-claims-court-limit-raised/#:~:text=Conciliation%20court%2C%20also%20called%20%E2%80%9CSmall,3a))). The only lower cap is $4,000 for claims involving a consumer credit debt ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,involves%20a%20consumer%20credit%20transaction)). You cannot exceed these amounts or divide one larger dispute into smaller claims. There is no yearly filing limit on the number of cases, as long as each case stays within the monetary cap.

## 2. What Types of Cases Qualify — Everything You Can Sue For

**Minnesota Conciliation Court can handle almost any civil dispute for money or the return of personal property** within the jurisdictional limit ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,involves%20a%20consumer%20credit%20transaction)) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=The%20conciliation%20court%20does%20not,jurisdiction%20over%20the%20following%20actions)), **except for a handful of excluded matters** (covered later in this section). The guiding rule is that if you seek a **definite amount of money (or item of property)** as compensation for some harm or agreement, and it’s under the cap, you likely can use small claims. The process is informal and meant for ordinary disputes. Below is an exhaustive list of claim categories and how they are treated in Minnesota small claims, with examples and special rules:

### Contract & Money-Owed Disputes

- **Breach of Written Contract:** If someone fails to honor a written agreement – for example, a contractor doesn’t complete agreed work, or a buyer doesn’t pay for goods – you can sue for the financial loss. *Example:* You paid a contractor $5,000 upfront per a contract and they never did the work; you can demand your $5,000 back in Conciliation Court. Written contracts are enforceable in small claims as long as the damages are ≤ $20,000 ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,involves%20a%20consumer%20credit%20transaction)). (Large complex contract cases beyond that belong in regular court.) Ensure you bring a copy of the contract and any proof of the breach.
- **Breach of Oral/Handshake Agreement:** Oral contracts are generally valid in Minnesota (certain exceptions like real estate aside) and can be litigated in Conciliation Court. *Example:* Your friend agreed to sell you a car engine for $800 and took the money but never delivered the engine. You can sue for $800 on the oral contract. Be prepared with any texts, witnesses, or evidence showing the agreement, since nothing is written. The court will weigh credibility in absence of a writing.
- **Unpaid Personal Loans (Friends/Family IOUs):** Money you lent someone informally that wasn’t paid back is one of the most common small claims. If you can show the loan (even by personal check, Venmo record, or a signed note), you can sue for repayment. *Example:* You loaned your cousin $2,000 with a verbal promise to repay in 6 months, and it’s now a year with no payment – you can file a claim for $2,000 plus any small interest if agreed. Minnesota treats an action to recover a debt as a contract claim (typically a 6-year statute of limitations) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/1998/cite/541.07#:~:text=,remuneration%20for%20services%20or%20employment)).
- **Unpaid Promissory Notes:** If you have a promissory note (a written IOU) under $20k that’s in default, you may use Conciliation Court. The note serves as strong evidence of the debt. *Example:* A former employee signed a promissory note to repay a $3,000 advance and defaulted – you can sue on the note.
- **Unpaid Invoices or Accounts Receivable:** Small businesses frequently use Conciliation Court to recover unpaid invoices for goods delivered or services rendered. *Example:* A catering company billed a client $4,500 for an event per a contract, the client never paid – the company can sue for $4,500 in conciliation. These are treated as breach of contract or account stated claims.
- **Unpaid Rent (Residential or Commercial):** A landlord can sue a former tenant (or vice-versa) for unpaid rent or other money claims (though *eviction* itself must be handled in housing court, not conciliation). *Example:* A tenant moved out owing 2 months’ rent totaling $2,200; the landlord can file a small claim for that amount. Conversely, a tenant can sue a landlord who improperly kept prepaid last month’s rent. Note: Landlord-tenant money disputes are explicitly within conciliation jurisdiction ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=Subd)), and **venue** for these is special – it can be filed in the county where the rental property is located ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Common%20exceptions%20include%3A)) (even if the defendant now lives elsewhere).
- **Bounced/Dishonored Checks:** If someone paid you with a check that bounced, you can not only sue for the amount of the check, but Minnesota law also provides **civil penalties** for worthless checks. **After giving the legally required notice to the check writer** (a mailed notice of dishonor and 30-day opportunity to pay ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/1998/cite/332.50#:~:text=,holder%20of%20the%20check%20for))), you can seek the value of the check plus a service charge (up to $30) and potentially a penalty of up to **$100 (or the value of the check, whichever is greater)** ([caselaw.findlaw.com](https://caselaw.findlaw.com/us-8th-circuit/1365145.html#:~:text=Minnesota%27s%20worthless%20check%20statute%20provides,71%282%29%20%28emphasis)). *Example:* A customer wrote you a $250 check that bounced. You sent the statutory notice and 30 days passed without payment – in Conciliation Court you could sue for $250 + a $20 service fee + a $100 penalty under Minn. Stat. § 332.50 ([caselaw.findlaw.com](https://caselaw.findlaw.com/us-8th-circuit/1365145.html#:~:text=Minnesota%27s%20worthless%20check%20statute%20provides,71%282%29%20%28emphasis)). (If the check were $5,000 – within the $20k cap – the penalty could equal the check amount since it’s greater than $100.) **Important:** You *must* follow the notice procedure in Minn. Stat. § 332.50 by sending a certified letter citing the penalties and wait 30 days ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/1998/cite/332.50#:~:text=,holder%20of%20the%20check%20for)), otherwise the court might not award the statutory penalty.
- **Open Book Accounts/Running Tabs:** If you have an open account with someone (e.g. an ongoing tab at a store or a customer account) and they haven’t paid up, you can sue for the balance due. Treat it like an invoice claim – you’ll need an account statement or ledger as evidence.
- **Co-signer/Guarantor Reimbursement:** If you co-signed a loan or guaranteed a debt that the primary party defaulted on, and you had to pay, you can sue the primary borrower for what you paid. *Example:* You co-signed a $7,000 car loan; the other person stopped paying, and you ended up paying the bank $3,000. You can sue the co-signer for $3,000 in conciliation. This is essentially a claim of *indemnity* or reimbursement based on contract principles. Make sure to have proof of the payments you made on their behalf.

### Consumer & Service Disputes

- **Refunds for Defective Products:** If you bought a product that turned out to be defective or not as advertised, and the seller won’t refund or replace, you can sue for a refund (and possibly damages like repair costs). *Example:* You paid $1,200 for a used appliance that broke down immediately due to a hidden defect; the store refuses to refund – you can sue for the $1,200. Minnesota’s general consumer protection laws (like Minn. Stat. § 325F.69, the Consumer Fraud Act) allow consumers to recover actual damages caused by deceptive practices ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/325F.69#:~:text=,The%20act)), but in small claims you typically just ask for your money back or repair costs. Be prepared to show the item was defective through photos, expert repair estimates, etc. (Note: If the product had a manufacturer’s warranty, you should attempt warranty service first, but lack of success there can be explained to the court.)
- **Services Not Performed or Performed Poorly:** If you paid for services that were not provided at all, only partially provided, or done in a shoddy manner, you can sue for the amount you paid and any additional loss incurred. *Examples:* A photographer was paid $500 in advance for an event but never showed up – you can claim the $500. Or a home painter did a very poor job that will cost $1,000 to fix – you could sue for the cost of redo. Essentially, this is a breach of contract or failure of consideration claim. Bring your contracts, receipts, and any evidence of the poor work (photos of the bad paint job, etc.).
- **Auto Repair Overcharges or Shoddy Work:** Minnesota car owners frequently bring claims for auto repair issues – e.g., being charged for repairs not actually done, or damage caused by a mechanic. If a mechanic’s negligence or breach of estimate caused you loss, you can sue. *Example:* A mechanic charged $800 for a brake job that was either not done or done improperly, resulting in brake failure and damage – you could seek the $800 plus any related damage (like towing costs). Minnesota’s consumer protection statutes require that repair shops provide written estimates on request and not exceed them without consent (Minn. Stat. § 325F.56); violation can support your claim. Document conversations, keep invoices, and if possible, have a second mechanic’s report to show shoddy work.
- **Home Repair/Contractor Disputes:** Home improvement contractors are often defendants in conciliation court for incomplete or defective work. If the amount is within $20k, you can sue a contractor for the cost to fix defects or to recover payments for unfinished work. *Example:* You paid a contractor $10,000 to remodel a bathroom; they did half the work then disappeared – you could sue for the value of the unfinished work (say $5,000 if that’s what it would cost you to hire someone to finish). Minnesota law requires licensed contractors for many jobs and has a home warranty law for new homes (Minn. Stat. § 327A, not directly enforced in conciliation but provides standards), but there is no requirement that you must, for example, go through an arbitration or recovery fund first, unless you choose to involve the Contractor’s Recovery Fund for licensed contractors. Still, sending a **demand letter** and giving the contractor a chance to cure the problem is wise (and if the contractor is licensed, you might later need that documentation for any state recovery fund claim). You can also report issues to the Minnesota Department of Labor and Industry, but that doesn’t bar you from using small claims.
- **Appliance/Electronics Warranty Failures:** If a store or manufacturer isn’t honoring a warranty on a product and you had to pay for repairs or got a lemon, you can sue for those costs. *Example:* Your $1,500 TV failed under a 1-year warranty, but the manufacturer won’t fix or replace it – you could sue either the manufacturer or retailer for the cost of a replacement or repair. (Often, manufacturer warranty issues in small claims can be tricky if the manufacturer is out-of-state – but if they do business in Minnesota, you can get jurisdiction. Always check if the warranty had an arbitration clause; if so, that might complicate your ability to sue in court.)
- **Canceled or No-Show Service Providers:** If a service provider (caterer, DJ, wedding venue, movers, etc.) breached an agreement by canceling last-minute or simply not showing up, causing you financial loss (like extra costs to find a replacement or a lost deposit), you can sue for those losses. *Example:* You paid a DJ a $300 deposit for your party and they never showed – you can claim the $300 and any additional expenses you incurred due to their no-show. Provide the contract and evidence of their absence/cancellation.
- **Property Loss/Damage in Custody:** If you entrusted your property to someone (dry cleaner, repair shop, storage facility, valet, hotel, etc.) and it was lost, damaged, or stolen due to their negligence, you can sue for the value. *Example:* A dry cleaner ruined your dress and offers no compensation – you can sue for the dress’s value or cleaning cost. Or a garage that was supposed to store your classic car left it unlocked and it was vandalized – you could pursue the damage costs. Under Minnesota law, bailees (those holding others’ property) have a duty of care, and if they fail, conciliation is appropriate for resulting damages.
- **Gym/Club Membership Refunds:** If you properly canceled a gym, spa, or other membership under the contract terms or state law and the company won’t refund dues, you can use small claims. Minnesota’s consumer laws (Minn. Stat. § 325G.06-.12) give cancellation rights for gym and spa contracts. *Example:* You prepaid $900 for a year gym membership, canceled after 3 months per contract, but they refuse refund for the unused period – you could sue for the pro-rata unused amount.
- **Auto Dealer/Used-Car Disputes:** While new-car “lemon law” cases often exceed small claims (and have special arbitration requirements), **used car sales** frequently end up in conciliation. Minnesota has a Used Car Warranty Law (Minn. Stat. § 325F.662) requiring dealers to provide a basic warranty on used cars depending on mileage ([www.ag.state.mn.us](https://www.ag.state.mn.us/consumer/handbooks/mncarlaws/CH2.asp#:~:text=Minnesota%20has%20one%20of%20the,listed%20below)) ([www.ag.state.mn.us](https://www.ag.state.mn.us/consumer/handbooks/mncarlaws/CH2.asp#:~:text=The%20used%20car%20warranty%20law,the%20time%20you%20buy%20it)). If a dealer refuses to honor that warranty or lied about the condition of a used car, you can sue for repair costs or a partial refund. *Example:* A dealer sold you a car advertised as never having been in an accident, but you later discover frame damage – you could claim the diminished value or cancellation of sale (though note, you can only get money in conciliation, not force them to take back the car, so you’d quantify your loss in dollars). Also, rolling back odometers or concealing salvage title is illegal (Minn. Stat. §§ 325E.14, 325F.664), and you can sue for fraud. These cases often involve claims of misrepresentation. Bring any “Buyers Guide” window sticker (federally required), advertisements, and mechanic’s inspection reports.
- **False Advertising/Deceptive Trade Practices:** If a business’s false statements or deceptive practices caused you a financial loss, you can sue for the actual damages. Minnesota’s consumer protection statutes (like the Prevention of Consumer Fraud Act and Uniform Deceptive Trade Practices) allow private lawsuits for damages via Minn. Stat. § 8.31 subd.3a (the “private attorney general” statute) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/325F.69#:~:text=,The%20act)). In Conciliation Court, you would claim the dollar loss directly caused by the deception. *Example:* A travel agency falsely advertised a package as “all-inclusive” for $3,000, but upon arrival you were charged an extra $800 in mandatory fees – you could sue the agency for $800 in damages from the false advertising. Note that some statutes provide for **treble damages or attorney fees** (e.g., certain consumer fraud cases), but a conciliation court judge may or may not award multiples beyond the out-of-pocket loss. Generally, expect to recover your actual loss; statutory add-ons like multiple damages are possible only if clearly allowed and provable within the small claims context (and within the $20k cap).

### Property Damage

- **Vehicle Collision Damage:** If your car was damaged in a motor vehicle accident and the amount not paid by insurance is within $20,000, you can sue the at-fault driver for those damages. This includes your deductible or any uninsured losses. *Example:* Another driver sideswiped your car causing $4,500 in damage, and their insurance only paid $3,500; you can sue the driver in conciliation for the remaining $1,000 (or the full $4,500 if insurance denied the claim). You’ll need repair estimates or bills and evidence of fault (police report, witness statements, photos). *Important:* If the other driver was uninsured and you obtain a judgment, Minnesota law allows you to request their driver’s license suspension if the judgment isn’t paid within 30 days ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=risk%20losing%20their%20license,contact%20the%C2%A0MN%20Driver%20and%20Vehicle)). The license can be suspended until they pay or work out a payment plan.
- **Damage to Personal Property:** Any situation where someone damages your belongings – whether through negligence or intentional acts – can be brought to small claims as long as the value is within the limit. *Example:* Your neighbor accidentally dropped a tool from their balcony onto your $1,200 grill, destroying it – you can sue for $1,200. Or if a guest spilled red wine on your expensive rug and won’t pay for cleaning, you can claim the cleaning or replacement cost. Evidence can include photos of the damage and receipts or appraisals for the item’s value.
- **Tenant Damage to Landlord’s Property:** A landlord can use conciliation court to recover for property damage a tenant caused to a rental unit (beyond normal wear and tear) after the tenant moves out. *Example:* A tenant punched holes in walls and left extensive damage costing $5,000 to repair – the landlord can sue that tenant for $5,000. It’s common to also apply the security deposit to such damage first and then sue for the balance. (These claims often accompany or follow a security deposit dispute.)
- **Landlord Damage to Tenant’s Property:** Conversely, if a landlord’s negligence caused damage to a tenant’s personal property, the tenant can sue. *Example:* The landlord failed to fix a known leak; your furniture got water-damaged ($2,000 loss) – you could sue for that amount, potentially citing breach of the covenant to maintain premises. Note that claims related to habitability might also fit under tenant remedies in housing court, but pure money claims for property are fine in conciliation.
- **Tree/Fence/Boundary Damage:** Disputes between neighbors over property damage, such as a neighbor’s tree falling on your fence or their construction damaging your yard, are classic small claims. *Example:* A neighbor’s tree limb fell and crushed your shed ($3,500 damage) – you can sue the neighbor for the cost of repairs, especially if they knew the tree was hazardous. Bring photos, repair estimates, and any prior communications about the issue. Minnesota follows certain rules on tree law (generally, you can trim branches to the property line, etc.), but if their negligence led to damage, conciliation is appropriate.
- **Pet or Animal Damage:** If someone’s pet causes damage – e.g., their dog chews up your expensive shoes or injures your pet – you can seek compensation. *Example:* A neighbor’s dog got into your yard and destroyed $500 worth of landscaping, or bit your dog leading to $800 vet bills – such monetary losses can be claimed. (In Minnesota, dog owners are strictly liable for bites to persons under the dog bite statute, but for dog-on-dog injuries or property damage, you proceed under negligence or strict liability if applicable.)
- **Damage During a Move or by Hired Worker:** If movers you hired damaged your furniture or walls, or a handyman accidentally broke something, you can sue for the repair or replacement cost. Many moving companies have arbitration clauses, but if not or you opt out, small claims works. Provide photos of damage and any written contract terms (some moving companies try to limit liability – the court will consider those terms but can still award the actual damage up to the cap).

### Landlord–Tenant (Financial Claims Only)

*(Note: Eviction actions (unlawful detainer) are **not** handled in Conciliation Court  – those go to housing court or district court. But **money disputes** arising from tenancies can be brought in Conciliation Court.)*

- **Unreturned Security Deposits:** This is one of the most common small claims. If you’re a tenant and your landlord failed to return your security deposit without a valid reason, you can sue for the deposit and potentially penalties. Under Minnesota law, a landlord must return a residential security deposit (with interest) or send an itemized deduction statement within **3 weeks (21 days)** after tenancy ends, provided the tenant gave a forwarding address ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/504b.178#:~:text=)). If the landlord violates this, the tenant can recover not only the deposit but also a **penalty equal to the amount wrongfully withheld** (in effect, double the deposit) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/?id=504B.178#:~:text=,when%20requested%20by%20the%20tenant)). If the withholding was in “bad faith,” the court **may add up to $500** in punitive damages ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/504b.178#:~:text=The%20bad%20faith%20retention%20by,the%20recovery%20of%20the%20deposit)). *Example:* You paid a $1,000 deposit. You left the unit in good condition and gave your new address, but 21 days passed with no refund or letter. In conciliation, you can claim $1,000 (deposit) + $1,000 (penalty) = $2,000, and if evidence shows the landlord had no basis at all (bad faith), ask for up to $500 more ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/504b.178#:~:text=The%20bad%20faith%20retention%20by,the%20recovery%20of%20the%20deposit)). Bring evidence of the unit’s condition (photos, check-out list) and the date you provided your address. **Important:** If the landlord provided an itemized list of damages within 21 days, you can still dispute those deductions in court – the judge will decide if they were valid. If not valid and thus “wrongfully withheld,” the double-damages rule applies ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/?id=504B.178#:~:text=after%20receipt%20of%20the%20tenant%27s,the%20landlord%20and%20interest%20thereon)). *Statute:* Minn. Stat. § 504B.178 governs deposits, and Conciliation Court is explicitly allowed to handle these claims ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=Subd)).
- **Return of Prepaid Rent:** If you had paid last month’s rent in advance or other pre-paid rent and move out early or properly terminated the lease, but the landlord does not return the unused portion, you can sue for that amount. Minnesota law forbids landlords from withholding last month’s rent in lieu of the security deposit rules ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/504b.178#:~:text=2025%20Minnesota%20Statutes%20,which%20is%20to%20secure%20the)) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/504b.178#:~:text=provisions%20of%20subdivision%203%20or,the%20recovery%20of%20the%20deposit)) (they can’t just keep both). *Example:* You gave first and last month’s rent at move-in. You properly gave notice and moved, and the landlord re-rented the unit immediately, effectively getting double rent for that last month – you can claim your prepaid last month’s rent back since the landlord had no loss.
- **Habitability Repairs / Rent Abatement (Tenant Counterclaims):** While serious habitability issues (lack of heat, etc.) are often addressed via “rent escrow” or tenant remedies actions in housing court, a tenant can raise related money claims in conciliation. For instance, if you paid for a necessary repair out-of-pocket because the landlord failed to, you can sue to be reimbursed under the “repair and deduct” law (Minn. Stat. § 504B.385). Or if conditions were so bad that the reasonable rental value was lower, you might seek a partial rent refund (though judges may find that complex for conciliation unless there’s a clear basis). *Example:* Your landlord refused to fix a broken furnace for two weeks in winter, so you paid $400 for an emergency repair – you could sue for $400. Document that you gave the landlord notice and reasonable time as required before repairing yourself.
- **Former Landlord’s Claims for Unpaid Rent or Damages:** A landlord can sue a former tenant for unpaid rent, late fees, or property damage (beyond normal wear) after move-out. These are typically straightforward contract damage claims. *Example:* The tenant skipped out owing $800 rent and $300 in late fees, and caused $1,000 damage to carpets beyond deposit – the landlord could file one claim for $2,100. It’s wise for the landlord to itemize each category in the claim. They should also show they complied with the deposit law if a deposit was involved, because failure to follow it can nullify some damage claims or even expose the landlord to the penalties described above.
- **Wrongful Retention of Tenant’s Property:** If a landlord improperly withholds a tenant’s personal property after an eviction or move-out (e.g., locks the tenant out and keeps their belongings), the tenant can sue for the value of the property. Minnesota law requires landlords to store and make reasonably available a tenant’s left-behind property for a time (Minn. Stat. § 504B.271). *Example:* After you moved, the landlord kept your TV and furniture claiming you owed rent (a “landlord’s lien” is not generally allowed) – you could sue for the return or value of those items. Conciliation Court can **order the return of personal property** itself (not just money) if the items are worth ≤ $20k ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/2022/cite/491A/full#:~:text=If%20the%20controversy%20concerns%20the,entry%20upon%20the%20premises%20for)). The court’s judgment can direct the landlord to give back the property, and the sheriff can enforce that ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/2022/cite/491A/full#:~:text=If%20the%20controversy%20concerns%20the,entry%20upon%20the%20premises%20for)).
- **Disputes Over Fees and Charges:** Small claims can handle various fee disputes: cleaning fees, late payment fees, pet fees, key replacement charges, etc. Often the question is whether those charges were lawfully applied under the lease or statute. *Example:* A landlord charges a tenant $300 for “cleaning” despite the unit being left clean – the tenant can sue to get that $300 back if unwarranted. The court will look at lease terms and reasonableness. (Unreasonable punitive fees might be deemed an unenforceable penalty.)

### Employment & Wage Claims

- **Unpaid Wages or Final Paycheck:** If your employer didn’t pay wages you earned, you can absolutely use Conciliation Court (regardless of amount, as long as it’s ≤ $20k, which covers most individual wage claims). Minnesota law requires prompt payment of wages at separation – if you were fired, final wages are due within **24 hours of your demand** ([www.dli.mn.gov](https://www.dli.mn.gov/business/employment-practices/payment-hours-worked#:~:text=,24%20hours%20of%20demand%3B%20and)); if you quit, by the next regular payday (not beyond 20 days) ([www.dli.mn.gov](https://www.dli.mn.gov/business/employment-practices/payment-hours-worked#:~:text=,24%20hours%20of%20demand%3B%20and)). If the employer doesn’t pay on time *after* you demanded, you can recover not only the wages but also a **waiting-time penalty**: an additional day’s wages for each day payment was late, up to 15 days ([www.casemine.com](https://www.casemine.com/act/us/640db853b9c46b56ccb214da#:~:text=policy%2C%20contract%2C%20or%20other%20legal,settlement%2C%20satisfactory%20to%20the%20discharged)) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/181.14/version/2023-11-11%2009%3A10%3A13%2B00%3A00#:~:text=Wages%20or%20commissions%20not%20paid,An%20employee%27s%20demand)). *Example:* You were terminated and your last paycheck of $1,000 was 10 days late despite written demand – you can sue for $1,000 + $**(10 days of your average daily wage)** as a penalty under Minn. Stat. §§ 181.13–.14 ([www.casemine.com](https://www.casemine.com/act/us/640db853b9c46b56ccb214da#:~:text=policy%2C%20contract%2C%20or%20other%20legal,settlement%2C%20satisfactory%20to%20the%20discharged)) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/181.14/version/2023-11-11%2009%3A10%3A13%2B00%3A00#:~:text=Wages%20or%20commissions%20not%20paid,An%20employee%27s%20demand)). The law also mandates the court award you your attorney’s fees if you win (though in conciliation, you likely have no attorney, so just court costs). You should file a wage claim with the Minnesota Department of Labor & Industry as well (they can help mediate or enforce) ([dli.mn.gov](https://dli.mn.gov/wage-claim#:~:text=If%20you%20haven%27t%20received%20the,claim%20and%20recover%20unpaid%20wages)), but you are not required to – you can go straight to court if you prefer. Conciliation Court is often faster for a one-time wage issue.
- **Unpaid Overtime, Minimum Wage, Commissions, or Bonuses:** Any earned compensation that was not paid can be claimed. *Example:* Your sales job promised a $5,000 bonus if you hit targets, you did but the company never paid it – you can sue for that $5,000. Or if you suspect your employer failed to pay overtime properly (violating state/federal wage laws), you can claim the unpaid overtime. Minnesota follows the federal FLSA framework; if willful non-payment, you could get 2x damages or more, but proving willfulness for extra damages might be complex for conciliation. Usually you claim the straight unpaid amount (and note any law allowing doubling). Keep in mind, the Department of Labor or an attorney can pursue statutory liquidated damages, but small claims tends to focus on actual earnings owed.
- **Unreimbursed Business Expenses:** If you as an employee paid for work-related expenses (mileage, equipment, travel) that your employer agreed to reimburse and did not, you can sue for those amounts as a contract claim. *Example:* You spent $600 on supplies for work with the promise of reimbursement under company policy, and they never paid you back – that’s suitable for small claims.
- **Misclassification/Overtime or Break Pay Issues:** While broad “employee misclassification” (e.g., independent contractor vs employee) disputes can involve legal questions beyond small claims, you can still claim the *money* you believe you’re owed due to misclassification – e.g., unpaid overtime or vacation that you would have gotten if properly classified. The court will consider the underlying issue in order to decide if you were owed that money. If the amounts are small, conciliation may handle it, but if it implicates complex employment law, it might get tricky. Often it’s better to involve the Minnesota **Labor Standards** unit or an employment lawyer for such cases. Still, for a straightforward claim like “I worked 50 hours per week but was salaried without overtime even though I should get overtime – here’s the extra 10 hours/week for X weeks at my rate,” you can attempt to recover those wages in Conciliation.
- **When the Department of Labor is a Better Forum:** Minnesota’s Department of Labor & Industry (DLI) Labor Standards Division will take wage claims (generally for amounts up to $15,000) and attempt to collect from the employer without you needing to go to court ([dli.mn.gov](https://dli.mn.gov/wage-claim#:~:text=If%20you%20haven%27t%20received%20the,claim%20and%20recover%20unpaid%20wages)) ([dli.mn.gov](https://dli.mn.gov/wage-claim#:~:text=How%20to%20file%20a%20wage,claim)). This is often free and effective, especially if multiple employees are affected or if you prefer not to confront the employer in court. If you file with DLI, they may resolve it or refer it to the Attorney General for enforcement. You *can* still choose Conciliation Court for a faster, direct resolution or if DLI’s process isn’t yielding results. Be aware that some specific claims like **workers’ compensation** or certain union-related wage claims are handled exclusively in other systems (workers’ comp court, NLRB, etc., not small claims).

### Personal Services & Professional Services Disputes

- **Hairdressers/Barbers/Nail Salons (Botched Services):** If a salon service goes awry and causes damage (like hair damage requiring correction, or a nail technician causing an infection, etc.), you can sue for the cost of fixing the issue or related medical bills. *Example:* A hair treatment at a salon burned your scalp and you had to pay $300 for a doctor and corrective products – you could claim those costs. Keep in mind the challenge of proving the negligence; photos of the damage and maybe a statement from a different stylist or doctor would help.
- **Tutors, Lessons, Coaches Not Delivered:** If you paid for classes or lessons (music teacher, tutor, personal trainer sessions) and the person didn’t provide them or provided far fewer than promised, you can seek a refund for the undelivered portion. *Example:* You paid a tutor $500 for 10 sessions, but they only did 4 and then quit – you could claim the value of the remaining 6 sessions ($300). A written agreement or receipt helps, but even texts scheduling sessions can be evidence.
- **Pet Boarding/Grooming/Training Disputes:** If you incurred veterinary bills or losses due to a pet service provider’s negligence (e.g., kennel lost your dog, groomer injured your pet, trainer didn’t fulfill guaranteed results if such guarantee was in contract), you can sue for related costs. *Example:* A dog groomer accidentally cut your dog, leading to a $200 vet bill – you can claim that $200. Pets are considered property in law, so your damages are typically the economic costs (vet bills, etc.), not “emotional” damages.
- **Medical/Dental Billing Disputes (non-insurance):** If you were overcharged or charged for services not provided, and it’s a straightforward billing issue, you can use conciliation. *Example:* A dentist charged you for a crown they never actually put in – you can sue for that amount. However, if you are alleging **malpractice** (negligence in treatment causing injury), note that *medical malpractice claims are explicitly excluded from Conciliation Court jurisdiction* ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,by%20libel%20or%20slander)) because they typically require expert testimony and exceed the limit. For small amounts of improper billing, though, it’s okay. Also, if the dispute is about whether insurance should cover something, that might be beyond small claims – conciliation won’t resolve coverage issues with an insurer (and insurers often aren’t proper defendants unless it’s clearly a straightforward reimbursement or subrogation matter).
- **Daycare/School Tuition Refunds:** If you prepaid for a service like daycare, preschool, or a camp and it was closed or you withdrew properly but didn’t get a refund per agreement, you can sue for the unused portion. *Example:* You paid $1,000 for a summer camp, but it was canceled and they haven’t refunded you – this is a valid small claim. Attach any enrollment agreement or cancellation policy to show you’re entitled to the refund.

### Loans, Debts, and Financial Disputes (Between Individuals)

- **Money Lent to Friends/Family:** As noted earlier, if you loaned personal funds and weren’t repaid, this is a classic small claim. It doesn’t matter that there was no formal contract – your testimony, any notes, emails, or proof of payment to the borrower can establish the loan. If there was an agreement on interest, you can claim interest too (pre-judgment interest on such liquidated sums is allowed at the statutory rate, which is typically around 4% per year ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/549.09#:~:text=,to%20the%20judgment%20or%20award)) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/549.09#:~:text=,on%20the%20judgment%20or%20award)) unless a different rate was agreed).
- **Shared Expenses Between Roommates or Ex-Partners:** Disentangling finances after a roommate moves out or a romantic relationship ends is often done in small claims. *Examples:* You and a roommate each paid a security deposit and one got the whole refund – you can sue for your share. Or an ex-couple jointly bought furniture, one kept it all and didn’t compensate the other – the other can sue for half the value. Another scenario is one person paying all the rent or utilities for a period expecting contribution from the other – you can claim that contribution if an agreement (express or implied) existed. Provide any informal written understandings (texts like “I’ll pay you back next month” can be gold) or demonstrate a pattern of splitting costs that was breached.
- **Wedding/Engagement Gifts and Rings:** Disputes over who keeps an engagement ring or expensive gifts after a wedding is called off can be filed in Conciliation Court (these are typically emotional but legally about property ownership). Minnesota law has precedents for engagement rings: generally the ring is considered a conditional gift – if the engagement is broken off, courts often require the ring to be returned to the giver *if* the giver was not at fault for the breakup ([www.martinwagnerlaw.com](https://www.martinwagnerlaw.com/news-events/45-family-law/185-who-keeps-the-engagement-ring-in-mn#:~:text=Who%20Gets%20the%20Engagement%20Ring,the%20Engagement%20Ring%20Depends%20Minnesota)). In small claims, you can only demand **money** or the **return of the ring** (as property). So a jilted fiancé could sue for the ring or its value. The judge will decide based on equity/fault. It’s tricky but within conciliation jurisdiction (the ring value must be ≤ $20k; many are).
- **Jointly Titled Property After Breakup:** If you and someone else jointly own property (a car, furniture, etc.) and split up, Conciliation Court can decide the **division of personal property valued ≤ $20,000** ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/2022/cite/491A/full#:~:text=If%20the%20controversy%20concerns%20the,entry%20upon%20the%20premises%20for)). The statute actually empowers the court to determine ownership or possession and *order a party to deliver the property* to the other ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/2022/cite/491A/full#:~:text=If%20the%20controversy%20concerns%20the,entry%20upon%20the%20premises%20for)). *Example:* You and your ex bought a $5,000 boat together (both names on title). After separating, the boat is in your ex’s possession and they refuse to share or sell. You could sue for a determination that the boat should be yours (maybe because you paid most of it) or force a sale and split. The court could order the boat turned over to you, or if it’s been sold, order your ex to pay you your share. (Real estate and titled real property is excluded, but personal property like vehicles, boats are fine up to $20k value.)
- **Return of Deposits/Earnest Money:** If you put down a cash deposit in any transaction (for example, earnest money on a home purchase that fell through, a deposit on a rental or a product) and you believe you’re entitled to get it back, you can pursue that. *Example:* You gave a $2,000 earnest money check in a home purchase and properly canceled within the contingency period, but the seller or broker won’t release the money – small claims can handle that $2,000. (Purchase agreement disputes can sometimes be complex, but for a straightforward refund of earnest money per contract terms, conciliation is a quick route.)
- **Creditors Suing Consumers:** Banks or collection agencies *can* file in Conciliation Court for smaller debts (credit card balances, etc.), but if it’s a consumer credit transaction, they’re capped at $4,000 as noted ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,involves%20a%20consumer%20credit%20transaction)). Minnesota *does not* prohibit collection agencies or assignees from using Conciliation Court, but many large creditors sue in regular court especially if amounts are higher. If you are a debtor being sued in conciliation by a debt collector for, say, $3,500, know that the case is allowed there. (If a creditor tries to sue for more than $4,000 on a consumer purchase loan, you should point out in court that the claim “involves a consumer credit transaction” and exceeds the special $4,000 limit – the court must dismiss the amount above $4k ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,involves%20a%20consumer%20credit%20transaction)).) **Note:** If the debt was sold to an assignee, check if the assignee meets the definition of “claimant is the seller or lender” in the law; if not, arguably the $4k limit might not apply, but this is a legal gray area. Regardless, the general $20k limit always applies.
  
### Vehicle-Specific Claims

- **Accident Damage (Not Covered by Insurance):** As mentioned, you can sue an at-fault driver for your **uninsured losses** from a car accident. Ensure jurisdiction by filing in the county where the accident happened or where the defendant lives. Minnesota has no-fault insurance thresholds, but property damage claims are not under no-fault – you can directly sue for vehicle damage. If you already got partial insurance payout, sue only for the remaining loss (or your deductible). If your car was totaled and the insurance low-balled the value, you *could* sue the other driver for the difference, but that assumes the other driver was at fault and not fully covered – often if insurance was involved and accepted by you, you’re done. However, if the other driver had no insurance, you might have a default judgment scenario – conciliation is a way to get a judgment which you can use to suspend their license ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=risk%20losing%20their%20license,contact%20the%C2%A0MN%20Driver%20and%20Vehicle)) as leverage for payment.
- **Mechanic/Body Shop Disputes:** Claims against auto repair shops include both **negligence** (they damaged your vehicle or performed faulty repair causing harm) and **breach of contract** (they charged for work not done or violated an estimate). *Example:* A body shop was paid $5,000 to repair an accident but left many issues unresolved and perhaps caused new damage – you can sue for the cost to have another shop fix the mistakes. Use any written estimate or invoice as evidence, and possibly a statement from a different mechanic about the poor quality. Minnesota’s Motor Vehicle Repair Act (Minn. Stat. §§ 325F.56-.66) might provide additional rights, such as requiring a written estimate and authorization for excess charges, which you can cite if the shop overcharged without permission.
- **Dealership Sales Disputes:** Aside from misrepresentation (addressed under consumer fraud above), there are specific statutory protections. Minnesota’s **Lemon Law** for new vehicles (Minn. Stat. § 325F.665) provides that if a new car has substantial defects and meets certain criteria, the manufacturer must replace or refund ([www.ag.state.mn.us](https://www.ag.state.mn.us/Consumer/Handbooks/MNCarLaws/CH1.asp#:~:text=The%20Manufacturer%E2%80%99s%20Duty%20to%20Refund,or%20Replace)) – but usually these involve higher dollar amounts and often manufacturers require arbitration first ([www.ag.state.mn.us](https://www.ag.state.mn.us/Consumer/Handbooks/MNCarLaws/CH1.asp#:~:text=The%20law%20has%20special%20refund,any%20one%20of%20the%20following)) ([www.ag.state.mn.us](https://www.ag.state.mn.us/Consumer/Handbooks/MNCarLaws/CH1.asp#:~:text=repair%20a%20car%E2%80%99s%20problem%20after,any%20one%20of%20the%20following)). However, if you fit a lemon law scenario and the refund amount is under $20k (maybe a cheap new motorcycle or so), you could use conciliation to seek the refund. More commonly, **used car** issues or ancillary charges are fought in small claims: e.g., a dealer failing to pay off a trade-in as promised (you could sue for resulting finance charges), or withholding license/registration fees improperly. If a dealer violated Minnesota’s Used Car Warranty Law (failed to repair a covered defect within the warranty period), you could sue for the cost of repair you had to shoulder ([www.ag.state.mn.us](https://www.ag.state.mn.us/consumer/handbooks/mncarlaws/CH2.asp#:~:text=Minnesota%20has%20one%20of%20the,listed%20below)) ([www.ag.state.mn.us](https://www.ag.state.mn.us/consumer/handbooks/mncarlaws/CH2.asp#:~:text=The%20used%20car%20warranty%20law,the%20time%20you%20buy%20it)).
- **Towing and Impound Fees:** If your car was towed or impounded improperly – say from private property without legal authorization or by a city in error – you can sue the towing company or possibly the entity that ordered the tow for a refund of fees or damages. Minnesota statutes regulate towing from private lots (requiring signs, etc.). *Example:* Your car was wrongly towed from your apartment despite your having a parking permit, costing you $300 – you can sue the towing company for that amount. Provide evidence like a valid permit or photos showing signage was inadequate. If a police impound was improper, typically you’d have an administrative appeal first; but for small amounts, court is an option if other remedies fail.
- **Parking Lot Damage (with known driver):** If your parked vehicle was hit by someone – and you know who (perhaps via a note or surveillance or witness) – you can sue that driver for the repair costs. Often these are simple property damage claims. *Example:* A coworker admitted to scraping your car in the lot but never paid the $500 for the paint repair – that’s a straightforward conciliation case.

### Personal Injury (Minor Cases)

Small claims can adjudicate **personal injury or small tort claims** as long as the claimed damages are within $20,000. In practice, many personal injury cases (car accidents with injuries, slip-and-fall injuries, etc.) involve more than $20k or complex medical evidence, so they go to regular court. But for **minor injuries** where medical bills + lost wages are within the limit, you can choose Conciliation Court. *Examples:* A minor dog bite that required a few doctor visits costing $1,500, or a slip on ice leading to a $5,000 ER bill and some missed work – these could be brought in conciliation. Minnesota law for injuries like dog bites imposes strict liability on the owner (you’d cite Minn. Stat. § 347.22, though conciliation judges know the basics). **Be aware**: You will need to prove the other party’s liability (except dog bites which don’t require proving fault, just that you were lawfully on premises and didn’t provoke). Bring medical bills, proof of payments, and ideally a short doctor’s note linking the injury to the incident, especially if there’s any gap or complexity. You cannot demand “pain and suffering” in a precise dollar unless you have a reasonable way to quantify it, and punitive damages are not awarded in conciliation for personal injury. Also note, **medical malpractice** claims (e.g., suing a doctor for an injury from treatment) are explicitly *not allowed* in Conciliation Court ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,by%20libel%20or%20slander)). Those must go to district court and have special requirements (expert affidavits, etc.). But a simple injury caused by a person or business’s negligence is fair game if the amount is small. The statute of limitations on personal injury in Minnesota is generally **2 years** ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/1998/cite/541.07#:~:text=,a%20physician%2C%20surgeon%2C%20dentist%2C%20occupational)), so don’t wait too long to file.

### Specific Statutory Claims and Special Situations

Minnesota law provides for certain statutory causes of action that can be pursued in small claims if the damages are within the limit:

- **Civil Theft or Bad Check Penalties:** We discussed bad checks (NSF) – you can get statutory service fees and penalties up to $100 or the check’s value ([caselaw.findlaw.com](https://caselaw.findlaw.com/us-8th-circuit/1365145.html#:~:text=Minnesota%27s%20worthless%20check%20statute%20provides,71%282%29%20%28emphasis)). There’s also a civil theft law (Minn. Stat. § 604.14) that allows victims of theft to sue for either $500 or twice the value of the stolen goods, whichever is greater, from the thief (plus attorney fees). That could be invoked in conciliation if, say, someone stole your property and was caught – you could sue for the statutory penalty. *Example:* Your employee stole $200 cash; you could sue for $500 (minimum under the statute) as a penalty. Be prepared with evidence (e.g., a police report or admission of theft).
- **Consumer Protection/Unlawful Practice Statutes:** If a specific Minnesota statute provides a set damage amount or multiple for violations (for instance, some **telemarketing** or **tenant law** violations have penalties), you can claim those in conciliation as long as you prove the violation. Minnesota’s **Uniform Consumer Protection Act** (like illegal cancellation fees, door-to-door sale cancellations, etc.) often allow suing for a refund and sometimes damages/fees. For example, Minnesota’s law on door-to-door sales (the “Cooling-Off” law) lets you cancel within 3 days – if the seller doesn’t honor that, you can sue for your money back. Another example: **Auto lemon law** – if you qualify, you could sue for the refund of the car’s price (usually over $20k for a new car, but maybe a smaller vehicle).
- **Lemon Law (New Vehicles):** Minnesota’s lemon law (Minn. Stat. § 325F.665) covers new motor vehicles that have substantial defects not fixed after a reasonable number of attempts. The law requires manufacturers to either replace the vehicle or refund the purchase price (less a usage fee) ([www.ag.state.mn.us](https://www.ag.state.mn.us/Consumer/Handbooks/MNCarLaws/CH1.asp#:~:text=The%20Manufacturer%E2%80%99s%20Duty%20to%20Refund,or%20Replace)). Typically, these disputes are large and go through manufacturer arbitration first ([www.ag.state.mn.us](https://www.ag.state.mn.us/Consumer/Handbooks/MNCarLaws/CH1.asp#:~:text=repair%20a%20car%E2%80%99s%20problem%20after,any%20one%20of%20the%20following)). But conceivably, if the refund amount is under $20k (for instance, a motorcycle or cheap car), you could use conciliation to claim that refund. You’d need documentation of all repair attempts and compliance with the lemon law procedure (like participating in any required arbitration). This is a niche scenario – most lemon law cases exceed the conciliation limit.
- **Identity Theft-Related Claims:** If someone fraudulently opened accounts in your name (identity theft), you generally cannot directly sue them in conciliation unless you locate the perpetrator (often difficult) – and often it’s handled criminally. However, Minnesota law (Minn. Stat. § 609.527) lets identity theft victims recover civil damages, and federal law allows suing for at least $1,000 under the Fair Credit Reporting Act if a company allowed identity theft by not following procedures. These are complex for conciliation. A more straightforward situation: you might sue a person you know who incurred a debt in your name (imposter) for the money you had to pay or spent clearing it. The court would treat it as fraud damages. Keep this within the scope: e.g., “My roommate opened a utility account in my name and ran up $500 – I paid it to avoid credit damage, now I want that $500 back.” That’s viable.
- **Wage and Labor Statutory Damages:** Minnesota’s wage laws, as described, allow **penalty wages** (waiting time penalties up to 15 days pay) ([www.casemine.com](https://www.casemine.com/act/us/640db853b9c46b56ccb214da#:~:text=demand%2C%20whether%20the%20employment%20was,settlement%2C%20satisfactory%20to%20the%20discharged)) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/181.14/version/2023-11-11%2009%3A10%3A13%2B00%3A00#:~:text=Wages%20or%20commissions%20not%20paid,An%20employee%27s%20demand)) and also **minimum wage/overtime** double damages and attorney fees. You can claim these statutory damages in conciliation. Judges in conciliation are empowered to award double or treble damages if a statute clearly provides for it (they do so for deposit cases, for instance, by doubling the deposit ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/?id=504B.178#:~:text=after%20receipt%20of%20the%20tenant%27s,the%20landlord%20and%20interest%20thereon))). So if your wage claim is clearly willful under Minn. Stat. § 181.14, you should request the 15-day wage penalty. Or if it’s an unpaid minimum wage case under Minn. Stat. § 177.27, you could ask for double the unpaid wages as the law allows. Provide the statutory citation to the judge (bring a printout) to support any enhanced damages.
- **Forfeitures (Property Seized by Police):** Unusual but important: Minnesota law allows a person to challenge certain property forfeitures (like vehicles seized in a DWI or property seized in a drug case) in Conciliation Court if the property is worth $15,000 or less (now $20,000 due to the jurisdiction increase) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,The%C2%A0%2015must%20be%20filed%20within)) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=How%20to%20file%20a%20claim,seized%20in%20a%20DWI%20arrest)). There is **no filing fee** for these forfeiture petitions in conciliation ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,The%C2%A0%2015must%20be%20filed%20within)) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=This%20section%20refers%C2%A0ONLY%20to%20a,63)), and strict deadlines (usually you must file within 60 days of the notice of seizure) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=and%20Intent%20to%20Forfeit%20Property,the%20claim%20form%20must%20be)). *Example:* The police seized your car in a DWI arrest, but you are an innocent owner (or the forfeiture was improper); if the car’s value is ≤ $20k, you can file a conciliation petition for judicial determination of the forfeiture. This is a special conciliation process where the court can order the vehicle returned. These cases have specific procedures (you must attach the notice of seizure form, etc.), so consult the statute (Minn. Stat. § 169A.63 for DWI forfeitures, § 609.5314 for drug forfeitures) and the court’s instructions carefully. This is one of the rare instances Conciliation Court can issue orders that aren’t just money (return of seized property) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,The%C2%A0%2015must%20be%20filed%20within)).
- **Other State-Specific Causes:** Minnesota doesn’t have unique small-claims-only causes of action, but many niche statutes allow recovery of money that you could fit into conciliation. For instance, if a towing company illegally tows your car, Minn. Stat. § 168B.07 allows you to recover the towing and storage fees; if a landlord unlawfully locks you out or shuts off utilities, Minn. Stat. § 504B.225 gives you a claim for damages (potentially treble damages) – you could bring that in conciliation. Homeowners could sue a contractor who violated the Minnesota Home Solicitation Sales Act or the Right to Cancel (getting their payment back). Victims of a data breach might sue under Minn. Stat. § 325E.61 (though actual damages there may be hard to quantify for small claims). Always check if the statute provides for attorney fees or multiple damages – if so, conciliation can still award those, but you must prove the elements clearly. If official administrative complaint processes exist (for example, human rights or discrimination claims must go through the Department of Human Rights first, not directly to court), then you cannot circumvent those via conciliation.

### Counterclaims in Conciliation Court

If you are sued in Conciliation Court, you **can file a counterclaim** against the plaintiff if you believe they owe *you* money or property. The counterclaim will be heard at the same hearing. Key points on counterclaims:

- **Allowed Amount:** You can counter-sue for up to the same jurisdictional limits ($20,000 generally, $4,000 if it’s a consumer credit counterclaim) ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=What%20happens%20if%20my%20counterclaim,is%20more%20than%20%2420%2C000)) ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Minnesota%20Judicial%20Branch%20,or%20have%20the%20claim%20and)). If your counterclaim *alone* would exceed the limit, you have options (see below).
- **No Counterclaim Needed for Simple Defenses:** If you *don’t* think the plaintiff is owed anything and you just want to argue against their claim, you do **not** need to file anything – you can simply show up and defend yourself. A counterclaim is only if *you* seek an affirmative recovery from the *plaintiff*.
- **Relation to the Original Claim:** Generally, counterclaims arise from the same events (e.g., plaintiff says you owe them $5,000 for a car sale, and you counterclaim that actually *they* owe *you* because the car was defective). But in Minnesota conciliation, even counterclaims from different events are allowed ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=the%20plaintiff%E2%80%99s%20claim%2C%20you%20can,file%20the%20counterclaim%20and%20pay)). If it’s completely unrelated, the court might handle it separately, but the rule doesn’t forbid it. Practically, if unrelated, the judge might question convenience.
- **Filing Procedure:** To assert a counterclaim formally, you should file a **“Defendant’s Statement of Counterclaim and Summons”** form with the court at least **7 days before the hearing** ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=you%20have%20questions%20about%20whether,same%20hearing%20as%20the%20claim)). You also must pay a filing fee (the same fee amount the plaintiff paid, $65 base) or get a fee waiver ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=or%20to%20file%20a%20new,days%20before%20the%20scheduled%20hearing)). *Importantly*, unlike initial claims, **you do *not* need to personally serve the plaintiff** with your counterclaim ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=the%20filing%20fee%20%20,same%20hearing%20as%20the%20claim)). The court administrator will mail notice of your counterclaim to them. (Still, it’s wise to bring an extra copy for the plaintiff on the hearing day in case.) If you miss the 7-day filing deadline, some courts may let you proceed if the plaintiff agrees, or the court may just treat your claims as defenses or advise you to file your own separate case later.
- **Counterclaims Exceeding the Limit:** What if your counterclaim is bigger than $20,000? Minnesota provides that you have two choices ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=What%20happens%20if%20my%20counterclaim,is%20more%20than%20%2420%2C000)): (1) **reduce/waive the amount** above $20k and stay in Conciliation Court – essentially cap your counterclaim at $20,000; or (2) **ask to remove the entire case to District Court** (regular civil court) so that your larger counterclaim can be heard in full. To do option (2), you must file an **Affidavit of Removal** (often called an Affidavit of Good Faith) with the conciliation court **at least 7 days before the hearing** ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=What%20happens%20if%20my%20counterclaim,is%20more%20than%20%2420%2C000)). This affidavit should state that you have a counterclaim exceeding the limit and that you desire to have the case heard in District Court. Once you do that and pay the district court filing fee, the conciliation case will be put on hold and effectively *transferred* (“removed”) to District Court ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,Pay%20the%C2%A0filing%20fee)). There, it will start anew as a regular civil action with a new case number ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Service%20shall%20be%20by%20first,Pay%20the%C2%A0filing%20fee)). *Tip:* Only choose removal if your counterclaim is genuinely worth more than $20k and you are prepared for the more complex and costly district court process. Otherwise, it may be better to waive the excess and keep it in Conciliation.
- **Effect on Plaintiff’s Claim:** Filing a counterclaim does not erase the plaintiff’s claim; both will be decided together. If your counterclaim is higher than their claim, you could potentially win a net judgment in your favor. If both sides prove their claims, the court might offset the amounts. For example, plaintiff claims $5,000, you counterclaim $3,000 – if the court finds both valid in full, it could net out to plaintiff winning $2,000 (or the court could issue two judgments that offset).
- **Form & Preparation:** On the counterclaim form, clearly state the amount and reason. It’s the same concept as a regular claim – you are now “Plaintiff-in-Counterclaim” and must prove your case just as you would if you filed first. Cite any relevant contracts or facts. The judge will hear both stories at once.
- **No New Service Needed if Case Removed:** If you remove to District Court due to a large counterclaim, the conciliation judgment is not entered; instead the whole case (claim and counterclaim) is heard afresh in district court. You (now effectively the defendant/counter-plaintiff in district court) must follow the Rules of Civil Procedure going forward. Also note, in District Court a business must be represented by an attorney ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,legal%20advice%20if%20you%20have)), so if you’re a business defendant who up-filed the case, you’ll need counsel.

### What You *Cannot* Sue for in Minnesota Conciliation Court

Minnesota law **excludes certain types of cases from Conciliation Court** jurisdiction. If your case falls into one of these categories, small claims is not the right venue (and your filing will be rejected or dismissed). Here are the specific exclusions and other non-eligible matters:

- **Title to Real Estate:** You cannot use conciliation to resolve disputes over ownership of real property or to determine boundary lines of real estate ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=The%20conciliation%20court%20does%20not,jurisdiction%20over%20the%20following%20actions)). For example, quiet title actions, adverse possession claims, or boundary encroachments that require a court to decide property lines must go to District Court. (If it’s simply about money owed in a real estate deal, that part could be, but anything affecting the title or interest in land is out.)
- **Evictions and Unlawful Detainer:** Eviction proceedings (to recover possession of rental property from a tenant) are handled in housing court or district court, not conciliation . Likewise, if you want an order to force someone off your property, that’s not a small-claims matter. Conciliation is only for money or return of personal property.
- **Claims of Defamation (Libel/Slander):** You cannot sue for defamation in Conciliation Court ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=The%20conciliation%20court%20does%20not,jurisdiction%20over%20the%20following%20actions)). Slander and libel claims are excluded likely because they often involve non-monetary harm and complex proof. These must be brought in District Court (and note the **statute of limitations for defamation is 2 years** in Minnesota ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/1998/cite/541.07#:~:text=,a%20physician%2C%20surgeon%2C%20dentist%2C%20occupational))).
- **Malpractice Claims:** Claims against professionals for malpractice (like against doctors for medical malpractice, lawyers for legal malpractice, etc.) are not allowed in conciliation ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,by%20libel%20or%20slander)). These cases typically require expert testimony and usually exceed $20k anyway. Even if your damages are small, you must file in District Court if alleging professional negligence. *(One narrow exception: if it’s a simple billing dispute with a professional, that’s not “malpractice” – you can do that in conciliation. But if you’re claiming substandard care caused injury, no.)*
- **Injunctions or Specific Performance (except personal property returns):** Conciliation Court cannot issue injunctions (orders to do or not do something) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,by%20libel%20or%20slander)). It also generally cannot order “specific performance” of contracts (like force someone to complete a service or deliver unique goods) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,by%20libel%20or%20slander)) – it awards only money damages or the return of personal property. The one carve-out we noted is that it can order return of property valued ≤ $20k ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/2022/cite/491A/full#:~:text=If%20the%20controversy%20concerns%20the,entry%20upon%20the%20premises%20for)). But you cannot ask the court to, say, force a contractor to finish a job or make a landlord fix something – you’d instead sue for the cost of completion or repair. If you need a restraining order or injunction, you must go to District Court.
- **Class Actions:** You cannot bring a class action lawsuit in Conciliation Court ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,extent%20authorized%20in%20subdivision%205)). It’s built for individual claims only.
- **Pre-Judgment Remedies:** You can’t seek things like prejudgment attachment, garnishment, or replevin orders from Conciliation Court ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,extent%20authorized%20in%20subdivision%205)). For example, you can’t ask the small-claims judge to freeze the defendant’s assets before judgment or seize property before the hearing. Those remedies (attachments, etc.) are exclusively via district court processes. (After you win a conciliation judgment, you *can* pursue garnishment or execution, but through the enforcement process in district court – see Post-Judgment section.)
- **Claims Under Certain Statutes Assigned Elsewhere:** Minnesota law lists several chapters of law that Conciliation Court *cannot* handle cases under ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,that%20arise%20under%20those%20chapters)). These include chapters relating to **family law and probate**:
  - Chapter 518 (divorce, child support, spousal maintenance, property division) – you can’t use small claims to modify/support those; e.g., you can’t sue your ex for unpaid child support in conciliation, that stays in family court.
  - Chapters 518A, 518B, 518C (child support enforcement, domestic abuse, interstate support) – likewise out.
  - Chapter 256, 257, 259, 260 (public assistance, paternity, adoption, juvenile matters) – except the rule does allow if it’s **a claim for debt owed to a state or local agency arising under those chapters** ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,that%20arise%20under%20those%20chapters)). So, for instance, a county could use conciliation to recover a welfare overpayment (since that’s a debt to a state agency under ch. 256) – that is allowed. But a private party cannot, say, litigate a paternity matter or an adoption matter in conciliation.
  - Chapters 524 and 525 (probate – estates, trusts, guardianships) – excluded ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,that%20arise%20under%20those%20chapters)). So you cannot file a small claim *against an estate* or over a will. If someone dies and owed you money, you must file a creditor claim in the probate proceeding, not conciliation. If the estate is closed or no probate, you might sue the estate’s representative, but likely it will be kicked to probate or dismissed as beyond conciliation jurisdiction. Also “actions against deceased people” are explicitly mentioned as not allowed ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,when%20another%20court%20is%20involved)) – you cannot sue someone who has died; any such claim has to go through their estate.
- **Cases Where Another Court Has Exclusive Jurisdiction:** If by law some other court or forum has exclusive authority, conciliation can’t be used . Examples: workers’ compensation claims must go through the workers’ comp court (you can’t sue your employer in conciliation for a workplace injury or comp benefits – that’s an exclusive administrative process)；**bankruptcy** matters are federal – if someone owes you money but they filed bankruptcy, you must go to the bankruptcy court to assert your claim (and the automatic stay would prevent you from filing in conciliation anyway). **Federal law** claims like patent infringement, federal civil rights under §1983, ERISA, etc., often have to be in federal court or at least not in conciliation due to complexity and often injunction relief sought.
- **Suing the Federal Government or State Government:** You generally cannot sue the United States (federal government or its agencies) in state small claims court – such suits must be in federal court and the U.S. has sovereign immunity except where waived (like the Federal Tort Claims Act, which requires federal court). Similarly, you can’t sue the State of Minnesota itself in conciliation unless the state has consented and the claim is appropriately small (most state liability is handled via the State Claims Commission or lawsuits in district court under the Minnesota Tort Claims Act). *However*, Minnesota agencies can appear as plaintiffs in conciliation for debts owed to them (as noted). If you have a claim *against* a state agency (e.g., a tax refund dispute or damages caused by a state employee), that typically must follow the state’s claims statute and be in district court. 
- **Professional License or Permit Disputes:** If you’re contesting a fine or a license suspension by a government entity, that often has to go through an administrative hearing or appeal, not small claims. For example, challenging a parking ticket or a municipal fine is done through the city’s process (or district court for an appeal), not conciliation.
- **Criminal Fines or Restitution:** You cannot use Conciliation Court to adjust criminal matters. If you were ordered in criminal court to pay restitution or a fine, and you think it’s unfair, you must address it in that criminal case – small claims has no authority to change those.
- **Insurance Claim Disputes:** If your dispute is directly with your insurance company (e.g., they denied your claim or underpaid), you generally can’t sue them in conciliation *if* the policy mandates arbitration or appraisal or another forum. Many auto and homeowner policies require arbitration for certain claims. Check your policy. If not, you could sue for breach of contract, but these often exceed $20k or involve expert evidence. If it’s a small amount – say the insurance shorted you $1,500 – you could try conciliation for that contractual amount, but expect the insurer to remove it to district court or invoke the policy’s dispute resolution clause. Also, claims about an insurer’s bad faith handling are beyond conciliation scope.
- **Cases Already in Another Court:** If there’s an ongoing case involving the same parties and issues in district court, you can’t file a duplicate small claim – the conciliation court would dismiss it as the matter is already pending (or if already decided, as res judicata). For instance, if your ex is already suing you in regular court over a debt, you can’t try to races to conciliation to get a quick judgment on the same debt. The first-filed case in the appropriate forum will take precedence.
- **Complex Cases Needing Discovery or Experts:** While not a formal jurisdictional bar, practically any case that would require extensive discovery (pre-trial evidence exchange) or expert testimony (beyond perhaps a simple mechanic’s statement or medical bill affidavit) is unsuitable for small claims. The court may dismiss it without prejudice, suggesting you file in district court, if it appears too complex. E.g., a product liability claim that a tool was defectively designed causing $10k injury – under $20k but complex – likely not appropriate for conciliation.

If you file a claim that is outside conciliation jurisdiction, the court **will dismiss it** (usually “without prejudice,” meaning you can file in the correct court). The Clerk might even refuse to accept the filing if it’s obviously not allowed (e.g., your claim form says “Libel – $5,000” which they know is not allowed). Pay attention to these exclusions to avoid wasting time and fees.

### Damages – What You Can and Cannot Recover

In Conciliation Court, you are generally limited to **compensatory damages**, i.e. money to compensate your actual loss. Here’s a breakdown of the damage types:

- **Compensatory Damages:** These are **allowed**. This includes out-of-pocket losses, the contract amount owed, the cost to repair or replace property, medical bills, lost wages, etc. You should be prepared to prove the dollar amount of your loss with receipts, estimates, or other evidence. Conciliation judges commonly award the exact proven economic loss. If the exact amount is a bit uncertain (e.g. value of used items), the judge will make a reasonable estimate.
- **Incidental/Consequential Damages:** If they are direct results of the breach or injury, you can claim them. For example, the cost of renting a car because your car was damaged, or the extra fees you incurred because of someone’s breach. Make sure they are not too speculative and are a natural result of the other party’s action.
- **Pre-Judgment Interest:** Minnesota allows interest on liquidated claims from the time the lawsuit is commenced (or from a prior demand) until judgment ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/549.09#:~:text=,on%20the%20judgment%20or%20award)). In practice, for small claims, if your claim is a fixed amount like an unpaid debt, you can request interest for the time you were deprived of the money. The statutory interest rate changes annually (set by the State Court Administrator) and is typically around 4% per annum in recent years ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/court_rules/rule/msinte/#:~:text=MINNE%C2%ADSOTA%20COURT%20RULES%20,court%20judgments%2C%20verdicts%2C%20and%20arbitration)). It applies except where the claim is unliquidated or an exception (e.g., personal injury pain and suffering doesn’t accrue interest until judgment, but medical bills do from when suit started). The judge may or may not add interest unless you explicitly ask. It’s good to calculate it beforehand (rate information is published by the state – for example, judgments under $50k from 2016–2022 accrued at 4% simple interest per year). Post-judgment, interest will accrue on the amount of the judgment at the same statutory rate ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/549.09#:~:text=,to%20the%20judgment%20or%20award)) until paid.
- **Court Costs:** **Yes**, you can recover your filing fee and **service of process costs** if you win. In Minnesota, if you win the case, the judgment *should* include reimbursement of the filing fee and any amount you paid for service (sheriff fee or certified mail cost). In fact, if you had a fee waiver, Rule 506 requires that the waived fee be added to the judgment and paid to the court by the defendant ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Yes,you%20collect%20on%20the%20judgment)). So always ask the judge to award “court costs.” Typically, the clerks will automatically include the filing fee in the judgment if you win. Do bring proof of what you spent on service (like a receipt from the sheriff or postal certified mail receipt) to get that included.
- **Attorney’s Fees:** In Conciliation Court, *each side generally bears their own attorney’s fees*. Since most people don’t have lawyers there, it’s usually moot. You **cannot** ask for attorney’s fees unless a **law or contract specifically authorizes them** in your case. For instance, if you’re suing under a contract that says “prevailing party gets attorney fees,” or under a statute (like a consumer protection law or wage law) that awards attorney fees to the winner, the conciliation court can award reasonable attorney fees. However, be aware that if you did not actually *incur* attorney fees (because you didn’t hire one), you won’t get any – you can’t “value” your own time as legal fees. If you did hire an attorney to help prepare the case (even though attorneys usually cannot *appear* at the hearing for you in MN conciliation), you could ask for those costs if applicable by law, but this is rare. In summary, don’t expect attorney fees in small claims, with the exception of some clear statutory entitlement (e.g., you sued for unpaid wages and Minn. Stat. § 181.171 mandates the court award you attorney fees – if you had an attorney or perhaps even pro se, you could argue for some compensation, but pro se litigants generally don’t get fees).
- **Punitive/Exemplary Damages:** Punitive damages (meant to punish the wrongdoer beyond actual loss) are **generally not available** in Conciliation Court. The conciliation judge does not have authority to award pure punitive damages except where a specific statute allows a small fixed penalty as part of the claim. For example, the $500 bad-faith landlord penalty for deposit withholding ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/504b.178#:~:text=The%20bad%20faith%20retention%20by,the%20recovery%20of%20the%20deposit)) or the civil penalty for a bad check up to $100 ([caselaw.findlaw.com](https://caselaw.findlaw.com/us-8th-circuit/1365145.html#:~:text=Minnesota%27s%20worthless%20check%20statute%20provides,71%282%29%20%28emphasis)) – those are considered additional damages authorized by statute. But you cannot ask for, say, “$5,000 for pain and suffering and an extra $5,000 to punish the defendant.” Punitive damages in Minnesota also require a special motion and court approval in District Court. So, with very limited exceptions (statutory penalties), you should only expect compensation for your loss, not extra punishment for the defendant in small claims.
- **Multiple Damages/Statutory Penalties:** If a statute explicitly provides for double or treble damages (e.g., double rent deposit, treble damages for certain willful wage violations, etc.), those **can** be awarded in conciliation because they are part of the legal remedy. We’ve seen examples: security deposit double damage ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/?id=504B.178#:~:text=after%20receipt%20of%20the%20tenant%27s,the%20landlord%20and%20interest%20thereon)), wage 2-week penalty ([www.casemine.com](https://www.casemine.com/act/us/640db853b9c46b56ccb214da#:~:text=demand%2C%20whether%20the%20employment%20was,settlement%2C%20satisfactory%20to%20the%20discharged)), NSF check penalty ([caselaw.findlaw.com](https://caselaw.findlaw.com/us-8th-circuit/1365145.html#:~:text=Minnesota%27s%20worthless%20check%20statute%20provides,71%282%29%20%28emphasis)). The conciliation judge will follow the statute if you prove the criteria. Be sure to cite the statute in your claim or at the hearing. These are not considered “punitive” in the court’s eyes but rather statutory liquidated damages, so they’re allowable.
- **Pain and Suffering:** Non-economic damages for personal injury (pain, suffering, emotional distress) are technically allowable (they are part of compensatory damages in injury cases). However, in practice, if your case involves such intangible losses, it’s likely above the scope of conciliation. If you have a minor injury claim with some pain and suffering, you could claim a reasonable amount for it (within the $20k cap), but you should be prepared to explain and justify it. Keep in mind Minnesota’s no-fault law bars suing for minor car injury unless medical expenses exceed a threshold or certain injuries are present, so pain & suffering from car accidents might not be collectible if the threshold isn’t met (that threshold is $4,000 in medical bills or a permanent injury, etc.). For other injuries like dog bites, you can include pain and suffering, but judges in small claims may be conservative and only award out-of-pocket medical costs, especially if you don’t have a very clear way to value your pain.
- **Interest on Judgments:** As noted, once you have a judgment, it will accrue interest at the statutory rate until paid ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/549.09#:~:text=,to%20the%20judgment%20or%20award)). You do not need to ask for post-judgment interest; it’s automatic by law (Minn. Stat. § 549.09). Currently that rate is 4% for most small judgments, but it can change year to year.
- **Filing and Service Fees:** These are recoverable as costs if you win, as discussed (they’ll be added to the judgment against the other side) ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Yes,you%20collect%20on%20the%20judgment)). If you lose, you lose your own fees (and potentially have to pay the other side’s filing fee if they had a counterclaim that they won).
- **Other Costs:** In conciliation, you typically cannot separately claim litigation costs like postage, copying, transportation, or lost earnings for attending court – those are considered your own expenses. The exception is if a statute awards “costs and disbursements,” but conciliation doesn’t really calculate those beyond the court fees. There is no mechanism for awarding expert witness fees or anything in small claims.
- **No Injunctive Relief:** As emphasized, you can’t get an order making the defendant do or stop doing something – only money or return of property. If you try to phrase your desired outcome as, “I want them to complete the contract,” the court will instead consider the monetary equivalent.
- **No Declaratory Judgments:** You can’t ask the court to simply declare rights or interpret a contract without awarding money or property. Small claims is not for advisory opinions or declaratory relief.

In summary, **ask for a specific dollar amount** (or specific property). That amount can include every element of damages you’re entitled to (principal loss, interest up to date, penalties, etc.), but it must be a sum certain ≤ $20,000 (or ≤ $4,000 for consumer credit claims). The court will award what you prove, possibly less if some parts aren’t proven, but not more than you ask. So include everything appropriate, and bring proof for each part.

## 3. Where to File

Small claims cases in Minnesota are heard in the **Conciliation Court division of the District Court** of each county ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/2022/cite/491A/full#:~:text=Subdivision%201)). You need to choose the correct county (venue) when filing. Here are the venue rules:

- **County of Defendant’s Residence/Business:** Generally, you should file in the county where the **defendant lives** (if an individual) or has their primary place of business (if a company) ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Where%20do%20I%20file%20a,Conciliation%20Court%20claim)). For a business that is a corporation or LLC, you can also file in the county where that business’s office or registered agent is located ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Where%20do%20I%20file%20a,Conciliation%20Court%20claim)) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,Chapter%20491A)). You can find a corporation’s registered address by searching the Minnesota Secretary of State’s business registry (the court FAQ even suggests doing so) ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Generally%2C%20a%20Conciliation%20Court%20claim,There%20are%20two%20common%20exceptions)) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,Chapter%20491A)). *Examples:* If you live in Ramsey County and the person you’re suing lives in Hennepin County, you should file in Hennepin County Conciliation Court. If suing a corporation, check their **registered office** – e.g., if it’s a national company but their registered agent’s address is in Saint Paul, you can file in Ramsey County.
- **Contract Exception (County of Performance):** Minnesota law historically allowed some flexibility to sue in the county where a contract was to be performed or executed. However, the Conciliation statute doesn’t explicitly mention contract venue aside from the general residence rule. It’s safest to stick with defendant’s county unless another specific venue rule applies.
- **Landlord-Tenant Exception:** If the case involves a landlord-tenant relationship (e.g., return of deposit, unpaid rent, damage to rental), you may file in the county **where the rental property is located** ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Common%20exceptions%20include%3A)). This is explicitly allowed by Minn. Stat. § 491A.01 subd.9 ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=Subd)). So if the tenant and landlord have both moved elsewhere, you still use the rental county. *Example:* You moved out of an apartment in Olmsted County, landlord lives in Iowa now – file in Olmsted County.
- **Bad Check Exception:** If your claim is about a **bad check**, the law says file in the county where the check was issued (usually, where it was handed over or written) ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=,Court)) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Common%20exceptions%20include%3A)). *Example:* A client from County A gave you (a business in County B) a check that bounced – you should file in County B (where the check was written/passed to you). This is a special rule presumably to allow merchants to sue bad-check writers locally.
- **Multiple Defendants:** If you have multiple defendants in one case, you can file in a county where *any one* of the defendants resides ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/2022/cite/491A/full#:~:text=Subd)). So if one defendant lives in your county and the others elsewhere, you can sue them all in your county under the “multiple defendants” venue rule ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/2022/cite/491A/full#:~:text=Subd)).
- **Corporate Foreign Defendant:** If you are suing an out-of-state corporation that does business in Minnesota, Minn. Stat. § 491A.01 subd.7 provides options ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/2022/cite/491A/full#:~:text=,commenced%20against%20the%20foreign%20corporation)). You can file in the Minnesota county where the corporation’s registered agent is, or if the cause of action arose in a certain county where the company has a business presence, then that county ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/2022/cite/491A/full#:~:text=corporation%3A)). If the corp has withdrawn from MN or no agent, you can even file in your county as plaintiff ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/2022/cite/491A/full#:~:text=,corporation%27s%20registered%20agent%20is%20located)). 
- **Out-of-State Individual Defendant:** If you need to sue someone who lives out of Minnesota, you can do so in Conciliation Court *if* Minnesota has personal jurisdiction (e.g., the claim arises from something that happened in Minnesota). The statute allows service outside the state under the long-arm statute ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/2022/cite/491A/full#:~:text=,commenced%20against%20the%20foreign%20corporation)) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/2022/cite/491A/full#:~:text=,in%20which%20the%20plaintiff%20resides)). The venue in such a case can be the county where you (the plaintiff) reside if the defendant is non-resident ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/2022/cite/491A/full#:~:text=or%20the%20certificate%20of%20authority,corporation%20is%20canceled%20or%20revoked)) *and* the claim has the required connection to Minnesota. For example, you live in Minnesota and loaned money to someone who then moved to another state – you could file in your Minnesota county and serve them out-of-state, since the transaction had a Minnesota nexus. Always check with the court if in doubt; you may need to show that Minnesota’s long-arm statute (Minn. Stat. § 543.19) applies (generally it will if the transaction or injury occurred in MN).
- **Improper Venue:** If you file in the wrong county, the case could be dismissed for lack of jurisdiction *or* possibly transferred. Conciliation Courts in Minnesota typically will dismiss without prejudice if venue is clearly wrong. It’s on the defendant to object to venue by the hearing; if they don’t, the court might hear it anyway. But it’s best to file correctly to avoid delays. Court clerks cannot simply transfer a conciliation case to a different county (you’d have to refile in the correct county if dismissed). So use the venue exceptions above to determine the right location.
- **Venue for Removal (Appeal):** If a case is removed to District Court (e.g., due to a counterclaim or an appeal), it goes to the district court of the *same county*. You can’t change counties upon appeal – it stays in the original venue.

**Filing Location:** Once you identify the proper county, you will file your case in that county’s Conciliation Court, which is usually a division of the **District (Trial) Court** for that county. Most counties have a specific office or window for Conciliation Court in the courthouse. For example, in Hennepin County (Minneapolis), Conciliation Court is on the 3rd floor of the Courts Tower, Hennepin Government Center ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Hennepin%20County%20Conciliation%20Court%20claims%C2%A0may,and%20File%2C%C2%A0or%20by%20mail%20at)) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Civil%20Division%20,Courts%20Tower%20Minneapolis%2C%20MN%2055487)); in Ramsey County (Saint Paul), it’s at the Ramsey County Courthouse. 

**Online Filing:** Minnesota does allow **electronic filing (e-filing)** for conciliation cases in all counties. You can use the **Minnesota Guide & File** online interview to create forms and, in many counties, submit them electronically ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=There%20are%20several%20ways%20you,You%20can)) ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/GetForms.aspx?c=10&p=41#:~:text=,Guide%20%26%20File%20Help%20Topic)). Alternatively, if you have an e-filing account (intended mainly for attorneys but available to self-represented parties), you can upload your completed Conciliation Court Claim form via the eFS system ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=To%20start%20a%20case%2C%20you,need%20to%20start%20a%20case)) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Filing%20the%20Form)). Note: There is an extra **$5 cost** if you file electronically ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=to%20waive%20the%20fee%20by,forms%20with%20the%20court%20electronically)). If you’re not comfortable with e-filing, you can still file **in person or by mail** in every county ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=To%20start%20a%20case%2C%20you,need%20to%20start%20a%20case)) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=If%20you%20financially%20qualify%2C%20you,Pay%20Conciliation%20Court%20Filing%20Fee)). If filing by mail, send the signed original form and the filing fee (check payable to District Court) to the court’s address. The court will then issue a hearing date and serve the summons as appropriate.

**Effect of Venue on Judgment:** A judgment from Conciliation Court is only automatically effective in the county where it’s entered. If the debtor has real estate in that county, docketing the judgment there creates a lien. If you need to enforce it in another county, you can “transcribe” it to the other county’s district court. Thus, if it’s equally convenient, sometimes a creditor might sue in the county where the debtor’s property is located (to ease lien creation). But generally, follow the rules above. 

In summary, file where the **defendant** can be reached: their home or business county, or use the specific exceptions (rental county, check location, your county if none other applies due to foreign defendant). If unsure, ask the court clerk – they can’t give legal advice, but they can define venue rules and might help confirm if a given county is acceptable for your scenario.

## 4. Filing Fees

Filing a case in Conciliation Court requires paying a filing fee, which is **set by state law** and is the same statewide (excluding small local add-ons). **As of 2026, the base filing fee is $65** for a plaintiff to file a claim ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/help-topics/court-fees/district-court-fees?cat=conciliation&cookiecheck=true#:~:text=)). Defendants who file a counterclaim must also pay $65. In addition, each county adds a small **Law Library fee** (often around $10–$15). For example, in Hennepin County the total fee is about $77 (a $65 base plus a $12 law library fee); in some rural counties with a smaller law library fee, it might be $70. When you go to the court’s fee website, select your county to see the exact total ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/help-topics/court-fees/district-court-fees?cat=conciliation&cookiecheck=true#:~:text=Minnesota%20District%20Court%20Fees%20,law%20library%20fees)), but roughly expect ~$70-$80 total in most places.

The filing fee covers the court’s processing and, in claims under $2,500, it also covers service by first-class mail to the defendant ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=When%20and%20how%20do%20I,serve%20the%20defendant)) (the court handles that). If your claim is over $2,500, you will have to arrange (and pay for) service yourself (see next section), but the filing fee remains the same.

**Additional Fees:**
- If you choose to file your forms electronically via eFS or Guide & File, there is an extra **$5 electronic filing convenience fee** ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=to%20waive%20the%20fee%20by,forms%20with%20the%20court%20electronically)) (this is charged by the system vendor). Filing in person or by mail avoids that $5.
- If you later **remove/appeal** the case to District Court for a new trial, there is a substantial fee for that – $310 (or $410 if you demand a jury trial on appeal) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/help-topics/court-fees/district-court-fees?cat=conciliation&cookiecheck=true#:~:text=Removal%20,10%2C%20General%20Rule%20521%28b)). So you want to win in conciliation if possible to avoid that.
- Post-judgment enforcement actions (like obtaining a **Writ of Execution** to garnish wages, etc.) have separate fees (usually around $55 for the writ, plus maybe $15–$20 if the sheriff charges a service fee) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/2019/cite/550.135#:~:text=,creditor%20shall%20provide%20the%20%2415)). Those are paid later, only if needed.

**Fee Waiver (In Forma Pauperis):** If you cannot afford the filing fee, you can ask the court to waive it. This requires filling out an **Affidavit of Inability to Pay Conciliation Court Filing Fee** (sometimes called an IFP application) ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=You%20can%20find%20the%20Conciliation,For%20example%2C%20there%20is)) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=If%20you%20financially%20qualify%2C%20you,Pay%20Conciliation%20Court%20Filing%20Fee)). In this form, you disclose your income, assets, and expenses. If you are receiving need-based public assistance (like MFIP, General Assistance, food stamps, etc.), or your income is below a certain threshold (usually 125% of poverty level), or you have some special financial hardship, the court may waive the fee. A judge reviews IFP requests. There is no fee to apply for IFP. If granted, you file your case without paying the fee. **Note:** As mentioned, if you win the case, that waived fee will be added to your judgment against the other party ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Yes,you%20collect%20on%20the%20judgment)) – meaning the other party ultimately pays it. If you lose, nobody pays the fee (the court eats it).
  
**Serving Fees:** Separately from the filing fee, if you have to arrange service (for claims over $2,500, or if mail service fails or for counterclaims), you’ll have either:
  - **Sheriff or Process Server Fee:** The sheriff in most counties charges around $40–$60 to personally serve one defendant (plus mileage). Private process servers may charge similar or a bit more. This is per defendant address.
  - **Certified Mail Postage:** If you serve by certified mail (allowed for out-of-county defendants by plaintiff after $2,500 threshold), a USPS certified mail with restricted delivery is usually about $7–$10.
These service costs can be recovered if you win (the judgment can include them as costs). If you had a fee waiver, sometimes the court may also waive service costs by having the court deputy serve papers, but typically you’d advance and then recover them.

**Payment Methods:** If filing in person or by mail, you can pay by check or money order to “District Court Administrator.” Some courts accept cash in person. For e-filing, you’ll pay via credit/debit card or e-check online, with a small processing fee.

**Multiple Defendants:** The filing fee is per case, not per defendant. So if you sue two people in one case, it’s still one $65+ fee total. (You will have additional service costs for the extra defendant, but no extra court fee.)

**If Defendant files a Counterclaim:** The defendant must pay a filing fee for the counterclaim too ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=or%20to%20file%20a%20new,days%20before%20the%20scheduled%20hearing)), equal to yours ($65 base). If they don’t pay it, the counterclaim might not be accepted/entered, though the court often will hear it and require payment before entry of judgment. If the defendant wins the counterclaim, they can ask that you reimburse their counterclaim filing fee as part of costs (the court usually offsets costs depending on net winner).

**Recovery of Fees:** As noted, if you win, the judgment should include your filing fee and reasonable service costs ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Yes,you%20collect%20on%20the%20judgment)). If the court inadvertently doesn’t mention it, you can remind them at the hearing or file a motion to amend (but usually it’s automatic). If both parties win something (like you win on your claim, they win on a counterclaim), typically each will bear their own fees or the court will offset.

In short, expect to pay about **$70-$80 upfront** to start your case (unless waived). This is much cheaper than normal civil court (which is ~$285-$315 in Minnesota). And if you prevail, that amount can be added to what the other side owes you ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Yes,you%20collect%20on%20the%20judgment)).

## 5. Statute of Limitations

A statute of limitations is the deadline to start a lawsuit. If you file too late, the defendant can ask for dismissal because the claim is time-barred. The time limits in Minnesota depend on the type of claim. They are the same in Conciliation Court as in any court. Below is a summary of common civil statutes of limitation in Minnesota (current as of 2026):

| **Type of Claim**                   | **Limitations Period** | **Statute**                              |
|-------------------------------------|------------------------|------------------------------------------|

| **Oral contracts**                  | 6 years from breach (treated the same as written in MN) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/1998/cite/541.07#:~:text=,remuneration%20for%20services%20or%20employment)) | Minn. Stat. § 541.05, subd.1(1) |
| **Sale of goods (UCC contracts)**   | 4 years from breach ([mn.gov](https://mn.gov/web/prod/static/lawlib/live/archive/ctapun/0410/opa040509-1012.htm#:~:text=be%20assessed%20in%20the%20event,Jansen%2C%20303%20Minn)) (for contracts of sale of goods) | Minn. Stat. § 336.2–725 (UCC) |

**Important details:**
- The “clock” generally starts when your claim **accrues** – for contracts, when the breach occurred (e.g., when payment was due and not made). For torts, when the injury happened. Minnesota does recognize the **discovery rule** in cases of fraud (and some injury cases like professional malpractice or where the injury is not immediately known). For example, fraud SOL starts when the fraud was discovered or reasonably should have been ([injurynextsteps.com](https://injurynextsteps.com/minnesota/statute-of-limitations/#:~:text=Statute%20of%20Limitations%20Updated%20March,For%20wrongful)).
- If a debt has a series of payments (installments), the SOL might run from each missed payment for that payment, or from acceleration of the debt if the whole thing is accelerated.
- Some causes have special tolling: e.g., if the defendant is out of state (absence from state can toll up to a certain period) ([minnesotalegalservicesauthority.com](https://minnesotalegalservicesauthority.com/minnesota-statute-of-limitations#:~:text=Minnesota%20Statute%20of%20Limitations%3A%20Time,prosecution%20before%20the%20right%20to)), or if the plaintiff is a minor or legally incapacitated at the time of cause, the period may not run until majority or capacity regained (with some limitations).
- For **security deposits**: The law doesn’t give a specific SOL, so it likely falls under the 6-year contract rule as an implied contract claim. Tenants usually sue within a year or two out of practicality. 
- **Statute of Limitations is an affirmative defense.** That means if you file late, the court won’t automatically dismiss; the defendant must raise it. But you should assume they will if it’s clearly expired. If you know it’s expired, better not file (or if you do, be prepared to argue a tolling exception).
- Just because you can file within the SOL doesn’t mean you should wait – evidence gets stale, people move. It’s best to file timely.

In practice, for small claims:
- Many contract and debt cases will use the **6-year limit**.
- Many injury/property cases will be **2-year** (if involving personal injury) or **6-year** (if only property).
- If you’re unsure, err on the side of the shorter limitations period. For example, if you have a mixed claim of property damage and personal injury from the same incident, assume 2 years for the personal injury part. 
- If a claim is just slightly over a time limit, but the defendant doesn’t show up to assert the defense, the court could still award judgment. But if they do appear and invoke SOL, the judge will likely dismiss that portion of the claim.

**Tolling Exceptions:** Minnesota statutes contain some tolling provisions, like:
  - Defendant out of state – tolls SOL (the time they are absent might not count, but MN also has long-arm jurisdiction, so there are details here in Minn. Stat. § 541.13).
  - If the person you want to sue is in bankruptcy, the automatic stay means you cannot sue them during bankruptcy, and the SOL might be tolled during that period.
  - For judgments, there is no tolling – after 10 years, a judgment expires unless you renew it (by action within those 10 years).
  - Parties can also agree to extend a limitations period (not common, but, e.g., an insurer might agree not to assert the SOL while negotiations continue – get that in writing).

To be safe: **Don’t delay filing your conciliation case**. If you’re within a few months of a deadline, file it. The process in conciliation (from filing to hearing) does not stop the SOL clock; it stops once filed. Filing by the last day of the limitations period is sufficient (service can occur afterward).

Finally, note that **minors** or someone under disability: if you have a claim *against* a minor, the SOL doesn’t extend just because they’re a minor – you can sue a minor (but practically, often you’d sue through their parent or wait until 18, depending on claim). If you *are* a minor with a claim, normally the SOL is tolled until you turn 18 (for personal injury, you then have 2 years after 18th birthday). But in small claims, most plaintiffs are adults or businesses.

Always verify the limitation for your specific claim if in doubt, by checking Chapter 541 of Minnesota Statutes or consulting an attorney. Missing the deadline can be fatal to your case.

## 6. Pre-Filing Requirements

One advantage of Conciliation Court is that there are usually no formal prerequisites before filing – you typically do **not** have to send a formal demand or go through mediation (unless a special law or contract requires it). However, there are a few things to consider before you sue:

- **Demand Letters (Optional but Recommended):** While not mandated by law for most cases, it’s **good practice** to send the person or business a written demand for payment before suing. Often, a firm but polite demand can lead to a settlement without court. If you later end up in court, a copy of that demand letter (especially if sent by certified mail or with proof of delivery) shows the judge you gave the other side a chance to resolve the matter. It can also sometimes be basis for additional remedies: e.g., in wage claims, you must demand final wages to trigger penalty wages ([www.casemine.com](https://www.casemine.com/act/us/640db853b9c46b56ccb214da#:~:text=policy%2C%20contract%2C%20or%20other%20legal,settlement%2C%20satisfactory%20to%20the%20discharged)); in bad check cases, you must send the statutory notice of dishonor to claim the penalty ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/1998/cite/332.50#:~:text=,holder%20of%20the%20check%20for)). In deposit cases, while not required, it’s wise to remind the landlord of their obligations after 21 days. If your dispute is with a business, a written demand citing that you will go to Conciliation Court if not resolved might spur payment (businesses know it adds costs to fight in court).
- **Statutory Notice Requirements:** Some types of claims require specific notice by law:
  - **Bad Checks:** As noted, to get the civil penalty, you *must* send a **Notice of Dishonored Check** (usually a form letter referencing Minn. Stat. § 609.535 and § 332.50) and wait 30 days after mailing for payment ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/1998/cite/332.50#:~:text=,holder%20of%20the%20check%20for)). If you haven’t done that, do it before filing (or you can still sue for the face amount without penalty). The court will ask if notice was given before awarding the service charge and penalty.
  - **Landlord-Tenant Repair Issues:** If you are a tenant suing for repair costs or habitability issues, you ideally should have notified the landlord in writing of the problems and given them a reasonable chance to fix (Minn. Stat. § 504B.385 requires written notice 14 days before repair-and-deduct). While not a court requirement to have done so, failing to notify could hurt your case on the merits.
  - **Manufactured Home Park Evictions:** Not applicable in conciliation (evictions aren’t in this court), but some statutes require mediation attempt before eviction. This doesn’t apply to conciliation money claims.
  - **Construction Defect (Contractor) Claims:** Minnesota has a “right to repair” statute for home construction defects (Minn. Stat. § 327A.02, subd.4) which asks homeowners to give written notice of defects and allow the contractor opportunity to inspect and repair before litigation. If you’re a homeowner suing a contractor for defective work, sending that **327A notice**  (and waiting 60 days) is advisable, even for small claims, as judges may expect that you gave the contractor a chance. It’s not a jurisdictional bar, but it can affect your credibility if you didn’t follow the statutory process.
  - **Attorney or Veterinarian Malpractice Claims:** For professional malpractice (which aren’t in conciliation anyway), MN requires an expert affidavit after filing in district court. So no need here since you can’t bring those in conciliation.
  - **No Mandatory ADR:** Conciliation Court itself does not mandate mediation or arbitration before the hearing. (Some states require mediation in small claims; Minnesota does not statewide. However, a few counties offer voluntary mediation on the hearing day or before. For instance, Hennepin Conciliation Court provides a mediation option if parties agree ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Conciliation%20Court%2C%20also%20known%20as%C2%A0small,not%20intended%20to%20be%C2%A0legal%20advice)), but it’s not required.)
- **Contractual Requirements:** Check your contract for any clause about disputes. Some contracts (like with contractors or certain services) require **arbitration** or a **specific notice** of claims. If you have such a clause (e.g., a cell phone contract mandating arbitration, or a home improvement contract requiring written punchlist of defects), you might be barred from court or expected to comply with that first. Arbitration clauses often say “except for small claims” – if so, you’re free to use conciliation. If not, the defendant might move to dismiss in favor of arbitration. It’s up to the judge, but they generally enforce valid arbitration agreements. So be aware if you are bypassing an agreed ADR method.
- **Government Claims Notice:** If your claim is against a city, county, or the state (for example, you hit a pothole and want to sue the city for tire damage), Minnesota law requires that you give the municipality **notice of the claim within 180 days** of the incident (for municipalities) ([ag.state.mn.us](https://ag.state.mn.us/Consumer/Handbooks/LT/CH4.asp#:~:text=Other%20Important%20Laws%20,by%20raising%20the%20tenant%E2%80%99s%20rent)). For the state, it’s 180 days for tort claims (Minn. Stat. § 3.736). If you didn’t give such notice, the government can use that as a defense. In conciliation, the judge will expect compliance. So always send a written notice to the government entity’s clerk or claims office describing the incident and damage within 6 months. Then you can file in conciliation (still within the SOL) if they don’t pay. Without notice, your case likely will be dismissed.
- **Insurance Claims:** If you’re suing for something that might be covered by insurance (like a car accident or property damage), it’s wise (and sometimes required) to notify the insurance company and let them handle it. If you sue someone who has insurance without giving them a chance to involve their insurer, the court might question whether the matter could have been resolved outside court. While not a formal requirement, it can smooth the process. For example, in a car accident case, you’d typically exchange insurance info; if the insurer denies your claim or offers too little, at that point using conciliation is reasonable.
- **Mediation Offers:** Even though not required, you can always attempt mediation pre-suit. Minnesota has community dispute resolution centers in some counties that offer free/low-cost mediation for civil disputes. Hennepin and Ramsey, for example, have such services. If mediation is successful, you save time; if not, you haven’t lost much except a bit of time (the SOL is running, so don’t mediate beyond the deadline). 
- **Document Gathering:** Before filing, gather key documents. Some evidence is easier to get with a subpoena *after* filing (but formal discovery is not allowed in conciliation). You can, however, subpoena documents or witnesses for the hearing. If there is something the other side has that you need (like an accounting ledger, or repair records), consider requesting it informally in writing before suing. They might comply, or they might not – but showing that you attempted to get information shows you acted in good faith.
- **Identify Correct Defendant:** Make sure you know exactly who to sue (person’s full name, or business legal name). Check the Secretary of State for businesses, as mentioned. If it’s an estate of someone deceased, you usually have to go against the estate via probate, not conciliation (except perhaps if estate is closed and under $20k you might find a way, but generally no). Suing the right party is crucial; if you sue the wrong name and they don’t show up, you might end up with a useless judgment. See Section 15 on pitfalls for more on naming defendants.

In summary, *most* small claims have no formal pre-condition like a demand letter, but doing one is prudent. **The one absolute pre-condition in many cases is giving notice for any statutory penalty you want to claim** (e.g., bad check notice, final wage demand for penalty, etc.). Also, comply with any contractually required notice of breach. Taking these steps not only preserves your rights to extra damages, it also often convinces the other side to pay up once they see you know your rights and are serious (sometimes simply mentioning “Minnesota Statute X allows me to recover double if you don’t respond” in a letter jolts a response).

If no resolution after a reasonable notice period (say 1–2 weeks for an individual, maybe 30 days for a business if not urgent), then proceed to file your Conciliation Court claim. Minnesota’s Attorney General publishes consumer handbooks often advising a written demand before suing, which aligns with this approach ([ag.state.mn.us](https://ag.state.mn.us/Consumer/Handbooks/ConCourt/#:~:text=,Complaints%20Do%20Conciliation%20Courts%20Handle)) ([ag.state.mn.us](https://ag.state.mn.us/Consumer/Handbooks/ConCourt/#:~:text=,Settlement%20Prior%20to%20the%20Hearing)).

## 7. Forms Required

To start a Conciliation Court case, you will need to prepare and file specific forms. Fortunately, Minnesota provides standardized forms for small claims. Here’s what you’ll use:

- **Statement of Claim and Summons (Plaintiff’s Claim Form):** This is the main form to initiate a case. Sometimes it’s just called “Conciliation Court Claim Form.” On this form, you fill in:
  - Your name and address (plaintiff).
  - The defendant’s name and **physical address** (no P.O. boxes) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=have%20the%20following%20information%3A)) – the address where the court can serve them or send hearing notice.
  - The **amount** you are claiming (and whether it includes costs or interest).
  - The **reason** for the claim – a brief description of what happened and why the defendant owes you. (Keep it concise, e.g., “Unpaid loan – defendant borrowed $1,000 on Jan 1, 2023, never repaid” or “Negligent car repair on Mar 5, 2023 causing $500 damage.”)
  - The **date** the claim arose (date of incident or breach) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,statement%20to%20see%20attached%20documents)).
  - There’s language on the form where you sign under penalty of perjury that your claim is truthful.
  The form also acts as a Summons for the defendant – when filed, the court will assign a hearing date and that info will appear on the summons that gets served to the defendant.
  
  This form is available as a **fillable PDF** (Statewide Form # CCT102) or via the online Guide & File interview ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/GetForms.aspx?c=10&p=41#:~:text=,Guide%20%26%20File%20Help%20Topic)). It’s often titled “Plaintiff’s Statement of Claim and Summons.” If filing in person, you can also obtain a paper copy at the courthouse. Some courts have a multi-part carbonless form you can fill out by hand.

- **Affidavit of Service (for plaintiff)**: If the court administrator handles service (for small claims under $2,500, by mail), you won’t use this. But if you have to arrange service (for claims over $2,500 or out-of-state service or if initial mail fails) you will need to have the person who served the papers fill out an Affidavit of Service (Form # CCT104 perhaps). This form simply states when, where, and how the defendant was served (personal or via certified mail etc.), and by whom. It must be filed with the court within 60 days of the Summons issuance or at least 7 days before hearing ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=on%20how%20you%20have%20to,court%20date%2C%20whichever%20is%20sooner)). The court provides a template – in fact, the hearing notice packet often includes a blank Affidavit of Service form and instructions if self-service is needed.
  
- **Statement of Counterclaim (Defendant’s Claim):** If you are a defendant who chooses to file a counterclaim, you use the “Defendant’s Statement of Counterclaim and Summons” form. It’s similar to the claim form but for counterclaims. You fill in your info as counter-claimant (defendant), the plaintiff’s info as counter-defendant, amount and reason for your counterclaim, and sign. This form must be filed at least 7 days before the hearing with the fee ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=or%20to%20file%20a%20new,filed%20a%20counterclaim%2C%20and%20it)). The court then sends a notice of counterclaim to the plaintiff, so you typically don’t have to serve it separately (though some courts may mail a copy to the plaintiff and also hand one to them at hearing). Form number might be CCT103.
  
- **Power of Attorney to Appear (Conciliation Court Power of Attorney):** If a business or organization is being represented by a non-lawyer employee or officer, Minnesota requires a “Power of Attorney” form to be filed giving that person authority to represent the entity ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Conciliation%20Court%20and%20District%20Court,Court%20judgment%20to%20District%20Court)) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.02#:~:text=Subd)). The Minnesota Judicial Branch has a specific form (often called “Power of Attorney for Conciliation Court”) ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Conciliation%20Court%20and%20District%20Court,Court%20judgment%20to%20District%20Court)). Despite the name, it’s not for attorneys – it’s for non-lawyer representatives. For example, if an LLC wants its property manager to handle the case, the LLC’s managing member would sign this form authorizing that manager. If you are a sole proprietor and want an employee to go to court for you, you’d sign this. *Important:* As mentioned, some courts will reject filings submitted by someone via POA if they are not an actual party ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Pursuant%20to%20a%C2%A0Standing%20Order%20for,this%C2%A0Power%20of%20Attorney%20Fact%20Sheet)). Only use this for businesses, or an authorized person for an estate or trust. An individual cannot give a friend POA to appear for them – the court won’t allow it ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Pursuant%20to%20a%C2%A0Standing%20Order%20for,this%C2%A0Power%20of%20Attorney%20Fact%20Sheet)). The form typically needs to be notarized or signed under penalty of perjury by the party giving the authority.
  
- **Affidavit of Inability to Pay (IFP Affidavit):** If you seek a fee waiver, you’ll fill out the “Affidavit for Proceeding In Forma Pauperis.” Minnesota has a general IFP form (often CIV102). You list income, assets, expenses, any public assistance, etc. A judge will review it (sometimes same day). You might also need a proposed Order for IFP for the judge to sign – some counties have a combined form. Court administration can provide these. There’s also a specific one for conciliation (some places refers to “Conciliation IFP”).
  
- **Removal/Appeal Forms:** If after judgment you or the other party appeals (removes) to District Court, there are forms for that: “Demand for Removal / Affidavit of Good Faith” (to appeal, you assert the appeal is in good faith and not just to delay) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,vacated)) and an “Affidavit of Service of Removal” (to show you mailed a copy to the other side) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Minneapolis%2C%20MN%2055487,Pay%20the%C2%A0filing%20fee)). The court will provide these forms at the conciliation hearing or with the judgment notice. Rule 521 covers this. If a business is appealing, note that an attorney must sign the removal demand for them ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=or%20manager%2C%20only%20in%20Conciliation,the%20General%20Rules%20of%20Practice)).
  
- **Motion to Vacate Judgment:** If a default judgment was entered and the defendant wants to reopen the case, the defendant can file a “Notice of Motion and Motion to Vacate Conciliation Court Judgment” and a supporting Affidavit, especially if after the 20-day easy period. The court self-help center or website has a specific form for vacating after the deadline ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,for%20a%20new%20hearing%20date)). Within 20 days of judgment, a simpler letter might suffice, but after that, the formal motion form (and a filing fee, usually equivalent to the appeal fee $... but verify) is needed.
  
- **Subpoena:** If you need a witness or documents at trial, you can request the court issue a subpoena. The court has subpoena forms (they’re generally the same as in district court). In conciliation, you would typically ask the court clerk for a signed subpoena form, then serve it yourself. The form must be delivered to the witness along with the statutory witness fee (currently $20 plus mileage – see Minn. Stat. § 357.22). Common subpoena usage: commanding a mechanic to testify or bring records, or a phone company to provide a record. Use sparingly due to cost and complexity.
  
- **Financial Disclosure form (post-judgment):** If you win and the debtor doesn’t pay, Minnesota law (Minn. Stat. § 491A.02, subd. 9) allows you to request the debtor fill out a Financial Disclosure within 30 days of judgment. The court can provide an “Order for Disclosure” form which the judge signs and is sent to the debtor. The debtor must list their employer, bank, assets, etc., under oath and return it or face potential contempt. This occurs post-judgment, but the form is typically available at court administration. If needed, ask the court for the Financial Disclosure form after you win – you fill out a simple request and the court issues it without fee. (If the debtor fails to respond in 15 days, you may then get an order to show cause to enforce.)

All forms are available on the **Minnesota Judicial Branch website** under **Forms – Conciliation/Small Claims Court** ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/GetForms.aspx?c=10&p=41#:~:text=Court%20Forms%20Category%20List%20,10)). They have packets like “Forms Packet: Filing a Conciliation Court Claim” ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/GetForms.aspx?c=10&p=41#:~:text=Court%20Forms%20Category%20List%20,10)) which includes instructions and the needed forms. The site offers forms in multiple languages (for informational purposes – you must complete the English version for the official filing) ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/GetForms.aspx?c=10&p=41#:~:text=Plaintiff%27s%20Statement%20of%20Claim)).

**Filling them out:**  Be concise and clear. The claim description should be a short statement of why the defendant owes you money. You don’t need to attach evidence to the filing; in fact, the instructions say *do not* attach separate pages or exhibits ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,statement%20to%20see%20attached%20documents)). Save the detailed evidence for the hearing, unless a particular document is required by rule. (One example: in a property forfeiture conciliation case, you *do* attach the notice of seizure form to your Petition to get property back ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=filing%20fee.%20,and%20Intent%20to%20Forfeit%20Property)).)

**Forms differ by county?** Generally, the state-approved forms are used statewide. A few counties may have slight variations (like an extra line for case numbers), but you won’t have to worry about that if using the official forms. Always use the latest version from the state website (the forms were updated to “Smart Forms” which can be filled and e-filed easily) ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/GetForms.aspx?c=10&p=41#:~:text=Forms%20Packet%3A%20Filing%20a%20Conciliation,Court%20Claim)).

In summary, to commence your case you will typically need:
  - **Plaintiff’s Statement of Claim and Summons** (the main filing)
  - Filing fee or **IFP Affidavit** (if fee waiver needed)
  - Possibly a **Power of Attorney** form if you’re representing a business or someone else.
Once filed, the court will provide a **Notice of Hearing** (often just a copy of your claim form with the hearing date stamped on it) that will be mailed to the defendant (or to you for service if applicable).

Make sure names and addresses on the forms are accurate. Use full legal names (no nicknames). For businesses, include Inc., LLC, etc., and address of registered agent if you know it. If suing a sole proprietorship, you might list “John Doe, d/b/a John’s Plumbing” to cover both the person and trade name.

If you have any doubt, the Court Self-Help Center or the Conciliation Court clerk can often point you to the right form. They cannot fill it for you, but they do have blank forms and basic instructions ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=How%20do%20I%20file%20a,claim%20in%20Conciliation%20Court)). Also, Minnesota’s Law Libraries or the Attorney General’s Handbook online have sample filled-out forms for guidance ([ag.state.mn.us](https://ag.state.mn.us/Consumer/Handbooks/ConCourt/#:~:text=,Complaints%20Do%20Conciliation%20Courts%20Handle)) ([ag.state.mn.us](https://ag.state.mn.us/Consumer/Handbooks/ConCourt/#:~:text=,Settlement%20Prior%20to%20the%20Hearing)).

## 8. Service of Process

After filing your Statement of Claim, the defendant must be “served” with the court summons (notice of the lawsuit and hearing date). Proper **service of process** is critical – without it, the court cannot issue a valid judgment. Minnesota’s conciliation court has some unique service rules:

- **Who Serves:** In many cases, the court administrator will handle service for you **by mail**. Specifically, if your claim is for **$2,500 or less**, the court will **send the summons to the defendant by first-class mail** to the address you provided ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=When%20and%20how%20do%20I,serve%20the%20defendant)). This is done automatically once you file and pay. You and the defendant will both receive a Notice of Hearing stating the date/time of the hearing. If the mailing to the defendant is not returned by the post office, it’s presumed delivered. If it *is* returned undeliverable or the defendant fails to appear, then you may need to arrange personal service and get a new hearing date.
  
  If your claim is **over $2,500**, Minnesota law requires **you (the plaintiff) to arrange for service** ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,served%20by%20the%20plaintiff%20by)). The court will still issue a hearing date, but give you instructions to serve the **Summons and Complaint** on the defendant and to provide proof of service. You have two main options for serving:
  1. **Certified Mail (restricted delivery) by Plaintiff:** You can serve the summons yourself by mailing it to the defendant via Certified Mail, with a return receipt “green card” and **Restricted Delivery** (meaning only the addressee can sign). This must be done through the U.S. Postal Service. If the defendant signs the green card, that counts as service (you’d then file an Affidavit of Service by Mail attaching the signed receipt). If they refuse or never pick it up, service is not completed and you’ll have to try personal service.
  2. **Personal Service:** You can have the defendant personally served by any adult who is not a party to the case – typically the **Sheriff** or a professional **process server**. The server will hand-deliver the hearing notice and claim to the defendant (or leave it with a suitable person at their home of suitable age, which is considered valid “substituted service” under civil rules). The process server or sheriff then completes an Affidavit of Service (the sheriff often has their own form) stating date/time of delivery. You file that with the court within 60 days of when the summons was issued or before the hearing, whichever comes first ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=on%20how%20you%20have%20to,court%20date%2C%20whichever%20is%20sooner)).
  
  Personal service is often the most reliable method for amounts over $2,500 since certified mail can be dodged. It might cost ~$50 but ensures the defendant actually gets notice.

- **Timing of Service:** The rules do not specify an exact number of days before the hearing by which service must be achieved, but obviously, the defendant needs reasonable notice. The hearing date is typically set 20–60 days out from filing. As a practical matter, try to get the defendant served at least **2 weeks before the hearing**. If using certified mail, send it promptly so there’s time for the green card to come back. And remember the **60-day** limit: you must complete service (and file proof) within 60 days of the court issuing the summons ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=on%20how%20you%20have%20to,court%20date%2C%20whichever%20is%20sooner)), or else the case may be dismissed for lack of service (or you’d have to ask for an extension).

- **Service on Businesses:** If the defendant is a company, personal service can usually be done by serving an officer, managing agent, or the registered agent. The Minnesota Secretary of State’s site will list a corporation’s registered agent and address. Serving that agent at the listed address is a good method (or any corporate officer at the business’s principal office). Process servers know how to serve businesses (or at least the sheriff will leave it with a manager in charge at a business location). If serving by certified mail, address it to the business’s registered agent or an officer.

- **Out-of-State Defendants:** Minnesota conciliation summons can be served outside Minnesota **in the same manner as any civil summons** if the court has jurisdiction ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=county%20in%20which%20the%20court,secure%20the%20attendance%20of%20nonparty)) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=summons%20in%20a%20conciliation%20court,the%20manner%20provided%20by%20law)). That means you’d likely need to use **personal service** through a process server or sheriff in the other state (certified mail is not sufficient for out-of-state unless perhaps the defendant voluntarily signs). The Minnesota statute refers to service under Minn. Stat. § 543.19 (long-arm) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/2022/cite/491A/full#:~:text=,commenced%20against%20the%20foreign%20corporation)). Typically, you’d hire a process server in the defendant’s state (the court admin won’t mail out of state for you). Once served, file that proof just like in-state. Note: Serving out of state often requires using Minnesota’s long-arm statute affidavit too, but usually just a proper service is enough. If the defendant is out-of-state and you cannot find them, you may have to consider alternative service (publication) by motion, but that’s uncommon in small claims as it’s expensive and only granted if you show diligent effort to find them.

- **Alternative Service / Publication:** If you truly cannot locate the defendant for personal service, you can motion the court for permission to serve by **alternate means** (like service by mail to last known address combined with publication in a legal newspaper). This is rare in conciliation because it’s costly relative to typical claim amounts, and courts prefer actual notice. But it can be done with a judge’s order if you show due diligence in trying to find the person (checking phone books, contacting acquaintances, etc.). Minn. R. Civ. P. 4.04 allows publication as a last resort. You’d likely need to remove the case to district court to get such an order, as conciliation judges may not entertain motion practice easily. So practically, small claims cases proceed only when you have an address for the defendant. If you don’t know where they are, you might need to hire a skip-tracer or use internet resources to find an address before filing.

- **Proof of Service:** After serving via certified mail or personal delivery, the person who served must sign an **Affidavit of Service** detailing how it was done ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=your%20Statement%20of%20Claim%20and,court%20date%2C%20whichever%20is%20sooner)). For certified mail, you (as plaintiff) can sign the affidavit stating you sent it and attach the signed green card. For personal service, the server (sheriff or process server) signs. The **Affidavit of Service** form is filed with the court (some counties require it be filed a certain number of days before the hearing; the statewide rule says within 60 days of issuance or before hearing, whichever is sooner ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=After%20service%20is%20completed%2C%20the,court%20date%2C%20whichever%20is%20sooner))). If you forget to file it and show up at the hearing, the judge may continue the case until proof is filed or may take testimony of the server if present. It’s better to file it in advance so the court record shows service completed.

- **If Initial Service Fails:** If the court-mailed notice comes back as undeliverable, the court will notify you (often by mail) that service failed. Usually, the case doesn’t automatically dismiss; the court might set a new hearing and instruct you to attempt service yourself. You should then try personal service or find a better address. If you can’t find them, you may have to dismiss the case without prejudice until you can. Pay attention to any notice from court about returned mail. Also, if the defendant simply doesn’t show up at the hearing and the mail wasn’t returned, the court usually assumes service was okay and may enter default – but if it turns out they weren’t at that address (and they later move to vacate saying they never got notice because wrong address), the judgment can be vacated. So it’s in your interest to verify the address is correct.

- **Serving a New Hearing Notice:** If you had to reschedule or the court continued the case, and especially if there were service issues, make sure the defendant gets notice of the new date. The court typically mails notice of a continuance to both sides if addresses are known. If a defendant was never served initially and you get a second try, you serve the *new* hearing date notice similarly.

- **Who Can Serve:** By rule, anyone 18 or older and not party to the case can serve. Many plaintiffs use a friend or family member to do personal service to save money (that friend then must sign the affidavit). This is allowed, but ensure the person is reliable and will properly hand it to the correct individual and fill out the affidavit fully. Sometimes, having the sheriff do it is worth the fee because they know how to document it well and their affidavit carries weight.

- **Cost of Service:** The plaintiff initially pays for service if required (sheriff fees, etc.), but as noted you can add those costs to your claim for reimbursement ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Yes,you%20collect%20on%20the%20judgment)). Keep receipts. When you win, ask the judge to include $X for service fee. If you had to serve by publication (in extremely rare cases), those publication fees could be hundreds of dollars, which is another reason small claims by publication is seldom done.

- **Service of Subpoenas:** If you subpoena a witness or documents, you (as the requesting party) are responsible for serving those subpoenas and paying the witness fee. Subpoenas can be served like any process – by anyone 18+ not a party. You usually should use personal service for subpoenas. The timeline for a subpoena should be well before hearing so the witness has reasonable time to comply.

- **Defendant’s Address Changes:** If you suspect the defendant will move or is transient, it’s wise to attempt service as quickly as possible. If mail service fails because they moved, try to get a forwarding address or do a skip-trace. Also, the USPS “Address Service Requested” on a certified mail may provide a new address if they moved and filed a forwarding order.

- **Special Situations:** If the defendant is a minor (under 18), you should serve their parent or guardian in addition to the minor. If the defendant is a legally incapacitated adult with a guardian, serve the guardian. If you’re suing a partnership that is not an LLC or corp, you can serve any partner. If suing a sole proprietor, serve the owner personally (suing them by personal name is key; the business name alone isn’t a legal entity).

Remember, proper service is required for the court’s judgment to be enforceable. If the defendant shows up at the hearing, service is basically conceded/waived (their appearance means they got notice). If they don’t show up, the judge will double-check that service was done correctly via the affidavit before entering default judgment. So do it by the book to avoid any doubt.

In summary:
- Under $2,500: Court will mail notice to defendant’s address you gave ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=When%20and%20how%20do%20I,serve%20the%20defendant)). You should still confirm that address is good.
- Over $2,500: You must serve by certified mail (restricted) or personal service ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,served%20by%20the%20plaintiff%20by)).
- File proof of service timely ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=on%20how%20you%20have%20to,court%20date%2C%20whichever%20is%20sooner)).
- If unable to serve, seek continuance or alternate service order. 
- Always serve each defendant.

Once service is done, the defendant either will appear or default. 

*(One more note: Minnesota rules specifically say if the defendant is a non-resident, “service on nonresident defendants must be made in accordance with applicable law or rule” ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=county%20in%20which%20the%20court,secure%20the%20attendance%20of%20nonparty)). That means you can’t use the easy mail service for out-of-state – go with personal service via long-arm. Also, if you choose personal service within Minnesota even when mail was an option, that’s fine too. Many plaintiffs do that to be sure, especially for larger claims near $2,500 threshold.)*

## 9. Defendant’s Response and Default

In Conciliation Court, the defendant is **not required to file a written answer** or response before the hearing. The normal civil procedure of an “answer” doesn’t apply here. Instead:

- If the **defendant intends to dispute the claim**, they simply appear at the scheduled hearing and present their side. They can bring evidence and argue why they don’t owe the money. There is no pre-hearing exchange of pleadings (though the defendant could file a counterclaim as discussed, or in some cases they might send a letter to the court, but that is not an official answer).
- If the defendant believes they owe some but not all of the money, they can try to settle with you before the hearing or just show up and explain/contest the amount.

**Time to Respond:** The summons (hearing notice) will provide the court date, which is effectively the deadline – the defendant should attend that hearing. In Minnesota, that hearing date is typically 21–60 days after filing. So in effect, the defendant has until the hearing to prepare a defense or pay the claim. Unlike some states, there is no separate “answer by X days” – just the hearing date.

**Options for Defendant Upon Receiving Notice:**
  - **Do nothing / ignore:** If the defendant fails to show up at the hearing, the court will likely issue a **default judgment** in favor of the plaintiff (assuming the plaintiff can show at least basic proof of the claim and that service was done). Default means the plaintiff wins by defendant’s failure to appear. The judge will usually ask the plaintiff to briefly testify to the claim and amount, then will enter judgment since the defendant isn’t there to contest.
  - **Contact the plaintiff to settle:** It’s common that once served, a defendant may reach out to negotiate. If you settle, you can file a dismissal or simply inform the court at hearing that it’s settled (it’s better to put any settlement in writing and have it signed, in case the defendant doesn’t pay up before you dismiss). If a settlement is reached *and* payment made before the hearing, the plaintiff should notify the court to dismiss the case (so the defendant isn’t dragged to court unnecessarily).
  - **File a Counterclaim:** If the defendant has a claim against the plaintiff, they can file a counterclaim at least 7 days before the hearing with the court (as covered in Section 2 and 7) ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=you%20have%20questions%20about%20whether,same%20hearing%20as%20the%20claim)). If they do, the court will notify you and both claim and counterclaim are heard together.
  - **Request Continuance (Postponement):** If the defendant cannot attend on the scheduled date, they can request one continuance (adjournment) from the court, ideally in writing and well before the hearing. Minnesota’s Rule says a party can be charged up to $50 in costs for a continuance ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=court%20to%20vacate%20the%20judgment,Civil%20Procedure%20for%20District%20Court)) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=legal%20advice%20consults%20on%20general,on%20appeals%20to%20District%20Court)), and each party is allowed only one continuance as of right (further continuances are at judge’s discretion). So a defendant might send a letter or file a short motion to continue if, say, they are out of town on the hearing date.
  
**Default Judgments:**
- If the defendant doesn’t show, the plaintiff can ask the judge for a default judgment. The judge will usually require the plaintiff to swear to the facts and possibly provide some evidence to ensure the claim seems valid and the amount is justified (especially for large sums). Assuming everything is in order (including proof of service), the court will mail out a default **Order for Judgment** in favor of the plaintiff shortly after the hearing. That becomes a judgment if not contested.
- **Setting Aside Default:** If a default judgment is entered, the defendant has options to get it vacated (removed) *if* they have a valid excuse or defense:
    - If the defendant acts **quickly (within 20 days)** of the default judgment entry, Minnesota conciliation rules allow them to file a simple motion or request to vacate the judgment without a hearing. On the conciliation judgment notice, there’s often a “deadline to vacate” date printed (often 20 days out) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,strongly%20recommended%20that%20the%20removing)). If the defendant submits a written request to reopen the case before that date – commonly stating a reason like “I didn’t receive notice” or “I was unable to attend due to emergency” and that they have a defense – the court may administratively grant a new hearing.
    - After that initial deadline (or after judgment is entered if no specific deadline given, often within the 20-30 day window before it’s final), the defendant must file a formal **Motion to Vacate Judgment** (post-deadline) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,strongly%20recommended%20that%20the%20removing)). This requires showing a good cause (e.g., excusable neglect, mistake, lack of service, or a meritorious defense) similar to Rule 60 of civil procedure. The defendant would file a Notice of Motion and Motion to Vacate with an affidavit explaining the circumstances ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,strongly%20recommended%20that%20the%20removing)), pay a motion fee (if any, or a new conciliation filing fee possibly), and the court will set a hearing on whether to reopen. Common reasons courts accept: improper service (never got notice), or an emergency (hospitalization) prevented attendance, or some agreement that confused the defendant.
    - If the motion to vacate is within a reasonable time and the defendant shows both a valid reason for missing the hearing and a potentially valid defense, judges often vacate (because conciliation favors deciding cases on merits). If vacated, the case gets reset for a new hearing on the merits. If the judge denies vacating, the default stands.
    - **Appeal vs. Vacate:** Note that a defendant who defaulted cannot “appeal” (remove) the case to district court – removal is only allowed after a contested hearing (where both sides appeared) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=After%20a%20contested%20trial%2C%20a%C2%A0party,the%20judgment%20debtor%20or%20creditor)). So their only remedy is to ask conciliation to reopen. If that fails and time to appeal lapses, the default judgment is final.
  
- **If Both Parties Don’t Show:** If neither you nor defendant show at the hearing, typically the case is **dismissed without prejudice** (meaning you can file again). It’s not a judgment on merits. Some courts simply strike the case. You would have to refile (and repay fee) to restart it. If plaintiff appears and defendant doesn’t, default for plaintiff. If defendant appears and plaintiff doesn’t, often the case is dismissed (with prejudice if the plaintiff had proper notice, meaning plaintiff loses the claim). Actually, conciliation rule states if plaintiff doesn’t appear, the case may be dismissed with or without prejudice at judge’s discretion (usually without prejudice on first non-appearance, with prejudice if it’s second time or if dismissal otherwise appropriate). Defendants sometimes show up when plaintiff doesn’t – they can ask for dismissal. If the claim is dismissed and the plaintiff had no good cause, that’s likely the end (unless they appeal or move to vacate the dismissal).
  
- **Response Before Hearing:** If the defendant wants to respond in writing, they technically could send a letter to the court or to you, but the court will not decide based on a letter alone if plaintiff is present. Minnesota law allows evidence by affidavit in certain instances, but generally the defendant should appear in person to contest. If a defendant absolutely cannot appear (e.g., deployed military, incarcerated, etc.), they might write the court in advance – the court might continue the hearing or allow a sworn written statement, but that’s discretionary.

- **Military Members:** Under the federal Servicemembers Civil Relief Act (SCRA), if the defendant is on active duty and cannot attend, the court should not enter default without appointing an attorney to represent their interests. If you know the defendant is active-duty military, inform the court. They may stay the case. If a default happened and the defendant later shows they were on active duty and unable to respond, SCRA gives grounds to reopen.

- **After Judgment – Payment or Appeal:** If judgment is entered (either by default or after hearing), the losing party (defendant, if they lost) has **30 days** to appeal (remove) for a new trial ([zmattorneys.com](https://zmattorneys.com/small-claims-court-limit-raised/#:~:text=entity%20to%20represent%20it%20in,appeals%20it%20within%2030%20days)). If they don’t, the judgment becomes final and you can enforce it. A defaulted defendant who did not contest cannot appeal; their remedy is as above (vacate motion). Only a party who appeared and lost on merits can appeal. For plaintiffs: if you as plaintiff lose (your claim dismissed or you get less than you sought), *you* can appeal within 30 days too, except note that if you sued and lost, you often can’t re-sue the same claim (that’s adjudicated) unless it was dismissed without prejudice. But you can remove to district for a fresh trial if unsatisfied.

- **Settling before judgment:** If at the hearing or just before, you and defendant settle the case by agreement (maybe partial payment or a payment plan), you can usually ask the judge to enter that as a mediated agreement or just dismiss the case if satisfied. Some conciliation courts allow a “settlement” to be put on record (if both agree, the judge can issue judgment per the settlement terms – but often they’ll just dismiss so you’d have to re-sue if breach, or sometimes they issue a judgment that will be vacated if payment is made timely – procedures vary). If you reach a settlement outside of court after filing, you can file a Stipulation of Dismissal (both sign) or notify the court.

**Key takeaway:** The defendant’s main “response” is to show up at the hearing prepared to present their side. If they fail to show, you likely get a default judgment ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Steps%20to%20File%20a%20Removal,Conciliation%20Court%20to%20District%20Court)). If you fail to show, case likely gets dismissed. Always confirm the hearing date and appear. If you’re the plaintiff and the defendant contacts you before the hearing with a resolution, you can avoid default by handling that properly (document payment or continue case until payment, etc.). Conciliation Court is designed so that an unanswered claim by a no-show defendant results in a relatively quick default judgment for the plaintiff, which then can be enforced like any judgment.

## 10. Hearing Procedure

The Conciliation Court hearing is an informal trial in front of a judge or a judicial officer (often called a referee or magistrate in some counties). **No jury** – it’s always a bench trial (judge decides). Here’s what to expect and how to prepare:

- **Setting and Who Presides:** The hearing is usually in a small courtroom or conference-room-style setting at the courthouse. A District Court Judge or an appointed Conciliation Court Referee will preside. For example, Hennepin and Ramsey often use referees (experienced attorneys appointed to hear small claims). Whoever it is, they have authority to administer oaths and make decisions. It’s not as formal as a regular court – judges may sit at a desk rather than the elevated bench, and might not wear robes (though many do). Still, it’s a court proceeding, and you should show respect (call the referee “Your Honor” or “Judge,” stand when speaking if asked, etc.).
- **Role of Attorneys:** In Minnesota Conciliation Court, **attorneys are allowed** to represent parties (unlike some states that ban lawyers in small claims) ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Individuals%20can%20represent%20themselves%20or,Court%20judgment%20to%20District%20Court)). However, it’s common that neither side has a lawyer due to the cost relative to claim size. Individuals can choose to have an attorney, but will have to pay those costs themselves (and typically can’t recover attorney fees). Businesses can be represented by an officer or employee with a Power of Attorney form instead of a lawyer ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Individuals%20can%20represent%20themselves%20or,Court%20judgment%20to%20District%20Court)). If one side brings an attorney and the other doesn’t, the hearing proceeds just the same – attorneys know that conciliation is informal, so they won’t be allowed to intimidate or overcomplicate matters. The judge will keep things simple. **Bottom line:** You do not need a lawyer, and most people self-represent. If the other side has a lawyer, don’t panic – present your facts clearly; the judge will ensure fairness.
- **Language/Interpreters:** If you or a witness is not fluent in English, you should request a court interpreter **ahead of time** (when filing, or as soon as you know one is needed). Minnesota courts will provide interpreters at no cost in civil cases when necessary. There is a form or you can call the court’s interpreter services. On the day, the interpreter will translate everything said. Do not bring your own friend to interpret – the court prefers certified interpreters. If an interpreter isn’t arranged and one party struggles with English, the judge may postpone the hearing to get one. Similarly, for hearing-impaired parties, sign language interpreters can be arranged.
- **Before the Hearing:** On the scheduled day, arrive early (10-15 minutes) to check in. Most courts require you to check in with the clerk or bailiff. They may verify your name and ask if you’ve talked to the other party about settlement. In some counties, a volunteer mediator may be present offering last-minute mediation (e.g., “Would you like to step outside and try to settle?”). This is optional. If both parties agree, they can attempt mediation on site. If it fails, you come back in to have the hearing. 
- **Documents/Evidence:** Bring **3 copies** of any evidence: one for you, one for the judge, one for the opponent. Typical evidence includes: contracts, receipts, estimates, photographs, texts or emails (print them out), relevant account statements, etc. Organize them and, if possible, bring a sheet listing what you have (“Exhibit 1: Contract; Exhibit 2: Photo of damage; …”). Some judges will mark exhibits, others just look at them informally. Keep originals separate and give copies to the court and opponent when you present them. If you have electronic evidence (like a video), try to have it on paper or screenshots. Some courts might allow showing phone pictures, but it’s better to have printed photos. The rules of evidence are relaxed – judges often consider hearsay if it seems reliable. But firsthand evidence is given more weight. If say a mechanic gave you a written statement about a car’s condition, that’s technically hearsay but many referees will read it and consider it with a grain of salt (since the mechanic isn’t there for cross-exam). If something is crucial and hearsay, try to get the person in court or subpoena them.
- **Witnesses:** If you have witnesses who saw or know something relevant, you can bring them to testify. Each witness will be sworn in. Because hearings are short (often scheduled 10–20 minutes apart), only bring witnesses that are truly necessary. If a witness can’t attend voluntarily, you could subpoena them (see Service of Process on how to subpoena). But weigh the benefit – small claims judges often prefer firsthand accounts from the actual parties. If an expert opinion is needed (like a contractor to say a repair was bad), either bring the expert or at least a written estimate or statement. Minnesota allows “subpoena duces tecum” (for documents) so you could subpoena records (like get phone records if needed to prove calls), but again, in small claims that’s not commonly done pre-hearing. 
- **Hearing Procedure:** The judge will call the case name (you and defendant go up to the table or podium). Both parties (and any witnesses) will be placed under oath to tell the truth. Usually the **plaintiff speaks first**, since they have the burden of proof. You as plaintiff should concisely tell **what happened, why the defendant owes you money, and how much**. Focus on key facts, not extraneous detail. It’s wise to practice a 2-3 minute opening statement:
   - Example: “Your Honor, I am suing because the defendant breached our contract. On March 1, 2023, I paid the defendant $500 to paint my garage (here is the signed agreement) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=have%20the%20following%20information%3A)). He never performed the work nor refunded me. I have a copy of the canceled check I gave him and texts where he acknowledged receiving payment and then later refused to come. I am asking for $500 plus my $85 filing and service costs.”
  The judge may interrupt with questions. They often ask the defendant, “Is this true? What’s your side?” Then the **defendant responds** with their version: maybe they dispute the facts or have some defense (“I did the work, but the plaintiff wasn’t satisfied” or “I didn’t get any money” or “The product I sold was fine; it was the plaintiff who broke it,” etc.).
  Then both can engage in a bit of back-and-forth, guided by the judge. The judge will usually allow the plaintiff to rebut the defendant’s points.
- **Presenting Evidence:** As you tell your story, you can present documents or photos at the appropriate times. Some judges prefer you hand them everything at once at the start; others will ask for them as needed. Follow the judge’s lead. Always explain what a document is before handing it over: “This is a photo of the damage to my car after the accident” or “This is the invoice showing how much I paid.” The judge may look at it and also glance to see if the other party has any objection. Most conciliation evidence is straightforward – objections on grounds like hearsay or relevance are rare. If the other side says “That’s not accurate” or “That number is wrong,” the judge will note that and weigh credibility.
- **Witness Testimony:** If you brought a witness, you would tell the judge, “I have a witness, John Doe, who was present during the transaction” and the judge will swear them in and hear their short statement. Let the witness speak to what they directly observed or know. Judges may ask witnesses questions directly.
- **Courtroom Etiquette:** Even though informal, do not interrupt the judge or the opponent. Each side will get a turn. Speak clearly and calmly. Address your remarks to the judge, not the opponent (e.g., say “Your Honor, he did X” not “You did X!”). If the opponent is speaking and you disagree, wait – you’ll get a chance to respond. Keep your cool; emotional outbursts or rudeness will hurt your case. If you don’t understand something the judge asks, politely say you don’t understand. If you’re nervous (common in small claims), just remember the judge has likely seen hundreds of people like you – they expect some nervousness and will try to help along. 
- **Burden of Proof:** The burden is on the plaintiff to prove their case by the civil standard, **preponderance of evidence** (more likely than not). So as plaintiff, you want to make it >50% likely in the judge’s mind that your claim is valid. If it’s a close call, whoever has the burden might lose. The defendant, if raising a counterclaim or an affirmative defense, has burden on those aspects. For instance, if the defendant claims “the product was fine, and any issue was due to plaintiff’s misuse,” that’s an argument; you don’t have to disprove every hypothetical – you just have to make your story more credible. The judge may spontaneously sort out credibility by asking each of you questions. Answer directly and truthfully. 
- **No Formal Rules of Evidence:** Conciliation Court is not strict on evidence rules ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.02#:~:text=The%20determination%20of%20claims%20in,that%20has%20been%20properly%20completed)). Hearsay (second-hand statements) is usually permitted to some extent, though it may be given less weight. For example, you can submit an estimate from a mechanic who isn’t present; the court will accept it as evidence of cost, though it might carry less weight than if the mechanic testified. Judges have broad discretion to consider any evidence that reasonable people would find reliable ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.02#:~:text=The%20determination%20of%20claims%20in,that%20has%20been%20properly%20completed)). So don’t worry about saying “He told me…” – in regular court hearsay might be objected to, but in conciliation likely allowed.
- **Length of Hearing:** Typically, each case might be scheduled in a 10-15 minute slot. Complex cases might get a bit longer or be set last on the calendar. Some judges try to get through many in an hour. So it’s important to focus on the key points. If you start digressing into irrelevant details, the judge may cut you off or refocus you with a question. Come prepared with a concise narrative and relevant evidence to back it.
- **Attitude:** Be honest. Judges do this a lot and can sense exaggeration. If you don’t know an answer, say so. If the defendant says something true that doesn’t hurt your case much, you can acknowledge it. That builds credibility. Conversely, if you get caught in a lie or contradiction, you’ll likely lose. 
- **After Both Sides Speak:** The judge may ask each if they have anything more to add or any other evidence. Make sure you’ve introduced all your important items. This is your last chance to mention, for example, “I’m also asking for interest” or “Don’t forget my filing fee cost.” Judges know to add costs, but a gentle reminder is fine.
- **Decision – Immediately or Later:** In many cases, the judge will not announce a decision on the spot. They often take the case under advisement and mail the decision (Order for Judgment) within a week or two. Some judges might give a hint or an outright decision verbally if the case is very clear. But generally expect to get the judgment in the mail. Minnesota law requires the court to issue its decision within 20 days after the hearing in conciliation cases ([zmattorneys.com](https://zmattorneys.com/small-claims-court-limit-raised/#:~:text=represent%20themselves%20and%20most%20business,appeals%20it%20within%2030%20days)) (in practice, usually much quicker).
- **If You Win at Hearing:** If the judge issues a judgment for you, it will be mailed as an official notice (often called “Conciliation Court Findings of Fact and Order for Judgment”). It tells the amount awarded and maybe some notes. The judgment is not final for **23 days** (the appeal period: 20 days plus 3 days for mailing) ([zmattorneys.com](https://zmattorneys.com/small-claims-court-limit-raised/#:~:text=and%20less%20expensive%20than%20for,district%20court)). If the defendant doesn’t appeal in that time, you can then enforce it (see Post-Judgment section for enforcement steps). Sometimes the judge may encourage parties to step outside and settle even after hearing but before judgment is issued – it’s uncommon, but occasionally if it feels like a close case, a judge might ask “Would you two like a few minutes to discuss settlement outside?” You’re not obliged to settle; you can insist on a judgment.
- **If You Lose:** If the judgment is not in your favor (claim dismissed or judgment for defendant on a counterclaim), and you disagree, you have **the right to appeal (“remove”) to District Court** within 30 days of mailing of judgment ([zmattorneys.com](https://zmattorneys.com/small-claims-court-limit-raised/#:~:text=entity%20to%20represent%20it%20in,appeals%20it%20within%2030%20days)). On appeal, it’s a brand-new trial (trial de novo) in front of a District Court Judge, with formal procedures. We’ll cover appeals next. Note: a plaintiff who loses cannot refile the same claim in conciliation (it’s res judicata); the only recourse is appeal. So take the conciliation hearing seriously – it is your “day in court” for that claim, short of an appeal.

- **Failure to Appear by Plaintiff:** If as plaintiff you miss the hearing, likely a dismissal is entered (often “with prejudice” meaning you can’t bring it again, unless the judge specifies without prejudice for some reason). You could file a Motion to Vacate that dismissal if you had good cause (similar to defendant vacating a default) – you’d need to act within 20 days and explain your absence. Judges are slightly more forgiving if a plaintiff no-shows due to emergency, but if it’s simply forgetfulness, they might not restore it. Always call the court asap if you realize you might miss a hearing.

- **Court Atmosphere:** Typically, multiple cases are scheduled in the same session (like 10 cases at 1:00 p.m.). So you may watch a few other small hearings while you wait your turn. This can actually be helpful to get a feel for how the referee handles things. Some cases might settle or one side might not show, making them go quickly. Be patient.

- **Dress Code:** There is no strict dress code, but you should dress neatly to show respect (business casual is fine – e.g., collared shirt, nice pants; avoid shorts, flip flops, tank tops). Remove hats in courtroom. Turn off your phone before entering.

- **Covid / Remote Hearings:** In recent times, courts sometimes hold remote hearings via phone or Zoom. As of 2026, many conciliation courts returned to in-person, but some may allow remote appearances on request. If your hearing notice offers a remote option or is scheduled by Zoom, treat it seriously: test your tech, join from a quiet space, have your documents ready to email or show. If you prefer in-person and only remote is offered, you can ask the court if an in-person option is available (not guaranteed).

In summary, the conciliation hearing is an informal but structured opportunity to present your case. It usually lasts only minutes, so preparation and sticking to the point are key. The judge will guide the process with questions. As long as you are truthful, organized, and civil, you’ll have done your part – then the decision lies in the judge’s hands.

## 11. Appeals

Either party who is dissatisfied with the Conciliation Court judgment has a right to appeal for a new trial in a higher court (the District Court of the county). In Minnesota this process is called “**removal to District Court**” and results in a **trial de novo** (completely new trial as if the first never happened) ([zmattorneys.com](https://zmattorneys.com/small-claims-court-limit-raised/#:~:text=and%20less%20expensive%20than%20for,district%20court)) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=After%20a%20contested%20trial%2C%20a%C2%A0party,the%20judgment%20debtor%20or%20creditor)). Here are the key points about appeals:

- **Who Can Appeal:** Generally, *either party* may appeal, but only a party who is “aggrieved” or who *appeared* at the conciliation hearing can do so. If you’re the **defendant and you lost** (judgment against you), you can appeal. If you’re the **plaintiff and your claim was dismissed or you were awarded less than you sought**, you can appeal. If you defaulted (didn’t show up) in conciliation, you **cannot directly appeal** – you first must successfully move to vacate the default (as discussed in section 9). So, for appeals, assume it’s for cases that were actually contested at the hearing ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=After%20a%20contested%20trial%2C%20a%C2%A0party,the%20judgment%20debtor%20or%20creditor)). Additionally, if only one side appeared and the other defaulted, the defaulting party’s remedy is motion to vacate, not appeal ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,for%20a%20new%20hearing%20date)). So appeals are mainly after a trial on the merits.
- **Deadline to Appeal:** You must act **within 20 days** of the Court Administrator serving the notice of judgment (which is typically done by mail) ([zmattorneys.com](https://zmattorneys.com/small-claims-court-limit-raised/#:~:text=entity%20to%20represent%20it%20in,appeals%20it%20within%2030%20days)). Actually, the rule states within 20 days “after the date the clerk mailed notice of judgment to the parties.” However, Minnesota also provides an extra 3 days for mailing, making it effectively 23 days from the date of mailing ([zmattorneys.com](https://zmattorneys.com/small-claims-court-limit-raised/#:~:text=entity%20to%20represent%20it%20in,appeals%20it%20within%2030%20days)). To be safe, assume 20 days. The exact deadline date is often written on the judgment forms (e.g., “Removal (appeal) must be filed by [date].”). Do not miss this deadline – if you do, the conciliation judgment becomes final and conclusive.
- **How to Appeal (Removal Procedure):** To appeal, you must file a **“Demand for Removal / Affidavit of Good Faith”** with the Court Administrator ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,vacated)). The court provides a form for this. On that form, you’ll state that you (the appealing party) “remove” the case to District Court for trial de novo, and you swear the appeal is made in good faith and not just for delay ([zmattorneys.com](https://zmattorneys.com/small-claims-court-limit-raised/#:~:text=One%20item%20to%20be%20wary,6)) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,vacated)). You file this in the same county court office (the conciliation court clerk). They will then assign a new District Court case number.
- **Appeal Filing Fees and Costs:** The appealing party must pay the **District Court civil filing fee** (which is much higher than the conciliation fee). As of 2026, the fee is $310 for a bench trial appeal, or $410 if a jury trial is demanded ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/help-topics/court-fees/district-court-fees?cat=conciliation&cookiecheck=true#:~:text=Removal%20,10%2C%20General%20Rule%20521%28b)). These amounts include the base fee and possibly a law library fee, similar to normal civil cases. In addition, Minnesota requires the appellant to pay a small **removal fee to conciliation** (usually around $5 or so) and possibly post a **cost bond** or filing a **cost bond affidavit**. Specifically, Minn. R. Gen. Pract. 521(b) says a bond of $50 (unless waived by the court) must be provided to cover potential respondent costs if appellant loses ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/help-topics/court-fees/district-court-fees?cat=conciliation&cookiecheck=true#:~:text=Removal%20,10%2C%20General%20Rule%20521%28b)). In practice, many appellants deposit $50 cash or a surety bond or have an attorney’s guarantee. Check with the court – some waive the bond requirement if the appellant is an individual unable to afford it (IFP status can extend to appeals). If you can’t afford the appeal fee, you could apply for an IFP for the appeal as well (but that’s rarely granted for appeals unless you had IFP initially).
- **Serve Notice on Other Party:** The appellant must **serve a copy of the Demand for Removal on the other party** (or their attorney) and file proof of service ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,Pay%20the%C2%A0filing%20fee)). This is typically done by mail – often the court clerk will mail a notice of removal to the other side once you file, but technically the rule says you serve it. The form has a place for an Affidavit of Service by mail. Make sure to send a copy to your opponent on the same day you file, and then fill out that affidavit (or have someone else mail it and sign).
- **Trial De Novo in District Court:** Once removal is perfected (fee paid, documents filed timely), the Conciliation Court judgment is **vacated** (nullified) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=accordance%20with%20the%20provisions%20for,Pay%20the%C2%A0filing%20fee)). The case is now docketed as a regular civil case in District Court. It will effectively start anew: the claim from conciliation becomes a new complaint (often the conciliation statement of claim serves as the complaint, but sometimes the judge may require a more formal complaint – usually not, however, they accept the conciliation claim as sufficient). The case proceeds under the **Minnesota Rules of Civil Procedure** from that point on ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,legal%20advice%20if%20you%20have)). That means:
    - If you didn’t have a lawyer in conciliation, consider if you need one now, because district court is more formal with motions, discovery, etc., especially if the amount is large or the issues complex.
    - The court will set a scheduling order or at least a trial date. Often, appeals from conciliation are put on the trial calendar fairly quickly (maybe a few months out) since they’re usually simple cases. However, some might be subject to standard procedure including an answer filing by defendant, etc. In many counties, when you appeal, the court will mail both parties a notice instructing the original plaintiff to file a formal complaint within 30 days and the original defendant to answer it within 20 days after, unless they treat the conciliation claim and counterclaim as the pleadings already. It’s important to read the District Court’s instructions carefully.
    - **Business Representation:** In District Court, a corporate entity or LLC *must* be represented by a licensed attorney (except in very limited small exceptions) ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=or%20manager%2C%20only%20in%20Conciliation,the%20General%20Rules%20of%20Practice)) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,legal%20advice%20if%20you%20have)). So if a business was using a non-lawyer in conciliation, they now need a lawyer to continue the case on appeal. Often, this deters some appeals by businesses if the amount is small, because the cost of an attorney might outweigh the judgment.
    - **Jury Trial Option:** On the removal form, the appellant can request a jury trial (with the additional $100 fee) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/help-topics/court-fees/district-court-fees?cat=conciliation&cookiecheck=true#:~:text=Removal%20,10%2C%20General%20Rule%20521%28b)). If neither party requests a jury, the new trial will be a bench trial before a judge. Most conciliation appeals remain bench trials because the sums are small. But a party has a right to a jury for amounts over $750 (Minnesota’s threshold; small claims typically exceed that anyway) if they demand it on time (the removal form or shortly after).
- **Scope of Trial De Novo:** It’s as if the first trial never happened. You can present new evidence, make new arguments, even increase or adjust the claim (though usually you can’t increase beyond conciliation limits – but now you’re in district court which has no $20k cap, theoretically the plaintiff could amend to seek more if justified, except some cases say you can’t “add” to the amount sought in removal beyond what was claimed originally). For safety, assume you should not expand the claim beyond what was originally at issue, as removal is meant to re-try the same controversy.
- **Outcome of Appeal:** The District Court will issue a new judgment which replaces the conciliation judgment. That could either affirm what happened before, or change it. If the appellant (whoever appealed) loses again, that’s the end of the road in most cases – you had your two chances. There is technically a possibility to appeal further to the Minnesota Court of Appeals, but that is **not** an appeal by right for a conciliation matter *unless* there is a legal issue, because the trial de novo is considered you’ve had your “appeal.” You could only appeal further on some legal error from the district court trial (and you’d need a transcript, etc. – likely not practical for small sums).
- **Costs on Appeal:** If the party who appealed (removed) does worse in District Court than they did in conciliation, the court may award **costs** to the other party. For example, if a defendant appeals and in the new trial the plaintiff wins the same or more, the defendant might be ordered to pay the plaintiff’s costs or even attorney fees (if the judge finds the appeal was frivolous or in bad faith). The $50 cost bond posted can be applied to those costs ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/help-topics/court-fees/district-court-fees?cat=conciliation&cookiecheck=true#:~:text=Plaintiff%20%2F%20Petitioner%20and%20Defendant,10)). Conversely, if the appellant does better (like a plaintiff who got zero in conciliation but wins something on appeal, or a defendant reduces the judgment amount), usually each bears their own costs except regular taxable costs like filing fees get awarded to the prevailing party.
- **Settlement on Appeal:** Once appealed, parties often settle to avoid the formal trial. The District Court may schedule a mediation or encourage ADR since it’s a normal civil case now (some counties require non-binding arbitration or mediation for small cases).
- **Withdrawal of Appeal:** If the appellant changes their mind, they can withdraw the appeal before the new trial. However, be aware if you withdraw your removal, the original conciliation judgment is typically reinstated and becomes final. You might need consent of other party or a court order to dismiss the entire case if settlement occurs. If a case is settled post-removal, often the settlement includes dismissing the appeal (which leaves no enforceable judgment if it’s a full settlement payment, or may involve entering a stipulated judgment).
- **Timing:** An appeal will delay final resolution by some months. If you’re a respondent (the one who won in conciliation) but now facing an appeal, you have to wait out the appeal to enforce. The conciliation judgment is stayed (not enforceable) once removal is filed.
- **Example:** You sued for $5,000, won in conciliation. Defendant appeals. In District Court, a judge or jury hears it all over. Say you win $5,000 again. Now you have a district court judgment – you can enforce that; the conciliation judgment is superseded. If instead the judge awards you only $3,000, that $3,000 is the final judgment (it replaces the earlier $5k, effectively the appeal was partly successful for defendant).
- **No Partial Appeals:** Removal applies to the entire case. One cannot appeal just part of it (like just liability and not damages). It’s an “all or nothing” new trial. However, practically, if only one party appeals, the whole case is up, but the other could choose not to participate vigorously. Still, outcome can change for either side.
- **Joint Appeals:** If both sides are unhappy (say plaintiff wanted more money, defendant wanted to pay nothing), either can appeal. Only one appeal is necessary to get it to district court. Once there, both can argue their issues. For instance, plaintiff can effectively seek an increase via its arguments even if defendant filed the removal.

**Important:** If you are the **respondent** (non-appealing party) in an appeal, **do not ignore the appeal**. If you skip the district court trial, a default judgment could be entered against you, wiping out the conciliation result. You must participate as if the first trial never happened. If you had a lawyer in conciliation, you’ll definitely need one in district (and maybe the same one will continue). If you didn’t, consider whether you can handle the formal process. For straightforward disputes you often can.

To summarize:
- You have **30 days from notice of judgment (20 + 3 mailing) to file an appeal** ([zmattorneys.com](https://zmattorneys.com/small-claims-court-limit-raised/#:~:text=entity%20to%20represent%20it%20in,appeals%20it%20within%2030%20days)).
- File a **Demand for Removal/Affidavit of Good Faith** and pay the **required fees and bond** ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/help-topics/court-fees/district-court-fees?cat=conciliation&cookiecheck=true#:~:text=Plaintiff%20%2F%20Petitioner%20and%20Defendant,10)).
- Serve notice of appeal on opponent.
- Prepare for a **new trial** in District Court with formal rules. Possibly get legal counsel, especially if the amount or principles are significant.
- The conciliation judgment is on hold pending the outcome.
- The new judgment can be further appealed to Court of Appeals only on legal grounds (rare for small claims).
- If no appeal is taken within the deadline, the Conciliation Court judgment becomes final.

Most cases do *not* get appealed because of the hassle and cost. However, if you strongly believe the conciliation judge erred and the amount justifies it, the removal process gives you a fresh shot at justice.

## 12. Post-Judgment Collection

Winning a judgment is one thing – actually getting paid is another. The court does **not** collect the money for you ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=IMPORTANT%3A%20Winning%20a%20case%20in,filing%20fees%20and%20other%20costs)). In fact, many small-claims winners have to take active steps to enforce the judgment. Minnesota provides several tools for judgment creditors (the winning party) to collect, similar to normal civil judgments:

**When Judgment Becomes Final:** A Conciliation Court judgment becomes final once the appeal period expires (typically 20 days after notice of judgment, plus 3 days for mailing) ([zmattorneys.com](https://zmattorneys.com/small-claims-court-limit-raised/#:~:text=entity%20to%20represent%20it%20in,appeals%20it%20within%2030%20days)). If the defendant didn’t appeal or overturn it, the court administrator will “enter” and “docket” the judgment. You can check with the court if the judgment is now docketed (entered into the official record). The judgment is valid for **10 years** from entry, and can be renewed for another 10 by filing a new action before it expires ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/minnesota/chapters-540-552/chapter-541/section-541-04/#:~:text=Minnesota%20Statutes%20Section%20541,No%20action%20shall%20be)).

**Voluntary Payment:** Often, if the debtor is amenable, you might receive payment without enforcement. You can send a letter or invoice right after judgment saying “Please pay this amount by [date] to avoid further collection steps or interest accrual.” Some debtors will comply to avoid wage garnishment or credit issues.

If the debtor doesn’t pay, here are the main enforcement methods:

- **Docketing and Judgment Lien on Real Property:** By law, once the judgment is transcribed to District Court (either automatically if conciliation is a division of district court, or by your request), and **docketed**, it becomes a lien on any real property the debtor owns in that county ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Judgment%3A%20Docketing)). 
  - In Minnesota, Conciliation Court judgments are effectively part of the district court, but often you need to ensure it’s “entered on the judgment roll” a.k.a. docketed. Many counties automatically docket conciliation judgments in the district court system (especially if the amount is above a threshold like $3,000 or if property involved). 
  - To lien property in other counties, you can get a certified copy of the judgment and file it (“transcribe”) in another county’s District Court. Minnesota statutes explicitly allow transcribing a conciliation judgment to district court of another county ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=certain%20real%20estate%20owned%20by,District%20Court%20in%20another%20county)). Once docketed there, it’s a lien in that county.
  - The lien lasts for 10 years (the life of the judgment) and encumbers any real estate (house, land) the debtor has in that county. If they try to sell or refinance, they’ll have to pay off the judgment to clear title.
  - **How to docket**: In many counties, after 30 days, conciliation judgments are automatically docketed in the court’s system if not paid. In some, you might have to request “judgment docketing” or pay a small fee (like $40) to transcribe to district court. Check your judgment notice; it often gives instructions if any. The judgment creditor may also ask the court administrator for a “transcript of judgment” to file in another county – often a small fee ($10) for the certified copy.
- **Financial Disclosure from Debtor:** Minnesota law (Minn. Stat. § 491A.02, subd. 9) allows a judgment creditor to request that the judgment debtor fill out a **Financial Disclosure form** listing their employer, income, bank accounts, assets, etc., as soon as the judgment is final ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Judgment%3A%20Docketing)). Practically, the creditor can request the court to issue an “Order for Disclosure.” Many Conciliation Court judgment notices will include a warning to the debtor: if you don’t pay, you must send a financial disclosure within 16 days of creditor’s demand or face penalty. 
  - To invoke this, you (creditor) can file a simple request with court or sometimes it’s automatic. E.g. Hennepin Conciliation provides if no appeal after 23 days, the creditor can ask the court for an Order for Disclosure for free – the court then mails the debtor a form that they must complete and return within 16 days to you ([www.house.mn.gov](https://www.house.mn.gov/cco/journals/2009-10/J0416034.htm#:~:text=2009%20www.house.mn.gov%20%20...,to%20enable%20the%20judgment%20creditor)).
  - If the debtor fails to comply, you can ask the court for an order to show cause (which could lead to contempt of court). Also, a *driver’s license* suspension is possible for non-payment of a motor vehicle accident judgment after 30 days ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=risk%20losing%20their%20license,contact%20the%C2%A0MN%20Driver%20and%20Vehicle)), but that’s separate from disclosure.
  - The disclosure form typically asks about employment, bank accounts, real property, personal property of significant value, etc. This info is valuable to decide which collection method to use.
- **Wage Garnishment:** If the debtor is employed, you can garnish their wages. Minnesota follows federal limits: generally up to 25% of disposable earnings can be taken, or the amount by which weekly disposable earnings exceed 40 times the federal minimum wage – whichever is less ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/571.922#:~:text=Sec,pay%20period%20subjected%20to%20garnishment)). (This is Minn. Stat. § 571.922, limiting garnishment ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/571.922#:~:text=Sec,pay%20period%20subjected%20to%20garnishment)).) If the debtor is low-income, you might get little or nothing (e.g., if they make under about $12/hr full-time, basically 40*minwage, their wages might be largely exempt). 
  - **Procedure:** First, you must obtain a **Writ of Execution** from the court administrator for the unpaid judgment ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=Subd)). In conciliation, after judgment is transcribed to district court, you apply for a Writ (there’s a fee ~$55). The Writ is a court document directing the sheriff to satisfy the judgment from the debtor’s assets. For wage garnishment, some use a separate **Garnishment Summons** procedure under Chapter 571: Minnesota allows an attorney (or creditor, if pro se) to issue a garnishment summons to the employer (no prior court motion needed since you already have a judgment) ([caselaw.findlaw.com](https://caselaw.findlaw.com/us-8th-circuit/1365145.html#:~:text=Minnesota%27s%20worthless%20check%20statute%20provides,71%282%29%20%28emphasis)). The employer then withholds wages and sends them either to the creditor or to the sheriff. 
  - Typically, you’d fill out a **Garnishment Exemption Notice** and **Garnishment Summons** and serve them on the employer and debtor (the debtor is served a notice of their rights). If the debtor doesn’t claim an exemption or doesn’t contest, the employer will start remitting wages (often the first garnishment can capture wages up to 70 days). 
  - You can ask the Court Self-Help for a packet on garnishing wages – it includes forms and instructions.
  - Minnesota law requires that the debtor be left with at least a certain amount of take-home pay (the 40x minwage rule ensures roughly at least ~$370 per week is protected, since min wage $9.86*40 = $394, or if wages are lower than that, can’t garnish at all) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/571.922#:~:text=Sec,pay%20period%20subjected%20to%20garnishment)). Child support or tax debts have higher allowances, but for your civil judgment, 25% is the max.
- **Bank Account Levy (Bank Garnishment):** If you know the debtor’s bank account, you can try to levy it. Process is similar: you issue a Garnishment Summons to the bank along with an Exemption notice. The bank will freeze funds up to the judgment amount. Minnesota law automatically protects certain direct-deposited funds in bank accounts (like Social Security, some government benefits, some wages) – banks are required to auto-exempt some benefits from garnishment (since 2012 federal rules). If the funds are regular wages or other non-exempt, the bank will freeze them. The debtor has 14 days to claim any exemptions. If none are claimed, the bank releases the money to you (via sheriff or directly, depending on method).
  - You usually need a Writ of Execution for a bank levy handled by the sheriff, or if attorneys handle garnishments, they often bypass needing the sheriff by using the garnishment summons method. As a pro se, you might prefer to involve the sheriff for bank levies: you give the sheriff the Writ, bank name, account info if known (account number, or at least name and branch), and the sheriff serves the bank with the Writ and levy instructions. The sheriff then receives any funds and will send them to you after deducting their fee ($15).
  - Many Minnesota counties have a packet "How to garnish bank accounts" – follow it to ensure compliance with required exemption notices (Minn. Stat. § 571.912 and § 571.914 require giving debtor a notice and form to claim exemptions).
  - **Exempt Funds:** Minnesota exempts quite a lot: social security, unemployment, workers comp, certain pensions, disability, certain amount of equity in home/car, etc. If the debtor’s money in the account came from those sources, they can claim exemption and you won’t get it ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/550.37#:~:text=,subsequent%20attachment%2C%20garnishment%2C%20or%20levy)) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/1998/cite/541.07#:~:text=,other%20than%20cash%2C%20where%20the)). Up to $1,260 of deposits in a bank within last 60 days traceable to exempt sources are automatically protected. The bank only freezes amounts above that if source might have been exempt. It’s complicated but bottom line: if the debtor lives on government benefits only, you likely won’t collect from their account.
- **Seizing Personal Property (Levy/Till Tap):** If the debtor owns valuable non-exempt personal property (like a boat, a second car that’s not exempt, expensive tools beyond exemption limit, etc.), the sheriff can seize and sell them under a Writ of Execution. This is less commonly done for small judgments because it’s labor intensive and sometimes the property is exempt up to a value (e.g., one motor vehicle up to about $5,000 in value is exempt ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/550.37#:~:text=,subsequent%20attachment%2C%20garnishment%2C%20or%20levy)), tools of trade up to $12,500 are exempt ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/550.37#:~:text=,subsequent%20attachment%2C%20garnishment%2C%20or%20levy)), basic household goods, etc.). But if, say, the debtor has a fancy sports car and owes you $10k, you could direct the sheriff to levy that car (if the equity is above the ~ $5,000 exemption and if there’s no loan or you are ready to pay it off through sale).
  - “Till Tap”: This refers to the sheriff going to a business that the debtor owns and taking cash from the register. For example, if your debtor runs a restaurant, you could instruct the sheriff to do a till tap at the restaurant. You’d need a Writ and coordinate with sheriff. They show up, take cash on hand up to the judgment amount. A variation is a "continuing garnishment" on a business's receipts, but MN doesn’t specifically allow taking a percentage of revenues, just lumps that are currently on hand (like doing repeated till taps).
  - **Execution Sale:** The sheriff can seize non-exempt property and auction it. For small claims, this is rare because items often either fall under exemptions or

  - **Property Exemptions:** Be aware Minnesota law exempts a lot of a debtor’s property from execution ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/550.37#:~:text=550)) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/550.37#:~:text=impound%20only%20that%20pay%20period%27s,Garnishments%20shall)). Common exemptions include a homestead (up to a certain value, around $450,000 for a home in 2026), one motor vehicle up to about $5,000 value ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/550.37#:~:text=If%20a%20debtor%20has%20property,taken%2C%20and%20a%20creditor%20may)) (more if the debtor is disabled), personal household goods up to $11,250, tools of trade up to $12,500 ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/550.37#:~:text=If%20a%20debtor%20has%20property,taken%2C%20and%20a%20creditor%20may)), and absolutely all government assistance, Social Security, and certain pensions ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/550.37#:~:text=match%20at%20L296%20who%20is,is%20not%20limited%20to%20Minnesota)) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/550.37#:~:text=who%20is%20a%20recipient%20of,is%20not%20limited%20to%20Minnesota)). Also, a portion of earned but unpaid wages (75% or more) is exempt ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/550.37#:~:text=impound%20only%20that%20pay%20period%27s,Garnishments%20shall)), and even after deposit in a bank, a portion remains exempt for 20 days ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/550.37#:~:text=employer%20when%20due,in%20which%20the%20earnings%20are)). So, if the debtor truly has low income and basic assets, they may be “judgment-proof,” meaning you can’t legally take those assets. In that case, the judgment might be uncollectible until their situation improves (you have 10 years, and can renew for another 10, so you might wait and monitor if they come into money).
  - **Credit Reporting:** A judgment will typically appear on the debtor’s credit report. Sometimes the mere existence of the judgment will motivate them to pay eventually (especially if they want to clear it for a loan). Once they pay, you as the creditor must file a **Satisfaction of Judgment** with the court to officially record it’s paid ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Legal%20Advice%20Clinic)), and that helps their credit.

If you’re the **debtor** (losing party), note that Minnesota offers some protections:
  - If you can’t afford to pay at once, you can try to work out a payment plan with the creditor. Some creditors will agree to installment payments rather than pursue garnishment. If they do garnish wages, you might be able to fill out a **Debtor’s Exemption Claim** for certain income or make a **Motion for Installment Payments** in District Court to limit what they can take (Minnesota allows a debtor to apply for a court order to pay in installments, which if granted can stop garnishments as long as you pay per the order).
  - Never ignore a Financial Disclosure order or a notice of garnishment—respond and assert exemptions if applicable (for example, if your only income is SSI or certain retirement funds, you must claim that so the creditor or bank knows those are protected by law ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/550.37#:~:text=match%20at%20L296%20who%20is,is%20not%20limited%20to%20Minnesota))).
  - The law forbids firing an employee due to a single garnishment (but multiple garnishments can risk employment; pay voluntarily if possible to avoid that).
  
**Priority of Liens and Multiple Creditors:** If the debtor has multiple judgments or liens:
  - Real estate liens take priority by the date they were docketed. So if you docketed your judgment first, you’re ahead of later judgments.
  - Wage garnishments: they generally take priority in order served ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/550.37#:~:text=match%20at%20L276%20impound%20the,earnings%20are%20payable%20by%20the)). Only one garnishment runs at a time up to the 25% cap; others wait in line.
  - Bank levies: typically the one that hits the account first gets whatever is there at that time (subsequent levies catch future deposits, but note that the law protects the last 20 days of wages deposited ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/550.37#:~:text=employer%20when%20due,in%20which%20the%20earnings%20are))).

**Renewal of Judgment:** A judgment can be enforced for 10 years. If not paid by then, you can file a new action to renew it for another 10 before it expires (essentially suing on the judgment or filing a motion in district court). Don’t let it expire if still unpaid.

**Keep Records:** Document any amounts collected or paid because if partial payments are made, you must credit them and, when fully satisfied, ensure a Satisfaction of Judgment is filed ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Volunteers%20from%20the%C2%A0Volunteer%20Lawyers%20Network,on%20appeals%20to%20District%20Court)). Also, docketing, transcripts, writ issuances all involve small fees which can usually be added to the judgment as costs.

Collecting a judgment can be the hardest part. Use the tools systematically:
1. Get the debtor’s financial info (through disclosure or your own research).
2. Go after wages or bank accounts if available, keeping in mind exemptions.
3. Place a lien on any property – you might get paid when they sell or refinance.
4. Be patient and follow up. Judgments accrue interest annually (currently around 4% simple interest ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/549.09#:~:text=,to%20the%20judgment%20or%20award))), so the amount will grow if they don’t pay, giving some incentive to pay sooner.

If all attempts fail, you may consider hiring a collection attorney or agency (they will take a percentage but might have more means to track assets). However, weigh that against the judgment amount.

In summary, **post-judgment, the court won’t chase the debtor for you** ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=IMPORTANT%3A%20Winning%20a%20case%20in,filing%20fees%20and%20other%20costs)). But Minnesota law equips you with:
- Judgment liens on real estate ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Judgment%3A%20Docketing)).
- Wage garnishment (typically up to 25% of take-home pay) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/550.37#:~:text=match%20at%20L276%20impound%20the,earnings%20are%20payable%20by%20the)).
- Bank account garnishment (for non-exempt funds).
- Sheriff execution against non-exempt personal property.
- Ability to compel the debtor’s financial disclosure under oath ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Judgment%3A%20Docketing)).
- Continual accrual of interest on the judgment until paid ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/549.09#:~:text=,to%20the%20judgment%20or%20award)).

Using these, you improve your chances of getting what’s owed. Many creditors do get paid eventually, especially if the debtor is steadily employed or owns property. Just be persistent and knowledgeable about the process (and the debtor’s rights as well). 

## 13. Special Rules for Businesses

If the plaintiff or defendant in a small claims case is a business entity (corporation, LLC, partnership, etc.), there are some special considerations in Minnesota:

- **Representation in Court:** In Conciliation Court, a business *can* appear without an attorney, but only through a duly authorized officer or agent. Minnesota explicitly allows a corporation, partnership, or LLC to be represented by an **officer, manager, or partner, or an authorized employee** in Conciliation Court ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.02#:~:text=Subd)). The business must file a **Power of Attorney** authorizing that person to act in court for the business ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Conciliation%20Court%20and%20District%20Court,See%20the)). (This is not a power of attorney for other uses, just a court authorization form.) For example, an LLC can have its owner or a managerial employee go to court on its behalf as long as that form is filed. **However, if the case is appealed to District Court, the business must be represented by a licensed attorney**; an officer or employee cannot handle the trial de novo ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=A%20business%20or%20association%20may,the%20General%20Rules%20of%20Practice)) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,legal%20advice%20if%20you%20have)).

- **Number of Cases Limit:** Unlike some states, Minnesota does not impose a strict numeric limit on how many small claims a business (or anyone) can file per year. (California, for instance, limits a frequent-filer business to two claims over $2,500 each year – Minnesota has no such rule.) A business can file any number of conciliation claims, as long as each claim is within jurisdiction and filed in good faith (not harassing). That being said, if a company is using conciliation to mass-collect debts, the court might scrutinize that they’re filed in the correct venue and that an actual agent can attend all those hearings.

- **Assumed Name / Trade Name issues:** If a business operates under an assumed name (DBA) that is not a separate legal entity, the proper party to sue or be sued is the underlying person or entity. For example, “Joe’s Plumbing” (not an LLC or corp) is just Joe operating under a name, so the suit should name **Joe Smith d/b/a Joe’s Plumbing**. If you sue just the trade name, you technically haven’t sued anyone legally. The MN Conciliation form will ask for defendant’s name and maybe “doing business as” if applicable. As a business plaintiff, ensure you are named correctly too. Minnesota requires businesses operating under an assumed name to file a certificate with the Secretary of State ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/333.01#:~:text=No%20person%20shall%20hereafter%20carry,corporation%2C%20incorporated%2C%20limited%2C%20chartered%2C%20professional)), but failing that doesn’t bar them from suing – although if a business hasn’t filed its assumed name, it might cause confusion. It’s good practice to always identify the legal entity. If you’re unsure of a business’s legal status, check the Secretary of State’s online business search.

- **Foreign (Out-of-State) Businesses:** Minnesota law (Minn. Stat. § 303.20) states that a foreign corporation **transacting business in Minnesota without a certificate of authority cannot maintain a lawsuit in Minnesota courts** until it obtains one ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/303.20#:~:text=303,state%20until%20such%20corporation%20shall)). This means if an out-of-state company that should be registered in MN (but isn’t) files a conciliation claim, the defendant could object and seek dismissal unless and until the business becomes properly registered. For small claims, this rarely comes up explicitly, but it’s the law. If you are a business based elsewhere but suing in MN for a transaction here, consider registering with the MN Secretary of State or be prepared that the case might be stayed or dismissed until compliance ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/303.20#:~:text=303,state%20until%20such%20corporation%20shall)). Conversely, if you’re defending against a foreign company not registered, you might raise that as a defense (though the court might allow them to register late and continue).

- **Fictitious Name in Contracts:** If you’re suing a business, make sure you sue the real entity. For example, if you have a contract with “Sunshine Contractors” and discover it’s not an LLC or corp, it might be a sole proprietorship of John Doe. You’d want to sue John Doe (individually) d/b/a Sunshine Contractors. If you only sue “Sunshine Contractors” and it’s not a legal entity, the judgment technically is against a non-entity. Minnesota does allow you to amend judgment to correct the defendant’s name if it was just a naming mistake. But it’s best to get it right initially. Use the Secretary of State search to find if the name is an LLC/corp (search by business name). If not found, maybe it’s a tradename of an individual or general partnership.

- **Attorney requirement on appeal:** Reiterating, if a business loses or wins and either side appeals, the business **must hire an attorney** for District Court ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,legal%20advice%20if%20you%20have)). (Exception: A sole proprietor is just an individual, so they can self-represent as themselves. But an LLC, even single-member, is separate and requires an attorney on appeal. Partnerships likewise, unless all partners sign filings – not typical.)

- **Collection Agencies / Assignments:** Minnesota small claims does allow assignments of claims (for example, a collection agency who bought a debt can sue in conciliation). But check the jurisdictional limit for consumer credit: if a debt buyer is collecting a consumer credit sale debt, the $4,000 cap applies ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,involves%20a%20consumer%20credit%20transaction)). Some jurisdictions are wary of default judgments for debt buyers if documentation is lacking, so collection businesses should be prepared with proof of assignment and debt details. (Unlike some states, Minnesota does not bar assignees from small claims categorically, but they must prove standing like anyone else.)

- **Multiple cases per year by same plaintiff:** No statutory limit, but if a business floods the court with many cases, the court may schedule them on the same day or ensure the business’s rep can handle them. In general, Minnesota’s conciliation court is used by many businesses (landlords for deposits, banks for small loans, contractors for fees, etc.) extensively. That’s acceptable.

- **DBA Registration Requirements:** While not a court requirement, state law mandates that if a person or partnership uses a business name not including their true full names, they must file a Certificate of Assumed Name with the Secretary of State ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/333.01#:~:text=No%20person%20shall%20hereafter%20carry,corporation%2C%20incorporated%2C%20limited%2C%20chartered%2C%20professional)). Failure to do so is a misdemeanor and can be a barrier to suing. Historically, some courts might not allow an unregistered DBA to sue until they register (because they’re technically not supposed to "transact business" without it). That said, conciliation courts usually don’t police this; it’s more a technicality. Still, best practice: if you’re running a business under a fictitious name, get it registered. And if you’re suing such a business, you can check if they’re registered; if not, you might consider suing the individual behind it directly.

- **Plaintiff Name on Judgment:** Ensure the business’s correct name is on the judgment. If “ABC, Inc.” is plaintiff and wins, the judgment should list “ABC, Inc.” so it can be enforced by the company. Likewise, if you get a judgment against a business, it should list the entity name (and possibly agent) clearly so you can levy the right assets.

- **Out-of-State Business Plaintiffs (Registration to Do Business):** If an out-of-state biz is just suing on an isolated transaction (not regularly doing business in MN), they may not be required to register in MN. But if the contract was made or performed in MN, the defendant might argue the company was “transacting business” here without authority. The court could stay the case to allow registration. Getting a certificate of authority isn’t too hard (file with SecState and pay a fee ~$155). Also, foreign businesses must not be *suspended* in their home state – if they are not in good standing there, they also cannot sue in MN until revived.

- **Filing for Businesses or by Agents:** The individual who signs and files the conciliation paperwork on behalf of a business should indeed be the officer/employee with the power of attorney. Some conciliation courts may reject a claim filed by “Jane Doe” on behalf of “XYZ Corp.” if Jane doesn’t provide the POA form. So include that form at filing or soon after ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Conciliation%20Court%20and%20District%20Court,See%20the)). Hennepin has a standing order to reject any claim filed by someone via POA for another *individual* (you can’t appear via POA for another person) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Pursuant%20to%20a%C2%A0Standing%20Order%20for,this%C2%A0Power%20of%20Attorney%20Fact%20Sheet)), but for a business it’s allowed with proper documentation.

In short, **businesses can use Minnesota Conciliation Court**, but:
  - They can appear through non-lawyer agents in conciliation (with proper authorization) ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Individuals%20can%20represent%20themselves%20or,Court%20judgment%20to%20District%20Court)).
  - They must use lawyers in any appeal or district court proceeding ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Attorney%20for%20Conciliation%20Court%20mncourts,the%20General%20Rules%20of%20Practice)).
  - No special caps or limits beyond the $20k (or $4k for consumer debts) apply specifically to businesses (e.g., a corporation can sue for up to $20k just like an individual).
  - Make sure the business is properly named (and if foreign, consider registration).
  - If a sole proprietor plaintiff didn’t file a required assumed name, the suit isn’t automatically void, but it’s technically a violation. A quick fix is to file the certificate and continue the case if challenged. This rarely comes up, but it’s a pitfall to be aware of.

- **Multiple Owners/Partners:** If a partnership or jointly owned business is party, one partner can represent the partnership, but ideally all partners’ names should be included (or the firm name if it’s a registered partnership). Partnerships in conciliation can have an employee or partner appear ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.02#:~:text=Subd)). If partners are individually named defendants, each must be served, and a judgment will list all their names (creditor can then pursue each for the full amount, but only collect total once).

- **Collections on Judgment:** If you get a judgment against a business, collection is similar as against an individual, except no wage garnishment (you can’t garnish wages of a corporation, obviously). You’d target the business’s bank accounts or assets. If the business is an LLC or corporation that closes shop with no assets, that judgment may be uncollectible (you generally cannot go after owners personally unless you convince a higher court to pierce the corporate veil, which is beyond conciliation scope). If the business was a sole prop or partnership, the judgment is against the owner/partners personally too, so you can collect from their personal assets.

**Tip:** Before suing a business, verify it is solvent enough to pay. If it’s defunct, you might decide not to waste time. If it’s active, you likely can enforce via normal means (bank levy etc.).

## 14. County-Level Differences

Minnesota’s Conciliation Court procedures are mostly uniform statewide (governed by Minn. Stat. Ch. 491A and General Rules of Practice, Title VI). However, there are slight differences or local practices in some of the more populous counties:

- **Hennepin County (Minneapolis):** Hennepin has a dedicated Conciliation Court department and a **Standing Order** with some local rules. For example, as mentioned, Hennepin will **reject filings where someone attempts to file on behalf of another individual via Power of Attorney** ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Pursuant%20to%20a%C2%A0Standing%20Order%20for,this%C2%A0Power%20of%20Attorney%20Fact%20Sheet)) – they emphasize individuals must file their own cases, whereas businesses can authorize an employee. Hennepin also provides **mediation services** right at the courthouse on hearing day via volunteer mediators ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Conciliation%20Court%2C%20also%20known%20as%C2%A0small,not%20intended%20to%20be%C2%A0legal%20advice)) – often, before your case is heard, you’ll be offered a chance to mediate in a separate room for free. If both agree, they can attempt settlement; if not, proceed to hearing. Hennepin allows **e-filing** of conciliation cases through Guide & File or eFS, and charges a $5 e-filing fee in addition to the $65 base ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=to%20waive%20the%20fee%20by,forms%20with%20the%20court%20electronically)). Hennepin’s filing fee with law library fee currently totals around $80 (as of 2026). Hennepin schedules a large number of cases per calendar; you might wait a while in court for yours to be called. They often group cases by whether mediation is happening. Also, Hennepin tends to automatically mail **Disclosure forms** to debtors after judgment if unpaid (making it somewhat easier for creditors to get info). 
- **Ramsey County (Saint Paul):** Ramsey’s Conciliation Court is similar. They often set hearings about 6–8 weeks out. They have a law library fee that brings filing fee to around $73. They also offer mediation through the Dispute Resolution Center of St. Paul for certain cases (like neighbor disputes). Ramsey’s conciliation sometimes uses referees to hear cases (referees’ decisions are confirmed by a judge if no appeal).
- **Dakota County:** Has multiple locations (Apple Valley, Hastings) where conciliation hearings might be held depending on where in the county the dispute arises. Check your notice for the correct court location. Dakota permits eFiling as well and often schedules an initial hearing within 4–6 weeks. They might require proof of service filed a week ahead or the case could be continued.
- **Scott/Carver Counties:** Smaller counties often have the judge or a judicial officer hear conciliation cases maybe once or twice a month. The environment is a bit less formal just due to being a smaller docket. But core rules are the same. Some smaller courts might allow or encourage telephone appearances for long-distance parties (check with court in advance).
- **St. Louis County (Duluth/Virginia):** St. Louis has multiple courthouses. They schedule conciliation based on geographic region. Make sure you file in the appropriate district (Duluth if it arose there, etc.). There might be slight differences in how far out they schedule hearings.
- **Anoka County:** They often schedule evening conciliation court sessions (some counties offer night court as convenience). Anoka’s filing fee ~ $75. They have mediators available from the local Conflict Resolution Center.
- **Stearns County (St. Cloud):** Similar procedures. Possibly a pilot allowing certain cases to be heard via ITV (interactive video) if parties consent.
- **County Venue differences for Landlord/Tenant:** In Hennepin and Ramsey (which have separate Housing Court for evictions), conciliation is still used for post-eviction money claims. E.g., In Hennepin, if a landlord wants to sue for damages after an eviction case is closed, they actually file in Conciliation (or bring it as counter in eviction maybe but usually conciliation separately). Hennepin’s rule: rent deposit cases can be conciliation (some places with Housing Court might route deposit disputes there, but MPLS uses conciliation for deposit if it’s only money).
- **Forms availability:** All counties use the state-approved forms (some counties’ websites link to them, but ultimately they direct to the same uniform forms).
- **Judges vs. Referees:** In smaller counties, you’ll get an elected judge hearing conciliation. In urban counties, you likely get a referee. Either way, you have a right to appeal to District Court judge if you disagree. If a referee hears it, typically you cannot “appeal” just to a judge’s review in conciliation – you still must do removal to District Court. (Some states like New York allow small claims appeal on the record to a panel; Minnesota does not – it’s de novo only.)
- **Hearing time limits:** Some busy counties may have an unwritten time limit (like in Hennepin, they may aim to finish each case in 10 minutes, whereas in a rural county, a judge might allow you 30 minutes if needed because docket is lighter). Adjust your presentation accordingly – in Hennepin or Ramsey, be very concise.
- **Language assistance differences:** Hennepin and Ramsey have interpreters on staff and can arrange them more readily, even for obscure languages due to volume. In a rural county, you may need to more proactively request and confirm interpreter availability ahead of time.
- **Continuances:** Some counties (like Hennepin) strictly allow only one continuance per side, filed 5 days before hearing and with up to $50 costs possible ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Legal%20Advice%20Clinic)). Other counties might be more lenient if parties agree to reschedule. Always check local practice; often the info packet on your hearing notice will address how to request a continuance in that county (some require a written motion vs. some allow a phone call if both sides agree).

Despite these small differences, the *substance* of conciliation process is the same everywhere in Minnesota. Always check the **Minnesota Judicial Branch website** for your specific county’s conciliation court page. Many have local FAQs or instructions:
  - e.g., The **Fourth District (Hennepin)** website details mediation option and the necessity of POA for business reps ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Types%20of%20claims%20allowed%20in,Conciliation%20Court)) ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Claims%20not%20Allowed%3A)).
  - The **Second District (Ramsey)** site has a helpful PDF on how to collect a conciliation judgment in Ramsey.
  - **Outstate counties** often have one clerk assigned to conciliation who can answer procedure questions by phone; bigger counties route you to self-help centers.

**Notable County Specifics:**
- **Hennepin & Ramsey**: These two have the biggest volume and thus have refined procedures (like mass mediation and stringent check-in processes).
- **Olmsted County (Rochester)**: They provide a local guide and emphasize that if either party doesn’t show, case may be immediately decided/dismissed.
- **Clay County (Moorhead)**: In border areas, they sometimes see more out-of-state defendants (from North Dakota). They’re accustomed to long-arm serving ND parties; just give them a bit more time to schedule to allow for service across state.
- **Cook County (Grand Marais)**: Tiny docket; they may schedule your case on a date when a circuit judge is in town. You might wait longer for a hearing date because their court sessions are infrequent.

In conclusion, while the rules are uniform, **always read any local instructions** that come with your summons or judgment. Those will highlight any county-specific procedures (like how to file an appeal in that county, or any local forms needed). If in doubt, call the Conciliation Court clerk of that county – they can’t give legal advice but can clarify local process (e.g., “Do I need a motion form to continue? How do I get a transcript of judgment here?” etc.). Minnesota’s self-help resources are also statewide and will note differences when relevant.

## 15. Common Pitfalls and Dismissal Reasons

Many small claims cases get derailed due to avoidable mistakes. Here are common pitfalls in Minnesota Conciliation Court (and how to avoid them):

- **Suing the Wrong Defendant (Misidentification):** This is VERY common. If you sue the wrong party, you might win a useless judgment or get dismissed. Examples: suing an individual when you should have sued their LLC; suing a trade name rather than the owner; spelling the name incorrectly; suing “ABC Corp” when the real name is “ABC Corp., Inc.” Always double-check the exact legal name of the person or business you need to sue:
  - For individuals, use their full legal name (and any known aliases or “also known as” if relevant). If they recently changed names (marriage, etc.), note that (e.g., “Jane Doe (formerly Jane Smith)”).
  - For businesses, search the Secretary of State records to get the full legal entity name and status. If the business is a corporation or LLC, that entity should be the defendant (not the human owners, unless you have grounds to hold them personally liable). If it’s a small sole proprietorship, the individual owns it should be defendant.
  - Example Pitfall: A tenant sues “Sunrise Apartments” for the deposit. But “Sunrise Apartments” is just the building’s name; the property is owned by “123 Investments, LLC.” The tenant should sue 123 Investments, LLC (maybe d/b/a Sunrise Apts). If they just put Sunrise Apts, the LLC could later possibly avoid paying by claiming the judgment isn’t technically against them. **Solution:** Find out who exactly holds your contract or caused your damage. Name them precisely. If in doubt, you can name multiple related parties and let the judge sort it (e.g., John Doe and XYZ LLC).
  - Also, if someone’s acting under a trade name, list them both: “John Doe d/b/a Doe’s Lawn Care.” 
  - If you sue and realize you misnamed, you can ask to amend the claim (some judges will allow you to correct the name at hearing if it’s basically the same entity). But if it’s a completely wrong person, the case will be dismissed. Suing a parent company when responsibility was with a subsidiary can also result in dismissal if the wrong entity wasn’t party to the contract.
- **Wrong Venue:** Filing in the wrong county can get your case thrown out or transferred. For instance, suing a defendant in your home county when they have no ties there (and no venue exception applies). The defendant might not show and you might get a default, but later they could vacate saying improper venue. Or the judge on reviewing might question jurisdiction. **Solution:** Follow venue rules strictly (see Section 3). If you inadvertently file in the wrong venue, you can voluntarily dismiss and refile correctly (though you’ll lose the filing fee and time). 
  - Pitfall example: You live in Duluth (St. Louis County) and sue someone who lives in Minneapolis (Hennepin County) for a car sale that happened in Hennepin. Proper venue is Hennepin, but you filed in St. Louis. The Hennepin resident doesn’t show up in Duluth – you might get default, but since venue was improper, the defendant can later get it vacated due to lack of jurisdiction. 
  - Another: Landlord-tenant money dispute – must be filed where rental property is ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,2803)). If you erroneously file where the landlord lives instead, that’s wrong – likely dismissal unless landlord doesn’t object. 
- **Statute of Limitations Expired:** If you file after the legal deadline for that type of claim, the case can be dismissed with prejudice. Judges won’t automatically catch it in every instance, but if the defendant raises it (or if it’s very obviously beyond the limit and the defendant is present to object), you lose. **Solution:** Always calculate the SOL (see Section 5). If close, maybe the defendant won’t know and default – but that’s a gamble, and even then, they could later seek to vacate if they learn. It’s best not to bring stale claims. If you’re beyond the limit, you mostly have no recourse in court (except for narrow exceptions like fraud discovery rule, etc.). 
  - Pitfall ex: You try to sue for a slip-and-fall injury from four years ago. Personal injury SOL is 2 years – case will be dismissed if defendant points it out. 
  - Another: Suing on a written contract from 8 years ago – SOL is 6 years, you’re too late (unless partial payments or written acknowledgments restarted the clock, which you’d need to prove).
- **Failure to Properly Serve the Defendant:** Even if you filed correctly, if you didn’t follow through on service (for claims over $2,500 or if court mail was returned), your case can be dismissed at hearing. Or you show up but the defendant wasn’t served – the judge cannot proceed (no jurisdiction over defendant). That usually results in a continuance or dismissal without prejudice. **Solution:** Carefully follow service rules (see Section 8). If doing certified mail, use restricted delivery and track it. If doing personal service, get that affidavit filed timely. If you haven’t successfully served by hearing, ask for a continuance *before* the hearing so you don’t have to appear only to find out it’s not going forward.
  - Pitfall ex: You sue for $5,000, the court tells you to serve. You send regular mail or forget to do restricted – defendant doesn’t sign anything. You have no proof. You go to hearing, defendant absent. The judge asks for service proof – you have none. Case likely continued or dismissed. You wasted time. 
  - Another: The address you provided was wrong (maybe plaintiff didn’t update the defendant’s new address). The hearing notice returned undeliverable – some courts will automatically dismiss in that event for failure to prosecute. Watch your mail – if the court sends you a notice that service failed, act to find a better address and request refiling or continuance.
- **Insufficient Evidence / Lack of Preparation:** Small claims is informal, but you still need evidence to meet your burden. Some plaintiffs mistakenly think “I’ll just tell the judge the story” with nothing to back it up. That may not be enough, especially if the defendant disputes. For example, claiming $5,000 in damages without any receipts or estimates – judge might only award what’s proven (maybe nothing beyond nominal if you have zero documentation). **Solution:** Bring the relevant documents, photos, and witnesses if needed. Organize your thoughts – if you ramble or get emotional without focusing on legal reasons, the judge may miss your key points.
  - Pitfall ex: A tenant claims $2,000 of personal property was ruined by landlord’s leaky roof. At hearing, they have no photos of the damaged items, no list of values, nothing – the landlord says it wasn’t that bad. The judge might not award much since the tenant provided no substantiation. 
  - Another: A contractor sues a client for unpaid work but doesn’t bring the contract or invoices – if the client says “I already paid some of that” and contractor can’t show the accounting, the contractor could lose out on what’s actually owed.
- **Demanding Unallowed Remedies:** Some litigants ask for things beyond the court’s power, like “I want him jailed” or “I want an order that she do the work properly.” As covered, conciliation can’t issue injunctions or specific performance ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,by%20libel%20or%20slander)), only money (or return of personal property). If you only seek a non-monetary remedy, your case will be dismissed (wrong court). If you mix a request for money and an equitable request, the judge will ignore the non-money part. **Solution:** Convert your harm into a dollar amount as best you can. If you primarily need an action (like a repair), consider District Court or another forum. For small claims, stick to money.
  - Pitfall: Someone sues “to make neighbor cut down a tree and pay $500 for distress.” The judge will dismiss because title to land or injunctive relief (tree removal) is not allowed ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=The%20conciliation%20court%20does%20not,jurisdiction%20over%20the%20following%20actions)). The $500 distress is likely not a valid measure of damage either (emotional distress not usually awarded in property disputes). This is the wrong remedy; the claim should have been either filed in regular court for an injunction or not at all in conciliation.
- **Not Appearing / Late to Court:** If you as plaintiff don’t show up on time for your hearing, expect a dismissal (often with prejudice). If defendant doesn’t show, you may get default but sometimes judges will require you to prove you served them. If no proof, they may not enter default and might continue or dismiss. **Solution:** Always attend your hearing. Arrive early. Check in. If an emergency truly prevents you, notify the court asap (before hearing) and request a continuance with proof (e.g., medical note). Each party only gets one continuance (and must request 5 days before) in many counties ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=Legal%20Advice%20Clinic)), except for real emergencies. If you just forget or mix up date, you can try to file a Motion to Vacate within 20 days explaining, but it’s up to the judge’s discretion to reopen a dismissed case for plaintiff’s no-show.
  - Pitfall: Plaintiff arrives 30 minutes late; case was already called and dismissed. Some judges will just say “case dismissed” and you’ll have to refile (and maybe the statute or defendant’s whereabouts changed).
  - Another: Defendant no-shows because they moved and didn’t get notice (due to plaintiff using an old address). They later find out about default and move to vacate – the judge will likely grant it because of lack of notice. Now your case restarts months later.
- **Appealing the Wrong Way or Missing Appeal Deadline:** If you lose and want to appeal, failing to follow the removal process exactly (file within 20 days, pay fee, serve notice) will forfeit your right. Some folks try sending a letter to conciliation judge or something – that does nothing. **Solution:** Follow Section 11 carefully for appeals. If you snooze past 20 days, the judgment is final. You can’t appeal just because you regret not showing up; in that case your remedy is motion to vacate, not appeal.
  - Pitfall: A defendant thinks, “I’ll just wait and see if the creditor tries to collect; I won’t appeal now.” Later, wages get garnished – too late to appeal then.
  - Another: Plaintiff who lost tries to file a new conciliation claim for the same issue hoping for a different result. The court will dismiss it as res judicata (already decided). The proper route was appeal, not refiling.
- **Not Considering Collectability:** This isn’t a procedural dismissal reason, but a practical pitfall. You win a case against someone who has no job, no assets, and maybe is on SSI (fully exempt). Your judgment becomes uncollectible. You spent time and fees for a moral victory only. **Solution:** Before suing, try to assess if the defendant can pay. If not, maybe try mediation or write it off, unless you just want the judgment for principle or future possibilities. Don’t expect the court to magically make money appear. Many default judgments go unpaid – some plaintiffs are dismayed to learn “winning” wasn’t the end. Knowing about exemptions and collection (see Section 12) beforehand is wise. If the debtor is judgment-proof, you might allocate your time accordingly.
- **Poor Behavior in Court:** Being disrespectful, yelling, interrupting, or engaging in personal attacks can annoy the judge and undermine your case. Some litigants get emotional and stray into irrelevant grievances (“he’s always been a scammer” or “she’s lying about everything in life!”). Judges tune that out. **Solution:** Stay calm, stick to facts, address the court respectfully. Let the other side finish speaking. If you need to rebut, do so when it’s your turn. Judges are human – a reasonable, polite litigant is more credible. An obnoxious one may not get the benefit of the doubt.
  - Pitfall: A plaintiff starts insulting the defendant in profane terms in front of judge – the judge might cut the hearing short or even dismiss if decorum is too out of hand. Or your anger could cause you to forget key facts or not present evidence well.
- **Ignoring Court’s Instructions:** If the court asks for something (filing proof of service by certain date, or bringing a document, etc.) and you ignore it, you risk losing. **Solution:** Read every notice from the court carefully. For example, Hennepin’s Notice of Hearing says to file the Affidavit of Service at least 7 days prior if you had to serve, and to bring specific evidence. Follow those to the letter. If the court continues your case and tells you “get an estimate” or “bring the contract next time,” that’s a huge hint – be sure to do it by the next hearing.
- **Settling & Not Informing Court:** If parties settle out of court and then neither shows up, the case might be dismissed but possibly “with prejudice” (effectively enforcing your settlement if plaintiff agreed to it). However, sometimes it could be dismissed without prejudice, theoretically allowing re-filing. Or if plaintiff doesn’t show and defendant does, the defendant could ask for dismissal with prejudice to end it. If you settle, best practice is to file a written stipulation to dismiss (or payment plan terms) with the court before the hearing. Or at least one of you appear to tell the judge “we’ve settled; please dismiss.” 
  - Pitfall: Parties settle that defendant will pay in installments, so plaintiff doesn’t appear, thinking it’s fine. Defendant shows up, says “we settled, I request dismissal.” Court dismisses with prejudice immediately. If defendant fails to pay later, plaintiff can’t re-sue because it’s dismissed on the merits (plaintiff should have asked for a continuance or at least a without-prejudice dismissal contingent on payment).
  - To avoid: Put settlement in writing that case can be reopened if default on payments, or better yet, ask court for a continuance until payments are complete, then you (plaintiff) will dismiss if paid. That retains jurisdiction.

- **Forgetting Court Costs or Interest:** If you win, you should ask the judge to include your **filing fee** and **service costs** in the judgment (and statutory interest if significant time passed). Minnesota rules say filing fee *must* be added if you won ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/help-topics/conciliation-court.aspx#:~:text=Yes,you%20collect%20on%20the%20judgment)), but occasionally a judgment might omit it. **Solution:** Politely remind the judge: “Your Honor, I also ask for $___ costs (the filing fee and $___ service fee).” Judges will almost always add that if asked, since it’s allowed by rule. If interest from date of suit is meaningful (maybe a year of delay, etc.), you can ask for pre-judgment interest too (they may or may not calculate it on the spot, but they could).
  - Pitfall: You paid $70 fee and $50 sheriff fee, win $5,000 but didn’t mention costs, judgment comes out $5,000. You essentially donated $120. While you could file a post-judgment motion to amend for costs, that’s hassle – asking at hearing is easier.
- **Not Following Through Post-Judgment:** If you win but then do nothing (like don’t docket the judgment or don’t attempt collection), that judgment may sit unpaid forever. Many winners assume the court will mail them a check or the debtor will magically pay – not so. **Solution:** After judgment, if not paid promptly, take initiative: docket it, send a demand letter, then pursue garnishment or execution as needed. Many judgments expire uncollected simply because creditors didn’t try the available remedies (or waited too long).
  - Pitfall: A landlord wins $3,000 from ex-tenant, doesn’t bother to docket or find tenant’s new job for wage garnishment. Tenant moves on; 10 years later landlord wants to collect but judgment expired. All that effort wasted.
- **Misunderstanding Appeal Scope:** Some who appeal think it’s a simple review for errors. They might not prepare evidence again, assuming the district judge will read the conciliation case file. No – it’s a brand new trial. **Solution:** Prepare for the appeal as if starting over. Don’t assume anything from conciliation carries over. Also, if you were plaintiff and didn't claim something first time, you typically can’t add new causes of action on appeal (it’s an appeal of that case, not a chance to throw in unrelated claims).
  - Pitfall: Conciliation plaintiff forgot to claim court costs in first trial and lost, appeals, then tries to add a whole new claim for something else between the parties – the judge might not allow expanding the scope beyond the original claim issues.

In summary, success in small claims comes from careful attention to detail: correct party names, proper venue, timely service, solid evidence, meeting deadlines, and understanding what the court can and cannot do. If you avoid these pitfalls, you greatly increase your chance of a smooth case and a meaningful judgment.

## 16. Recent Changes

Minnesota’s small claims (Conciliation Court) procedures have seen a few notable changes and updates in the last few years (2023–2026):

- **Jurisdictional Limit Increase (2024):** The most significant recent change is the **increase of the Conciliation Court monetary limit to $20,000** ([zmattorneys.com](https://zmattorneys.com/small-claims-court-limit-raised/#:~:text=Conciliation%20court%2C%20also%20called%20%E2%80%9CSmall,3a)). Effective **August 1, 2024**, the general claim cap went from $15,000 to **$20,000** (the first increase in several years). This was enacted by the legislature in 2023. It means you can now bring larger disputes to small claims than before. *Important:* The special $4,000 limit for consumer credit transaction claims was **not** increased – it remains $4,000 ([zmattorneys.com](https://zmattorneys.com/small-claims-court-limit-raised/#:~:text=Conciliation%20court%2C%20also%20called%20%E2%80%9CSmall,3a)). This law change (Laws 2023 c 64 art.4 §51) is fully incorporated in Minn. Stat. § 491A.01 now ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,involves%20a%20consumer%20credit%20transaction)). For context, the last increase prior was in 2014 (from $10k to $15k), so 2024’s bump to $20k is a major update. Plaintiffs with claims between $15k–$20k who previously had to go to District Court can now use conciliation (e.g., a $18,000 contractor dispute).
- **Remote Hearings (2020–2021):** During the COVID-19 pandemic, Minnesota courts pivoted to remote hearings via telephone or Zoom for conciliation cases. As of 2022–2023, many counties have returned to **in-person hearings by default**, but some are still offering remote options or hybrid models. For example, in early 2022, the State Court Administrator allowed Conciliation Courts to hold proceedings remotely and many did. By 2023, some counties (like Hennepin) resumed mainly in-person hearings, but parties could request remote appearance for good cause. If there’s another health emergency, remote hearings could become common again. Always check your hearing notice – post-2020, it might specify if it’s via Zoom and provide a Meeting ID. This is a change from pre-2020 when nearly all hearings were in person. 
- **E-Filing Expansion:** Minnesota’s **eFiling system (eFS)** became more accessible to self-represented parties through the **Guide & File** interface. In 2021–2022, the Judicial Branch rolled out more user-friendly online forms for conciliation (Smart Forms) ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/GetForms.aspx?c=10&p=41#:~:text=Forms%20Packet%3A%20Filing%20a%20Conciliation,Court%20Claim)) and integrated them with Guide & File, allowing you to fill out the Statement of Claim online and directly submit electronically in many counties ([www.mncourts.gov](https://www.mncourts.gov/GetForms.aspx?c=10&p=41#:~:text=,Guide%20%26%20File%20Help%20Topic)). This was a tech improvement making it easier to initiate a case without printing and mailing. By 2026, essentially all counties accept conciliation filings electronically, whereas a few years ago e-filing by self-represented litigants was optional and not widely used in small claims. 
- **Filing Fee Adjustments:** While the base state conciliation fee stayed $65 for many years, county law library fees have fluctuated slightly. Also, an additional Tech fee ($2) was added to some filings. For example, Hennepin’s total fee went from $75 to $77 around 2023 (due to a slight library fee increase). These minor fee changes aren’t dramatic, but you may notice a few dollar differences year to year. Always check the updated fee schedule on the MNCourts website ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/help-topics/court-fees/district-court-fees?cat=conciliation&cookiecheck=true#:~:text=)). As of 2025, the legislature hadn’t raised the $65 base, but future increases are possible.
- **Interest Rate on Judgments:** Minnesota’s judgment interest rate calculation changed in 2022 under Minn. Stat. § 549.09. It’s now based on the secondary market yield of one-year U.S. treasury bills, rounded to nearest percent, minimum 4%. For many years it was a fixed 4% or bumped to 10% if over $50k. In 2023–2026, because of higher T-bill yields, the state court administrator announced interest rates around 5%–6% for judgments ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/court_rules/rule/msinte/#:~:text=MINNE%C2%ADSOTA%20COURT%20RULES%20,court%20judgments%2C%20verdicts%2C%20and%20arbitration)). E.g., for 2024, it was about 5%. This is relevant for conciliation winners as their judgment accrues that interest. This is a change from the steady 4% from 2014–2021. It’s not a procedure change, but a substantive difference that a judgment accruing 6% will grow faster (an incentive for debtors to pay sooner).
- **Procedure Clarifications (2021 Rules Update):** The Minnesota General Rules of Practice for District Courts had some housekeeping amendments effective 2021 affecting conciliation. One notable clarification was Rule 512, which deals with when conciliation judgments must be paid or removed – it made clear that cost bonds on appeal are $50 unless waived ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/help-topics/court-fees/district-court-fees?cat=conciliation&cookiecheck=true#:~:text=Removal%20,10%2C%20General%20Rule%20521%28b)), which matches statute. Also, they updated the affidavit of good faith language (no big change in practice). Rule 514 was updated regarding **vacating judgments**, highlighting the 20-day period for administrative vacate vs. formal motion after that ([mncourts.gov](https://mncourts.gov/find-courts/hennepin/hennepincivilcourt/conciliation-court#:~:text=,strongly%20recommended%20that%20the%20removing)). These changes basically codified existing practice and made forms consistent.
- **Eviction moratorium impact:** During 2020–2021, Minnesota had an eviction moratorium for COVID. Some landlords waited and then filed conciliation claims for back rent once allowed. The legislature in 2021 required landlords to apply for rental assistance before collecting certain COVID-period rent. This indirectly affected conciliation cases for rent: if a landlord did not comply (e.g., get rental assistance or do required notice), their conciliation claim for that rent could be barred. This was a time-limited change: by 2022, those protections phased out. But someone dealing with pandemic-era rent claims in 2024 should be aware of those conditions (see 2021 Minn. Sess. Law Chap. 8). Now largely moot, but a small handful of cases might still reference it.
- **Consumer Protection Developments:** Minnesota amended some consumer statutes that could affect small claims. For example, in 2023 the legislature tightened **bad check penalty** laws: the service charge max increased (from $30 to $40) and the treble damages provision (civil penalty) was slightly tweaked. This means if you sue for a bounced check in 2026, you might claim up to $40 service charge plus up to $100 or check amount penalty ([caselaw.findlaw.com](https://caselaw.findlaw.com/us-8th-circuit/1365145.html#:~:text=Minnesota%27s%20worthless%20check%20statute%20provides,71%282%29%20%28emphasis)) (the base statutes in Chapter 332 were updated). Minor change, but relevant in small NSF check cases (common in conciliation).
- **Triple Damages for Landlords:** An amendment effective 2021 to Minn. Stat. § 504B.178 added that landlords who fail to provide required move-in/move-out inspection disclosure can lose some deposit rights. Also, bad-faith retention penalty for deposits remained $500 ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/504b.178#:~:text=The%20bad%20faith%20retention%20by,the%20recovery%20of%20the%20deposit)). Not a change in conciliation procedure, but new law that could show up in deposit disputes now.
- **Notarization not Required:** Minnesota in 2022 clarified that the “Statement of Claim” and “Counterclaim” forms may be signed under penalty of perjury instead of notarized. Many forms were updated to remove notary requirement (they now just have “I declare under penalty of perjury that this is true”). This change (stemming from Minn. Stat. § 358.116) made filing easier especially via e-file. So you no longer need to get the form notarized, which was a barrier for some self-filers. If you have an old form with a notary block, know that newer forms replaced it with a simple date and signature.
- **Power of Attorney Form Update:** The Judicial Council updated the “Power of Attorney for Conciliation Court” form in 2023 to clarify it’s only valid for conciliation representation and doesn’t make the person a general agent. This was in response to some confusion. The updated form (perhaps number CCT702) is more explicit and must be filed. Not a substantive law change, but a form improvement.
- **Inflation Adjustments:** The legislature sometimes indexes certain exemption amounts to inflation (e.g., debtor’s personal property exemptions were raised in 2023 – see Minn. Stat. § 550.37: motor vehicle exemption went from ~$5,000 to $5,500; tools of trade from $12,000 to $15,000, etc.). This doesn’t change conciliation process, but it affects post-judgment collection (what property you can seize). Those changes in 2023 mean judgment creditors may find a little more of a debtor’s property exempt than before.
- **Name Change of Court Websites:** In mid-2024, the Minnesota Judicial Branch moved to a new website system (as you might note with certain URLs in sources). Some content moved; e.g., local county court pages updated their Help Topic links. If you had old bookmarks or forms from before 2024, verify you have the latest versions (the website now is mobile-friendly and updated).
- **No-Fault Conciliation Pilot:** There’s talk (as of 2025) of a pilot project to route smaller auto insurance subrogation claims through conciliation to ease District Court dockets. If implemented, insurance companies could use conciliation for, say, $8,000 property damage subrogation cases. Not yet law, but keep an eye.

All these show that **Minnesota’s Conciliation Court is periodically adjusted to reflect economic conditions and streamline access**. The big headline is definitely the $20,000 limit as of August 2024 ([zmattorneys.com](https://zmattorneys.com/small-claims-court-limit-raised/#:~:text=Conciliation%20court%2C%20also%20called%20%E2%80%9CSmall,3a)), which opens the door to more cases being handled cheaply. If you learned older rules (like $15k cap), update your knowledge. 

Always check the Minnesota Courts website or talk to court administration for the most current rules. The statutes referenced are current through the 2025 published statutes, and no major amendments were pending for 2026 at time of writing aside from possibly further raising the consumer credit limit (some consumer advocates push for $4k to be increased too, but none passed yet). Laws and rules can change, so when in doubt, look up the statute (Minn. Stat. § 491A.01) and the General Rules (Rule 508–521) to ensure you have the latest parameters.

**In summary**, as of 2026:
- You can sue up to $20k (except $4k on consumer debts) ([www.revisor.mn.gov](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/491A.01#:~:text=,involves%20a%20consumer%20credit%20transaction)).
- Use conciliation even for moderately larger disputes thanks to the 2024 increase ([zmattorneys.com](https://zmattorneys.com/small-claims-court-limit-raised/#:~:text=Conciliation%20court%2C%20also%20called%20%E2%80%9CSmall,3a)).
- The process is more tech-friendly (e-filing, remote options) post-2020.
- Pay attention to any new legislative changes that could tweak forms or amounts (Minnesota tends to adjust limits every so often with inflation and policy shifts).

Staying informed of these updates will help you navigate small claims court effectively and take full advantage of its benefits.
