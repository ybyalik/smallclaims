## 1. Jurisdictional limits — MAXIMUM AMOUNT YOU CAN SUIT FOR  
**Dollar cap:** In Texas, the maximum amount you can sue for in a small claims case is **$20,000** (not including interest). This cap applies regardless of whether the plaintiff is an individual, a sole proprietorship, or a business entity ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=)). Unlike some states, Texas does **not** impose a lower cap on corporations or limited liability companies (LLCs) – the $20,000 limit is uniform for all plaintiffs ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=)). The cap refers to the principal amount of the claim; statutory interest and court costs are excluded from the calculation, but **attorney’s fees are included** if you are claiming them under a contract or statute ([legalatoms.com](https://legalatoms.com/texas/texas-small-claims-court-limit-amount/#:~:text=sums%20of%20money,or%20District%20Court%20civil%20actions)). For example, if you are owed $19,500 on a contract and also seek $2,000 in attorney fees, the total exceeds $20,000 – you would have to waive the excess or file in a higher court ([legalatoms.com](https://legalatoms.com/texas/texas-small-claims-court-limit-amount/#:~:text=sums%20of%20money,or%20District%20Court%20civil%20actions)) ([legalatoms.com](https://legalatoms.com/texas/texas-small-claims-court-limit-amount/#:~:text=represents%20%E2%80%9Cthe%20total%20amount%20of,include%20every%20component%20of%20direct)).  

**No per-year filing limit:** Texas law does not limit how many small claims cases a party can file per year. Some states restrict frequent filers (especially businesses), but Texas imposes no such numeric limit – you can file multiple suits as long as each claim is within the $20,000 jurisdictional amount. (However, extremely repetitive filings could draw scrutiny for abuse of process.)  

**No claim-splitting to evade the cap:** You **cannot split one larger claim into multiple small claims suits** to circumvent the $20,000 limit. Texas courts treat such “claim-splitting” as improper – if your cause of action exceeds $20,000, you must either **waive** the amount over $20,000 or file in a higher court ([legalatoms.com](https://legalatoms.com/texas/texas-small-claims-court-limit-amount/#:~:text=%2420%2C000%20exclusive%20of%20interest%20and,appear%20in%20the%20%E2%80%9CPrayer%20for)) ([legalatoms.com](https://legalatoms.com/texas/texas-small-claims-court-limit-amount/#:~:text=that%20as%20%E2%80%9Cclaim%20splitting,fees%20and%20stricter%20rules%20apply)). For instance, if a customer owes you $25,000, you cannot file two suits for $12,500 each; you must either sue for up to $20,000 and forgive the rest, or take the case to county/district court. If you attempt to split a single claim, the justice court may dismiss or consolidate the actions as a violation of the one-recovery rule. The proper method to stay within the limit is to **expressly waive** any amount above $20,000 in your pleadings, which the Texas rules allow in order to confer jurisdiction ([legalatoms.com](https://legalatoms.com/texas/texas-small-claims-court-limit-amount/#:~:text=%2420%2C000%20exclusive%20of%20interest%20and,appear%20in%20the%20%E2%80%9CPrayer%20for)). By waiving the excess, you can proceed in small claims, but you’ll be unable to later recover the waived portion.  

**Raising or lowering the cap:** The $20,000 limit was raised from $10,000 effective September 1, 2020 ([docket.wp.txstate.edu](https://docket.wp.txstate.edu/2020/09/10/jurisdictional-limit-increase-now-in-effect/#:~:text=limit%20increase%20to%20%2420%2C000%20went,regarding%20the%20jurisdictional%20limit%20increase)). It may be adjusted by future legislation, but as of 2026 it remains $20,000. Always verify the current cap before filing, as legal changes can occur.  

## 2. What types of cases qualify — EVERYTHING YOU CAN SUE FOR  

Texas’s justice courts (small claims courts) can hear a **very broad range of civil disputes** so long as the relief sought is monetary (or the recovery of specific personal property) and within the $20,000 limit ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=)). Below is an exhaustive list of claim types commonly brought in Texas small claims, with notes on eligibility, examples, and any special rules or state-specific nuances for each. If your case doesn’t neatly fit a category, don’t worry – Texas law allows small claims for essentially any civil claim for money damages not specifically excluded (see the “Cannot sue for” section).  

### Contract & money-owed disputes  
**Breach of written contract:** If someone fails to perform as promised in a written agreement (e.g. a contractor doesn’t complete work you paid for, or a client refuses to pay your invoice), you can sue for the unpaid amount or damages up to $20,000. **Examples:** a homeowner sues a remodeler for abandoning a project after being paid, or a freelancer sues a client for non-payment under a written consulting contract. Texas justice courts routinely handle breach of contract cases . Be sure to bring a copy of the written contract and evidence of the breach (emails, invoices, etc.). If the contract has an attorney-fee clause or Texas law provides fees (see **Section 5** on statutes of limitations and attorney fees), you can claim those as part of the damages. Texas requires that if you want to recover attorney fees on a contract, you must first send the opposing party a **30-day demand letter** for payment; if they don’t pay, and you later win, you can get your reasonable attorney fees added ([tcss.legis.texas.gov](https://tcss.legis.texas.gov/resources/CP/htm/CP.38.htm#:~:text=,be%20represented%20by%20an%20attorney)).  

**Breach of oral/handshake agreement:** Oral contracts are generally enforceable in Texas (except certain agreements that must be in writing under the Statute of Frauds). If you had a clear verbal agreement that the other party didn’t honor – for example, you lent your neighbor equipment in exchange for $500 and they never paid – you can sue in small claims. **Example:** A homeowner orally contracts with a painter for a $2,000 job; the painter never shows up after taking a deposit. You can sue for the deposit and any extra cost you incur hiring someone else. In Texas, breach of oral contract is treated similarly to written contracts and subject to a 4-year statute of limitations ([www.statutechecker.com](https://www.statutechecker.com/limitations/texas/breach-of-contract-oral#:~:text=4%20Years%20Limit%3A%20Texas%20Breach,Texas%20Four%20years%20from%20date)) ([pbateslaw.com](https://pbateslaw.com/statute-of-limitations-breach-of-contract/#:~:text=PLLC%20pbateslaw,contract%20cases%2C%20where%20the%20Texas)). It’s wise to compile any evidence of the agreement (texts, notes, witnesses) since nothing is on paper. The justice court will consider the credibility of each side’s story.  

**Unpaid personal loans (friends & family IOUs):** Small claims court is a common venue to recover a personal loan you made that wasn’t repaid. A signed IOU or promissory note is ideal evidence, but even if the loan was informal, you can sue if you can prove the money was a loan (not a gift) and the terms of repayment. **Example:** You loaned an acquaintance $5,000 to start a business, with an agreement they’d repay in 6 months, but a year has passed with no payment. Bring any proof of the loan (a note, Venmo/paypal records, texts where they acknowledge the debt). If the loan was documented by a **promissory note**, Texas law treats it as a written contract ([statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/GetStatute.aspx?Code=CP&Value=16#:~:text=)). If it was entirely verbal, it’s an oral contract. Either way, it’s within small claims jurisdiction up to $20k.  

**Unpaid promissory notes:** A promissory note is a written promise to pay a certain sum. If someone signed a note to repay you and defaulted, it’s a straightforward small claim. Texas courts can award the balance due plus any interest specified in the note (as long as the interest rate is not usurious – Texas Finance Code §302.001 generally caps non-usurious contract interest, e.g., 10% if not otherwise agreed). **Example:** A former employee signed a promissory note to repay a $3,000 advance, then left and didn’t pay. You can sue for the $3,000 plus any contract interest. Attach a copy of the note to your petition and provide a sworn statement that it’s true and the amount due ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,plaintiff%20is%20entitled%2C%20if%20any)). In fact, if the note is signed by the defendant and you provide a sworn copy and calculation, the justice of the peace can enter a default judgment for that amount without a hearing if the defendant fails to answer ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,plaintiff%20is%20entitled%2C%20if%20any)).  

**Unpaid commercial invoices / accounts receivable:** Businesses can use small claims court to collect on unpaid invoices for goods or services, as long as the total is ≤ $20,000. **Example:** A wholesaler sues a retailer for $8,000 of delivered inventory that wasn’t paid for. Attach copies of the invoices, any delivery receipts, and demand letters you sent. Texas allows **“sworn account”** suits for certain commercial debts (Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 508); in a debt claim case, you can file a sworn statement of the account. The defendant then must file a sworn denial if they dispute it, or else the account is taken as true. This can simplify the case. Note that if you’re an **assignee or debt collector** collecting a debt, Texas categorizes that as a “debt claim” case with slightly different procedural rules (e.g., Rule 508) – but it’s still in justice court and still subject to the $20k cap ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28b%29%20Debt%20Claim%20Case,Part%20V%2C%20Rule%20508%20applies)).  

**Unpaid rent (residential or commercial):** A landlord can sue in small claims for overdue rent owed under a lease (but **not** to evict – eviction cases are a separate docket). **Example:** A residential landlord, after evicting a tenant, files a claim for $5,000 in unpaid rent and late fees that accrued before move-out. Or a commercial landlord sues a former tenant for 3 months’ rent left on the lease after they abandoned the property. These are contract claims (the lease is the contract). Attach a copy of the lease and an itemized rent ledger. Note: If you’re simultaneously seeking possession (eviction), that part must be filed as an **Eviction Case**, not a small claim ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=)) ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=civil%20matters%20in%20which%20exclusive,than%20%2420%2C000%2C%20exclusive%20of%20interest)). The unpaid rent, however, *can* be claimed within the eviction case or separately in small claims. Texas law allows eviction suits to include a rent claim, but only for rent and only up to the justice court’s jurisdiction. If you miss including some damages in an eviction, you can still use small claims afterward for those amounts (e.g., property damage by the tenant).  

**Bounced / dishonored checks:** If someone paid you with a bad check (non-sufficient funds or “NSF”), you can sue for the amount of the check and in many cases additional damages or fees allowed by statute. Texas law permits the holder of a bounced check to recover a **service fee up to $30** in addition to the check amount ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/business-and-commerce-code/title-1/chapter-3/subchapter-e/section-3-506/#:~:text=,maximum%20processing%20fee%20of%20%2430)). (This is set by Texas Business & Commerce Code §3.506.) Some merchants print this fee on signs or receipts, but even if not, the law allows up to $30 per check ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/business-and-commerce-code/title-1/chapter-3/subchapter-e/section-3-506/#:~:text=,maximum%20processing%20fee%20of%20%2430)). **Example:** A customer wrote a $500 check that bounced; you can sue for $500 + up to $30 processing fee. Unlike some states, Texas does not provide a civil penalty of double or treble the check’s amount by default – it’s more limited. However, you should send the statutory **demand letter for bad checks** (often a 30-day notice) because under the Texas Penal Code, bouncing a check can be criminal and such a letter can sometimes prompt payment. If the letter was sent and they still didn’t pay, that letter can be evidence of their knowing the check was bad. Remember to bring a copy of the bounced check (or bank return slip) and proof of any mail notices.  

**Open accounts / running tabs:** An “open account” usually refers to an ongoing account where the customer makes purchases on credit (like a materials supplier extending credit to a contractor, or a bar tab). These are collectible in small claims. **Example:** A feed store sues a rancher for $4,000 balance on an open account for feed deliveries over months. In Texas, suits on an open account can be filed as a **“Suit on Account”** which, if properly verified, shifts the burden to the defendant to refute the account. Be sure to provide a **sworn statement of the account** (an affidavit verifying the total owed and that all lawful offsets/credits are applied). This satisfies Rule 508’s requirements for a debt claim case. The defendant must then file a sworn denial or specific disputes, or else the account is presumed correct. This can significantly streamline your case.  

**Co-signer/guarantor disputes:** If you co-signed a loan or guaranteed an obligation and ended up having to pay because the primary party defaulted, you can sue that party in small claims to recover what you paid (this is essentially seeking **reimbursement under the contract of guaranty or equitable subrogation**). **Example:** You co-signed an auto loan for a friend who defaulted; you paid the bank $10,000 to settle it. You can sue your friend for $10,000 in small claims. Bring proof of the guaranty and your payment (e.g., the guaranty agreement and receipts of what you paid). Conversely, if someone guaranteed *your* debt and is now suing you after paying it, that is allowed too. Such cases are treated as contract enforcement. Texas law will generally enforce guaranty agreements according to their terms.  

### Consumer disputes  
**Refunds for defective products:** If you bought a product that turned out to be defective or not as advertised, you can sue for a refund or the cost of repair (plus possibly related damages) as long as the amount is within $20k. **Examples:** A consumer sues a furniture store for $2,000 because a “new” sofa delivered was broken and the store won’t refund; or a person buys a used boat “as is” that immediately fails – while “as is” makes it harder due to waiver of warranty, if there was an active misrepresentation or undisclosed defect, the buyer might claim fraud or DTPA (Deceptive Trade Practices Act) violations. Texas’s **Deceptive Trade Practices–Consumer Protection Act (DTPA)** is a powerful statute for defective goods or false advertising. It allows consumers to recover not just actual damages but potentially **treble damages** (triple) if the seller acted knowingly, plus court costs and attorney fees ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/business-commerce-code/title-2/chapter-17/subchapter-e/section-17-50/#:~:text=,mental%20anguish%20and%20economic%20damages)). Importantly, before suing under the DTPA, the consumer must send a **60-day demand letter** to the seller describing the problem and giving them a chance to make it right ([guides.sll.texas.gov](https://guides.sll.texas.gov/consumer-protection/relief#:~:text=If%20you%20have%20a%20DTPA,to%20avoid%20going%20to%20court)). If you intend to invoke the DTPA (which is common for consumer product cases), be sure you sent that notice letter at least 60 days prior ([guides.sll.texas.gov](https://guides.sll.texas.gov/consumer-protection/relief#:~:text=If%20you%20have%20a%20DTPA,to%20avoid%20going%20to%20court)). In small claims, DTPA suits are allowed (so long as total damages sought – even if trebled – don’t exceed $20k, or you waive the excess). For instance, if a defective product caused you $6,000 in loss, which you believe qualifies for triple damages ($18,000), that fits under $20k. Bring evidence of the defect (the product itself or photos, repair reports, etc.) and any warranty or advertisement that was breached. Texas also has implied warranties (e.g. implied warranty of merchantability for goods), which can form the basis of a claim if not disclaimed. Implied warranty breach suits have a 4-year limit if treated as contract ([www.statutechecker.com](https://www.statutechecker.com/limitations/texas/breach-of-contract-oral#:~:text=4%20Years%20Limit%3A%20Texas%20Breach,Texas%20Four%20years%20from%20date)), but if you sue under DTPA for it, the DTPA’s 2-year limit from discovery applies ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/business-and-commerce-code/bus-com-sect-17-565/#:~:text=Texas%20Business%20and%20Commerce%20Code,years%20after%20the%20date%20on)).  

**Refunds for services not performed or done poorly:** If you paid for a service and the provider didn’t do the work at all, or did such a shoddy job that it must be redone, you can sue to get your money back (and possibly the cost to fix their mistakes). **Examples:** You pay a web designer $1,500 but they never deliver a finished website; or you hire a tree-trimming service and they do such poor work that you must hire another company to correct it. These are breaches of contract. In Texas, you can recover the amount you paid and any additional damages caused by the breach (like what you spent to fix issues). If the work was done poorly, you should give the provider an opportunity to make it right (as a practical matter and sometimes as a contract requirement), but if they refuse or fail, small claims is appropriate. For services that implicate professional licenses (e.g., a licensed contractor), you might also complain to any regulatory board, but that doesn’t bar your lawsuit. If the conduct was deceptive (they misrepresented their skills or outcome), it could fall under the DTPA as well, possibly allowing multiple damages. Bring your contract or receipt and photographs or witness testimony showing the poor quality.  

**Auto repair overcharges or shoddy work:** Disputes with mechanics and body shops are common in small claims. **Examples:** A mechanic charged you for new parts that were never installed, or did an engine rebuild that failed immediately due to improper work. Texas law (Texas Occupations Code Chapter 2305 / DMV rules) provides some protections – for instance, repair shops generally should provide an estimate and get authorization for work beyond the estimate. If you were overcharged (billed more than the agreed estimate without consent) or charged for work not actually done, those are strong claims. Suing for shoddy workmanship is also viable: you can claim the cost you paid and the cost to redo or repair the damage. There is no specific statute requiring a pre-suit notice in auto repair cases (unless you claim under DTPA for deceptive acts – then the 60-day notice applies). If the shop is certified or offers a warranty, use that to bolster your case. Bring the repair invoices and, if possible, a written evaluation from another mechanic explaining what was done wrong or not done at all. Texas’s DTPA “laundry list” of deceptive acts includes making false statements about the need for parts/service or about repairs performed, which is directly applicable to a mechanic who, say, lies about replacing your brakes ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/business-commerce-code/title-2/chapter-17/subchapter-e/section-17-50/#:~:text=,mental%20anguish%20and%20economic%20damages)). A successful DTPA claim could yield up to 3× damages if acted knowingly.  

**Home repair / contractor disputes (incomplete work, defects, abandonment):** Home improvement contractors who take money and don’t finish the job – or do it improperly – can be sued in small claims as long as the amount is within the limit. Many home repair disputes exceed $20k, but smaller projects or partial claims often qualify. **Example:** A homeowner paid a handyman $5,000 to remodel a bathroom and he never finished the job, or did subpar work causing leaks. This is breach of contract. Also, Texas has a Residential Construction Commission Act (now largely repealed) that used to require certain steps for defects, but that largely doesn’t apply anymore for small contractors. Some contracts might require arbitration or have notice provisions – check your contract. Otherwise, you may file suit. If the contractor was unlicensed (Texas doesn’t have a general contractor license requirement except for certain trades like electricians, plumbers), you can still sue. If they engaged in **fraud** (e.g., took money with no intent to perform), you might also report it to authorities as theft, and pursue punitive damages in court. Under Texas law, you cannot get punitive damages in small claims without presenting a solid case of fraud, malice, or gross negligence (and even then, remember the $20k cap likely includes any exemplary damages). The DTPA can be invoked if the contractor’s actions were deceptive or unconscionable – e.g., lying about being licensed, or promising to use high-grade materials and using cheap ones. For workmanship defects, often the measure of damages is the cost to repair the work. You might hire another contractor to fix it and sue the original for that cost. Provide photos of defects and expert statements if available.  

**Appliance/electronics warranty failures:** If you bought an appliance or electronic device that failed and the seller/manufacturer won’t honor the warranty, you can sue for the cost of repair or replacement. **Example:** A $1,200 refrigerator dies within its warranty period, but the manufacturer drags its feet or refuses to fix/replace it. You can file a small claim for the cost of a new fridge or the amount you had to spend on repairs plus any spoilage losses (within reason). Federal law (the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act) gives consumers rights to enforce warranties, and you can use state court to do so. Texas courts will enforce written warranties; you can also claim breach of the **implied warranty of merchantability** (the product should be fit for ordinary use) if it’s a consumer good sold by a merchant. The implied warranty exists by default unless disclaimed. Most big manufacturers have arbitration clauses now – check if you inadvertently agreed to arbitration (sometimes in the product registration or terms). If not, small claims is fine. The DTPA can also apply here if the refusal to honor a warranty is deemed a deceptive trade practice. In a Magnuson-Moss Act claim, if you win, you might also recover attorney fees, but note that in small claims you’re likely self-represented or the fees would be limited.  

**Cancelled or no-show service providers (photographers, caterers, event venues):** If a service provider for an event (wedding vendor, DJ, etc.) breaches their contract – for example, they simply don’t show up on the day or cancel last-minute – you can sue for any payments you made **and** the extra costs you incurred finding a replacement. **Example:** A wedding photographer who was prepaid $2,000 fails to appear, causing you to hire a last-minute photographer for $2,500. You could sue to recover the $2,000 plus the $500 difference (and possibly any demonstrable consequential losses, though intangible damages like “ruined memories” are not compensable in contract). Texas allows recovery of **actual damages** in such cases; if the conduct was extreme (the vendor took your money knowing they wouldn’t perform), it could be fraud – possibly supporting mental anguish or punitive damages, but those are rarely awarded in small claims due to proof requirements and the general focus on economic loss. Be sure to have the contract and proof of your payments. If you communicated by email or text about their cancellation, include that. In Texas, certain event contracts might also fall under DTPA if the vendor misrepresented anything.  

**Dry cleaning / property loss in custody (bailees):** If you gave your property to someone for service (dry cleaner, repair shop, valet parking, storage facility, movers, etc.) and they lost it or damaged it, you can sue for the value of the item or cost of repair. **Examples:** A dry cleaner lost your $500 suit; a phone repair shop broke your device; a valet service’s employee crashed your car; a storage unit facility’s negligence led to your stored furniture being ruined by water. These situations involve the law of **bailment** – when you hand over property, the custodian has a duty of care. If they fail to return it in good condition, there’s a presumption of negligence (unless contract terms or posted signs limit liability – e.g. many dry cleaners post a limited liability per garment or require proof of value). Texas allows you to recover the fair market value of lost/destroyed property. Fair market value is generally what the item was worth used, not replacement cost brand-new (unless it was new). However, if the item had special value, you might need an appraisal or receipts. Many of these businesses ask customers to sign waivers or have fine print limiting their liability (e.g., “Not responsible for items left over 30 days” or “maximum $50 per garment”). Such clauses are not always ironclad in court, especially if the loss was due to their gross negligence or violation of a consumer protection law. Bring any ticket or contract you got and explain why you should be paid full value. Often, simply filing suit will prompt the business to settle.  

**Gym / subscription / membership cancellation refunds:** If you canceled a membership or subscription per the contract terms but keep getting charged (or weren’t refunded a prepaid amount), you can sue for the money owed. **Example:** You paid a local gym $900 for a 1-year membership, but after 3 months the gym went out of business and refused to refund the unused portion. Or you followed all the steps to cancel a weight-loss program free trial, yet your credit card was charged $200. These cases can fall under both contract law and Texas consumer protection law. Texas has specific statutes regulating some of these (for instance, Texas Occupations Code Chapter 702 for health spa (gym) contracts requires certain refund rules and bonding). If the gym violated those, mention it. But even without a specific statute, you have a straightforward claim for the unused services. Provide evidence of your cancellation notice and the continued charges. If it’s an automatic renewal issue – Texas law (Bus. & Comm. Code § 605.001) requires certain disclosures for automatic renewals; failure to comply can be a defense to the charge. The DTPA might allow you to recover up to 3× if a company willfully employed deceptive practices in subscription renewals or cancellations.  

**Auto dealer / used-car disputes:** Many car disputes exceed $20k, but for lower-value used cars or specific issues you may use small claims. **Examples:** A used car dealer sold you a car for $8,000 and misrepresented that it had never been in a wreck (but it had frame damage), or rolled back the odometer – you can sue for the diminished value or cancel the sale. Texas has a **Lemon Law** for new vehicles (administered by TxDMV) which usually requires going through a state arbitration process for a replacement or refund; you generally wouldn’t use small claims for an official “lemon” vehicle because the Lemon Law remedy is exclusive when invoked. However, for **used cars or dealer fraud**, small claims is available. If a dealer committed odometer fraud or lied about the title or condition, that’s a DTPA violation and possibly a federal violation (odometer fraud has separate penalties). If it’s a private seller, the DTPA typically doesn’t apply (it covers sellers in business of selling), but you could claim common-law fraud if you can prove they knowingly misled you. Keep in mind “as is” clauses: in Texas, if you bought a used car “as is” and the seller didn’t commit fraud, you usually can’t recover for undisclosed defects (the “as is” negates implied warranties). But if you can show the seller actively concealed or lied, an “as is” might not protect them. For small claims, gather evidence like pre-sale texts/emails, the ad listing (print it out), and any inspection reports. The **Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act** specifically lists false representations about a vehicle’s condition or prior repairs as actionable ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/business-commerce-code/title-2/chapter-17/subchapter-e/section-17-50/#:~:text=,mental%20anguish%20and%20economic%20damages)). If successful, you could get your actual damages (like cost of repairs, or difference in value) and possibly additional damages if the conduct was knowing.  

**False advertising / deceptive trade practices:** Any scenario where a business’s false or misleading statements caused you loss can potentially be brought as a DTPA claim in small claims (if within the dollar limit). **Examples:** A store advertised a TV as 4K resolution when it was actually 1080p – you can sue for the difference in value, or a travel company promised a “luxury 5-star hotel” for your prepaid vacation but provided a 3-star motel – you might seek a refund. Texas’s DTPA (Business & Commerce Code §17.41 et seq.) is very consumer-friendly: it has a list of prohibited acts (the “laundry list”) like false advertising, misrepresenting characteristics or benefits of goods/services, false guarantees, etc., and if the conduct fits, you can win damages and mandatory attorney fees. In egregious cases, if the company acted “knowingly” (they knew the ad was false) you can get up to **three times** your economic damages ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/business-commerce-code/title-2/chapter-17/subchapter-e/section-17-50/#:~:text=,mental%20anguish%20and%20economic%20damages)). Remember the **60-day pre-suit notice** requirement for DTPA ([guides.sll.texas.gov](https://guides.sll.texas.gov/consumer-protection/relief#:~:text=If%20you%20have%20a%20DTPA,to%20avoid%20going%20to%20court)) – send a certified letter to the business outlining your complaint and damages at least 60 days before filing suit. If they make a reasonable offer in that time, it can limit their exposure. If they ignore or lowball, you can proceed. In small claims court, the judge will apply the same DTPA law as any court. Keep your claim within $20k – note that if the law allows triple damages, you should generally cap your actual damages such that even if tripled they wouldn’t exceed the limit. (Alternatively, you might explicitly waive seeking more than the cap.) Provide the offending advertisement or written misrepresentation as evidence.  

### Property damage  
**Vehicle collisions (minor accidents):** If you were in a car accident and your property damage (vehicle repair/replacement cost) is within $20k, you can use small claims. Most car accident claims, especially those with injury, exceed the limit, but for minor crashes it’s a good forum – for example, a fender-bender causing $3,500 in damage. You can sue the at-fault driver for the repair costs, rental car expenses, and related out-of-pocket costs (but not pain-and-suffering in justice court, because that would be a personal injury element – see note below on personal injury). Usually, if insurance is involved, you won’t need to sue unless the insurer is disputing liability or value. In Texas, you *cannot directly sue the other driver’s insurer* in small claims (they have no privity with you unless they violated something like Chapter 541 Insurance Code, which is complex for small claims). You sue the driver (or owner if different). Bring repair estimates or bills, photos of the damage, and ideally a police report or witness statement showing fault. If you already repaired the car, use the paid invoice as proof of damages. If it’s a **diminished value** claim (vehicle lost market value even after repair), you may need an expert appraisal to prove that amount – consider whether it’s cost-effective. Note: If you had injuries, small claims can technically award personal injury damages up to $20k (see “personal injury” below), but for anything beyond minor soreness, it’s usually better in county court.  

**Damage to personal property by another person:** Any situation where someone negligently or intentionally damaged your belongings can be brought as a property damage claim. **Example:** A neighbor backing out of their driveway ran over your lawn furniture; or a guest in your home accidentally spilled red wine on your $3,000 rug and won’t compensate you. If it was negligence, you must show the person’s duty and breach (which for everyday acts like driving or being a guest is straightforward). If it was intentional (vandalism), you can sue for civil damages (and they might also face criminal charges, but that’s separate). Bring proof of the item’s value – original receipts, repair estimates, or online valuation for similar used items. Texas allows recovery of either the **reasonable cost to repair** the item, or if it’s beyond repair, the **fair market value** at the time it was destroyed. Sentimental value is not compensable except for certain items like heirlooms where market value is hard to ascertain (even then one usually uses replacement cost or a jury’s discretion, but in small claims the judge will be conservative). Keep your claimed amount grounded in evidence to convince the judge.  

**Damage by a tenant to a landlord’s property:** If a former tenant caused damage to your rental property beyond normal wear and tear, you can sue for the cost of repairs (if it exceeds what you withheld from their security deposit). Often landlords will deduct from the security deposit and then sue for any remainder if the deposit didn’t cover everything. **Example:** A tenant left with $5,000 of damage (holes in walls, broken appliances), and the deposit was $1,500, leaving $3,500 unpaid. A landlord can file in justice court for that $3,500. It’s a good idea to itemize the damages and costs and show that you complied with the **security deposit accounting requirements** – e.g., you provided the tenant with a written list of deductions within 30 days of them moving out, as required by Texas Property Code §92.104. If you failed to provide that accounting or wrongly withheld the deposit, the tenant might counter-sue (see landlord–tenant section). But assuming you did it right, you can recover the actual damage amount. Photo evidence (move-in vs move-out condition) is very persuasive. Also bring receipts or estimates for each repair (e.g., invoice for repainting walls). Texas does **not** allow landlords to charge the tenant for ordinary wear (like minor nail holes or carpet wear), so focus on clear damage beyond wear. The burden is on the landlord to prove the damage and cost, since the tenant’s money is at stake.  

**Damage by a landlord to a tenant’s property:** Conversely, if a landlord (or their negligence) caused damage to a tenant’s personal property, the tenant can sue. **Example:** A landlord’s failure to fix a leaky roof led to your furniture and electronics being ruined by rain ($10,000 loss), or the landlord’s employee mistakenly cleared out your apartment thinking you’d vacated and your belongings vanished. These would be claims for negligence or breach of lease (the lease might require the landlord to refrain from such actions). In Texas, landlords often insert clauses that they aren’t liable for tenant’s property damage and urge tenants to carry renters insurance. Some of those clauses can be enforceable to limit liability (except in cases of gross negligence or violation of law). Still, you can attempt to recover if you believe the landlord was clearly at fault. Document the value of your items (receipts, photos before/after). If it was negligence (like not fixing a leak they knew about), show the notices you gave requesting repair – under the **Property Code Chapter 92**, if you gave proper notice of a condition affecting safety and they didn’t repair, you can potentially recover for resulting property damage and even statutory remedies (though the specific “Repair and Remedy” subchapter B is more aimed at rent reduction and civil penalties ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/property-code/title-8/chapter-92/subchapter-b/section-92-0563/#:~:text=,one%20month%27s%20rent%20plus%20%24500))). If landlord’s actions were willful (like an illegal lockout or unlawful disposal of tenant’s goods), note that’s actually covered under Tex. Prop. Code §92.0081 and §92.0082 – which allow for civil penalties. For instance, if a landlord **wrongfully retains a tenant’s personal property** after an eviction, the tenant can sue for return of property or its value, and possibly statutory penalties (if it violated the property code). These scenarios can get complex, but small claims judges often just seek to compensate the loss in front of them.  

**Tree/fence/boundary disputes resulting in damage:** If a neighbor’s actions damaged your fence or trees, or vice versa, small claims can sort out modest boundary-related damages (though not boundary *title* disputes – those are excluded from JP court ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=))). **Examples:** A neighbor cut down your tree by mistake (or on purpose) thinking it was on their side – you can sue for the value of the tree. Texas recognizes trees as valuable property; the value can be measured by replacement cost or the tree’s contribution to property value. Another example: a construction crew on your neighbor’s lot accidentally knocked over your fence, costing $3,000 to fix – you’d sue the neighbor or the contractor for that repair cost. Provide evidence of the boundary (survey, if relevant) and photos of the damage. If it’s a living thing like a tree or landscaping, you might need an arborist’s statement on value. Texas allows treble damages for **timber theft** under certain circumstances (Nat. Res. Code §151.001) but that’s outside small claims unless value is low. Generally in small claims, you’ll stick to actual replacement/repair costs. Keep in mind if *you* do something like trim a neighbor’s tree that overhangs and accidentally kill it, they could likewise sue you in small claims. Many such disputes can also potentially fall under “self-help” (you are permitted to trim branches to the property line in Texas), but causing harm beyond your rights makes you liable. It’s wise to try an amicable resolution or mediation with neighbors, but small claims is there if needed.  

**Pet damage (dog bite or other pet-caused damage to property):** If someone’s pet damages your property, you can sue the owner for those costs. **Examples:** Your neighbor’s dog chewed up your $1,500 sprinkler system; or their dog attacked and injured your dog and you incurred $2,000 in vet bills. Pets are legally property in Texas, so injury to a pet is technically property damage (though many pet owners see them as family – the law limits recovery typically to vet bills and value, not emotional distress over a pet). You can also sue for **personal injury** if a dog bit you (that’s covered below under personal injury). For property damage caused by animals, Texas applies basic negligence and sometimes strict liability. Generally, for dog bites or damage, Texas requires that the owner was negligent or knew the dog had dangerous propensities (the “one bite rule” for strict liability in bites). In small claims, if a dog repeatedly came onto your property and caused damage, you’d argue the owner failed to confine it (which most city/county ordinances require – violation can be negligence per se). Provide any evidence like photos of the damage, vet bills, or eyewitness accounts of the pet causing it. Also document attempts you made to notify the owner or get them to pay voluntarily.  

**Damage during a move (by movers or friends):** If you hired a moving company and they broke or lost your belongings, you can sue for the damage. **Example:** A moving company dropped your 70” TV and it’s ruined. The wrinkle: moving companies often have contracts limiting liability (e.g., released value at a small per-pound rate) or requiring arbitration. Interstate moves are governed by federal law (Carmack Amendment) which limits suits to federal court typically, but intrastate Texas moves can be handled in JP court unless the contract compels arbitration. Many moving companies will pay a token amount (like $0.60 per pound) if you didn’t purchase added insurance – unfortunately those contract terms can be binding. Still, you can argue in equity if the limitation is unconscionable or if they intentionally damaged items. If friends helped you move and accidentally smashed something, you can also sue your friend in small claims (although you may wish to maintain the friendship and not litigate – but legally, you could). The judge will consider if it was truly an accident versus gross negligence. Typically, you’d get fair market value of the item or its repair cost. Always mitigate your damages – e.g., if something can be fixed for $200, you shouldn’t be asking for $1,000 replacement without justification.  

### Landlord–tenant (financial only — possession/eviction is separate)  
*Important:* Small claims court cannot grant you an order evicting someone or restoring possession of rental property – those are handled in **eviction cases** (forcible entry and detainer) which are also filed in justice court but under a different process ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=)). However, landlords and tenants **can** sue each other in small claims for **money** owed under the lease or by law, aside from possession. Here are common landlord-tenant money disputes suitable for small claims:  

**Unreturned security deposits:** This is perhaps the #1 common small claim by tenants. Under Texas law, a residential landlord must refund a tenant’s security deposit (minus any valid deductions for damages or back rent) within **30 days** after the tenant moves out and provides a forwarding address ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/property-code/prop-sect-92-109/#:~:text=,have%20acted%20in%20bad%20faith)). If any portion is kept, the landlord must send an itemized written list of deductions within that 30 days. If the landlord **fails to return the deposit or send a deduction list** by the deadline, or **keeps the deposit in bad faith**, the tenant can sue and may be entitled to **treble damages (3×)** the amount of the deposit wrongfully withheld, plus $100 and attorney’s fees ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/property-code/prop-sect-92-109/#:~:text=,suit%20to%20recover%20the%20deposit)). Specifically, Texas Property Code §92.109 says a landlord who **in bad faith** retains a deposit is liable for **three times the amount wrongfully withheld + $100 + reasonable attorney’s fees** ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/property-code/prop-sect-92-109/#:~:text=,suit%20to%20recover%20the%20deposit)). “Bad faith” is presumed if the landlord neither refunded nor sent a required accounting within 30 days ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/property-code/prop-sect-92-109/#:~:text=,have%20acted%20in%20bad%20faith)). The landlord can rebut that by showing reasons for delay, etc., but the law strongly incentivizes compliance. **Example:** Your $1,200 deposit was kept with no accounting – you could sue for $3,600 + $100, and you’d likely win at least triple the unaccounted amount if the court agrees it was bad faith. Make sure you **gave your landlord your forwarding address in writing** – Texas law does not obligate the landlord to refund until you give a forwarding address ([statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/DocViewer.aspx?DocKey=PR%2FPR.92&ExactPhrase=False&HighlightType=1&Phrases=What%7Ctypes%7Cof%7Cpayments%7Cmust%7Clandlord%7Caccept%7Cunless%7Cspecified%7Cotherwise%7Cin%7Cthe%7Cwritten%7Clease%3F&QueryText=What+types+of+payments+must+a+landlord+accept+unless+specified+otherwise+in+the+written+lease%3F#:~:text=PROPERTY%20CODE%20CHAPTER%2092,only%20if%20the%20requirement%20is)). If you didn’t, do that before suing. Bring evidence of the address notice, your lease, proof you left the unit in good condition (photos, check-out sheet), and any communications about the deposit. Small claims courts frequently award 2× or 3× deposits in egregious cases, as the statute directs. If the landlord provided an accounting but you dispute the deductions, you can still sue – the judge will decide if the deductions were valid. Even if not “bad faith,” you can get the wrongfully withheld portion plus perhaps your court costs. Note: For **commercial leases**, deposit rules are a bit different (Property Code §93.005 requires refund within 60 days and has a presumption of bad faith with similar penalties for landlords). So commercial tenants can also sue similarly, but with a 60-day timeline.  

**Refund of prepaid last month’s rent:** If you paid in advance for last month’s rent or any rent that ended up not being used (e.g., you moved out early by agreement or landlord re-rented the unit), you can claim a refund for any overlap. Sometimes tenants mistakenly pay rent beyond their move-out or the landlord double-collects rent for the same period after reletting – that money should be returned. **Example:** You gave 60-days notice and moved out in 30 days; the landlord promptly re-leased to a new tenant starting the next month, effectively getting double rent for that month you weren’t there, while keeping your prepaid rent. Texas law (Property Code §91.006) requires a landlord who mitigates damages by reletting to credit the previous tenant – they cannot keep double rent. You can sue for the rent covering the period after the new tenant moved in. Provide proof of the new tenant’s start date if possible (sometimes the landlord’s admission or an advertisement can suffice). Or if you simply overpaid (paid first and last upfront, and last month wasn’t needed), that’s straightforward: show proof of payment and that you owed nothing for that last month (because you were gone and no damages).  

**Habitability issues / “repair and deduct” counterclaims:** While small claims cannot make a landlord **perform repairs**, a tenant can sue for money damages if the landlord’s violation of the habitability or repair laws caused financial loss. Also, Texas law allows tenants, in certain serious situations, to pay for a repair and deduct it from rent (Property Code §92.0561) if the landlord failed to fix conditions affecting health/safety after notice ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/property-code/prop-sect-92-0561/#:~:text=,of%20rent%20under%20the%20circumstances)). If you as a tenant used the “repair-and-deduct” remedy properly (which has strict limits – you must give proper notices and the cost must be within one month’s rent or $500, whichever is greater ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/property-code/prop-sect-92-0561/#:~:text=,of%20rent%20under%20the%20circumstances))), and the landlord retaliates by trying to charge you or not reimburse you, you can defend that in court or sue to affirm your right to that deduction. **Example:** The landlord refused to fix an AC in a Texas summer; after giving written notices as required, you paid $400 to repair it and deducted that from your next rent. The landlord then charged you a $400 “unpaid rent” fee. You could sue for a declaratory judgment of sorts (or just wait for them to sue you) to show your deduction was lawful – though small claims doesn’t give formal declaratory relief, you effectively prove it as a defense if they come after you, or get a judgment if they withheld your deposit or other money due to it. Alternatively, if habitability issues made part of the unit unusable or caused you expenses (like high electric bills due to a faulty heater never fixed), you might sue for those damages. Note: Texas’s specific **“Repair and Remedy” case** (Rule 509) can be filed to seek a rent reduction or civil penalty and court-ordered repairs ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/property-code/title-8/chapter-92/subchapter-b/section-92-0563/#:~:text=,repair%20or%20remedy%20the%20condition)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/property-code/title-8/chapter-92/subchapter-b/section-92-0563/#:~:text=,one%20month%27s%20rent%20plus%20%24500)). That is a special type of justice court case for tenants. But you can also just sue for money if, say, you had to live with a leaky roof for 3 months and want a partial rent abatement. The Property Code allows certain rent deductions or penalties in extreme cases (e.g., if landlord’s bad-faith failure to repair after notice, tenant can sometimes get one month’s rent plus $500 as a penalty ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/property-code/title-8/chapter-92/subchapter-b/section-92-0563/#:~:text=,one%20month%27s%20rent%20plus%20%24500))). Those specific penalties usually would be sought via a Rule 509 Repair & Remedy action, but if that wasn’t done, you might still claim a rent credit in small claims. These cases can be legally complex, so a judge might steer tenants toward the Repair & Remedy process. But small claims is viable if the main thing you want is a **rent refund or reimbursement of expenses**.  

**Unpaid rent or damages claimed by landlord against former tenant:** Landlords can file in small claims for any unpaid rent that wasn’t recovered in an eviction suit or for property damage beyond normal wear (as discussed above). **Example:** A tenant skipped out owing 2 months’ rent ($2,400) and also left $1,000 in damages; the landlord applies the deposit but still has $2,000 unrecovered – they can sue the ex-tenant for that $2,000. Often, if the landlord sent the required deposit accounting and demand letter for the balance, they will get a default judgment if the tenant doesn’t respond. From the **tenant’s perspective**, if you believe the landlord is wrongly charging you (e.g., claiming bogus damages or excessive cleaning fees), you should appear and contest it. Provide evidence of the condition you left the property in (photos, witness statements). If you already paid and realize it was wrongful, you could have a claim to get that money back. Small claims will essentially adjudicate whether the landlord’s deductions from deposit or additional charges were valid. Bear in mind, **Texas does not allow punitive “penalty” fees** in leases unless they are a reasonable estimate of actual damages (with one exception: late fees are regulated by statute now – Prop Code §92.019 limits late fees to reasonable amounts, generally not more than 12% of rent for properties with four or fewer units, or 10% for larger, if rent is paid monthly). If a landlord is charging some absurd penalty not reflective of actual loss, point that out – Texas courts will typically only award actual damages, not arbitrary penalties.  

**Wrongful retention of tenant’s property after move-out:** If a landlord or anyone wrongfully holds your personal belongings (e.g., an illegal lockout where the landlord kept your stuff, or you left some items behind and the landlord refuses to return them but won’t claim they’re trash), you can sue for the value of the property or for its return. Texas law (Prop Code §92.0081) prohibits landlords from withholding a tenant’s property except in specific circumstances (like an authorized **landlord’s lien** for unpaid rent, which in residential tenancies is only for certain situations and usually requires a written lease provision and allowing the tenant to redeem by paying owed rent). If your landlord claims such a lien and kept your things, you can challenge it in small claims, arguing it wasn’t executed per the law or the items were exempt (certain personal items cannot be seized, like clothes, tools of trade, etc., under Prop Code §54.042). Often, a landlord who simply boxed up and held your belongings without letting you retrieve them will be seen as wrongful. The court can award you the **market value** of the items. If you prefer the items back and they still have them, you can ask the judge to order their return (Texas justice courts *can* handle suits for specific personal property recovery ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=))), but typically they convert it to a value. If the landlord’s conduct was willful, it overlaps with possible DTPA tie-in (e.g., if they withheld property to force you to pay an invalid charge, that could be “unconscionable” conduct under DTPA). But practically, small claims will resolve it as a straightforward property claim.  

**Disputes over fees (cleaning fees, late fees, pet fees, utility bills):** Many lease disputes involve the landlord charging miscellaneous fees from the tenant either during or after tenancy. If you (tenant) believe a fee was illegally or wrongfully charged, you can sue to recover it (if you paid it) or as a defense to a landlord’s claim for it. **Examples:** Landlord charged a **“redecoration fee”** of $300 out of your deposit without justification – you can sue to get that $300 back. Or a landlord is billing you $100 for pest control that was not in the lease. Texas residential lease law says **non-refundable fees** must be stated as non-refundable (else they are by default part of the deposit). Some cleaning fees, pet fees, etc., if non-refundable by lease, are tough to get back unless the lease was illegal somehow. But if the lease said a $200 non-refundable pet fee and you agreed, you likely can’t recover that. You’re more likely to succeed on fees that were not agreed or that are penal in nature (like an exorbitant late fee). Note: Texas now limits residential late fees – for most properties, a late fee must be a reasonable estimate of late-payment costs and cannot be imposed until rent is at least 2 full days late. Also, Prop Code §92.019 sets presumptive limits (for large complexes, up to 10% of rent; for small landlords, up to 12%). If your landlord charged, say, a $100 late fee on $500 rent (20%), that exceeds the safe harbor – you could argue it’s an unenforceable penalty and get it voided or refunded. Similarly, utility billing must comply with Public Utility Commission rules if it’s submetered or allocated – a landlord can’t arbitrarily upcharge beyond the actual cost. Bring your lease and billing statements as proof of what was agreed versus charged. Small claims judges often cut through complicated lease provisions and ask: was this fee actually incurred cost, or just an arbitrary amount? They will tend to award actual losses.  

### Employment & wages  
**Unpaid final paycheck / earned wages:** Texas workers who haven’t been paid their wages can absolutely use small claims court (though there is also an administrative option – the **Texas Workforce Commission (TWC)** wage claim process – which is free and might be easier for some). If your employer didn’t pay your last paycheck, overtime, or other earned compensation, you have a valid claim for breach of contract (even if employment was at-will, you at least had a contract to be paid for work performed) or under the Texas Payday Law. **Example:** Your employer fired you and never gave you your final check for $800. In Texas, final pay for a terminated employee is due **within 6 days** of termination (if you quit voluntarily, it’s due by the next regular payday) ([www.twc.texas.gov](https://www.twc.texas.gov/programs/wage-and-hour/texas-payday-law#:~:text=,the%20effective%20date%20of%20resignation)). If they missed that deadline, they violated the Payday Law – you can file with TWC within 180 days or sue directly. Unlike California, Texas doesn’t impose a daily penalty for late wages, but a delay can be evidence of claim validity. If you sue in small claims, you can claim the unpaid wages **plus any interest** (prejudgment interest on wages can accrue at 6% per annum if not otherwise agreed ([statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/DocViewer.aspx?DocKey=FI%2FFI.304&ExactPhrase=False&HighlightType=1&Phrases=interest%7Crate%7Con%7Cchild%7Csupport%7Cin%7Ctexas&QueryText=interest+rate+on+child+support+in+texas#:~:text=,prejudgment%20Image%3A%20Previous%20HitinterestImage%3A%20Next))). Also, if you had to hire a lawyer and the amount is significant, note that Texas Labor Code §61.066 allows the court to award attorneys’ fees to an employee who prevails in a wage claim. Even in small claims (where you may or may not have a lawyer), you could recover court costs and potentially fees if you did hire one. **Important:** If the amount is small and you don’t want to go to court, consider the TWC wage claim – but if the 180-day deadline passed or you prefer court, small claims is fine. Bring evidence of your employment and the hours worked or salary owed (timesheets, pay stubs, termination letter). If the employer gave a reason for not paying (e.g., “you damaged equipment so we’re withholding pay”), know that Texas law forbids withholding wages as an offset unless the employee agreed in writing or it’s otherwise allowed by law. So that defense usually won’t fly. You should also be aware of any **collective bargaining agreements** or arbitration clauses that might affect your ability to sue in court.  

**Unpaid commissions or bonuses:** If you earned sales commissions or a bonus under a compensation plan and the company didn’t pay, you can sue for those as wages. **Example:** A salesperson sues for $5,000 in commissions for sales made before leaving the company, which the employer refuses to pay citing a forfeiture-on-quit policy. Texas generally enforces the written commission agreements – some have clauses that you must be employed on the payout date to get the commission or bonus. If such a clause exists and is clear, it might bar your claim. But if not, or if the commission was earned (all services performed, just awaiting payout), you have a strong case. Texas courts often view commissions as wages if the employee’s performance was completed and only payment remained. A vague policy will usually be construed in the employee’s favor if not clearly disclosed. Also note, the Texas Payday Law covers commissions for purposes of filing with TWC (as long as calculation is verifiable). In court, bring the commission plan documents, and evidence of the sales closed or targets met. If it appears the employer is stiffing you out of spite or after you left, judges tend to be sympathetic to the employee if the contract supports your position. Attorney’s fees can also be recovered under Chapter 38 CPRC for a successful suit on an oral or written employment agreement ([tcss.legis.texas.gov](https://tcss.legis.texas.gov/resources/CP/htm/CP.38.htm#:~:text=,be%20represented%20by%20an%20attorney)) (though Chapter 38 historically allowed fees only from individuals and corporations as defendants – recent amendments in 2021 expanded it to other entities via §38.0015). If your employer is an LLC, you may now recover fees as well ([tcss.legis.texas.gov](https://tcss.legis.texas.gov/resources/CP/htm/CP.38.htm#:~:text=,be%20represented%20by%20an%20attorney)) (older law prevented it, but that’s changed).  

**Unreimbursed business expenses:** Texas doesn’t have a broad law forcing reimbursement of employee expenses (unlike California). It depends on the employer’s policies or agreements. If your employment agreement (or company policy) said you’d be reimbursed for certain expenses (mileage, tools, client meals, etc.) and they failed to reimburse, you can sue for those amounts as breach of contract. **Example:** A consultant had to travel for work, spent $800 on hotels and gas expecting reimbursement per policy, but the employer never paid them back after they quit. You’d present the expense reports, receipts, and the written policy or emails approving those expenses. If there’s no written promise, it might be tougher – Texas is generally at-will, and if an employer simply said “maybe we’ll cover that” without formalizing, the court will examine if an implied contract exists. Often, though, companies have clear expense guidelines. Small claims can order the company to pay you what you laid out from your own pocket for their benefit. This wouldn’t fall under the Payday Law (TWC doesn’t handle expense reimbursements, only wages), so court is the right path. If the amount is modest, some employers may prefer to settle once you file rather than send a rep to court.  

**Misclassification disputes (if recoverable as money)**: If you believe you were misclassified (e.g., treated as an independent contractor when you were actually an employee, thus missing overtime or benefits), small claims can address the monetary part *in some cases*. For example, you might say “I should have been paid overtime for 10 hours a week for a year, but they misclassified me as exempt.” You can sue for the unpaid overtime wages. However, many misclassification issues involve complex legal standards (what defines an employee vs contractor, or whether a position is exempt from overtime under the Fair Labor Standards Act). Small claims judges may not be well-versed in these, and some claims might exceed the court’s scope if they hinge on federal law determinations. But nothing stops you from suing for straight-up unpaid overtime or minimum wage under state contract law or the federal Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) – state courts have concurrent jurisdiction for FLSA claims, and JP courts can hear them if within the amount limit. Note that under FLSA, a prevailing worker gets not only the unpaid wages but an equal amount in **liquidated damages** (double damages) in most cases, plus attorney fees. If you claim that in small claims, it might push the total above $20k if you’re not careful. Also, the FLSA has a 2-year statute (3 years if willful). For independent contractor misclassification resulting in lost overtime or benefits, a simpler route can be through TWC (for wages) or through IRS or state workforce audits (for employment taxes) depending on context. But if, say, you were labeled contractor and never paid overtime – you can sue for the overtime pay (time-and-a-half for hours over 40 per week). You’d need records of hours (your own logs if not the company’s). The company might defend saying you were not an employee or were exempt. It gets technical, but a small claims judge can still rule on it. Just be aware you may have to educate the court on why you were actually an employee under Texas/common law tests (control, schedule, etc.). The absence of withholding and benefits is one factor but not dispositive. If you succeed, you get the unpaid wages and potentially the court could award additional “liquidated” sum under FLSA. But since small claims is money-only, it won’t reclassify your status in any official way or grant injunction; it only gives $$$. For many workers, the Texas Workforce Commission’s wage claim (for up to 180 days of wages) or a U.S. Dept. of Labor complaint might be easier to pursue for misclassification issues.  

**Note on Texas Labor Commissioner vs. court:** Texas’s labor agency (TWC) offers an **administrative wage claim** process, which is free and doesn’t require an attorney. It’s often quicker for straightforward wage claims under $20k. However, its jurisdiction is limited to wages (not expenses or other damages) and there is a 180-day filing deadline. The **benefit** of going to small claims court is that you can recover a broader range of damages (like unpaid bonuses, expenses, possibly penalty damages under statutes) and you have 2+ years to file (depending on the limitation period – typically 2 years for oral wage agreements or under FLSA, 4 years for written). The downside is you’ll have to do the legwork of suing and possibly dealing with the company’s lawyer. Generally, if it’s a simple unpaid-wage case and you’re within 180 days, TWC is a good first step; if that time has passed or TWC couldn’t get your money, then small claims is the way.  

### Personal services / professional disputes  
**Disputes with hairdressers/barbers/nail salons (botched service):** If you paid for personal care services and the result was disastrously below reasonable expectations – for instance, a hair stylist severely damaged your hair or gave a completely wrong treatment that needs correction – you can sue for the cost of fixing it and possibly a refund. **Example:** A chemical hair treatment went awry due to the salon’s error, and you had to pay $500 to a specialist to correct the damage; you can sue the original salon for that $500 plus any amount you already paid them. Another example: A nail salon’s technician injured you (cut you badly), leading to medical bills – that crosses into personal injury possibly, but for minor injuries or infections you can claim those medical costs in small claims. Texas does not have a specific statute for “bad haircuts” or the like, so it’s a straightforward negligence or breach-of-contract claim: you expected a competent service, they failed to deliver it or caused harm. Bring photos (before/after), receipts for corrective work, and perhaps testimony from the stylist who fixed it (or a note) explaining what was done wrong. Note: If a professional holds a license (cosmetologist, barber) and was grossly negligent, you can also file a complaint with the Texas Department of Licensing and Regulation (TDLR), but that’s separate from getting your money. In small claims, you likely won’t get compensation for pain or embarrassment (that ventures into personal injury or mental anguish, which are hard to quantify for a bad haircut unless extremely severe). You’ll typically get economic damages – cost of redo, refund, and maybe any medical expenses if infection or injury occurred.  

**Tutoring, lessons, or coaching not delivered:** If you paid for classes or lessons (academic tutoring, music lessons, sports coaching sessions, etc.) and the instructor didn’t provide them or provided far fewer than promised, you can seek a refund for the undelivered portion. **Example:** You prepay a tutor $600 for 10 sessions, they only conduct 3 and then disappear – you could sue for the value of the 7 missed sessions ($420 if pro rata). Or if a driving school went out of business after you paid the fee, leaving you without training, you’d claim what you paid. Present any written agreement or advertisement showing how many sessions or what result was promised. If the claim is that the quality was awful (e.g., “my child didn’t improve at all after this tutoring program”), be aware the court might consider that subjective unless you can point to an objective failing (like the tutor missed appointments or was clearly unqualified contrary to promises). If it’s purely a matter of “I didn’t get the improvement I hoped for,” that’s hard to quantify unless a guarantee was given (“improve SAT score by 200 points or your money back” type of guarantee could be enforced). Many lesson providers require you to sign liability waivers or no-guarantee clauses, but failing to provide the service at all is still actionable. Another angle: if it was a **vocational school or continuing education program**, Texas Education Code or Occ Code might have specific protections or bond requirements; but small claims can still be used to get your tuition back if they didn’t perform.  

**Pet boarding/grooming/training disputes:** If you paid someone to care for or train your pet and something went wrong – for example, the boarding kennel neglected your dog leading to vet bills, or a dog trainer failed to deliver any results promised in a “guaranteed obedience course” – you can pursue damages. **Example:** A pet groomer accidentally injures your pet (cuts the skin badly), and you incur $300 vet bills. You can sue the groomer for negligence to recover the vet costs. (They might have you sign a waiver for minor nicks etc., but major negligence isn’t excused.) Or if a dog trainer charged $1,000 claiming they’d achieve certain behavioral outcomes and nothing improved at all *and* the training was obviously not as advertised (like they barely spent time training), you could claim breach of contract and ask for some or all of your money back. Again, living creatures are involved, so if there’s emotional distress (e.g., the kennel lost someone’s cat or a pet died in their care), it gets very emotional but legally Texas treats pets as property for damages – you typically get vet bills and perhaps the market value of the pet if deceased (market value of a pet is often nominal unless it’s a pedigree animal with breeding value). Texas law doesn’t allow noneconomic damages for pet loss in a negligence context (only for willful acts sometimes). So small claims will focus on your economic losses: vet bills, refund of fees, maybe burial costs if applicable. Present any contracts – many boarding facilities limit liability or require arbitration. If there’s an arbitration clause and the other party raises it, the court might dismiss in favor of arbitration. But often for small sums, they may not bother or the clause might not clearly cover negligence. Summon any evidence of neglect (photos of your pet’s condition, etc.). If you suspect outright abuse or cruelty, also report to animal control – but that won’t get you money, just potentially penal actions against them.  

**Medical or dental billing disputes (when not covered by insurance or not subject to special laws):** For small medical bills that you believe you were overcharged for or charged for services not provided, you can use small claims, though it ventures into tricky territory. Typically, if you received medical treatment, disputes are handled through insurance or hospital billing departments, and suing for a “refund” of medical charges is rare unless it’s a clear breach (like you were billed for a procedure you didn’t get). **Example:** You went in for a simple procedure advertised at $500 cash price, but the clinic charged you $1,500 and refuses to adjust – if you have it in writing or an ad that it would be $500, you could sue for the overcharge. Or if a dentist charged for a crown they never actually placed in your mouth, obviously you can reclaim that payment. However, if the issue is the **quality** of medical services (malpractice) – that is beyond small claims because Texas requires that to be handled as a health care liability claim with expert testimony and other strict rules (and likely damages above $20k). Small claims is not the place to litigate professional medical negligence. But straightforward billing errors or breaches of a clear payment agreement can be addressed. Another scenario: **surprise medical bills** – Texas has laws protecting consumers from surprise out-of-network bills (SB 1264, effective 2020, set up an arbitration/mediation system for those). So if you got a surprise out-of-network ER bill, you actually **cannot** sue the provider in small claims – those must go through the state’s dispute resolution process (or now the federal No Surprises Act process) rather than court. So be cautious: some medical billing issues are preempted by special law. If it’s a simpler matter (like a doctor double-charged you or failed to honor a discount plan), small claims is fine. Provide documentation of what was promised vs. billed. For insurance co-pay disputes or denied insurance coverage, suing the provider may not help – often it’s between you and your insurance or you and the insurance’s network terms. Suing an insurance company for coverage belongs in a higher court usually.  

**Daycare/preschool refund disputes:** If you prepaid a childcare center or preschool and then withdrew your child (or the center closed), you may be entitled to a refund of unused tuition. **Example:** You paid $900 for a month of daycare, but after one week you pulled your child out due to safety concerns; the daycare refuses to refund the remaining 3 weeks. Check your enrollment agreement – many have notice requirements or say fees are nonrefundable after a certain date. If those terms are enforceable, you might be out of luck for a voluntary withdrawal. But if the daycare breached their obligations (e.g., suddenly shut down, or you withdrew because they weren’t providing proper care in violation of their contract or state standards), you have a case for a pro rata refund. Similarly, if you put down a deposit or registration fee that was supposed to be refundable in certain cases and they didn’t refund it, you can sue for that. In Texas, daycare facilities are regulated, but refunds are mostly a contract issue. If the daycare was **unsafe or fraudulent**, you can also report it to Texas Health and Human Services (which licenses them), but again that’s separate from getting your money. For court, bring the contract and any handbook or emails regarding withdrawal and refunds. If you withdrew for cause (like documented issues), bring proof of those issues (incident reports, etc.) to paint the picture that keeping your money is unfair. The judge will likely enforce clear contract terms, but any ambiguity will be resolved in your favor in equity.  

### Loans, debts, and financial  
**Recovery of money lent to a friend or family member:** (See also “personal loans” in Contract disputes above.) Small claims court is ideal for informal loans: you gave someone money expecting repayment and they didn’t pay. Even without a written contract, you can assert it was a loan and not a gift. **Example:** You let your cousin borrow $2,000 to pay moving expenses, and despite promises, they haven’t paid a cent back. Your evidence might simply be a bank transfer or check to them and perhaps text messages where they acknowledge it’s a loan or thank you and say “I will pay you back.” In Texas, an oral loan agreement is enforceable. File your claim naming the person and stating the amount loaned, date, and any agreed time frame for repayment (if none, you can argue a reasonable time has passed). The person might defend saying “it was a gift” – ultimately the judge decides whose story is more credible. If you previously requested payment and they made excuses or partial payments, that supports it being a loan. The court can award the principal plus maybe interest if there was an agreed interest (or statutory prejudgment interest of 6% per year can be applied by the judge for a definite sum due on a date ([statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/DocViewer.aspx?DocKey=FI%2FFI.304&ExactPhrase=False&HighlightType=1&Phrases=interest%7Crate%7Con%7Cchild%7Csupport%7Cin%7Ctexas&QueryText=interest+rate+on+child+support+in+texas#:~:text=,prejudgment%20Image%3A%20Previous%20HitinterestImage%3A%20Next)), but often in personal loans interest isn’t claimed). There is a 4-year statute for suing on an oral or written loan ([www.statutechecker.com](https://www.statutechecker.com/limitations/texas/breach-of-contract-oral#:~:text=4%20Years%20Limit%3A%20Texas%20Breach,Texas%20Four%20years%20from%20date)), so don’t wait too long. Suing friends/family can be awkward, but sometimes a demand letter or the filed suit itself motivates payment or settlement before trial.  

**Disputes over jointly-paid expenses (roommates, ex-partners, group trips):** When multiple people agree to split costs and one doesn’t pay their share, small claims provides a venue to sort it out. **Examples:** Two roommates agreed to split utility bills and the lease rent; one moved out early and didn’t pay their part of the last two months’ rent – one roommate covered it all and now sues the other for reimbursement of their half. Or a group of friends went on vacation and one person fronted the rental car and hotel on their card with the verbal understanding everyone would pay their portion, but one friend never paid $300 owed. These are essentially **informal contract** or **unjust enrichment** cases. Texas courts will enforce the clear agreement if evidence shows there was one. Even if not explicitly agreed, the court can find an implied contract or that fairness dictates reimbursement (quantum meruit/unjust enrichment) if someone benefited from another’s payment. Provide any texts/emails or Venmo requests that show the expectation of splitting and the amount. If nothing in writing, detail the circumstances to the judge: often it’s common sense that, say, each roommate is liable for half the rent absent a contrary agreement. It helps if you can show you asked for the money and they didn’t pay. These cases are usually straightforward if the defendant shows up – they might argue some offset (e.g., “I did more chores” or “I left furniture behind as compensation”) – the judge will decide what’s fair if no explicit agreement on those extras. Legally, chores don’t typically offset rent unless agreed, and leaving personal property usually doesn’t count unless it was meant to cover costs. Keep the case focused on the dollars.  

**Disputes over wedding/engagement gifts and rings upon cancellation:** In Texas, if an engagement is broken off, who keeps the ring? Texas follows the **conditional gift** rule for engagement rings: the ring is given in contemplation of marriage, so if the marriage doesn’t occur, the ring should be returned to the giver **if the giver was not at fault in the breakup** ([www.chron.com](https://www.chron.com/life/article/texas-law-says-engagement-ring-is-a-conditional-1458522.php#:~:text=Texas%20law%20says%20engagement%20ring,the%20first%20pitch%20before%20the)). (Some states do “no-fault” return to giver no matter what; Texas case law tends to treat the ring as a conditional gift that should be returned if the engagement is unjustifiably broken by the recipient, or possibly no-fault a return to giver as well – sources conflict, but a 2011 Texas case noted it’s a conditional gift ([www.chron.com](https://www.chron.com/life/article/texas-law-says-engagement-ring-is-a-conditional-1458522.php#:~:text=Texas%20law%20says%20engagement%20ring,the%20first%20pitch%20before%20the)).) So, if you gave an engagement ring and your fiancée ended the engagement, you can sue for the ring or its value. Conversely, if you were the recipient and the giver called it off, you may argue you get to keep it because they broke the promise – but litigation over that can be messy personally. Often people just return it or split the cost. For other wedding gifts, generally gifts to the couple are not expected to be returned legally (though morally some return if wedding canceled). But if a specific gift was given on a condition (like “here’s a $5,000 check for your wedding, but if you don’t actually have one, please return it”), that could be enforceable as a conditional gift. Small claims could handle these since it’s property/cash under $20k. **Example:** Ex-fiancé sues ex-fiancée for return of a $4,000 ring. If he can show it was an engagement ring and marriage didn’t occur, the court will likely order it returned or its value (if she already sold it – though selling someone else’s ring before legal entitlement might be conversion). Document the engagement (photos, proposal date, etc.) to establish context. This area is a mix of contract and family law principles, but since they weren’t married, it’s not a divorce property issue – it’s a civil claim over a gift. Texas courts have recognized engagement rings as conditional on marriage ([www.chron.com](https://www.chron.com/life/article/texas-law-says-engagement-ring-is-a-conditional-1458522.php#:~:text=Texas%20law%20says%20engagement%20ring,the%20first%20pitch%20before%20the)). For gifts between non-engaged romantic partners, if one wants it back after a breakup, usually it’s considered a true gift (no condition) so they cannot force return, unless it was a loan or explicitly stated condition.  

**Disputes over jointly-titled property after a breakup (unmarried couples):** If unmarried partners acquired property together (car, furniture, etc.) and then split up, small claims can be used to seek reimbursement or division if the amount in question is ≤ $20k. There is no divorce court since they weren’t married, so it falls to regular civil court. **Example:** You and your ex-boyfriend jointly bought a car, but the title and loan are in his name and you contributed $5,000 of the down payment – after breaking up, he keeps the car and won’t compensate you. You can sue for the $5,000 contribution under an **equitable claim** (like resulting trust or unjust enrichment) or argue there was an implicit partnership/joint venture in owning the car. Texas does recognize property claims by unmarried cohabitants if one can prove an agreement or contribution with expectation of ownership. Without a written agreement, it can be tricky, but evidence of payment and intent to share ownership helps. If the item is small (say a TV you split the cost of), it’s easier: you either get the item or value split. The JP can’t order *real estate* partition (that’s out of jurisdiction), but personal property or money contributions are fine. They may simply award money equal to your contribution or the value of your share. Provide receipts or bank statements showing you paid part of it, and any texts or emails indicating an understanding it was “ours.” If it’s an expensive item, one might argue it was a gift – the court will decide whose story is credible. Note that small claims is not well-suited for **complex property disputes or where title needs to be formally transferred** (they can’t change a car title for you, but awarding money is doable). If two people bought a puppy together and now fight over who keeps it – that’s technically property under law. JP courts have decided pet custody in small claims by effectively awarding the pet to one and ordering them to pay the other some amount (the pet’s value or contribution). They try to be fair but there’s not a clear legal formula.  

**Recovery of cash deposits / earnest money:** If you put down a **cash deposit** for something – e.g., a deposit to hold an apartment or a down payment/earnest money for a sale – and the deal fell through, you may claim that money back in small claims depending on the contract. **Examples:** A prospective tenant gave a $500 “security deposit” to hold an apartment but then was unable to move in; the landlord kept it. Under Texas law, if it was a **security deposit** and you didn’t move in, whether it’s refundable depends on if you were approved and then backed out. Many applications use the term “application deposit” which may become nonrefundable if you were approved and you backed out (Property Code 92.351-92.354 covers application fees and deposits – an application deposit must be refunded if the applicant is rejected, but can be kept if they were approved and then failed to sign the lease). If the landlord violated those rules, you can get your money plus $100 and attorney’s fees ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/property-code/prop-sect-92-109/#:~:text=,suit%20to%20recover%20the%20deposit)). If you simply changed your mind after approval, the landlord can likely keep it. Similarly, **real estate earnest money** in a home purchase is governed by the contract (TREC contract forms say if buyer backs out outside of allowed contingencies, seller keeps earnest money; if seller backs out or contingencies not met, earnest is returned to buyer). To get earnest money back, you’d usually file in county or use the title company’s interpleader process, but small claims could hear it if it’s not too tangled, since it’s basically money held under a contract. Provide the written agreement that shows the conditions of refund. If the other side clearly breached the deal, you should get it back. If you breached, likely not (except possibly if the amount exceeds actual damages, you could argue for excess). For other kinds of deposits (a deposit to hold a wedding venue, etc.), it depends on the contract cancellation terms. Many are “nonrefundable” after a certain date. If that date passed, the court often upholds it unless unconscionable. But if the vendor later filled the spot and didn’t actually lose money, you might argue unjust enrichment (some courts might pro-rate a refund if the vendor mitigated their loss). It’s on a case-by-case equitable basis.  

**Creditors suing consumers in Texas small claims:** Unlike some states that bar collection agencies from using small claims, Texas **allows creditors, debt buyers, and collection agencies** to sue in justice courts (they even created the separate “Debt Claim” case type for it ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28b%29%20Debt%20Claim%20Case,Part%20V%2C%20Rule%20508%20applies))). So if you are a creditor (a bank, a financing company, or even an individual who bought a debt) you can file here, or if you’re on the receiving end, know that such suits are valid. The plaintiff just must follow Rule 508 requiring certain information in the petition (like the account number, date of last payment, etc.). As a debt collector, you **are** allowed to be represented by an agent or attorney – note, businesses usually hire attorneys for debt claims. There is no requirement to be an attorney to file, but a collection agency often will use one. If you are a consumer being sued in small claims for a debt, you can use all the defenses you’d have in any court (e.g., statute of limitations – in Texas most consumer debts have a 4-year limit ([statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/GetStatute.aspx?Code=CP&Value=16#:~:text=Sec.%2016.004.%20%20FOUR,the%20cause%20of%20action%20accrues)), so check the last payment date; also, if it’s a debt buyer, they need to prove they own the debt). The question prompt asked whether a bank or collection agency is barred or not – in Texas, **they are not barred** from small claims. In fact, it’s common for medical bills, credit card debts, etc. to be sued on in JP court. If you’re a debt buyer plaintiff, ensure you have the proper assignment documents because Texas rules require attaching a **sworn statement of the account** and info about the original creditor for debt claim cases. If you lack documentation, the defendant can challenge and possibly win. From the plaintiff side, one advantage in justice court is the process is a bit simpler and attorneys’ fees might be limited (some JP judges may not award large attorney fees on a small debt). But given no bar, it’s an available forum. (One subtlety: a few states require a collection agency to be licensed/bonded to sue – Texas requires third-party debt collectors to have a bond filed with the Secretary of State. If not, and they sue, the defendant could raise that as a bar. It’s not commonly invoked, but check Finance Code §392.101).  

### Vehicle-specific  
**Accident damage where insurance won’t cover or denies claim:** We touched on car accidents under property damage. To reiterate in a vehicle context: suppose you had a minor collision and the other driver’s insurance denied liability or is offering too little. You can sue the at-fault driver (not the insurance) in small claims for the fair cost of repair, rental car, and related expenses (within $20k). Small claims has no power to force an insurance payout, but winning a judgment against the driver often results in their insurer paying up to cover it (they have a duty to indemnify the insured if it was a covered incident, up to policy limits). If the damage amount is modest, usually insurance companies handle it, but if they’re stonewalling, small claims can break the stalemate. Since Texas is a comparative negligence state, if there’s a dispute over who is at fault or percentage of fault, the judge will decide. Be prepared to show you were not negligent (or less negligent). Use photos of the scene, diagrams, etc. See the earlier discussion under property damage for more on evidence. If you already fixed the car, show the paid invoice. If not, get a couple of written estimates to present. Remember, you also have the option of mediation (some JPs refer accident cases to a mediator first). If a driver hit you and you have collision coverage, often your own insurer pays and then subrogates – if they didn’t or you didn’t have it, then you pursuing the other driver in JP is fine. Also note, if you suffered **bodily injury**, that portion (pain, medical bills) can technically be claimed in JP up to $20k, but personal injury factors apply (see personal injury section below).  

**Mechanic/body shop disputes (charging for unauthorized work or poor repairs):** Similar to earlier “auto repair disputes.” In vehicle-specific terms: If a mechanic performed unauthorized repairs or did the job incorrectly causing damage, you can sue. Texas’s **Motor Vehicle Repair Act** (Bus. & Commerce Code 90) is not as extensive as some states, but generally, if a repair shop charges for work not authorized or not performed, that’s a DTPA violation (false representation of work done) as well as breach of contract. **Example:** A body shop quote was $5,000 but they did extra work without approval raising it to $8,000, and refuse to release your car until paid. You could pay under protest to get your car (possibly under a mechanic’s lien they can hold it) then sue for the $3,000 unauthorized difference. Or a mechanic promised to rebuild your engine but instead installed a junkyard engine without telling you – definitely actionable for the cost difference and any harm caused. Texas Occupations Code Chapter 2305 requires shops to give estimates and get consent for extra work – if they don’t, small claims court can deem the extra charges void. Also, Texas’s DTPA specifically lists “performing unauthorized work” and “misrepresenting that work has been done” as deceptive practices ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/business-commerce-code/title-2/chapter-17/subchapter-e/section-17-50/#:~:text=,mental%20anguish%20and%20economic%20damages)). That means you could get twice or triple damages if it was done knowingly. For example, if they charged for a new part and you discover they left the old part in, that’s knowing misrepresentation – you might get 3× the cost of that part as damages to punish the deceit, plus your attorney fees if any. Document everything: the initial estimate, any written work orders, photos of old parts still on the car after they claimed new ones, perhaps a statement from an independent mechanic who inspected what was actually done. If the dispute is simply quality (the repair failed soon after), see if a warranty was given. Many shops have short warranties. You can sue for a refund or cost to redo the repair elsewhere.  

**Dealership disputes (fraud at sale, failure to honor warranties):** Some of these were discussed in consumer disputes. Recap in vehicle context: If a car dealer misrepresented a vehicle (rolled back odometer, lied about accident history, etc.), you can sue under DTPA for the difference in value or cancellation of the deal. If the car came with a **used car limited warranty** and they won’t honor repairs, you can sue for the cost of covered repairs they should have done. If it’s a **new car lemon issue**, you must go through TxDMV’s Lemon Law process rather than court initially. But for used cars, and **“as-is” sales**: if truly sold as-is, your recovery is limited unless you prove **fraud** (they knew of a serious defect and hid it or made a specific false statement). Evidence could include internal repair records you later find, or the fact they cleared engine codes right before sale, etc. Those cases are tough but possible. If a dealer included a service contract or third-party warranty and that entity is not paying, you could include them as a defendant maybe (if within small claims jurisdiction – likely yes if they do business in Texas). Or sue the dealer for selling a worthless warranty under DTPA. Texas also has a **“3-Day Rule”**? Actually, many think there’s a general 3-day cancellation on car sales – there isn’t for vehicles. Only certain door-to-door sales have that cooling-off right. So if you just have buyer’s remorse on a used car, you generally can’t unwind it via small claims unless there was some legal wrong. But if you find something like the title was salvage and they never disclosed (that’s required by law to disclose), that’s a strong claim – you could demand rescission (cancel sale) and get your money back by suing. The value of the car might determine if it’s a JP case or needs higher court.  

**Towing/impound disputes:** If your vehicle was towed without justification (from an apartment, parking lot, etc.), Texas law allows you to request a **Tow Hearing** in justice court *within 14 days* of the tow (Texas Occupations Code §2308.452-458). This is a special expedited process: you file a request (often a form from the JP) and the court sets a hearing quickly ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/occupations-code/occ-sect-2308-456/#:~:text=Texas%20Occupations%20Code%20,a%20hearing%20under%20this%20chapter)) (within 21 days of filing ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/occupations-code/occ-sect-2308-458/#:~:text=Texas%20Occupations%20Code%20,the%20date%20the%20court%20receives))). At the hearing, if the tow was wrongful (no proper signage, or not under a lawful reason), the court can award you: a refund of the tow and storage fees you paid, **plus** $**up to $1,000** in statutory damages, plus court costs and attorney’s fees (Occ. Code §2308.404). This is decided by the JP. So, rather than a normal small claim, you do the special tow hearing route. If you missed the 14-day window, you might sue the towing company or parking facility in small claims under general legal theories (like conversion or violation of the Towing Act), but you lose the streamlined benefit and possibly the automatic $1,000. So act fast. **Example:** Your car was towed from your apartment despite a valid permit – possibly illegal. File the request in the precinct where the tow occurred. The JP could order the tow company to reimburse fees and pay the penalty. The statute is particular – ensure to raise that signs weren’t compliant or you had authorization to park. If the tow was from a public roadway ordered by police, that falls under different rules (and you usually have to contest it through law enforcement or pay storage then maybe sue if improper). Similarly, **impound fees**: If your car was impounded by the city (e.g., for tickets or being stolen/recovered), you generally have to pay to get it out and disputes over those fees usually go through municipal processes, not small claims. But a private impound lot that wrongly refused to release your car or overcharged storage beyond legal rates could be sued for any overage. (Texas also sets maximum tow and storage rates by local ordinance or state Commission – charging above that is actionable).  

**Parking lot damage (hit-and-run when you identify responsible party):** Suppose someone dented your car in a parking lot and drove off but you got their plate or there’s camera footage – you can definitely sue that person for the damage. It’s essentially property damage like any accident. It might be harder if no police report or they deny it, but if you have proof (maybe a witness note or security video from the store), you can present that. JPs handle plenty of small fender-bender cases. Another scenario: A business’s parking lot was negligently maintained, causing damage to your car (like a poorly secured construction barrier fell on it). You could sue the business for negligence. Or if a valet service damaged your car (then it’s bailment as earlier – they should pay for repairs). Identify the correct party (the driver who hit you, or the company if it was an employee action within scope). The process is the same: prove they caused the damage and the cost.  

### Personal injury (small/minor)  
Small claims can hear **personal injury** lawsuits – there is no categorical ban in Texas. The only limitation is the $20,000 cap. Most significant injury cases (with high medical bills or serious harm) are well above that and go to county or district court. But for **minor injuries**, say a few thousand dollars of medical expenses and no lasting impairment, small claims is a viable forum, especially if neither side wants to involve attorneys’ fees. **Examples:** A claimant seeks $5,000 for a minor car accident soft-tissue injury (chiropractor bills and ER checkup), or someone sues a store for $3,000 in urgent care bills after slipping and bruising themselves. You must prove the other party’s liability (negligence, etc.) just as in a regular court, and you must substantiate your damages with evidence (medical records, bills, perhaps a doctor’s note linking injury to the incident). One challenge: Texas Rules of Evidence and Civil Procedure technically apply, but JPs often relax them. However, hearsay issues (like just handing the judge medical bills without proper foundation) could arise if the defendant objects. In larger courts, you’d need a medical expert (or at least affidavits under CPRC §18.001 to prove up medical expenses). In JP court, judges sometimes accept the medical bills and your testimony about the injury if it’s straightforward. But be aware the defense could insist on stricter proof. Also, Texas adheres to a **modified comparative fault** rule – if the plaintiff is more than 50% at fault, they get nothing; if 50% or less at fault, recovery is reduced by their percentage. That applies in JP as well. So if, in a dog bite case, they argue you provoked the dog, the judge might assign some fault to you which reduces your award. Punitive damages for personal injury (for gross negligence etc.) can’t exceed the cap and would be unusual to award in JP. Also note: If a person died or had very severe injuries, those wrongful death or major injury claims cannot realistically be tried in JP due to damage amounts and complexity (and justice courts do not have jurisdiction to adjudicate wrongful death or defamation by statute ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=a%20suit%20in%20behalf%20of,a%20penalty%2C%20forfeiture%2C%20or%20escheat)) ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=))). But a garden-variety injury with limited damages is fine. If you are considering suing for injury in small claims, keep the ask within $20k and ideally have mostly economic damages (bills, lost wages) that are easy to quantify. Pain & suffering can be claimed too, but intangible amounts might make the judge uncomfortable – they might award some nominal amount for pain if warranted. Texas law doesn’t forbid general damages in JP, but you must still prove them to the court’s satisfaction. In practice, many JP injury cases are for medical bills only, or medical bills + a modest sum for inconvenience.  

### Specific statutory claims (state-specific)  
Texas has various statutes that provide monetary remedies which can be pursued in small claims if the dollar amount isn’t too high. We’ve already woven in many such statutes above (bad check penalties, DTPA treble damages, landlord/tenant penalties, towing, etc.). Here is a summary of key ones and their interaction with small claims:  

- **Bad check civil penalty:** Texas does not provide an automatic double or treble damage on bounced checks like some states, but it does allow a **$30 service fee** per check ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/business-and-commerce-code/title-1/chapter-3/subchapter-e/section-3-506/#:~:text=,maximum%20processing%20fee%20of%20%2430)), and if there’s evidence of fraud, the penal code allows for criminal charges. In a civil small claim, you’re looking at check amount + $30 + perhaps court costs. (If you follow the formal procedure under Texas Bus. & Comm. Code §3.506 and send the statutory notice, that covers the fee. There’s also a provision that a **stolen check** or knowingly bad check could allow additional damages in some circumstances, but generally stick to the $30 + face value).  

- **Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA):** We’ve discussed it extensively. To recap key points: It’s a statutory cause of action for consumers that can yield *actual damages* (economic + mental anguish if applicable) and if the defendant acted “knowingly” the court can award up to **3× economic + 3× mental anguish** damages ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/business-commerce-code/title-2/chapter-17/subchapter-e/section-17-50/#:~:text=,mental%20anguish%20and%20economic%20damages)). Attorney’s fees to a prevailing consumer are mandatory. In small claims, you can bring a DTPA claim as long as the amount of *actual damages claimed* is ≤ $20,000 – any potential trebled portion is considered a penalty and doesn’t count toward jurisdiction (interestingly, the law says “exclusive of statutory interest and court costs” in the $20k cap ([legalatoms.com](https://legalatoms.com/texas/texas-small-claims-court-limit-amount/#:~:text=sums%20of%20money,or%20District%20Court%20civil%20actions)); it doesn’t mention excluding statutory multiple damages. Generally, one should keep even trebled amounts under the cap to be safe). Example: If actual damages are $7,000 and you allege knowing conduct, possible treble would be $21,000 – that’s over the cap. So you might just claim $7,000 actual and let the judge decide about multiples (the judge could still award up to $21k total, but they might be constrained by the cap – unclear, likely they won’t exceed $20k total). It’s safer to not overshoot. **Notice letter 60 days prior is required ([guides.sll.texas.gov](https://guides.sll.texas.gov/consumer-protection/relief#:~:text=If%20you%20have%20a%20DTPA,to%20avoid%20going%20to%20court))**. Also, DTPA claims cannot be assigned – only the consumer who was directly wronged can sue (so you, not say someone who bought your claim). Many specific Texas consumer statutes tie into the DTPA – e.g., Texas Debt Collection Act (Finance Code Ch. 392) says a violation “is a deceptive trade practice” ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/finance-code/fin-sect-392-404/#:~:text=,is%20actionable%20under%20that%20subchapter)), allowing you to sue under DTPA for debt harassment and get damages + fees. Also, certain **tie-in statutes** allow additional damage types to be recovered via DTPA. But in small claims, the main advantage is the possibility of attorney fees and multi damages for willful conduct to deter bad actors (like shady contractors or car sellers).  

- **Lemon Law:** Texas’s Lemon Law for new vehicles (Occupations Code Chapter 2301, Subchapter M) requires an administrative process through the Texas Department of Motor Vehicles. It typically results in repurchase, replacement, or repair orders. You cannot directly sue in small claims under the Lemon Law for a repurchase; you must go through TxDMV. However, if you qualify and the manufacturer doesn’t comply, enforcement would go to district court or an appellate court, not JP. For a used car lemon, as mentioned, you rely on DTPA or warranty law.  

- **Identity theft and credit report issues:** Texas Business & Commerce Code §521.103 allows victims of identity theft to sue the identity thief for actual damages and a $1,000 civil penalty. Suing the thief in small claims is possible if you can find them and they have assets, but often they don’t. More commonly, someone might want to sue a *creditor or credit bureau* who, after being informed of identity theft, still tried to collect or failed to correct credit info. Under Texas Finance Code Chapter 392 (debt collection), you can sue a debt collector who violates that law (e.g., by attempting to collect a debt not owed due to identity theft) for actual damages plus injunctive relief and attorney fees ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/finance-code/fin-sect-392-403/#:~:text=,for)). And any violation of that chapter is also a DTPA violation ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/finance-code/fin-sect-392-404/#:~:text=,is%20actionable%20under%20that%20subchapter)), so you could seek up to 3× damages and fees. Texas also has a specific statute (Bus. & Comm. Code §521.151) giving identity theft victims the right to sue for actual damages and, in some cases, attorneys fees. These types of claims might be a bit complex for JP, but if the damages are small (say you had to spend $500 sorting out credit issues, or you want the statutory $1,000), you could try in small claims. It may just be easier to leverage the threat of such claims to get the company to fix the error. If you sue under Finance Code 392, note that you must give the debt collector written notice of the claim at least 30 days before filing suit, to allow them to correct or resolve (Finance Code §392.403, it’s not super explicit but courts impose a notice requirement to recover **attorney fees**).  

- **Wage/hour statutory damages:** While Texas doesn’t have its own minimum wage beyond the federal, if you were owed minimum wage or overtime under the FLSA, you can bring that in state court as discussed. FLSA provides for **double damages** (100% liquidated damages) unless the employer proves good faith. Also mandatory attorney fees for a win. You can ask for those in JP, just keep total under $20k. Texas Payday Law doesn’t give penalty wages except for continuing violations (like failure to pay commission after TWC order can lead to penalties). If the wage issue is late final paycheck, Texas’s remedy is the TWC’s administrative penalty on the employer potentially, but not a direct award to employee beyond wages and interest.  

- **Texas Debt Collection Act (TDCA):** As noted, Tex. Finance Code Chapter 392 (the TDCA) lets a consumer sue a debt collector (including original creditors in some contexts) for improper practices (harassment, threats, false statements, etc.). Remedy: injunctive relief and actual damages, plus attorneys fees; if intentional or reckless, you also get at least $100 for each violation (Finance Code §392.403). And as mentioned, a violation is also actionable under DTPA ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/finance-code/fin-sect-392-404/#:~:text=,is%20actionable%20under%20that%20subchapter)), which can get you 3× damages. These claims are absolutely suitable for small claims if your actual damages are small (often it’s anger/stress – which might translate to maybe some nominal amount unless you have proof of concrete loss like missed job due to credit report or severe emotional distress requiring treatment). Many debt collection cases are taken by attorneys under fee-shift, but you could self-represent in JP if, say, a collector blatantly violated the law and you mainly want the $100 per violation and them to stop.  

- **Landlord/tenant penalties:** Texas Property Code includes some tenant remedies with specified damages: e.g., if a landlord **wrongfully withholds a residential security deposit in bad faith**, it’s 3× deposit + $100 ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/property-code/prop-sect-92-109/#:~:text=,suit%20to%20recover%20the%20deposit)) (as discussed); if a landlord **cuts off utilities or locks out a tenant illegally**, the tenant can recover **actual damages + one month’s rent + $1,000** (Prop. Code §92.008 and §92.009). Those amounts (one month rent + $1,000) often fit in JP. Similarly, if a landlord **retaliates** unlawfully (e.g., evicts you for asking for repairs), you may get **1 month’s rent + $500 + damages** (Prop. Code §92.333). These are all enforceable in JP court. Just be sure to cite the statute in your petition so the JP knows the specific remedy. Also, for **repair-and-deduct disputes** and the special Repair Remedy cases, note that after September 1, 2021, the legislature intended to raise the cap for repair remedy cases to $20k as well, aligning with other cases ([docket.wp.txstate.edu](https://docket.wp.txstate.edu/2020/09/10/jurisdictional-limit-increase-now-in-effect/#:~:text=change%20Rule%20509,20%2C000)). So tenants seeking up to $20k in required repairs or rent abatements can use JP court. That’s more injunctive in nature (order repairs), which JP can do under Rule 509 up to that limit ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/property-code/title-8/chapter-92/subchapter-b/section-92-0563/#:~:text=,one%20month%27s%20rent%20plus%20%24500)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/property-code/title-8/chapter-92/subchapter-b/section-92-0563/#:~:text=,10th%20day%20after%20that%20date)).  

- **HOA disputes:** If you have a dispute with your homeowners’ association that’s about money (like they wrongfully towed your car or fined you $500 without cause), you can use small claims for monetary relief. Texas Property Code Chapter 209 gives some rights (like notice before fines, etc.), and if HOA violates them, you might get fines reversed or such. But often HOA fights (architectural control, liens for dues) involve injunctions or declaratory relief which JP can’t issue. However, if an HOA imposed some charge improperly and you paid it under protest, you could sue for that refund. Or if the HOA damaged your property while doing maintenance, that’s a property damage claim.  

- **Texas “TPCA” (Telephone Privacy):** Texas has a law akin to the TCPA that gives damages for telemarketing violations, but realistically those cases usually go to higher court or handled via federal TCPA (and small claims not ideal for multiple call statutory damages that could exceed cap quickly).  

In summary, many Texas statutes with penalty or multiple damages can be leveraged in small claims, but keep the amounts small. Where a statutory damage alone is $10,000 or more (some federal laws have big penalties), that might push it out of JP. But the majority we see (2×, 3×, $500, $1,000 type penalties) are fine as long as total stays ≤ $20k. Always cite the statute and subsection in your claim so the judge is aware of your entitlement.  

### Other state-specific claim types  
*(Miscellaneous disputes that Texans might bring in small claims, even if niche):*  

- **Property code disputes in manufactured home communities:** For instance, if a mobile home park owner violates Chapter 94 (Manufactured Home Lot Tenancy Act) and you have a claim for say improperly withheld utilities or refunds, those would be similar to normal landlord cases that can go to JP.  

- **Towing company violations beyond improper tows:** E.g., towing company damaged your car during tow, or charged above the allowed fee. You can sue for property damage or the overcharge. Texas caps private property tow fees; if they overcharged, you might recover the difference. Or if they didn’t accept credit card as required by law and that caused you loss, possibly a claim.  

- **Auto title bond claims:** If you had to get a bonded title because someone failed to provide proper title on a car sale, you might sue the seller for the costs. (The bond is state-run though, usually one would claim on the bond. But small claims would be if the previous owner’s failure caused you to incur expenses you want back).  

- **Livestock or farm issues:** Texas is rural in parts; if a neighbor’s cattle got onto your land and ate/damaged $5,000 of your crops, you can sue for that damage (there are even stock laws but bottom line you can recover property damage by animals). Or disputes about stray cattle ownership (estray law) – generally handled by sheriff sale, but an original owner might sue for the animal’s value if wrongly sold. Bit uncommon in JP but possible.  

- **Business-to-business small claims:** Small claims isn’t only for consumers. E.g., one small business can sue another for up to $20k for breach of a small contract. Texas JP courts see, for example, a subcontractor suing a general contractor for $15,000 unpaid, or a supplier suing a restaurant for $5k of unpaid invoices. All the same contract and debt rules apply. The fact that both sides are businesses doesn’t limit jurisdiction. Just note, if the plaintiff is an LLC/corp, they can send an employee or owner to court (no lawyer required) ([www.stcl.edu](https://www.stcl.edu/lib/TexasRulesProject/TRCP474-522/rule500-42013.html#:~:text=,be%20represented%20by%20an%20attorney)).  

- **Professional malpractice limited to monetary loss under $20k:** If, say, a tax preparer completely botched your taxes causing you a $1,500 penalty from the IRS, you might sue them for that money in JP (since it’s basically negligence causing financial loss). Strictly speaking malpractice suits can be complex (and attorneys or doctors require expert testimony and have special procedural rules like an early expert report for med mal, which you cannot practically satisfy in JP – so do not bring med mal or legal mal cases here). But lower-level services like notary screw-ups, tax prep errors, photography that was ruined (and you want a refund plus some consequential cost), those can be framed as simple breach of service contract suits for money.  

- **Airbnb or short-term rental disputes:** If you rented a vacation property and it was misrepresented, you might claim some refund under DTPA for deceptive advertising. Or if you rented your home out and the renter caused damages beyond the deposit, you can sue the renter (if you know who they are) for property damage. Often these are handled through the platform’s insurance or arbitration, but small claims is an option if that fails.  

- **Contractor’s surety bond claims:** Some licensed contractors (like electricians, plumbers) must post a bond for consumer protection. If they did poor work, you theoretically can claim on the bond (through the bonding company). If that doesn’t work or is too slow, you can just sue the contractor in JP for the cost to fix.  

- **Solar panel contracts / HVAC / appliance purchase disputes:** Texas had many solar panel sales in recent years with tricky financing. Some consumers might try to use small claims to get out of deals or claim deceptive practices (likely beyond small claims if it involves canceling a lien or contract – that’s injunctive). But they could sue for promised rebates that weren’t given, etc.  

- **Scams and fraud under $20k:** If you were scammed by a person (e.g., you paid $3,000 to someone for what turned out to be a fake investment or a stolen vehicle), you can sue for your money. Winning is one thing; collecting is another, but at least you get a judgment. Sometimes pointing out to a scammer that you’ll go to court (and possibly law enforcement) yields a refund.  

- **Ticket broker / event ticket issues:** If a ticket reseller sold you invalid tickets and won’t refund, that’s a DTPA case potentially (sale of services that were not delivered). If amount paid is small, JP is perfect.  

- **Rideshare or gig economy pay disputes:** If you’re a rideshare driver or gig worker considered an independent contractor and the platform didn’t pay you correctly (maybe a referral bonus not honored), you could sue for that payment as a contract issue (some are doing arbitration due to terms of service, check if you waived court rights). If no arbitration clause, small claims works for that $500 bonus you never got.  

- **Cell phone bill or early termination fees:** Many times these are covered by contract and we’re stuck, but if a cell provider overcharged roaming or a termination fee that wasn’t actually applicable, you can dispute in JP if normal customer service fails. Be mindful: the contracts usually have arbitration clauses (major carriers do). If not, or if you opt out, you can sue for the overcharge as breach or DTPA (advertised “no contract” plan but charged an ETF = deceptive).  

- **Bank fees or consumer financial issues:** If a bank wrongly took money (say, didn’t honor a stop payment causing cascade of overdrafts, costing you $500), you can attempt small claims. Banks often have broad arbitration and are federally regulated, so these are uphill, but small claims suits sometimes prompt a settlement from a bank to avoid hassle. Legally, many banking disputes may be preempted or need to go through complaint processes. But a simple scenario like “I deposited $1,000 and the ATM only credited $100, bank refuses to investigate” – you could sue for the $900. The burden may push them to find it.  

In short, almost any scenario where one party owes another money and it’s not an exclusive federal or big state jurisdiction matter can land in small claims if under the amount. If you’re unsure and it’s under $20k, the justice court clerk can often tell you if a case is outside their scope. For example, they’ll refuse cases like divorce, title to real property, bankruptcy, foreclosure of real property liens, eviction for possession, etc. They also cannot order someone to do something (no injunction), only to pay money or turn over specific personal property.  

### Counterclaims  
If you, as a defendant, are sued in small claims, you **can** file a counterclaim against the plaintiff in the same case – effectively suing them back. Texas justice court rules explicitly allow counterclaims and even say it need not be related to the plaintiff’s claim ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20Counterclaim,4)). The **counterclaim** still must fall within the justice court’s jurisdiction: i.e., it must be for **$20,000 or less** (excluding interest and court costs) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20Counterclaim,4)). If your counterclaim would exceed $20k, you have a few options: you could waive the amount above $20k and file it in JP, or you could let the case proceed and separately sue in a higher court, or you could move to transfer the whole case to a court of broader jurisdiction (Texas rules allow a defendant to request transfer if a compulsory counterclaim exceeds JP limits – but since counterclaims in JP aren’t compulsory and can be unrelated ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20Counterclaim,4)), usually you just waive the excess and stay in JP or the counterclaim won’t be heard). If you do file a counterclaim over $20k without waiving, the judge will likely require you to reduce it or will dismiss the counterclaim for lack of jurisdiction. Some courts might transfer the whole case to county court at law (which can handle up to $250k+), but there’s no automatic right to transfer in Texas for that – typically, counterclaims must conform to JP limits ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20Counterclaim,4)). So practically, keep counter under $20k or waive extra.  

**Filing a counterclaim:** You must file a **Counterclaim Petition** in writing (basically similar to the original petition) by the deadline given. In justice court, your answer is due by day 14 after service ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=attorney%20must%20file%20an%20answer,a%20Saturday%2C%20Sunday%2C%20or%20legal)). A counterclaim can often be filed at the same time as the answer (or very shortly after – rules don’t give a hard cutoff, but preferably by the answer date). You’ll pay a filing fee for the counterclaim (the same as a new case fee, currently $54 in many counties ([www.jp1hillcounty.com](https://www.jp1hillcounty.com/fine-schedule#:~:text=%2454,the%20case%20with%20the%20Court))) unless you file a Statement of Inability (fee waiver) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=claim%20against%20a%20plaintiff%20that,4)). The court **does not issue a new citation** for a counterclaim – your counter petition just needs to be served on the plaintiff or their attorney by you (typically by mailing it to them) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=must%20file%20a%20counterclaim%20petition,4)). The rules say the defendant must serve a copy of the counterclaim on the plaintiff by any method allowed (mail, personal delivery) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=must%20file%20a%20counterclaim%20petition,4)). The plaintiff doesn’t have to file a formal answer to the counterclaim in JP, it’s assumed they’ll address it at trial.  

**Counterclaim examples:** If you’re sued by a contractor for $5,000 and you believe the contractor actually owes you money for damages they caused, you can counterclaim for those damages (say $2,000). Or if a credit card company sues you for $3,000 and you think they harassed you illegally while collecting, you might counterclaim under TDCA/DTPA for statutory damages. Counterclaims commonly seen: in landlord-tenant, landlord sues for rent, tenant counterclaims for property damage from landlord’s negligence or for the deposit. In car accident cases, often both parties claim the other was at fault – whoever is defendant can counter-sue for their damages (essentially making it a mini two-way trial). Note that **if your counterclaim makes the total case value exceed $20k**, some judges will effectively limit recovery to $20k each way. But technically each party’s claim can go up to $20k. If plaintiff at $20k and defendant $20k, the court could theoretically render judgment for one and against other offsetting, or conceivably a netting outcome. If a counterclaim (plus the main claim) so complicates matters that it feels like beyond JP scope (like complex issues or needing extensive discovery), occasionally a party might ask the JP to **“certify” the case up** to county court. The rules allow a judge to transfer a case if it’s beyond their jurisdiction or not suited (e.g., constitutional issue). It’s rare.  

**What if counterclaim exceeds cap?** As mentioned, you generally must **waive the excess** to use JP. If you absolutely want to recover more, you should not counterclaim there – instead, you could push the plaintiff’s case to a higher court by filing a motion if appropriate (though no formal mechanism exists like some states where a counter over the limit automatically divests jurisdiction). In Texas, if your claim is compulsory (arises from same transaction) and over the limit, theoretically the JP case might be dismissed so it can be refiled in a proper court together. But since Texas makes counterclaims permissive in JP (they “need not be related” ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20Counterclaim,4))), you are not forced to counterclaim – you could allow the JP case to resolve the plaintiff’s claim and separately sue in a higher court. However, practically, splitting like that is inefficient. Many defendants just waive some damages to settle everything in one go at JP. For example, if you believe you have $25,000 damages, you might waive $5,000 and counterclaim for $20,000 to cap it. A note: waiver of excess must be explicit in your counter petition (“Defendant pleads in the alternative that if the Court finds any amount over $20,000 to be due, Defendant waives recovery of such excess so as to fall within the Court’s jurisdiction”).  

**Attorneys & agents in counterclaims:** A business defendant can have its employee file the counterclaim for the business ([www.stcl.edu](https://www.stcl.edu/lib/TexasRulesProject/TRCP474-522/rule500-42013.html#:~:text=,be%20represented%20by%20an%20attorney)), they don’t need a lawyer (though they can use one). The counterclaim process is user-friendly – the Justice of the Peace court often provides simple forms or the clerks might help ensure you label it clearly as a counterclaim with case number etc.  

**Outcome of counterclaims:** The court at trial can end up issuing two judgments or one net judgment. Usually they will offset. For example, if a landlord sues for $1,000 rent and tenant counterclaims for $1,500 deposit withheld, and tenant proves more, the judge might net it and order landlord to pay tenant $500. Or judge might say landlord gets $1,000 on claim, tenant gets $1,500 on counterclaim, so net $500 to tenant. In the judgment they might actually write both and offset. If both parties have fault in a car accident, judge might apportion fault and award each something (this can be mathematically netted as well).  

### What you CANNOT sue for in small claims  
While Texas small claims courts cover a broad array of disputes, there are specific matters they **have no jurisdiction over** (by law) or types of relief they cannot grant. If your case falls into one of these categories, you’ll need to go to another court or forum. Key things you **cannot** pursue in Texas small claims (justice court):  

- **Eviction / Possession of real property:** You cannot file an eviction (also known as forcible entry and detainer) as a small claims case ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=)). Evictions are handled in justice court but under a separate “Eviction” docket (Rule 510). If you mistakenly file a small claim asking the judge to “make my tenant move out,” it will be dismissed. Similarly, a former homeowner cannot use small claims to regain possession from someone – that’s an eviction or trespass to try title (if complex). So, **possession of real property** is off-limits in small claims ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=)). (However, landlords or tenants *can* simultaneously in an eviction case ask for rent or use small claims later for money issues – just not the possession part.)  

- **Divorce, child support, or family law matters:** Justice courts cannot handle family law cases. You cannot get a divorce in small claims, or adjudicate child custody or support. Those belong in district or county family courts. If you have a small-dollar financial dispute arising from a family matter (like an ex-spouse who never paid back a $5,000 personal loan unrelated to support), you could sue on that like a normal contract, but anything involving the marriage dissolution or child support enforcement must go to the appropriate court. The Texas Government Code explicitly excludes “a suit for divorce” from JP jurisdiction ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=)). Also excluded implicitly are things like adoption, guardianship, name changes, etc.  

- **Defamation (libel/slander) above JP jurisdiction:** Texas law explicitly says a justice court has no jurisdiction over “a suit to recover damages for slander or defamation” ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=)). This means you cannot sue for libel or slander in small claims at all, regardless of amount. Even if someone defamed you and you only want $5,000, the JP court is *not* allowed to hear it ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=)). Such cases must go to a county or district court. (Likewise, malicious prosecution is typically beyond their scope, as it’s intentional tort akin to defamation in complexity). If someone files a defamation claim in JP, the judge will dismiss for lack of jurisdiction.  

- **Title to land (real estate ownership disputes):** Justice courts cannot decide who owns real property or decide on **title** issues ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=a%20suit%20to%20recover%20damages,slander%20or%20defamation%20of%20character)). So you cannot quiet title, resolve a boundary title overlap, or foreclose a lien in JP. If your small claim requires determining property title (like “I paid for part of a house and I want my name on title”), that’s too complex – those go to district court. JP can handle *personal property* ownership (like who gets a specific car or piece of equipment up to $20k value), but not land or buildings title.  

- **Malpractice by licensed professionals (medical, legal, etc.):** While not an explicit statutory exclusion, practically you can’t sue for medical malpractice in small claims. Texas has strict requirements (Chapter 74 CPRC) for health care liability claims: an expert report must be served within 120 days, etc. You won’t be able to meet those in JP; plus damages are usually high. If you attempt a med mal case in JP for under $20k, it will likely be dismissed or you’ll lose for lack of expert testimony. Same for legal malpractice – proving it requires expert lawyer testimony on standard of care, not feasible in small claims and likely more than $20k at stake. These belong in higher courts. (If it’s a trivial issue with a professional, you might frame it as a simple contract matter for a refund as discussed, but once it’s “malpractice,” avoid JP.)  

- **Federal claims beyond state jurisdiction:** Certain claims are exclusively federal: **bankruptcy** (only federal bankruptcy courts handle discharge of debts or bankruptcy estate matters – JP cannot, for example, void a debt because it was discharged; that is a defense that can be raised in JP, but if someone is in active bankruptcy, the JP case is stayed automatically by federal law and cannot proceed), **patent and copyright** infringement (federal courts have exclusive jurisdiction, so you can’t sue for someone infringing your patent or copyright in JP; trademarks are a bit different – they can be litigated in state courts concurrently, but practically not in JP due to complexity and likely injunctive relief needed), **federal civil rights under 42 USC §1983** or constitutional claims (those often go to federal court – a JP might not dismiss if someone filed a small one under state equivalent, but likely to say not their purview if injunctive or declaratory relief is sought). **ERISA** benefit claims, **IRS/tax disputes** – definitely not in JP.  

- **Class actions or mass torts:** You cannot bring a class action suit in justice court. Procedural rules for class actions are in district court level only. If multiple people have the same claim, each must file separately in JP (and each under $20k). There’s no mechanism to consolidate into a class in small claims. Similarly, a “mass tort” (like many suing one company for toxic exposure) belongs in higher court.  

- **Orders to do or not do something (injunctions):** Small claims can award money or the recovery of specific personal property, but **cannot issue injunctions or restraining orders**. For example, you cannot ask a JP to order your neighbor to stop playing loud music or to remove a fence. You also cannot ask them to enforce a non-compete agreement by injunction. Even if the only harm is small, that kind of relief is equitable/injunctive – out of JP scope. They also cannot order specific performance of a contract (like force a painter to finish the job). It’s money only, or turning over a particular item if that item is the subject of the suit. If you want someone to do something or cease doing something, you’d need a higher court (and likely a lawyer).  

- **Relief against the government:** If your defendant is the federal government or State of Texas or a state agency, typically they have **sovereign immunity** unless a specific statute allows the suit. JP courts are not empowered to handle claims under the Texas Tort Claims Act (which waives immunity for some motor vehicle accidents or premises claims up to certain amounts) – such cases are filed in county or district court. Also, if you try to sue, say, the USPS for losing a package, federal sovereign immunity means you have to file under the Federal Tort Claims Act in federal court, not JP. Suing a city or county in JP for a small contract or damage might be possible if immunity is waived (cities can be sued in some contract cases or if under Tort Claims Act for simple property damage up to $10k etc., but typically you’d go to a higher court or at least assume they’d remove it). Usually, claims against government are filed in a court of record and often require administrative notices first.  

- **Workers’ compensation claims:** If you were hurt on the job and your employer had workers’ comp insurance, you generally cannot sue the employer – you have to go through the workers’ comp system. A JP would dismiss a suit for an on-job injury against a subscribing employer as it’s barred by law. If employer didn’t have comp (nonsubscriber), you could sue them, but usually those cases are big enough to exceed $20k anyway. But if small (say you had $5k in medical bills from a minor injury and employer with no comp refused to pay), you could in theory sue in JP. However, be aware if you’re in the comp system, any disputes (like if you disagree with outcome) go to the Division of Workers’ Comp and then to district court, not JP.  

- **Automated bankruptcy stays:** If someone files bankruptcy, and they are a defendant in a JP suit, that suit cannot proceed – it’s automatically stayed by federal law 11 USC §362. The JP will typically abate or dismiss without prejudice. You can’t get around that in small claims. You’d have to get relief from stay in bankruptcy court or wait until bankruptcy is over. If a plaintiff files bankruptcy, whether their JP case can continue depends – if it’s an asset of the estate, the trustee might step in or abandon it. But from a practical view, any involvement of active bankruptcy puts things outside JP’s realm until resolved by bankruptcy court.  

- **Contempt powers:** Note, JP courts do not have general contempt powers to enforce their judgments beyond what any civil court has (they can’t jail someone for not paying a judgment). So while you can get a money judgment, enforcement is via writs (execution, garnishment) or judgment liens, not by contempt.  

- **Arbitration-required disputes:** If your contract has a clause that all disputes go to arbitration (and the other party moves to compel arbitration), the JP must halt the case and, if the clause is enforceable, send it to arbitration (Texas law and the FAA apply). So, if you sue, say, AT&T in small claims and they present an arbitration agreement you agreed to, the JP will likely stay or dismiss in favor of arbitration. Arbitration agreements are common in consumer/business contracts. JP judges do honor them when properly raised. So some disputes you technically can file in small claims, but you’ll get kicked out due to an arbitration clause. This isn’t a jurisdictional issue per se, but a contractual one.  

- **Probate and estate matters:** Justice courts don’t handle distribution of a deceased person’s estate or will contests. If, for example, your relative died and someone owes the estate $5,000, the executor could sue in JP to collect that debt *on behalf of the estate* as a regular claim. But the process of administering estates, determining heirs, etc., is strictly in county probate court or district court. So you can’t file in JP to remove an executor or get your inheritance.  

- **Tax disputes:** Can’t use JP to contest a property tax appraisal or IRS decision. Those have their own forums (appraisal review boards, state district court for property tax protests, U.S. Tax Court or federal court for IRS).  

- **Public utility or insurance rate disputes:** e.g., you think your electricity provider overcharged rates beyond PUC approvals – those go to the Public Utility Commission or Texas Dept. of Insurance, not small claims.  

- **Criminal matters & fines:** Obviously, JP in small claims capacity doesn’t handle criminal charges or traffic tickets (though interestingly JPs do have a separate role handling Class C misdemeanors and traffic as magistrates, but that’s a different court function). You cannot sue in small claims to “press charges” or recover statutory criminal fines – you can only sue for damages. Restitution from a crime must be pursued in criminal court or by separate civil action.  

To summarize the exclusions: Texas Justice Courts are meant for straightforward disputes over money or personal property under $20k. They do not entertain divorces, land title fights, slander suits, injunction requests, or anything that effectively requires equitable relief or a declaration of rights beyond a money judgment. If you try to file something outside their scope, the judge will either dismiss it or decline to grant the forbidden relief. The Government Code §27.031(b) explicitly lists: suits on behalf of the state for penalties, divorce, slander/defamation, land title, and lien foreclosures on land as off-limits ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=)) ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=a%20suit%20to%20recover%20damages,slander%20or%20defamation%20of%20character)). Those are bright lines. Use that as a checklist – if your case touches one of those, small claims is not the right venue.  

### Damages — what you can and cannot recover in small claims  
**Compensatory damages (actual damages):** You **can** recover the actual quantifiable losses you suffered. This includes out-of-pocket costs, amounts you paid and didn’t receive benefit for, property repair or replacement costs, lost wages (if proven), and similar direct economic harm. It also includes things like interest that you lost use of money (often calculated as prejudgment interest), though prejudgment interest is usually awarded under statutory guidelines (see below). Essentially, small claims can make you whole for your financial loss. Example: if a contractor took $5,000 and did nothing, you get $5,000 back. If someone damaged your $2,000 appliance, you get $2,000 (value) or the repair bill, whichever appropriate.  

**Consequential damages:** These are compensatory too – if they are foreseeable results of the breach or tort, you can claim them. For instance, if a dry cleaner ruined your suit on the day of an interview and you had to emergency buy a $300 suit, you might claim that $300 as consequential loss. JPs often have leeway to award such if they feel it’s directly tied and fair. But if damages get too speculative or indirect, they won’t award them. Keep your consequential damages reasonable and clearly linked.  

**Exemplary (punitive) damages:** Generally, **punitive damages** (meant to punish the defendant for egregious conduct) are **not available** in Texas small claims — not because the court lacks power, but because to get punitive awards you must meet high standards of proof (clear & convincing evidence of fraud, malice, or gross negligence) and it often requires a separate phase of trial in higher courts. A JP *could*, in theory, award punitive damages if a case truly called for it, but practically it’s exceedingly rare. Most punitives come through specific statutes (like DTPA’s treble damages or the bad-faith landlord deposit triple) which are considered **“statutory additional damages”** rather than common-law punitive. Those **statutory multipliers** are allowed as long as they don’t exceed the cap or are expressly provided by law. For example, DTPA “additional damages” up to 3× are allowed ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/business-commerce-code/title-2/chapter-17/subchapter-e/section-17-50/#:~:text=,mental%20anguish%20and%20economic%20damages)), deposit triple damages are allowed ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/property-code/prop-sect-92-109/#:~:text=,suit%20to%20recover%20the%20deposit)), etc. A JP will award those if proven because the statute says so. But if you simply want to punish someone outside a statute – say a scammer, and you ask for “$5,000 punitive in addition to my $1,000 loss,” the JP likely will deny that because it’s not statutory and outside typical small claims scope. In sum: **Statutory punitive or multiple damages are available** (and they are common in consumer cases), but **common-law punitive damages are theoretically available but uncommon in JP**. If you pursue them, state the legal basis (e.g., “Defendant’s conduct was fraudulent, warranting exemplary damages under Chapter 41 of CPRC”). Be aware Chapter 41’s cap on punitives is 2× economic + up to $750k non-economic (or $200k, whichever greater) ([www.barsalou-law.com](https://www.barsalou-law.com/how-much-is-post-judgment-interest-in-texas-understanding-accrual-rates-and-collection-under-texas-law#:~:text=,per%20year)) – the small claims cap of $20k is lower, effectively limiting any punitive award.  

**Statutory multipliers or penalties:** As noted, these are recoverable if the statute applies. Many examples given: 3× deposit, 3× DTPA, $1000 towing, etc. The JP will follow the statute. You must prove the conditions for the multiplier (e.g., defendant acted “knowingly” under DTPA to get treble). They also won’t give more than allowed: e.g., deposit bad-faith is exactly 3× deposit + $100, not any more fancy amount.  

**Attorney’s fees and court costs:** In Texas, **court costs** (filing fees, service fees) are normally awarded to the **prevailing party** as a matter of course ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=match%20at%20L1169%20%28d%29%20Costs,law%20to%20the%20successful%20party)). So if you win, the judge will add your filing fee and service fee to the judgment. (This is explicit in Rule 505.2 – “The judge must award costs allowed by law to the successful party.” ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=match%20at%20L1169%20%28d%29%20Costs,law%20to%20the%20successful%20party))) If each party wins something (mixed result), the court could apportion costs, but usually the net winner gets costs. Costs are small anyway (tens or a couple hundred dollars typically).  

**Attorney’s fees:** If you hired a lawyer (rare for small claims, but possible especially for businesses or if statutes guarantee fees), you can recover attorney fees **only if there’s a law or contract that provides for it**. Texas follows the American rule, no automatic fee shifting except by statute or agreement. Common situations where fees are allowed: breach of certain contracts (Texas CPRC §38.001 traditionally allowed an individual to recover fees from a corporation/entity on oral or written contracts for services or labor or material or a sworn account, etc., and 2021 amendments expanded it to allow corporations/LLCs to recover from each other too ([tcss.legis.texas.gov](https://tcss.legis.texas.gov/resources/CP/htm/CP.38.htm#:~:text=C,%28b)) ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/civil-practice-and-remedies-code/civ-prac-rem-sect-38-0015/#:~:text=FindLaw%20codes,may%20recover%20reasonable%20attorney%27s%20fees)) – so basically in a typical business contract case, prevailing party who was represented by an attorney can get fees), DTPA (prevailing consumer *must* get reasonable attorney fees, and a prevailing defendant can get fees if suit was groundless/bad faith), Texas Debt Collection Act (mandatory fees for successful plaintiff ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/finance-code/fin-sect-392-403/#:~:text=,a%20violation%20of%20this%20chapter))), landlord-tenant deposit (tenant gets fees if landlord acted in bad faith ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/property-code/prop-sect-92-109/#:~:text=,suit%20to%20recover%20the%20deposit)), landlord gets fees if tenant’s suit was in bad faith, per §92.109), repair-and-remedy (if tenant wins, they get fees by statute; if landlord wins and shows tenant’s claim was groundless, landlord can get fees, per Prop Code §92.0563), and some others (Texas Finance Code 392 and Property Code 27 (contractor’s deceptive trade act) allow fees). If you have a contract with an attorney fee clause, that’s enforceable. Notably, **if you are pro se (not represented by attorney)**, you cannot claim “attorney’s fees” for your own time – Texas doesn’t allow fee awards to pro se individuals for their own work, even if they’re a lawyer by profession. So you only get fees if you actually paid or owe a lawyer. In JP, many come without lawyers, thus fee awards are moot, except if a statute gives even pro se the right (but I don’t think any do – e.g. DTPA says “shall be awarded reasonable and necessary attorneys fees”, implying you have an attorney). If a business sends a non-lawyer officer, that’s not “attorney” so no fees unless perhaps contract had a clause for, say, “collection costs” or something – then maybe the business could claim some fixed amount if it’s in contract, but in general no. 

**Interest (pre- and post-judgment):** **Post-judgment interest**: Under Texas Finance Code §304.003, every judgment accrues interest from the date it’s signed until paid. The rate is based on a floating rate tied to federal prime rate, with a floor of 5% and cap of 15% ([www.barsalou-law.com](https://www.barsalou-law.com/how-much-is-post-judgment-interest-in-texas-understanding-accrual-rates-and-collection-under-texas-law#:~:text=,per%20year)). In recent years, interest rates have been low, so the judgment interest floor of 5% often applied ([texasjudgment.com](https://texasjudgment.com/post-judgment-interest/#:~:text=Post%20Judgment%20Interest%20After%20a,You%20can%20check)). As of 2026, rates are higher, so likely the judgment interest might be around 7-8% (whatever the formula yields) but never below 5% or above 15% in Texas ([www.barsalou-law.com](https://www.barsalou-law.com/how-much-is-post-judgment-interest-in-texas-understanding-accrual-rates-and-collection-under-texas-law#:~:text=,per%20year)). Post-judgment interest is automatic – you don’t have to ask for it; the law attaches it. It’s computed annually and added to the amount owed. The small claims judgment form will typically state “with interest at X% per annum from judgment until paid.” So yes, you can recover interest on your judgment. 

**Pre-judgment interest**: This is interest on damages from the time of injury or loss to the time of judgment. In contract cases, Texas allows prejudgment interest either by statute or equity typically at the same rate as post-judgment (or 6% if it’s a contract ascertaining interest). If a claim is for a specific sum due on a date (like a loan due on Jan 1, 2025), Tex. Finance Code §302.002 entitles you to 6% per year interest from 30 days after that due date if no other rate agreed ([statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/DocViewer.aspx?DocKey=FI%2FFI.304&ExactPhrase=False&HighlightType=1&Phrases=interest%7Crate%7Con%7Cchild%7Csupport%7Cin%7Ctexas&QueryText=interest+rate+on+child+support+in+texas#:~:text=,prejudgment%20Image%3A%20Previous%20HitinterestImage%3A%20Next)). In tort cases, prejudgment interest is available (CPRC §304.102) usually from 180 days after notice of claim or filing suit, at the post-judgment rate, but excluding punitive and future damages. You can request pre-judgment interest in your petition to ensure it’s considered. Many small claims judgments do include pre-judgment interest if appropriate (especially in contract debts or liquidated amounts). If the plaintiff doesn’t mention it, some JPs might not award it explicitly. It’s wise to ask for “pre-judgment interest at the highest rate allowed by law” in your pleadings when you have a liquidated claim (like unpaid principal amounts). For example, on an unpaid invoice from 1 year ago, you could get a year of interest at 6% or at prime rate (depending on scenario). The calculation is not complex, and the judge can add “plus $X interest” or just roll it into judgment total. But it must not push total over $20k cap – generally interest is expressly excluded from the cap calculation ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=civil%20matters%20in%20which%20exclusive,than%20%2420%2C000%2C%20exclusive%20of%20interest)), so awarding interest even if it goes above doesn’t violate jurisdiction. (E.g., you sued for $20k principal and got $1k pre-judgment interest, judgment total $21k – that’s okay because the cap is “exclusive of interest” ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=civil%20matters%20in%20which%20exclusive,than%20%2420%2C000%2C%20exclusive%20of%20interest)).) 

**Court costs and fees as damages:** These are not “damages” but as mentioned, the winner gets them on top ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=match%20at%20L1169%20%28d%29%20Costs,law%20to%20the%20successful%20party)). So you don’t need to sue for the filing fee – the system covers that by awarding it to prevailing party. Service of process fees similarly. If you had other litigation expenses (copies, parking, lost wages to attend court), those are not usually awarded – Texas doesn’t count those as recoverable costs except very specific ones (e.g. subpoena witness fees you paid can be taxed as costs possibly). Generally, expect only filing and service fees to be reimbursed.  

**Exemptions on collection:** While not part of the lawsuit’s award, it’s worth noting: after you get a judgment, Texas law exempts certain **assets of the debtor** from being seized to satisfy it. For instance, a debtor’s **homestead** (primary residence) is exempt from judgment liens (except for taxes, purchase money, home equity loans, etc. – a small claims creditor cannot force sale of someone’s homestead) – you can file an abstract of judgment lien ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/2005/pr/005.00.000052.00.html#:~:text=CHAPTER%2052,2005%20Texas%20Property%20Code)), but it won’t attach to a homestead, only to non-homestead real property ([statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/DocViewer.aspx?DocKey=PR%2FPR.52&ExactPhrase=False&HighlightType=1&Phrases=homestead%7Cexemption%7Cfor%7Ctexas&QueryText=homestead+exemption+for+texas#:~:text=,and%20on%20the%20filing%20of)). **Wages** for personal services are 100% exempt from garnishment by creditors in Texas (except for child/spousal support or certain federal debts) ([texaslegalguide.com](https://texaslegalguide.com/Texas_Constitution%3AArticle_XVI%2C_Section_28#:~:text=Article%20XVI%2C%20Section%2028%20of,2%29%20spousal%20maintenance)). So even after you win, you cannot garnish the debtor’s paycheck in Texas for a consumer or business debt ([texaslegalguide.com](https://texaslegalguide.com/Texas_Constitution%3AArticle_XVI%2C_Section_28#:~:text=Article%20XVI%2C%20Section%2028%20of,2%29%20spousal%20maintenance)). You can garnish bank accounts (once the wages are deposited, they are no longer “current wages” and can be captured unless they’re identifiable as exempt funds like Social Security). Texas law also exempts **most household goods, a vehicle (one per licensed adult)**, and various personal property up to $50k/$100k value (single/family) ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/property-code/prop-sect-42-001/#:~:text=of%20not%20more%20than%20%24100%2C000%2C,charges%20encumbering%20the%20property%3B%20or)) ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/property-code/prop-sect-42-001/#:~:text=,other%20charges%20encumbering%20the%20property)). So, realistically, many judgment debtors in Texas are “judgment-proof” – meaning their property and assets can’t be taken because they fall under exemptions (house, basic car, tools, etc., or they simply have no non-exempt property). It’s something to keep in mind before suing – winning might be hollow if debtor has nothing you can legally seize or they just do asset protection (like keeping money only in wages and spend down, or in retirement accounts which are exempt). But that’s an enforcement phase issue. The court will still grant judgment; it’s up to you to collect. The court can assist via writs and possibly a post-judgment discovery order if you motion for it (some JPs will order the debtor to answer questions about assets, but they don’t have strong contempt powers to enforce it; you might instead do a deposition under TRCP 621a in county after abstracting the judgment up, etc.).  

In summary, you can recover all forms of money damages (direct and indirect if proven, including authorized multiples and fees) and legally mandated interest and costs. You cannot get orders forcing behavior or award types not tied to a dollar amount (and you can’t get emotional distress or reputational damages unless integral to the authorized claim, e.g., mental anguish under DTPA if extreme and proven). Keep your damages in small claims **monetary and measurable**. The judgment will be a dollar amount (or an order to return a specific item or its value). If you aim for punish-the-defendant damages, anchor them in a statute or you likely won’t get them. The justice’s goal is to fairly compensate losses and perhaps apply any penalties the law requires – they are not going to venture into creative damage theories. 

## 3. Where to file  
**Court name and type:** In Texas, “small claims” cases are heard in the **Justice Courts**, presided over by Justices of the Peace (JPs) ([guides.sll.texas.gov](https://guides.sll.texas.gov/small-claims#:~:text=Small%20claims%20are%20cases%20filed,such%20as%20evictions%20and%20repairs)). These are often informally called **Justice of the Peace Courts** or just “JP Courts.” Historically, Texas had separate “Small Claims Courts,” but since 2013 they merged into the JP courts system ([tlrfoundation.org](https://tlrfoundation.org/foundation_papers/small-claims-in-a-big-state-how-texas-gets-it-right/#:~:text=Small%20Claims%20in%20a%20Big,In%202013%2C%20the%20Texas)). So you do not need to find a special building – you file at the appropriate county Justice Court. Each Texas county is divided into one or more **Justice of the Peace precincts**. For example, Harris County has 8 JP precincts (each with multiple places), Travis County has 5, etc. The Justice Court has jurisdiction up to $20,000 for civil cases ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=)). When you file, it will be styled **“In the Justice Court, Precinct __, Place __, [County Name] County, Texas.”** It might also be referred to as Small Claims Docket, but the paperwork is the same.  

**Venue rules (where within Texas to file):** You must file in a **proper county and precinct** as dictated by Texas venue statutes and rules. Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 502.4 and Civil Practice & Remedies Code Chapter 15, Subchapter E, provide guidance ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20Applicable%20Law,during%20the%20court%E2%80%99s%20business%20hours)) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,where%20the%20defendant%20resides)). Generally, for small claims:  

- You can file in the **county and precinct where the defendant resides** ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,where%20the%20defendant%20resides)). This is the default and often easiest to determine (e.g., you’re suing John Doe who lives in Precinct 3 of Tarrant County, so file JP Precinct 3, Tarrant County).  

- Alternatively, you can file in the **county and precinct where the incident or majority of incidents giving rise to the claim occurred** ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,where%20the%20defendant%20resides)). Example: a car crash happened in Precinct 2 of Bexar County and you’re suing the other driver – you may file in JP Pct 2, Bexar County (even if the defendant lives elsewhere).  

- If it’s a **contract or agreement** case, you can file in the **county and precinct where the contract was to be performed** ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,rise%20to%20the%20claim%20occurred)). For instance, if a contractor was supposed to renovate a house in Montgomery County Precinct 1, you can sue there (even if contractor’s office is in Harris County), because that’s where the service was to be performed. Performance location is often where the work or delivery was to occur, or where payment was to be made.  

- If the suit is to recover **personal property**, you can file where the property is located ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=rise%20to%20the%20claim%20was,to%20be%20performed%3B%20or)). E.g., you’re suing for return of your tractor currently sitting in the defendant’s barn in Precinct 4, Williamson County – file there.  

- There are special venue provisions: if defendant is a **non-resident of Texas or address unknown**, you can file in the **county/precinct where the plaintiff resides** ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28c%29%20Non,precinct%20where%20the%20plaintiff%20resides)). So if you’re suing an out-of-state company or someone who skipped town, JP rule says your home court is acceptable ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28c%29%20Non,precinct%20where%20the%20plaintiff%20resides)).  

- If a case involves **multiple defendants**, venue is proper in a precinct that is proper for *any one* of them under the above rules. So you could drag a defendant from another county if one local defendant makes venue okay. But all defendants could then move to transfer if it’s very inconvenient or doesn’t legally apply; usually if one defendant resides in the county, that anchors venue.  

Texas venue statutes (CPRC 15.002 etc.) apply mostly to higher courts but JP uses the simplified list above. **Bottom line:** Typically, file in the **defendant’s county** (and correct JP precinct within that county) or where the issue happened. If you choose the wrong venue, the defendant can file a **motion to transfer venue** before trial ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28d%29%20Motion%20to%20Transfer%20Venue,sworn%20statement%20that%20the%20venue)), swearing what the proper venue is. You’d then likely have your case moved to the correct JP precinct or county ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28d%29%20Motion%20to%20Transfer%20Venue,sworn%20statement%20that%20the%20venue)). If a case is filed in the wrong county and everyone shows up without objecting, the JP may still hear it (venue can be waived if not challenged timely). But a savvy defendant will challenge improper venue with a motion no later than 21 days after answering ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28d%29%20Motion%20to%20Transfer%20Venue,sworn%20statement%20that%20the%20venue)).  

**Choosing the precinct:** Counties are subdivided into JP precincts (numbered 1,2,3… and sometimes subdivided into Place A, B, etc. in populous counties). You need the correct **precinct** that covers the area in question (like defendant’s residence). Most counties have maps or you can call the JP clerk’s office. For example: Dallas County has JP courts in 5 precincts; Houston (Harris County) has 8. If you file in the wrong precinct of the right county, the defendant may move for transfer to the correct precinct ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28d%29%20Motion%20to%20Transfer%20Venue,sworn%20statement%20that%20the%20venue)), or possibly the JP might just transfer it on their own if obvious. It’s not as critical as county, but still, follow the rules.  

If you’re unsure of precinct, one helpful trick: Texas State Law Library or county websites often have a “Find Your JP” by address. Also, addresses for JPs are in the Texas Judicial Directory. You can also just call any JP office in the county and ask which precinct covers a given address – they usually help.  

**Filing if multiple options:** Sometimes venue is proper in multiple places (e.g., defendant lives in one county, but contract was signed in another where you live). You can pick any proper one. Some plaintiffs choose the precinct where they think it’s most convenient or plaintiff-friendly. But note, if you pick a venue purely for convenience that’s not technically allowed and defendant objects, it’ll move.  

**Effect of wrong venue:** If you file in a completely wrong county (say you sue someone in your county when they have no connection and incident happened elsewhere), the case can be **dismissed or transferred**. JP courts usually transfer rather than dismiss if a timely motion shows a clear proper venue. The rules say if a motion to transfer is granted, the case file is sent to the JP of the correct precinct or county, and you must pay a transfer fee and possibly a new service fee ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,the%20costs)). If you don’t pay, the case might be dismissed without prejudice. Also, if you chose a wrong venue in bad faith to harass the defendant, the judge could potentially award the defendant’s expenses for the transfer motion (though in JP that’s rarely done explicitly beyond just moving it).  

**Concurrent jurisdiction with other courts:** If your claim is exactly $20,000, you have the choice to file in JP or in a county/district court. Over $20k, only higher courts. Under $20k, county/district still have jurisdiction concurrently (except below $500, where JP is exclusive? Historically yes JP exclusive under $200, but I think now county courts might have concurrent even at low amounts, but practically no one would file a $1k suit in district court given cost). So you do have some forum choice if, say, your claim is $15,000 – you could go to county court at law (which could be more formal, maybe allow discovery and a jury trial with more process) or to JP (faster and cheaper). Many choose JP for speed and simplicity.  

**Online filing availability:** Texas has a statewide e-filing system (**eFileTexas**). Justice Courts are included in the e-filing mandate for attorneys in many counties (though not all JPs were initially on board, by now most accept e-file). For example, in Harris County, attorneys *must* e-file small claims cases ([efiletexas.gov](https://efiletexas.gov/#:~:text=e,Filing)). Self-represented individuals are **encouraged** but not required to e-file ([efiletexas.gov](https://efiletexas.gov/#:~:text=e,Filing)). If you want to file online, you can use the eFileTexas portal (through an Electronic Filing Service Provider) to submit your petition electronically. The Texas State Law Library notes that eFileTexas has a **self-help interview for small claims** that can generate your forms ([guides.sll.texas.gov](https://guides.sll.texas.gov/legal-forms/small-claims#:~:text=,gov)). Many counties also allow pro se filers to come in person or mail in filings if they prefer. **Link:** The e-filing portal for Texas is at [efiletexas.gov](https://efiletexas.gov/) – through which you register and pick a service provider (some are free, like the state’s own system). For instance, **Tyler Technologies**’ eFile system is commonly used. If you’re filing from out of state or far away, e-filing is a boon. If you file in person, you’ll go to the JP court clerk’s window.  

**Portal or website references:** If e-filing, you typically choose the **“Texas Small Claims Petition”** option in the filing type ([guides.sll.texas.gov](https://guides.sll.texas.gov/legal-forms/small-claims#:~:text=,gov)), and it will walk you through entering the info (the self-help system is like TurboTax for lawsuits). If you prefer, you can fill out a paper petition (available online or from clerk) and then scan and upload it via eFile as well. Notably, some counties (like Travis, Harris, Dallas) have user-friendly websites with forms and even online submission info. But ultimately, all tie into eFileTexas. 

**Filing in wrong venue effect:** If you accidentally file in wrong JP precinct but correct county, a defendant motion can get it moved to correct precinct (maybe just a different JP in same building if urban). If in the complete wrong county, a motion can move it to correct county's JP court (the files get mailed). That’s time and possibly extra cost for you, so try to get it right initially.  

**Does wrong venue void judgment?** If a case was heard in wrong venue and no one objected, the judgment generally stands (venue is waivable). However, one *could* appeal and contest venue on appeal (in some instances, or file a writ) – but by not objecting timely in JP, one might waive it. So as a plaintiff, you can’t just hope defendant won’t notice; better to pick a proper venue.  

**On dismissal vs transfer:** The JP rules prefer transfer if a proper venue is explicitly identified and motion made timely ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28d%29%20Motion%20to%20Transfer%20Venue,sworn%20statement%20that%20the%20venue)). The judge won’t punish you if it seems an honest mistake – they’ll just send it where it should be, and you pay a fee for re-filing there (the law says the case shall not be dismissed on venue grounds if it’s timely contested – it should be transferred, see Rule 502.4(d) and Govt Code 27.061).  

**Other considerations:** If you sue in a JP court in a county where neither party lives or where events didn’t occur just because you think judge might be friendlier, that’s forum shopping and the other side likely will challenge it. Also, if the contract has a **forum selection clause** (e.g., “any suit must be filed in X County”), Texas courts generally enforce those. A JP will honor a valid contractual venue clause, meaning if you sue in a different county/precinct, the defendant can move to transfer to the agreed county.  

**Addressing the court in filing:** On your petition, you’ll address it to the Justice of the Peace of the relevant precinct (e.g., “To the Honorable Justice of the Peace, Precinct 1, Place 2, Galveston County, Texas”). If e-filing, the system will route it to the chosen JP automatically.  

In summary, figure out **where the defendant is or where the problem happened** and go to that county's JP. If in doubt, ask the clerks – they’re usually helpful about which precinct is correct (they just can’t give legal advice beyond that).  

## 4. Filing fees  
**Statewide base filing fees:** Texas has largely standardized civil filing fees for justice courts effective January 1, 2022 (per SB 41). For a small claims case, the **filing fee** is approximately **$54** in most counties ([www.jp1hillcounty.com](https://www.jp1hillcounty.com/fine-schedule#:~:text=%2454,the%20case%20with%20the%20Court)). This typically includes a $21 state consolidated civil fee, a $33 local fee, etc., summing to ~$54 (exact components may vary by county a few dollars). For example, Hill County JP lists $54.00 to file a small claim ([www.jp1hillcounty.com](https://www.jp1hillcounty.com/fine-schedule#:~:text=%2454,the%20case%20with%20the%20Court)); Harris County JP shows $15 court cost + $65 service = $80 total when one defendant is served by constable ([www.jp.hctx.net](https://www.jp.hctx.net/civil/costs.htm#:~:text=Document%20Court%20Cost%20Service%20Fee,Tecum)) (which implies a $15 base court cost – but note, Harris’s figure might be outdated or subsidized; as of 2024, it should be closer to $54 base plus service). Many counties explicitly say $54 filing. Some breakdowns: $30 to state Judicial Fund, $10 security fee, $6 records management, $8 technology fund, etc., the combined effect is ~$54. Always verify with the specific JP’s posted fee schedule (often on their website). 

**Service of process fees:** In addition to filing, you must pay to have the defendant served with the citation (unless they waive or accept service). If you use the **constable or sheriff**, the fee is generally around **$75 – $100 per defendant**. For instance, Hill County JP charges $90 per defendant for constable service ([www.jp1hillcounty.com](https://www.jp1hillcounty.com/fine-schedule#:~:text=%2454,the%20case%20with%20the%20Court)). Harris County’s chart shows $65 service fee per defendant (plus a $10 if a witness subpoena, irrelevant here) ([www.jp.hctx.net](https://www.jp.hctx.net/civil/costs.htm#:~:text=Document%20Court%20Cost%20Service%20Fee,Tecum)). Many counties charge ~$75. These fees are set by each county commissioners court, so they vary. $100 is a statutory cap for service by certified mail in some cases, but personal service by constable is often in that range. If you have multiple defendants at different addresses, you pay per each. If multiple defendants at the same address, some constables only charge one fee to serve all together, but many still charge per person (because each needs a citation served). Check the local fee schedule. 

**Optional service methods and costs:** Texas allows **service by certified mail** performed by the clerk **if** the plaintiff requests it ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,order%20of%20the%20Supreme%20Court)). The clerk can send the citation via certified mail, restricted delivery. The fee for that is usually lower – e.g., some JPs charge ~$15-$20 for service by certified mail (covering postal costs and handling) – but it’s only effective if the defendant personally signs the green card. If they don’t claim or someone else signs, it’s not valid service ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=defendant%20by%20registered%20or%20certified,or%20electronic%20return%20receipt%20requested)). Many plaintiffs opt to pay for constable service as it’s more reliable. If you want to attempt certified mail first to save money, ask the clerk – the initial filing form often has a box "send citation by certified mail" and associated fee. If that fails (unclaimed), you might then pay later for a constable. Alternatively, you can hire a **private process server** (must be a certified server authorized by a court in Texas). Private server fees vary ($75-100 typically). JPs often allow it – you’d still need to get the citation from clerk and give to server. But paying the constable is easiest as they liaise with the court and file the return. 

**Fee waivers (in forma pauperis):** If you cannot afford the fees, you can file a **“Statement of Inability to Afford Payment of Court Costs”** (formerly called an affidavit of indigency or pauper’s oath). This form is available from the court or Texas courts website. It requires information about your income, assets, and expenses. If the court finds you qualify (usually if you receive certain public benefits or your income is below a percentage of poverty level), the filing fee and service fee can be waived ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,The)). There’s no additional charge to apply; just file the statement with your petition. The opposing party can contest the claim of inability, but it’s rare in small claims unless they suspect fraud. If contested, the judge holds a hearing to decide if you truly cannot afford costs ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20Demand,the%20judge%20for%20good%20cause)). Typically, if you’re on food stamps, SSI, Medicaid, or earning near minimum wage with dependents, the waiver is granted. The Statement of Inability is a standardized form promulgated by the Texas Supreme Court ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,The)) – using that form ensures you include required info. Once filed, the clerk will docket and issue citation without requiring fees up front ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=has%20filed%20a%20Statement%20of,sheriff%2C%20constable%2C%20or%20court%20clerk)) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,The)). If later found you lied about indigency, the court can impose the costs or dismiss the case. Also note: if you win, usually you wouldn’t recover fees (since none paid), but if defendant wins and you had a waiver, they cannot charge you those costs either unless maybe they prove you lied about indigency ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=perjury,provide%20any%20other%20customary%20services)) (the rules allow contesting veracity). 

**Recovering fees from the other side:** As mentioned in section 2 Damages: if you win, the judgment will typically include the fees you paid – filing and service – as part of the amount due ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=match%20at%20L1169%20%28d%29%20Costs,law%20to%20the%20successful%20party)). So effectively the defendant reimburses those (through the judgment). If you had a fee waiver, there were no fees. If defendant wins, they can similarly recover their court costs (if any – e.g., if they filed a counterclaim or certain motions that had fees). But if you had a waiver, the rules allow only challenging whether you really couldn’t pay ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=perjury,provide%20any%20other%20customary%20services)), not automatically shifting to you if you lose. Essentially, if you file in forma pauperis and lose, the court costs just remain unpaid (the county covers them) unless your inability was contested and found invalid, in which case presumably you’d have to pay.  

**Tiered fees by claim amount:** Unlike some states, Texas’s civil filing fee in JP court doesn’t increase with the claim size. A $500 suit and a $20,000 suit have the same base $54 fee. (In contrast, some higher courts have different fees for larger amounts or particular actions, but JP is flat). 

**Other potential fees:** If you request a **jury trial** in JP (which you can – see Appeals & Hearing sections for timeline), you must pay a $22 jury fee at least 14 days before trial ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20Demand,the%20judge%20for%20good%20cause)) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28b%29%20Jury%20Fee,written%20request%20for%20a%20jury)). If you don’t pay, no jury. If you appeal (to county court), there will be an **appeal bond or deposit** (see Appeals section – $500 bond for plaintiff, double judgment for defendant ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,rendered%20against%20it%20on%20appeal))) or a $54 filing fee in county plus costs there. But within JP, the main fees are filing and service (and possibly fees for any citations or subpoenas you later request – e.g., if you subpoena a witness, there’s usually a $10 issuance fee and you must pay witness a $10/day fee ([tcss.legis.texas.gov](https://tcss.legis.texas.gov/resources/CP/htm/CP.22.htm#:~:text=B,entitled%20to%2010%20dollars%20for))).  

**Method of payment:** If in person, most JPs accept cash, money order, or credit card (some add a small card processing fee). E-filing requires online payment via credit/debit or e-check through the eFile system. If mailing in a petition, include a check or money order payable to the Justice Court.  

**Can fees be added to judgment?** Yes, as costs. So eventually defendant reimburses the prevailing plaintiff for them ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=match%20at%20L1169%20%28d%29%20Costs,law%20to%20the%20successful%20party)). If plaintiff loses, they eat their own fees (though they had the chance to not file if risk). 

**Example fee calculation:** Suppose you sue two defendants. Filing fee $54. Service by constable on each: $80 × 2 = $160. Total you pay upfront: ~$214. If you win, judgment might be like “Plaintiff recovers $5,000 + court costs of $214” making $5,214 due, plus interest. If you had a lawyer and contract allows fees, you might get fees added too, but that’s separate from “court costs.” 

**No-salary for judge / no extra fees:** JPs do not require you to pay anything beyond set fees. The judge’s salary is covered by county; no “hearing fee” or “judgment fee.” The losing party can’t be ordered to pay the judge or jury in civil cases (except the small daily witness fees if any which are already part of costs).  

**Fee schedules differences by county:** The Texas Judicial Branch website publishes a **Justice Court Civil Filing Fees** document each year ([www.txcourts.gov](https://www.txcourts.gov/publications-training/publications/filing-fees-courts-costs/civil/justice-courts/#:~:text=Civil%20,Justice%20Court%20Civil%20Filing%20Fees)). For 2024, it shows all the standardized state fees plus any local additions. Most differences are minor (some counties might have a $2 extra record archive fee or something). So expect in the range of $50-60. For example: Travis County as of 2023 is $54 to file, Denton County $50, El Paso $52. The differences reflect how local $10 fees must be used for different funds. The major costs like service have bigger variances set by local policy (like rural counties sometimes have slightly higher mileage-based service fees). As a litigant, just ask the clerk or check their website for “Civil Fees.” Many clerks post a PDF list (like “Small Claims suit: $54; each citation: $75” etc.).  

**Waiver of fees recoverable?** If you had a fee waiver, you didn’t pay, so you can’t “recover” it. The county effectively covered your costs. If you win, the county doesn’t ask the defendant to pay those waived fees into county coffers – historically, maybe not. Actually, the law is a bit unclear: some states do require the losing defendant to pay waived fees to the state. Texas doesn’t explicitly say that for JP – likely no, the county absorbs it. (In higher courts, sometimes at final judgment, a judge might tax costs which could include waived fees, making the defendant pay them to the clerk if plaintiff had them waived. It’s possible a JP could order that as “costs to be paid to clerk.” It’s not common though; often fee waivers just remain waived). 

In summary: **Filing a small claim in Texas JP court typically costs around $54 to file + ~$75 per defendant to serve**, give or take a few dollars by locale. So for one-defendant case, budget ~$130. These fees will be added to your judgment if you win. If you can’t afford them, file a Statement of Inability and you may pay $0. The system is designed to be accessible, with costs relatively low compared to higher courts (where filing can be $300+).  

## 5. Statute of limitations  
The **statute of limitations** is the legal deadline by which you must file your lawsuit. In Texas, the limitations period depends on the type of claim. Filing after the period has run can result in your case being dismissed, as the defendant can assert a limitations defense (exceptions exist, but it’s risky to miss the deadline). Here are the standard Texas limitation periods for common claim types relevant to small claims, along with when the clock starts (“accrual”) and any tolling considerations:  

- **Written contract:** **4 years** from the date the contract was breached  . (Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code §16.004). For example, if a written loan was due on Jan 1, 2022 and not paid, you have until Jan 1, 2026 to sue for that breach. If a contractor breached a written agreement on completion date March 2020, file by March 2024. Accrual is when the breach occurred – usually either when payment was due and not made, or when the other party repudiated or failed to perform a promised act. If the breach is ongoing or a series of breaches, the clock might run from each breach (e.g., each missed installment payment is its own breach possibly). Partial payments can **toll or reset** the clock for debt contracts: under Texas law, a payment or a written acknowledgment of the debt can start the 4-year period anew from that date (Tex. Civ. Rem. Code §16.065). Also note, a written contract under seal or a promissory note has same 4-year limit – Texas doesn’t extend it for sealed documents like some states do (Texas doesn’t really use the seal concept nowadays).  

- **Oral (verbal) contract:** **4 years** as well, in Texas. This might surprise some (many states are shorter for oral), but Texas’s catch-all four-year limitations (Tex. Civ. Rem. Code §16.004 for “debt” and some other actions) has been interpreted to include oral contracts and implied contracts . There had been at one time a distinction (older law had some oral contracts at 2 years if not covered by debt or other categories), but currently, a suit for debt or breach of contract (oral or written) is generally four years . For example, you loaned someone money with no written note in Jan 2020 expecting a return in a “few months” but they didn’t pay – arguably by end of 2020 it’s breached, so you’d have until end of 2024 to sue. It’s safer not to rely on the long end if unclear when breach occurred. If the oral agreement did not specify a time of performance, the claim accrues when a reasonable time for performance has passed and demand for performance is made. If no demand was required, then a “reasonable time” concept can complicate accrual. Courts might look at circumstances; but practically, if it’s been years, they’ll say it’s accrued. Best practice: make a demand and treat that as breach if not honored.  

- **Open account or credit account:** **4 years**, treated as a form of debt. Texas law lumps “debt” actions as 4-year (this covers stated or open accounts, certain credit card suits, etc.) . Accrual on an open account is typically the date of the last item of the account or the last payment. For revolving credit, each charge might be its own contract or it might be all one continuing contract – Texas tends to consider credit card debt as a debt claim from when the account was charged-off or when the last charge or payment was made then default followed. Many credit card suits use last payment date to measure 4 years. If no payment, then from default after missed payment plus 30 days as per card agreement maybe.  

- **Promissory note (written, installment):** **4 years** after the note’s due date (if one lump sum due) or 4 years after the **accelerated** due date if the lender accelerates following default (if installments). If installments and not accelerated, each missed installment could be its own breach with its own 4-year period, but often notes have an acceleration clause that the lender can invoke to demand all due. Once accelerated, 4 years from acceleration date (Tex. Civ. Rem. Code §16.004). Partial payments after acceleration might dec

**...accelerate the clock**: If a borrower makes a payment after default, it can toll or reset the period (Tex. Civ. Rem. Code §16.065). But once a debt is fully accelerated and the 4-year clock runs, the lender loses the claim unless there’s an agreement to rescind acceleration or new payments.  

- **Property damage (to personal property):** **2 years** (Tex. Civ. Rem. Code §16.003) ([statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/GetStatute.aspx?Code=CP&Value=16#:~:text=Sec.%2016.003.%20%20TWO,the%20cause%20of%20action%20accrues)). This includes damage to or loss of *personal property* (car damage, destruction of goods, etc.). Accrual is when the injury occurred. For example, if your car was damaged on July 1, 2024, file by July 1, 2026. If damage is ongoing (e.g., a neighbor’s tree continually dropping limbs), arguably each incident is its own accrual. But usually it’s a single event.  

- **Trespass to real property:** **2 years** (considered an injury to property under §16.003) ([statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/GetStatute.aspx?Code=CP&Value=16#:~:text=Sec.%2016.003.%20%20TWO,the%20cause%20of%20action%20accrues)). If someone damaged your land or entered wrongfully, sue within 2 years of the trespass.  

- **Personal injury:** **2 years** from the date of injury (Tex. Civ. Rem. Code §16.003) ([statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/GetStatute.aspx?Code=CP&Value=16#:~:text=Sec.%2016.003.%20%20TWO,the%20cause%20of%20action%20accrues)). Car accidents, slip-and-falls, dog bites – all two years from the incident. If the injury wasn’t discovered immediately (like an illness from toxic exposure), Texas’s “discovery rule” can in certain cases delay accrual until the plaintiff knew or should have known of the injury. But that’s mainly in latent injury cases (e.g., medical malpractice or silica exposure). For a straightforward accident, it’s 2 years from the accident date. Minors or legally insane persons are under a **legal disability** and the period is tolled until disability is removed (i.e., minor turns 18, or person regains competency) ([statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/GetStatute.aspx?Code=CP&Value=16#:~:text=,included%20in%20a%20limitations%20period)). For minors, the 2-year clock starts on their 18th birthday for claims like personal injury ([statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/GetStatute.aspx?Code=CP&Value=16#:~:text=,included%20in%20a%20limitations%20period)). However, note Texas has a law for health care claims that doesn’t allow tolling beyond certain long-stops, but for ordinary injury it’s as above.  

- **Fraud:** **4 years** (Tex. Civ. Rem. Code §16.004(a)(4)) ([statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/GetStatute.aspx?Code=CP&Value=16#:~:text=)). Clock starts when the fraud was or reasonably could have been discovered. Texas courts apply the discovery rule to fraud inherently – so even though §16.004 says 4 years, accrual is upon discovery of the fraud (or when you should have discovered it with reasonable diligence). Example: You found out in 2023 that in 2019 the seller of a car rolled back the odometer – the 4-year period might start 2023 because you couldn’t have known earlier. Still, don’t delay once discovered. Proving when you discovered can be an issue, so act promptly.  

- **Consumer protection (DTPA) claims:** **2 years** (Tex. Bus. & Com. Code §17.565) ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._bus._and_com._code_section_17.565#:~:text=Texas%20Business%20and%20Commerce%20Code,within%20two%20years%20after%20the)). The DTPA’s statute says 2 years from when the false, misleading, or deceptive act occurred *or* 2 years from when it was discovered or should have been discovered with reasonable diligence ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._bus._and_com._code_section_17.565#:~:text=Texas%20Business%20and%20Commerce%20Code,within%20two%20years%20after%20the)). So a DTPA claim has a built-in discovery rule. For instance, if in 2020 you were sold a product with a hidden defect and you discover the deception in 2021, you have until 2023. Courts strictly apply the 2-year window unless there was fraudulent concealment to extend it slightly.  

- **Debt collection violations (Texas Finance Code Chapter 392):** The Finance Code doesn’t specify a limitations period, so courts apply the default 2-year period (for a statutory penalty or personal injury to reputation) or possibly 4-year if viewed akin to DTPA via tie-in. Generally safer to assume **2 years** from the violation. Federal FDCPA has 1 year, but state TDCA likely 2.  

- **Judgments:** If you’re suing to enforce a prior judgment (not typical in small claims, but say you have an earlier JP judgment and want a new one against another liable party), the limitation is **10 years** to enforce or revive a judgment (Tex. Civ. Rem. Code §34.001) ([tcss.legis.texas.gov](https://tcss.legis.texas.gov/resources/CP/htm/CP.34.htm#:~:text=C,a%20writ%20of%20execution%20is)). After 10 years, a judgment becomes dormant (unenforceable) ([tcss.legis.texas.gov](https://tcss.legis.texas.gov/resources/CP/htm/CP.34.htm#:~:text=C,a%20writ%20of%20execution%20is)) unless revived. You typically revive by filing a scire facias in the original court within 2 years of dormancy, so effectively up to 12 years. But this is about enforcement, not new filings.  

- **Special cases:** **Security deposit** claims by tenants – these are essentially contract claims (4 years), but practically if you wait that long, the “bad-faith” presumption might fade. Still, legally 4 years. **NSF check** under civil penalty statute – it’s basically written contract (the act of writing the check is like a promise to pay), so 4 years. **Right of reimbursement** (like co-signer paying debt) – seen as implied contract or equitable, likely 2 or 4 depending on theory; safer to treat as 4 if contractual in nature. **Malpractice** (medical, legal) – med is 2 years from occurrence or reasonable discovery (and a 10-year absolute cut-off) under Tex. Civ. Rem. Code §74.251, but med mal shouldn’t be in JP. Legal mal is 2 years from the malpractice or discovery.  

**Tolling exceptions:** Texas statutes toll during **legal disability** (minor or unsound mind) ([statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/GetStatute.aspx?Code=CP&Value=16#:~:text=,included%20in%20a%20limitations%20period)), as mentioned. Also if the defendant is **absent from the state** (and not subject to service in Texas) for a period, that period does not count – Tex. Civ. Rem. Code §16.063 ([statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/GetStatute.aspx?Code=CP&Value=16#:~:text=Sec,period%20of%20the%20person%27s%20absence)). For example, if someone who owes you money leaves Texas for 1 year, the clock stops running during their absence ([statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/GetStatute.aspx?Code=CP&Value=16#:~:text=Sec,period%20of%20the%20person%27s%20absence)) (though modern courts sometimes say if they can still be served via long-arm or have an agent, §16.063 may not apply – but generally it’s in the law). Fraudulent concealment of a cause of action by the defendant will toll until discovery. Bankruptcy can toll certain claims (the automatic stay tolls limitations for the duration of the stay plus 30 days). Also, if you file a case and it’s dismissed on a matter not on the merits, you might have the benefit of the **Texas saving statute** (Tex. Civ. Rem. Code §16.064) allowing 60 days to refile in the correct court if the first filing was before limitations expired and dismissal was for lack of jurisdiction or venue. For instance, if you mistakenly file in the wrong court and it’s thrown out after limitations ran, §16.064 might save you if you refile in proper court within 60 days of dismissal ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/civil-practice-and-remedies-code/title-2/subtitle-c/chapter-34/subchapter-a/section-34-001/#:~:text=Texas%20Civil%20Practice%20and%20Remedies,Trial%2C%20Judgment%2C%20and%20Appeal)). This doesn’t apply to dismissal for lack of prosecution or voluntary nonsuit generally.  

**Practical tip:** File well before the deadline. Don’t try to cut it close. If you’re even *near* the limitation expiration, get it on file. The petition doesn’t need to be elaborate. Once filed, it stops the clock (limitations stops upon filing, as long as you then exercise diligence in serving the defendant). In Texas you must also ensure defendant is served with “reasonable diligence.” If you file suit on Day 1, but then sit on it and don’t serve the defendant for many months without good reason, a court could rule you “didn’t toll” the statute because you didn’t pursue service diligently. So after filing, serve promptly – especially if you filed just before the deadline.

## 6. Pre-filing requirements  
Before suing, consider any **pre-filing steps** Texas law requires or recommends. Skipping these could affect your case or ability to recover certain damages:  

- **Demand letters:** While not universally mandated, it’s **highly recommended** to send a written demand for payment or performance and give a reasonable time (e.g. 10–30 days) for the other side to respond before filing. In some instances, a demand is required by law to preserve certain remedies: for example, to recover attorney fees in a breach of contract, Texas law generally requires you to have presented the claim to the opposing party at least 30 days before judgment (Tex. Civ. Rem. Code §38.002) ([tcss.legis.texas.gov](https://tcss.legis.texas.gov/resources/CP/htm/CP.38.htm#:~:text=,be%20represented%20by%20an%20attorney)) ([tcss.legis.texas.gov](https://tcss.legis.texas.gov/resources/CP/htm/CP.38.htm#:~:text=expiration%20of%20the%2030th%20day,after%20the%20claim%20is%20presented)). Basically, you must show you gave the defendant a chance to pay the debt after demand ([tcss.legis.texas.gov](https://tcss.legis.texas.gov/resources/CP/htm/CP.38.htm#:~:text=,be%20represented%20by%20an%20attorney)). So, if you want attorney fees on a contract, send a demand letter and keep proof. Likewise, under the **Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA)**, a consumer **must send a 60-day demand letter** stating the complaint and damages before filing suit ([guides.sll.texas.gov](https://guides.sll.texas.gov/consumer-protection/relief#:~:text=If%20you%20have%20a%20DTPA,to%20avoid%20going%20to%20court)). If you fail to, the court can abate (pause) the case until 60 days after notice, and it can prevent you from recovering attorney fees. So for any DTPA claim, send that letter via certified mail, 60+ days before suing ([guides.sll.texas.gov](https://guides.sll.texas.gov/consumer-protection/relief#:~:text=If%20you%20have%20a%20DTPA,to%20avoid%20going%20to%20court)). Other statutes: Texas Theft Liability Act (for theft of property) requires a written notice/demand 30 days prior to suit to be eligible for certain damages. Bad check demands (10-day letter under Penal Code §32.41) are technically for criminal presumption, but sending one is good practice for civil as well. Landlord-tenant: a tenant doesn’t strictly have to demand the deposit, because the statute creates a presumption of bad faith if not returned in 30 days without accounting ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/property-code/prop-sect-92-109/#:~:text=,have%20acted%20in%20bad%20faith)), but it’s wise to **give forwarding address in writing** (required) and perhaps remind the landlord in writing of the deposit after move-out. If you plan to use repair-and-deduct for a tenant repair, you must give required notices and wait periods before doing the repair (Property Code §92.0561) ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/property-code/prop-sect-92-0561/#:~:text=,as%20provided%20in%20this%20section)) ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/property-code/prop-sect-92-0561/#:~:text=,of%20rent%20under%20the%20circumstances)). Always follow those steps or you can’t recover that expense.  

- **Opportunity to cure/mediate:** Some contracts have clauses requiring mediation or arbitration before litigation. If so, attempt to comply (or at least offer to mediate) because a JP might enforce the contract clause by abating or dismissing if you jumped straight to court. Texas doesn’t impose a general pre-suit mediation requirement for small claims, but **some counties provide voluntary mediation** services (often on trial day, the JP might ask if parties want to mediate). It’s not mandatory unless required by contract or local rule. One exception: lawsuits against a **home builder** under the Residential Construction Liability Act require written notice and opportunity to inspect/repair 60 days before suit (Prop. Code §27.004). This could apply if you’re suing a contractor for construction defects; failing to give that notice can limit your damages. 

- **Notice for certain defendant types:** If suing a Texas **government unit** (city, county, etc.) for a tort, you must have given proper written notice of claim within 6 months of injury (Local Govt Code §101.101) or even shorter for some city charters (often 90 days). Without notice, your claim can be barred even if timely. Government claims also often must be in a higher court due to jurisdiction and sovereign immunity issues. But if you did have a small tort claim against a city (e.g., city vehicle hit your fence), ensure you filed the notice with the city as required, or they’ll move to dismiss. 

- **Registered agent/assumed name considerations:** Before filing against a business, check if the business is **an entity with a registered agent** (via Texas SOS or tax records). If so, not exactly a pre-filing “requirement,” but you’ll need that info for service. If a business is an **assumed name (dba)** of a person or entity, Texas law (Bus. & Com. Code §71.201) says an unincorporated business must file an assumed name certificate. If they haven’t, it doesn’t stop you from suing, but it can affect who you name – you should find the real owner’s name. Pre-suit, it’s wise to look up the assumed name certificate at the county clerk or SOS for corporations to identify the proper legal defendant. This avoids later delay if they claim “you sued the wrong name.” E.g., if Joe runs “Joe’s Plumbing” (no LLC), you should sue “Joe Smith d/b/a Joe’s Plumbing.” For an LLC “ABC LLC d/b/a Joe’s Plumbing,” you’d sue the LLC. A quick public records search beforehand helps. 

- **Insurance claims:** If it’s an auto accident or property damage and insurance is involved, you likely filed a claim with their insurer. While not legally mandatory to do so before suing, it’s practical. Also, if you settle or accept insurance payment, you might have to sign release – which would end your right to sue. If insurer denies or offers too low, then suing is the next step. But at least attempt the claim first. For work injuries, if it’s workers’ comp, you must go through that system (not small claims).  

- **Contractual notice provisions:** Some contracts require notice of default and time to cure before litigation. If your contract has such a clause (common in services or construction contracts, e.g., “buyer must notify seller of defects within 30 days and allow cure”), follow it: send the notice, give the specified time. If you ignore it and directly sue, the other party can argue lawsuit is premature or contractually barred. The JP might not dismiss but it could hurt your credibility or ability to claim attorneys’ fees. 

- **Bond requirements:** Rare in small disputes, but if you’re suing under a bond (like suing a contractor’s surety bond), you might need to formally notify the surety or principal by certified mail of the claim within certain time (common for contractor bonds or notary bonds). Check the statute for that bond. E.g., to claim on a notary’s bond, you must sue the notary and surety within limitations but no special pre-notice required beyond that the notary must be a defendant. But for public works payment bonds, notice to surety within 90 days of last work is required. These usually aren’t JP cases (they’d exceed $20k or go to other courts). 

- **Mold notice:** If a residential tenant is claiming property damage or health issues from mold, Texas Property Code §92.008 (I believe) requires the tenant to notify landlord of mold condition and allow inspection. Also, Texas doesn’t allow retaliatory eviction if tenant has complained about mold. Not directly small claims, but as pre-step, tell the landlord so they can attempt remediation. That strengthens your case if you later sue for damages. 

- **Forcible detainer vs suit for rent:** If you’re a *landlord*, to recover possession you must go through eviction proceedings (with a 3-day notice to vacate, etc.). But if you purely want rent money and not possession, you can either include it in an eviction or sue separately in small claims. There’s no statutory demand letter required for rent (aside from any lease notice requirements or the 3-day that applies to eviction). It’s wise to send a notice stating rent due and you’ll file suit if not paid by X date – but legally, filing without a specific rent demand isn’t barred (the unpaid rent itself puts them on notice). For *commercial* leases, check if the lease demands a notice of default and time to cure before litigation – many do.  

In summary, **send a polite but firm demand letter** for any money owed or issue to be fixed, giving a deadline and referencing that you’ll take legal action if not resolved. This often resolves the issue without court. It also produces a paper trail of reasonableness. For certain claims (consumer DTPA, contract attorney fees, landlord deposits, etc.), a demand is either required or strategically very important. Additionally, verify if any **statutory notice** or waiting period applies to your situation and complete it. Taking these steps not only preserves your rights (and fees) but also will present better in court – judges appreciate when plaintiffs gave the other side a chance to do the right thing before suing.  

## 7. Forms required  
Filing a Texas small claim doesn’t require complex paperwork – just a few **standard forms**:  

- **Original Petition (Small Claims Petition):** This is the primary form to start your case. Texas Justice Courts have user-friendly petition forms. You can often get a **pre-printed form from the JP court** or use the Texas State Law Library’s templates. The petition should include: the *style* (court name, case blank, names of parties), a statement of the claim (what happened and the relief you seek), and your signature ([guides.sll.texas.gov](https://guides.sll.texas.gov/legal-forms/small-claims#:~:text=,gov)) ([guides.sll.texas.gov](https://guides.sll.texas.gov/legal-forms/small-claims#:~:text=,Office%20of%20Court%20Administration)). Texas Supreme Court-approved forms exist for debt claims and small claims – eFileTexas even provides an **interactive interview** to generate a “Texas Small Claims Petition” ([guides.sll.texas.gov](https://guides.sll.texas.gov/legal-forms/small-claims#:~:text=,gov)). Key points to cover in the petition: each party’s name and address (for defendant, include their *serve* address if different), the amount you seek (and whether it’s within the court’s jurisdiction), a brief description of why the defendant is liable, and a statement of any statutory claims (e.g., “This suit is brought under the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act”). If you’re suing on an account or written contract, you might attach a copy (not required at filing, but recommended to solidify your case – JPs don’t require attachments, but doing so can expedite default judgments in debt cases ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,plaintiff%20is%20entitled%2C%20if%20any))). The petition form usually has check boxes and blanks to fill: cause of action, date of event, damages, etc. Ensure you correctly name the **defendant(s)** – use their full legal name (and any aliases or “dba” name). If it’s a business entity, use the LLC/corp name. Example: “John Doe d/b/a Doe’s Painting” or “Acme, Inc.” Also, identify if the defendant is in the military (courts ask due to default judgment protections under federal law – many petition forms include a non-military status declaration). You (the “Plaintiff”) will sign the petition – in Justice Court, you don’t need verification under oath except in specific cases (like a debt claim petition does require a verified account statement by Rule 508, but a “small claims” doesn’t require verification generally).  

- **Justice Court Civil Case Information Sheet:** This one-page form is required to accompany the petition ([guides.sll.texas.gov](https://guides.sll.texas.gov/legal-forms/small-claims#:~:text=,Office%20of%20Court%20Administration)). It’s not part of the petition served to defendant – it’s for administrative data. On it, you fill in your contact info, the defendant’s contact info, the type of case (there’s check boxes: debt claim, repair & remedy, eviction, small claims, etc.), and an indication of relief sought. It’s sometimes called the “Civil Case Info Sheet.” The Texas Office of Court Administration provides this form statewide ([guides.sll.texas.gov](https://guides.sll.texas.gov/legal-forms/small-claims#:~:text=,Office%20of%20Court%20Administration)). The form asks for names, emails, and last 3 digits of driver’s license or social (for each party) – this helps identify parties and for the court registry. It also asks for a short case description. Make sure to file this; though a case won’t be thrown out for missing it, the clerk is directed not to reject a petition for lack of it, but they’ll usually ask you to provide it. In e-filing, the system often prompts for case details in lieu of a physical sheet.  

- **Service documents:** After filing, the court clerk will prepare a **Citation** (summons) directed to the defendant. This is not a form you fill out (the clerk generates it). However, you do need to provide in your petition or a separate request the defendant’s address for service and pay the service fee. The **citation** will be delivered to a constable or authorized server. If you opted for certified mail service by the clerk, the clerk uses the citation for that. The citation basically says “You are commanded to appear by filing an answer by X date... if you do not, judgment by default may be rendered.” It also includes the plaintiff’s claims in brief. The clerk or server handles the citation – you just need to ensure correct addresses and pay fees.  

- **Military Status Affidavit (SCRA affidavit):** If you are seeking a default judgment, federal law (Servicemembers Civil Relief Act) requires the plaintiff to file an affidavit stating whether the defendant is in military service, or that you could not determine their status after a diligent search. Many Texas courts proactively want this **“Nonmilitary Affidavit”** form if the defendant doesn’t answer. Typically, at time of filing or before default hearing, you’ll provide a short affidavit (there’s a form) saying “Defendant John Doe is not on active military duty as of [date] based on [facts or database search].” There’s a Defense Dept. website to verify active duty status with their DOB or SSN (if known). If the defendant is in military, the court won’t enter default unless certain protections are addressed (like appointing an attorney ad litem). So, if you suspect defendant might be active duty, better find out upfront. Many Justice Courts have a form for this (Texas Supreme Court published a Military Status Affidavit form). Some JP clerks will give you the form to fill before they finalize a default. But it’s good practice to include it or be ready with it. Note: you **don’t need this to file** the case, only at default time, but some counties like Harris include a section in the petition to swear to defendant’s military status up front ([www.jp.hctx.net](https://www.jp.hctx.net/civil/costs.htm#:~:text=Image%20Court%20Costs%20and%20Service,Tecum)).  

- **Copies of evidence (attachments):** Not strictly “forms,” but if your claim is based on a **written contract, promissory note, or account**, attach a copy to your petition if available. For a **sworn account** (like suit on open account for goods/services), Rule 508 requires a **sworn statement** of the account terms and balance at filing for a debt claim ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,plaintiff%20is%20entitled%2C%20if%20any)). If you file as a “debt claim” (for assignees, collectors, or banks), you must include specific info (e.g., original creditor, last payment date) and often an **affidavit of the debt**. For a “small claims” case (individuals suing on their own contract), this is not required, but including the contract or invoice can strengthen your case, especially if seeking default – the judge can more readily assess damages. Similarly, if you have written communications (emails agreeing to terms), you can attach. But remember, whatever you attach becomes part of the pleadings served on defendant. That’s fine as long as it supports your claim. Don’t attach irrelevant sensitive documents (like a long string of texts with personal info – save evidence for trial).  

- **Counterclaim form:** If you’re a defendant and want to countersue, you’ll file a **Defendant’s Counterclaim Petition**. It’s essentially the same format as the original petition, just labeled as a counterclaim with the existing case info. It also requires a case info sheet (though you can reuse a lot of the same info). The JP can provide a generic blank for counterclaims too. The content needs to meet same standards: identify parties, state your claim and amount, and sign. See **Section 2 “Counterclaims”** above for details ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=RULE%20502.6.%20COUNTERCLAIM%3B%20CROSS,PARTY%20CLAIM)).  

- **Specific case-type forms:** If your case is a specialized “Repair and Remedy” (tenant enforcement case under Rule 509) or an eviction, there are separate petition forms for those. For small claims we focus on the generic small claims petition (Rule 502). But mention: if a tenant is filing to enforce repairs (under $20k and wanting rent reduction or penalty), they’d use a **Repair & Remedy Petition (Rule 509)** – which has additional fields like property condition, notice given to landlord, etc. These forms are on the Texas JP court websites. If you inadvertently use the wrong form, the clerk might let you know.  

- **Other supporting forms:** If you are filing by mail or e-file and requesting **service by certified mail**, some counties have a form or check-box. Most just require a written request in petition or cover letter: “Please serve defendant by certified mail.” If you want a **jury trial**, you don’t file a form at the start; you either check a box on info sheet or you can wait and file a “jury trial demand” later (but no later than 14 days before trial with $22 fee) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=match%20at%20L1064%20%28a%29%20Demand,the%20judge%20for%20good%20cause)) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28b%29%20Jury%20Fee,written%20request%20for%20a%20jury)). Also, if you get to **trial and need subpoenas** for witnesses or documents, you’ll request subpoena forms from the clerk (there’s a subpoena form you fill in names, the judge signs). For **post-judgment** enforcement, you might use forms for writ of execution, writ of garnishment, etc. – but those are after you win, not at filing stage.  

- **Filing online vs. paper:** If you e-file, you’ll fill out information in the e-filing system and upload your petition as a PDF. Many JPs provide **fillable PDF petitions** on their websites (e.g., “Small Claims Petition”) which you can download, complete, and upload to eFileTexas. Or use the Law Library’s guided interview for a generated petition PDF. If you file in person, bring copies: one for the court and one for each defendant (the clerk will stamp and use them for service). Typically, the clerk will make copies if needed but may charge a copying fee. It’s courteous to provide enough copies. If by mail, include the petition and case info sheet and a self-addressed return envelope if you want a file-stamped copy back.  

**Where to find forms:** The **Texas State Law Library’s website** has a “Small Claims Forms” section with links to the **Justice Court Training Center forms** and the **E-file Texas self-help** ([guides.sll.texas.gov](https://guides.sll.texas.gov/legal-forms/small-claims#:~:text=,gov)) ([guides.sll.texas.gov](https://guides.sll.texas.gov/legal-forms/small-claims#:~:text=,Justice%20Court%20Training%20Center)). For example, Taylor County’s website lists “Small Claims Petition” form (English and Spanish) ([www.taylorcounty.texas.gov](https://www.taylorcounty.texas.gov/463/Small-Claims-Forms#:~:text=Website%20www,Plaintiff%20Self%20Help)). Harris County JP site provides an online form. Many JP websites have downloadable **PDFs or Word docs** for common filings. The **Justice Court Training Center (TJCTC)** has example forms as well ([guides.sll.texas.gov](https://guides.sll.texas.gov/legal-forms/small-claims#:~:text=,Justice%20Court%20Training%20Center)). Using the official or recommended forms ensures you include all required info.  

**Filling out the Petition:** Key fields usually present: Plaintiff’s full name, address, phone; Defendant’s name (and any aliases or business names) and address; dollar amount of claim (you might put “$___, plus court costs, plus [pre-judgment interest/attorney fees] if applicable”); a short statement of claim (e.g., “Defendant failed to repay a $2,000 loan made on June 1, 2022” or “Plaintiff purchased a used car from Defendant on May 5, 2023, based on Defendant’s false representation that it had no accidents. The car was previously wrecked, making Defendant liable under the DTPA for $5,000 in diminished value.” Keep it concise but sufficient to notice what the case is about); and you’ll sign and date. There may be a jurat for a notary if needed (generally not for small claims, except if the form doubles as a verification for a debt claim). If you are filing as an assignee or debt collector, you might choose the “Debt Claim” petition variant which requires a verification. If you accidentally file a small claims pet. for a debt claim, no big worry – the court might ask for the extra info later.  

**After filing:** The court will assign a cause number and issue the citation. Ensure you **pay the service fee** or the clerk won’t send it out. If e-filing, you’ll pay all fees online and the clerk will arrange service according to your request (you can often select “Service by Constable” as a filing option, which adds the fee). Keep a copy of everything.  

**Forms the Defendant receives:** The defendant will be served with the citation and a copy of your **petition** (and any attachments you included). They also might get a blank **Answers** form – some JPs include an “Answer” form for pro se defendants to fill and return. By rule, an answer can be just an informal letter denying liability. But some courts provide an answer template to simplify it. A common form is an **“Answer to Small Claims Suit”** where the defendant can tick “I deny the claim” and list any defenses or counterclaims. This isn’t mandatory, but helpful. If you’re the plaintiff, be aware the defendant might use that form to respond.  

In conclusion, for a Texas small claim you’ll need: **(1)** the Petition (with relevant details), **(2)** the Case Info Sheet, **(3)** any special verification/affidavit if required by your case type (like debt cases’ sworn statement or military affidavit later for default), and **(4)** a method to get the citation served (the clerk handles citation issuance). Use the official forms to ensure you meet all requirements. They are designed to be straightforward – Texas wants these cases accessible without lawyers. If in doubt, ask the JP clerk for the form – they can’t give legal advice, but they will usually hand you the needed paperwork to fill out.  

## 8. Service of process  
After filing your claim, you must properly **serve** the defendant with notice of the lawsuit. “Service of process” ensures the defendant receives the citation (summons) and a copy of your petition, so the court can exercise jurisdiction. Texas rules for justice courts mirror standard civil procedure: 

- **Who can serve:** Typically, the county **constable or sheriff** will serve the citation (this is common and convenient). By rule, any sheriff, constable, or their deputy may serve papers statewide ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=)). Alternatively, a court-authorized **private process server** (certified under Supreme Court order) can serve ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=)). Also, the **court clerk** is allowed to serve by **registered or certified mail** if requested ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,order%20of%20the%20Supreme%20Court)). One thing: you (as a party) or anyone with an interest in the case **cannot** serve the papers yourself ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20Who%20May%20Serve,citations%20may%20be%20served%20by)). In fact, no party to the suit can serve, and no one “interested” (e.g., your close relative who might benefit) can either ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20Who%20May%20Serve,citations%20may%20be%20served%20by)). So use an impartial authorized person. If a process server (private) is used, they must either be a certified server or get a written court order authorizing them in that case ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20Who%20May%20Serve,citations%20may%20be%20served%20by)) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,order%20of%20the%20Supreme%20Court)). In practice, Texas has a list of certified civil process servers – you can hire one without needing a specific motion if they’re already certified.  

- **Methods of service:** The primary method is **personal delivery** to the defendant – handing a copy of the citation with petition attached in person ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,years%20of%20age%20or%20older)). This can be at their home, workplace, or anywhere they can be found. If the defendant is a company, serving an officer, managing agent, or registered agent is personal service on the company. Texas also allows service by **registered or certified mail (restricted delivery, return receipt requested)** ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=defendant%20by%20registered%20or%20certified,or%20electronic%20return%20receipt%20requested)). This is often done by the clerk if you opt for it. The mail must be sent “restricted delivery” meaning only the addressee can sign for it, and we need a signed green card or electronic receipt showing they received it ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=defendant%20by%20registered%20or%20certified,or%20electronic%20return%20receipt%20requested)). If you get the receipt signed by the defendant, that counts as service on the date they signed. If someone else signs or it’s unclaimed, that’s not good service and you’ll need another method ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=defendant%20by%20registered%20or%20certified,or%20electronic%20return%20receipt%20requested)). There is also provision for **alternative service** if standard attempts fail: you (or the constable) can motion for **substituted service** under Rule 501.2(e) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28e%29%20Alternative%20Service%20of%20Citation,This%20request%20must%20include)). You must show by affidavit that normal service wasn’t successful and detail efforts made. The judge can then authorize an alternative like **leaving the papers with someone over 16 at the location (e.g., with a roommate or receptionist) and also mailing a copy**, or **posting it to the door and mailing** ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28e%29%20Alternative%20Service%20of%20Citation,This%20request%20must%20include)) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=alternative%20service%3A)) – basically whatever method is likely to give actual notice. For example, if the defendant is dodging service, a judge might allow taping the citation to their front door and mailing a copy first class – then if that’s done, service is considered valid ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=alternative%20service%3A)). There’s even a possibility in hard cases to allow **service by publication** (printing notice in a newspaper) – JPs can do this for serving by publication if defendant’s address is truly unknown and no other way works. Rule 501.2(f) covers proof of publication method ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28f%29%20Alternative%20Service,manner%20ordered%20by%20the%20court)). Publication is rarely needed in small claims unless defendant vanished without a trace.  

- **Timing of service:** There is no set number of days before hearing to serve because in JP court, the hearing isn’t scheduled until after service generally. But once you file, you should promptly pursue service. Technically, a citation in Texas state courts remains valid for service until it’s served or returned – there’s no expiration in justice court rules (unlike federal or some state rules with 90-day limits). However, as noted in Limitations, if you delay too long after filing, it could jeopardize tolling. Aim to get defendant served within a few weeks of filing. The defendant’s **answer deadline** will be in the citation – for JPs, it’s by the end of the 14th day after service (if that day falls on a weekend/holiday, it extends to next business day) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=attorney%20must%20file%20an%20answer,a%20Saturday%2C%20Sunday%2C%20or%20legal)) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=answer%20is%20due%20by%20the,the%20citation%20and%20petition%2C%20but)). The citation will state that date. So service should ideally occur at least 14+ days before any scheduled trial so they have time to answer. JPs typically won’t set a trial until after the answer is due or received.  

- **Return of service:** Whoever serves must file a **Return of Service** with the court – a document or officer’s certificate showing how and when service was done ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20Endorsement%3B%20Execution%3B%20Return,whom%20process%20is%20delivered%20must)) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=match%20at%20L522%20a%20citation,in%20substantially%20the%20following%20form)). For a constable or sheriff, they’ll file a signed return (often electronically). For a private server, they must either get the return notarized or include a perjury statement per Rule 501.3 ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=match%20at%20L522%20a%20citation,in%20substantially%20the%20following%20form)). The return states date/time received the papers, date/time served, method (in person, by mail, etc.), name of person served (or if alt service, how executed), and it must be filed with the court before a default can be granted ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28b%29%20Contents%20of%20Return,must%20include%20the%20following%20information)) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=a%20citation%20must%20sign%20the,in%20substantially%20the%20following%20form)). If served by certified mail by the clerk, the signed green card (or electronic delivery confirmation) is attached to the return ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=match%20at%20L513%20mail%20as,receipt%20with%20the%20addressee%E2%80%99s%20signature)). The return essentially proves service happened. It should be on file at least a few days before any default judgment – Texas requires in district courts that a return be on file 10 days before default, but in justice court there’s not a strict 10-day rule explicitly, though many JPs follow it as a best practice.  

- **If defendant can’t be found:** After multiple attempts at known addresses, you’d move for **alternative service** as discussed. Provide an affidavit to the JP describing attempts (dates/times knocked, etc.) – a process server or constable typically prepares this. The judge can then sign an order for substituted service (often left with someone or posted). If even that fails and you truly have no idea where they are, you might try service by publication (talk to the JP – they can guide how to publish in an approved newspaper). Publication is a last resort; it gives very flimsy notice, so use only if necessary. With publication, the answer due date is extended: defendant gets 42 days from publication to answer ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,the%2042nd%20day%20after%20the)).  

- **Serving businesses and out-of-state defendants:** For a Texas corporation/LLC, serve its registered agent (listed with the Secretary of State). If that fails (agent can’t be found), Texas allows serving the Secretary of State as agent for the company (with a fee and process – usually done in higher courts, JPs also allow it). For an out-of-state company that does business here without a registered agent, the Texas SOS can often be served as agent under long-arm statutes (Tex. Civ. Rem. Code §17.044). The JP court will forward the citation to SOS, SOS then forwards to the company’s address on file. This is complex – might consult the clerk or a lawyer. But small claims can involve out-of-state defendants via long-arm if the transaction ties to Texas. For out-of-state individuals, any authorized Texas process server can serve them in another state (if allowed by that state’s law). You might need to hire a process server in that state; Texas law allows service outside state the same as inside if jurisdiction is established. Certified mail return receipt across state lines is also an option. If the person is truly abroad or location unknown, again, possibly service by publication (publication out of state or via social media with court permission – some creative methods have been allowed in Texas if reasonably calculated to notify).  

- **Acceptance or waiver:** A defendant can voluntarily accept service by signing a notarized **Waiver of Service or Acceptance**. If the defendant is cooperative, you can have them sign an acknowledgment that they received the petition on X date – file that, and that counts as service. Then no need for formal service by officer. Acceptance must be in writing, acknowledge receipt of petition, dated after suit was filed (cannot pre-waive before suit is even filed). This is rare in contested matters but sometimes with friends/family disputes they’ll accept to avoid fees.  

- **Difficulty scenarios:** If the defendant is evasive, a private process server often has more flexibility in times/hours to catch them. If they live in a gated community or secure apartment, the constable might not get access – a motion for substituted service to leave with concierge or tape on door might be needed. If defendant is in **the military** deployed – active duty service members are protected from default judgments. You can still serve them (if you have an address or via military channels), but if they don’t answer and they’re active, the court will require you to follow SCRA steps (appoint attorney, etc.) or wait. Sometimes better to postpone suing active duty until they’re back or at least notify their military legal office. If the defendant is a **minor or legally incompetent**, you must serve their parent, guardian, or someone appropriate – Texas requires minors be served via their parent or guardian (or if none, an adult in their household or a guardian ad litem may need appointment).  

- **Proof issues:** If service by mail comes back “unclaimed” or “refused,” that’s not good service. “Refused” (signed refusal) might allow one re-mailing by regular mail – sometimes courts accept that trifecta: certified refused + regular mail not returned, as evidence of receipt. But strictly, the safest is to then do personal service or substituted personal service.  

- **Service fees & costs recovery:** As noted, you pay service fees upfront. If you win, those fees are added to your judgment as court costs ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=match%20at%20L1169%20%28d%29%20Costs,law%20to%20the%20successful%20party)). If you had to do alternate service or hire a private server at higher cost, you can likely recover a reasonable amount (the court typically just includes the standard cost – but you can ask for the actual amount spent on special process as part of costs). Keep receipts/invoices from servers for documentation.  

In sum, Texas demands proper service on the defendant unless they waive it. Use constables for convenience – they serve thousands of JP cases and will file the return. Provide a good address. If you’re unsure of defendant’s current address, do some homework *before* filing: lookup on databases, ask acquaintances, get their work address. You can also use the **Secretary of State address search for entities** or assumed name records. The justice system expects you to exercise due diligence in locating the defendant for service. If you cannot find them at all, consider whether to file yet or try finding an address first (maybe hire a skip-tracer). Once served, the defendant is officially brought into the case and the clock for their response starts. Proper service is crucial – a judgment is invalid if service was improper. So follow Rule 501.2 closely ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20Who%20May%20Serve,citations%20may%20be%20served%20by)) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,order%20of%20the%20Supreme%20Court)). The JP clerk can answer procedural questions about service methods available but not advise which to choose. Generally, send a constable – they’ll make several attempts at varying times. If unsuccessful, go back to court for alternate service permission ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28e%29%20Alternative%20Service%20of%20Citation,This%20request%20must%20include)). With persistence, almost all defendants can be served one way or another.  

## 9. Defendant’s response and default  
After being served, the defendant has the opportunity (and obligation) to respond. Let’s break down what happens after service:  

- **Answer deadline:** In Texas justice courts, the defendant must file a written **answer** by the **end of the 14th day** after the date of service ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=attorney%20must%20file%20an%20answer,a%20Saturday%2C%20Sunday%2C%20or%20legal)). If the 14th day falls on a Saturday, Sunday, or legal holiday, the deadline extends to the next business day ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=attorney%20must%20file%20an%20answer,a%20Saturday%2C%20Sunday%2C%20or%20legal)). For example, if served on March 1, the answer is due by March 15; if that is a Sunday, then by March 16. The citation served on the defendant will explicitly state this deadline (“You must file an answer by the end of the 14th day after service” in bold). The answer can be a very simple statement – Texas rules don’t require a formal, verified answer in small claims (unless the defendant is raising certain defenses like usury or wants to avoid default without a general denial). **Most defendants just file a general denial**, either by letter or using an answer form. For instance: “The defendant John Doe denies the claims in Plaintiff’s petition.” That’s sufficient to avoid default and force the plaintiff to prove their case. The answer should include the case number, names of parties, and defendant’s mailing address, phone, etc. Many JP courts have a fill-in answer form as mentioned. Defendants can also include any affirmative defenses in the answer (like statute of limitations, payment, offset, etc.), though in JP court it’s not strictly required to plead all defenses – but it’s wise to mention key ones because the plaintiff might otherwise be unaware. Affirmative defenses (like limitations, res judicata, discharge in bankruptcy, etc.) should ideally be raised in the answer so the court can address them and the plaintiff is not ambushed at trial. 

- **How to file an answer:** The defendant can file in person at the JP court, or by mail, or even fax or email in some courts (some JPs allow email/fax for answers from pro se, but one should call to verify). If the plaintiff e-filed, the defendant can also e-file an answer through eFileTexas (self-represented defendants are encouraged but not mandated to e-file). There’s no filing fee for an answer. If multiple defendants, each should file their own answer (or one attorney can answer for all). If a defendant fails to sign their answer or it’s slightly defective, the court likely will still treat it as an appearance if intentions are clear. 

- **Consequences of no answer:** If the 14-day deadline passes with no answer, the defendant is in default and the plaintiff can move for a **default judgment**. The court will first check that service was proper and return on file ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20Default%20Judgment,judgment%20in%20the%20following%20manner)). If yes, and answer time lapsed, the judge may schedule a default hearing or simply enter judgment if the evidence of damages is straightforward (see below). Some JPs require the plaintiff to appear at a prove-up hearing, others will sign a default judgment by affidavit or petition if the claim is liquidated (certain contracts, sworn accounts). Rule 503.1 says if no answer, judge must ensure service was proper and then render default as appropriate ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20Default%20Judgment,judgment%20in%20the%20following%20manner)). For a **debt or contract with a written, signed document** filed and verified, the judge can give default for that amount without a hearing ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,plaintiff%20is%20entitled%2C%20if%20any)). For other cases (like tort or unliquidated damages), a hearing is required where you present proof of damages ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=plaintiff%20is%20entitled%2C%20if%20any)). At default, the defendant loses right to contest liability but can still appear at the prove-up to contest damages (though many defaulting defendants simply don’t show at all). 

- **Setting aside default:** If a default judgment is entered, the defendant has options to undo it: They can file a **motion to set aside default judgment (motion for new trial)** within **14 days** of judgment (JP’s timeline for new trial is 14 days after judgment signing, extendable to 21 if judge hasn’t acted – matching Rule 505.3) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=RULE%20506)) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20How%20Taken%3B%20Time,trial%2C%20if%20any%2C%20is%20denied)). If they show a good reason (e.g., they weren’t served, or had accident preventing answer, etc.), JP judges often will grant a new trial because they prefer cases decided on merits. Alternatively, the defendant can file an **appeal** to county court within **21 days** of judgment ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20How%20Taken%3B%20Time,trial%2C%20if%20any%2C%20is%20denied)) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=Costs%20with%20the%20justice%20court,trial%2C%20if%20any%2C%20is%20denied)) (more on appeals in section 11). An appeal to county court essentially vacates the JP judgment and results in a brand new trial (“trial de novo”) in the higher court, effectively giving the defendant another chance. So even if defaulted, defendants have that backup if timely. If they miss even the appeal deadline, the default stands unless some extraordinary equitable relief (bill of review in county court) is pursued, which is rare and difficult (must show no fault on their part and a meritorious defense etc.). So, as a plaintiff, a default judgment is great but not guaranteed final until 21 days pass with no appeal or motion. As a defendant, don’t rely on these – it’s much better to answer on time and fight in JP. 

- **Appearance without answer:** If a defendant shows up at trial without having filed a written answer, Texas rules state the act of showing up constitutes a general appearance, and the judge can allow them to answer orally on the spot. Typically, JP judges are lenient – if the defendant comes to court on the trial date (perhaps they got notice of hearing), the judge usually won’t hammer them for not filing answer; they’ll say “I’ll note you’re contesting the claim” and proceed. However, if defendant doesn’t file and doesn’t show, then default. Some JPs schedule an early hearing or docket call even if no answer, as a courtesy second chance. But not required. 

- **Default hearing/evidence:** For liquidated claims (exact sum determinable by writing or formula, like an invoice or promissory note with interest), the plaintiff can get default for that sum plus costs without oral testimony ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,plaintiff%20is%20entitled%2C%20if%20any)) (provided they filed the necessary proof, e.g., a sworn account or verified petition). For unliquidated (like pain & suffering, property damage requiring valuation), the judge will typically hold a **prove-up hearing** where plaintiff must testify or present affidavits of damages. Some JP courts allow submission of a sworn statement of damages instead of a hearing if the defendant’s liability is clear (like in a no-answer car accident case, a plaintiff might file an affidavit with repair bills). Check with the clerk – often they’ll set a default hearing date. At the hearing (which defendant likely won’t attend), the judge swears you in, you briefly state facts and prove how you got the number in damages. The judge may ask questions. Then judge decides. If you claim an excessive or unsupported amount, judge might only grant what’s supported. Always include court costs in your ask – but the clerk usually will add them by default. Include pre-judgment interest if applicable (especially in contract claims it should be included). And if attorney fees are allowed (and you actually incurred them), prove up the fee with testimony or affidavit from your attorney about hours and reasonableness.  

- **Setting trial if answer filed:** If the defendant answers, the case will be set for trial (or sometimes for a pre-trial mediation or hearing). Justice Courts often send both parties a letter or notice with a trial date and time. The timeframe can vary – many JPs schedule small claims trials within 1–3 months of answer. Some busy courts might be longer. If you don’t get a setting in a reasonable time, you can call the clerk to inquire. There isn’t formal discovery in JP (unless judge permits it), so usually the next step after answer is trial. 

- **Counterclaims & plaintiff’s reply:** If the defendant filed a counterclaim with their answer, you as plaintiff need to *answer that counterclaim* by the Monday following 14 days after you were served with it (similar 14-day rule applies to counter-defendant). Often they’ll just serve it by mail. You should file a written answer to counterclaim (even a general denial). If you don’t, you could theoretically have a default on the counterclaim! So don’t ignore that if it occurs. Most plaintiffs will answer with “Plaintiff denies the counterclaims” – that suffices. That answer deadline may or may not be before the trial; just do it promptly to be safe. 

- **Pre-trial motions:** It’s rare in small claims, but defendants (or plaintiffs) can file motions such as a motion to transfer venue (as discussed in Section 3) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28d%29%20Motion%20to%20Transfer%20Venue,sworn%20statement%20that%20the%20venue)) or a motion to dismiss (for example, if they think jurisdiction or limitations bars the claim). JPs can consider such motions. These typically should be filed by the answer deadline or at least before trial. Texas doesn’t have a formal motion to dismiss for failure to state a claim at JP level like higher courts, but one could argue lack of jurisdiction or that claim is barred by law. The JP might treat it as an affirmative defense to handle at trial rather than separate hearing. Also, if the defendant is in bankruptcy, they or you should notify the JP immediately – the case will be put on hold (automatic stay). 

- **No answer but partial contact:** Sometimes a defendant might call the court or send a letter to plaintiff trying to settle but never formally answers. A phone call to the court is not an answer – it must be written. The court likely tells them “file an answer letter.” If they don’t, default can proceed. Some JPs if the defendant calls and indicates intent to defend will not do default without checking – but that’s inconsistent. Legally, without a written answer, they can default them. So defendants should always submit that piece of paper on time, even if they plan to talk to plaintiff for settlement.  

- **Plaintiff no-show / plaintiff default (dismissal):** The burden is on the plaintiff to pursue the case. If the plaintiff doesn’t show up at trial (or a default hearing), the judge may **dismiss the case for want of prosecution**. This is effectively a plaintiff default. It might be without prejudice usually. If plaintiff had a good reason, they can file a motion to reinstate or new trial within 14 days of dismissal. If plaintiff doesn’t respond at all (like after filing they disappear), the court might eventually do a DWOP after a period.  

- **Rescheduling and extensions:** If a defendant needs more time to answer (rare, since it’s only 14 days), they can try to ask the court – but typically you just must meet that. However, JPs often allow slightly late answers if default judgment hasn’t been granted yet. If a default hearing is scheduled and defendant comes and asks to answer late, many JPs will allow it and proceed to trial (preferring merits). The rules allow judge to extend answer time for good cause. Similarly, for any scheduled trial, if a party can’t attend that day, they must file a **motion for continuance** as early as possible stating reasons (e.g., scheduled surgery, out-of-town work, etc.). JPs are fairly lenient on one continuance if a party has a valid conflict, especially first request. Not guaranteed though – each JP has discretion. 

In summary, once served, the defendant should *file an answer in writing within 14 days* to avoid default ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=attorney%20must%20file%20an%20answer,a%20Saturday%2C%20Sunday%2C%20or%20legal)). If they don’t, the plaintiff can move for a default judgment after confirming proper service and providing proof of claims ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20Default%20Judgment,judgment%20in%20the%20following%20manner)) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,plaintiff%20is%20entitled%2C%20if%20any)). Defaults can be reversed by prompt action (new trial motion or appeal) but it’s best to avoid by answering. Plaintiffs should diarize the answer due date – on day 15 with no answer, you can contact the court to request default steps. If the defendant does answer, prepare for trial. At that point, the case moves to resolution on the merits rather than procedural default.  

## 10. Hearing procedure  
A small claims **hearing** (trial) in Texas is relatively informal and streamlined. Here’s what to expect and how to prepare:  

- **Type of trial – bench or jury:** By default, your case will be a **bench trial**, meaning the Justice of the Peace (judge) will hear the evidence and decide the case (no jury) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=match%20at%20L1078%20,the%20case%20without%20a%20jury)). However, either party has the right to request a **jury trial**. To get a jury, a party must file a **written jury demand** and pay the $22 jury fee **no later than 14 days before the trial date** ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=match%20at%20L1064%20%28a%29%20Demand,the%20judge%20for%20good%20cause)) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28b%29%20Jury%20Fee,written%20request%20for%20a%20jury)). If a timely jury demand is made, the case will be heard by a jury of six citizens ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28f%29%20The%20Jury,jury%20to%20try%20the%20case)) (with a few alternates possible) selected from the precinct’s jury pool, and the JP will preside over jury selection and trial. In practice, most small claims are bench trials – juries are uncommon because of the added time and formality (and many defendants or plaintiffs don’t want to pay the fee or bother with it). If no one requests a jury by the deadline, the right is waived ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28d%29%20No%20Demand,the%20case%20without%20a%20jury)) and the judge alone will decide ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=match%20at%20L1078%20,the%20case%20without%20a%20jury)). 

- **Scheduling and notice:** The court will send both parties a **trial setting** notice indicating the date, time, and location of the trial. Justice courts often schedule multiple small claims on the same morning or afternoon docket. Be sure to **arrive on time**, typically at least 15 minutes early to check in. If you cannot attend on that date, file a motion for continuance well in advance explaining why (as mentioned in section 9). If you just don’t show, plaintiff risks dismissal, defendant risks default. 

- **Pre-trial exchange and discovery:** Unlike higher courts, formal **discovery (depositions, interrogatories)** is generally **not permitted** in justice court without leave of the judge. Rule 500.9 prohibits discovery unless the judge allows it on motion and for good cause. In most small claims, neither side will conduct depositions or exchange written discovery requests – it’s supposed to be simple. That said, you should **informally exchange information** if possible. Some JPs encourage parties to voluntarily show each other key documents to avoid surprises at trial. It’s not required by law, but it’s fair and often helpful (plus if something is truly surprising at trial, judge might give a recess or continuance to the other side to review it). Usually, each side just shows up with their evidence. If you worry the other side has a document needed for your case that they won’t voluntarily share (like an invoice or contract copy you lost), you can petition the court for a subpoena (more below) or possibly a judge-approved discovery request. But in practice, you often don’t get discovery in JP. 

- **Evidence to bring:** Prepare **all evidence** that supports your case or defense. This typically includes: **Contracts, receipts, invoices, canceled checks, emails or texts** relevant to the deal or dispute, **photographs** (printed out ideally) of any damage or condition, **repair estimates or bills**, etc. For any text messages or emails, print them out in a clear format (with dates and parties identified). If you have recordings (voicemails, etc.), have a way to play them (some courts allow a phone or bring a laptop, but check – some may require you provide a CD/USB). **Bring copies** of any document for the judge and the other side. Many JPs will accept into evidence a single copy, but it’s courteous to have at least 2 copies (one for judge, one for opponent). Mark your exhibits with numbers (Plaintiff’s Exhibit 1, 2, ... or Defendant’s A, B, ... if you want) to keep organized. The judge may or may not formally mark them – often in informal trials they just look at them and hand back or keep them in the file. But it helps you to label so you can refer: “As you can see on Exhibit 3, the text where he says he’ll refund me...”. **Witnesses:** If there are witnesses who saw or heard important things, bring them to court. First-hand witnesses are powerful – e.g., a friend who saw the transaction or damage occur can corroborate your story. If a witness is unwilling to attend, you can ask the court to issue a **subpoena** to compel them (Tex. R. Civ. Proc. 500.8 allows subpoenas in JP). Go to clerk at least a couple weeks before trial and ask for a **subpoena form**. Fill in the witness’s name and address and what they need to bring (if anything). Pay the subpoena issuance fee (usually ~$10) and a fee for service (like a service fee to deliver the subpoena). The law also requires you to tender the witness a fee of $10 for each day of attendance plus mileage (which is often negligible) ([tcss.legis.texas.gov](https://tcss.legis.texas.gov/resources/CP/htm/CP.22.htm#:~:text=B,entitled%20to%2010%20dollars%20for)). If you didn’t subpoena someone and they don’t show, you can’t force a delay to get them later (unless truly critical and judge is merciful). So anticipate who you need. Some typical witnesses: an auto mechanic who wrote your repair estimate (they can explain damage cause), a neighbor who overheard a conversation or saw the condition of property, etc. Professional witnesses can testify by affidavit in higher courts (e.g., medical bills aff). JPs might accept a written statement from a witness if both sides agree or if not objected to – but technically that’s hearsay if the other side objects. It’s best to get the witness in person or at least on phone (some JPs allow telephonic testimony if arranged). If a key witness is in another city and can’t appear, you could attempt to get a **deposition** (rare in JP, needs motion) or an affidavit and hope the opponent doesn’t object. But be aware that unsworn letters or statements can be excluded if objected to. If the witness can’t come, consider asking them to do a **notarized affidavit** detailing what they know. Under Tex. Govt Code §22.001, unsworn declarations can sometimes substitute for an affidavit if they follow proper format. It’s not as good as live testimony but better than nothing. The judge may allow it if the other side is absent or doesn’t contest it.  

- **Language and interpreters:** If you or your witness aren’t fluent in English, notify the court in advance and request an **interpreter**. Texas courts will provide foreign language interpreters when needed (the county may have a contract interpreter or use a phone service). There usually isn’t a fee to the parties in small claims for an interpreter – some counties cover it, some might ask you to bring one if it’s a less common language. By law, courts must provide language access for litigants. For deaf or hearing-impaired parties, the court will provide a sign language interpreter as required by law. Always request as early as possible (some JP websites have an Interpreter Request form). If an interpreter isn’t available that day, the judge might reschedule. Do NOT rely on children or friends to interpret official proceedings – courts prefer certified interpreters because nuance can matter.  

- **Courtroom setup:** Justice courtrooms are small and informal. Dress neatly (business casual is fine; no shorts, tank tops, hats). Go through security if any. When your case is called, you’ll stand at a podium or table facing the judge. There’s no court reporter typically (unless someone hires one at their own expense, which in small claims rarely happens). Proceedings may or may not be recorded – some JPs make an audio recording (especially if a party appeals, they’ll forward it to county court for reference on what happened, though county hears de novo so not needed). In any event, there’s often no verbatim transcript unless you arrange it, so be clear in your speech for the judge’s notes.  

- **Order of trial:** The **plaintiff** goes first since they bear the burden of proof. In a bench trial, the judge often guides the process: e.g., “Plaintiff, tell me what happened.” You (plaintiff) would present an opening summary, then dive into evidence: testify under oath to the facts, show documents to the judge (and hand to defendant to review), call any witness you have one by one. The judge may interject with questions anytime (JP trials are not strict about formal objections or procedure – the judge often asks parties directly for clarification). After plaintiff is done, the **defendant** can question the plaintiff’s witnesses (cross-examination). However, many self-represented litigants don’t do formal cross – the judge might instead ask, “Defendant, what is your response to that?” But you do have the right to ask questions of the other side or their witnesses. Keep questions relevant and not argumentative. Next, the **defendant** presents their side: any affirmative defenses or why they dispute the claim. They can testify, introduce their exhibits, and call their witnesses. Plaintiff can cross-examine the defendant and their witnesses too. The judge may again ask clarifying questions. Both sides should **address the judge**, not each other (don’t get into a shouting match; speak to the judge or through the judge for questions – “Your honor, may I ask him: ...”). The atmosphere is informal but respectful. Use proper titles (Mr./Ms. or first names if comfortable; call the judge “Judge” or “Your Honor”).  

- **Evidence and objections:** Texas Rules of Evidence apply loosely in JP. Officially, Rule 500.3(e) says the judge *shall admit evidence that “has relevance and bears on the credibility of a witness or the weight of evidence”*. It also says the judge “shall exclude evidence that is irrelevant, unreliable, or inflammatory” * ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=match%20at%20L1060%20RULE%20504,JURY))*. So JPs have broad discretion to consider what they think is fair and relevant. Formal objections (hearsay, etc.) can be raised, and the judge will rule, but JPs tend to be more relaxed. For example, hearsay letters might be allowed if no objection or if it seems trustworthy enough (or if it’s the only evidence on a minor point). But if you know evidence law, you can object to hearsay, irrelevant stuff, etc. The judge might allow it “for what it’s worth” or might sustain and not rely on it. For key documents, try to have the person who created or is custodian of them present to testify to authenticity if authenticity could be questioned. Often, both sides just want the judge to see everything, and neither objects. Judges are pretty good at filtering out B.S. Even if inadmissible evidence is let in, in a bench trial the judge likely won’t give it weight if not credible. So, don’t be overly worried about strict evidentiary rules, but know the basics so you can object if something really unfair is introduced (like defendant tries to submit a random letter from someone not present claiming something false – you can say “Objection, hearsay – the author isn’t here to be questioned.” The judge may agree and disregard it).  

- **Burden of proof:** The plaintiff carries the burden of proving their case by **“preponderance of the evidence”** – meaning more likely true than not (over 50%). For any defenses like limitations or payment, the defendant who asserts them bears burden to prove those. In practical terms, explain your story clearly and provide enough credible evidence to tilt the scales in your favor. If it’s just your word vs. the other’s, the judge will assess credibility (backing your testimony with documents or a neutral witness helps). If you have the burden on an issue, make sure to address it thoroughly; don’t assume the judge knows the law or will fill gaps – present all elements of your claim. For instance, if suing for breach of contract, show the contract existed, defendant’s breach, and the damages that resulted. If defendant asserts “I already paid,” then defendant should show proof of payment (like receipts). If they have no proof and you deny receiving payment, judge likely sides with you absent evidence.  

- **Conduct at trial:** Be polite and **don’t interrupt**. Let the other side finish speaking before you respond. The judge in small claims often lets people talk in a somewhat narrative form, but if two start bickering, the judge will intervene. Use a calm, respectful tone. Even if the other side lies, note the lie and respond when it’s your turn rather than exploding. You’ll get your chance to counter their points. 

- **Length of trial:** Most small claims trials are brief – often **15–30 minutes per side** at most, sometimes less if straightforward. Complex ones might go an hour or more, but JP dockets are busy; the judge will keep things moving. Be concise – focus on the key points and evidence. If you have many documents, organize and highlight the crucial parts; don’t spend time on trivial stuff.  

- **Witness coordination:** If you have witnesses, make sure they know when and where to show up. It’s smart to have them arrive at the same time as you – not much earlier because they often have to wait outside the courtroom until called (especially if one party invokes “the rule” – i.e., excluding witnesses from hearing others’ testimony, which in JP you can request). If there's a chance of needing witness separation, ask the judge to “invoke the rule” at the start (Tex. R. Evid. 614) – then witnesses not currently testifying typically wait in hall so they aren’t influenced by others’ statements. In informal JP cases, this is rarely invoked unless one side asks. If not invoked, witnesses can sit in courtroom from start (most small claims are open to public anyway). But if you prefer your witness not hear you or other witness to ensure pure testimony, you can request it.  

- **Presentation of damages:** If you’re claiming a specific dollar amount, be ready to explain how you calculated it and to produce documentation. For example, if suing for $5,000 in car repairs, have the repair bill or estimate. If claiming lost wages (say you had to miss work due to defendant’s breach), bring pay stubs or employer letter showing your rate and days missed. Judges won’t award speculative damages – have something tangible. If claiming interest, show contract interest rate or statutory basis (some plaintiffs provide a interest calculation sheet). If you want court costs and allowed attorney fees, mention them at the end so the judgment includes them (though typically the clerk adds costs automatically, and the judge awards attorney fees if proven reasonable).  

- **No attorneys / attorneys:** Parties can choose to be represented by lawyers in small claims (Texas allows lawyers in JP court – unlike some states that ban them). If one side has a lawyer and the other doesn’t, the case might feel a bit more formal because the lawyer might raise objections and follow procedure. The JP will still try to keep it understandable. Don’t be intimidated if the other side has counsel; just focus on telling truth and presenting evidence – the facts often carry the day. Conversely, if you can afford a lawyer for a tricky case, you’re allowed to hire one. Businesses can also be represented by an employee or agent (non-lawyer) in JP ([www.stcl.edu](https://www.stcl.edu/lib/TexasRulesProject/TRCP474-522/rule500-42013.html#:~:text=,be%20represented%20by%20an%20attorney)), but they also can hire counsel.  

- **After both sides:** Once both plaintiff and defendant have presented, the judge might allow brief **closing statements** – basically a summary of why you think you should win. In many JP trials, after evidence the judge just asks a few final questions and doesn’t need formal closings. But you can politely ask, “Your Honor, may I summarize our position?” if you feel it will help. Keep it short: restate how the evidence met each element of your claim and overcame any defenses. If you’re defendant, highlight any failure of plaintiff to prove a necessary point or emphasize your strong defense evidence.  

- **Judge’s decision:** The JP judge may announce a decision **immediately at the trial’s end** – “I rule in favor of the plaintiff for $___” or “I believe the defendant is not liable, case dismissed.” Sometimes, the judge will “take it under advisement,” meaning they want to think it over or review evidence quietly, and they’ll mail out a judgment later (or email). Many JPs decide on the spot for simple cases; for more complex, they might take a day or week. By law, the judgment should be rendered “promptly.” If they don’t announce in court, you’ll receive the **Judgment** in the mail. The judgment will state who wins, how much money (if any) is awarded, plus court costs and interest. If any counterclaims were involved, it will address those too (e.g., netting the amounts). The judgment is then final unless appealed within the allowed time (or unless a motion to reconsider/new trial is filed timely).  

- **Courtroom etiquette tips:** Address the judge as “Judge” or “Your Honor.” Stand when speaking if the judge or bailiff asks you to (some JPs are fine with sitting). Do not interrupt the judge or the other party. If you need to hand something to the judge, ask “May I approach to deliver this exhibit?” The judge usually says yes. If the judge starts to deliver a ruling, don’t argue or interrupt. If you vehemently disagree, your remedy is appeal, not yelling at the judge. Thank the judge at the end, regardless of outcome. Remember, the judge may handle future matters for you (like enforcement steps), so remain respectful.  

- **Burden of proof recap:** “Preponderance of evidence” means whoever has the more convincing evidence wins. If it’s *truly* 50/50, the party with burden loses. So as plaintiff, aim to leave no element uncertain and make your story clearly more believable than the defendant’s. Use chronology, facts, and evidence logically. Judges often follow common sense – if your narrative aligns with logic and evidence, and the defendant’s seems dodgy or unsupported, you win.  

- **Security and safety:** If you fear confrontation with the other party, note that there are court bailiffs present typically. Parties sit apart. You generally won’t have direct contact except possibly during evidence handover. If one party gets heated, the judge or bailiff will quell it.  

In summary, the hearing is your day in court to tell your story with evidence. It’s less formal than TV court shows might suggest, but it’s still a legal proceeding. Come prepared, organized, and courteous. The JP will guide the procedure to an extent, so follow their lead. And importantly, stick to **relevant facts** – the judge is interested in what happened, not personal attacks or wild tangents. If you disagree with the verdict, remember you typically have a right to appeal to a higher court (see next section). But give it your best shot here – many cases end at JP because appeals can be more involved.  

## 11. Appeals  
If you’re unhappy with the justice court’s judgment – whether you’re the plaintiff or defendant – you generally have the right to **appeal to the county court** (or a county court at law) for a fresh trial. Key points about appealing a Texas small claims case:  

- **Who can appeal:** In small claims, **either party** (plaintiff or defendant) can appeal an adverse judgment ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20How%20Taken%3B%20Time,trial%2C%20if%20any%2C%20is%20denied)). However, **if you’re the party who initially filed the claim and you lose, you can appeal** only on the law in most states. But Texas is different – Texas allows both plaintiff or defendant to appeal for a new trial (there’s no limitation like some states that forbid the plaintiff from appealing their own claim’s loss – in Texas JP, either side can). One exception: If a plaintiff wins a judgment but is unsatisfied with the amount (perhaps you sued for $5,000, judge gave $2,000), can you appeal seeking more? Generally, yes – a plaintiff can appeal seeking the rest, because the case is essentially retried de novo (no “remittitur” needed). But note, if the other party didn’t appeal, some case law suggests a plaintiff who “won” cannot appeal to seek more unless the judgment was zero in some contexts. In practice, though, either side not fully satisfied can appeal – the new trial in county court could result in more, less, or nothing. Defendants who defaulted can also appeal to start over (the appeal perfects a new trial de novo, though if they intentionally defaulted to delay, the county court could consider sanctions, but seldom do).  

- **Deadline to appeal:** **Within 21 days** after the judgment (or denial of any timely motion to reinstate/new trial) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20How%20Taken%3B%20Time,trial%2C%20if%20any%2C%20is%20denied)) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=Costs%20with%20the%20justice%20court,trial%2C%20if%20any%2C%20is%20denied)). This is jurisdictional – you must meet it. Day 1 is the day after judgment signed. Count 21 days including weekends (if 21st day falls weekend/holiday, next business day). If a party filed a motion to set aside default or new trial within 14 days, and the judge denies it, then you have a fresh 21 days from the denial to appeal ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20How%20Taken%3B%20Time,trial%2C%20if%20any%2C%20is%20denied)). But be cautious: sometimes a new trial motion might not be expressly ruled on – in JP, if not ruled on by 21st day after filing, it’s deemed denied. So rather than complicate, just aim for the original 21-day window from judgment. If you miss the deadline, the county court cannot take the appeal (the JP judgment becomes final). There’s no mechanism to extend – one old exception is if the 21st day you attempted to file but clerk was closed unexpectedly, you might get a slight equity, but best not to risk it.  

- **How to appeal – bond or cash deposit:** To perfect an appeal, you must file an **appeal bond** with the JP court, or a **cash deposit in lieu of bond**, or an **affidavit of inability (pauper’s oath)** if you can’t afford a bond ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20How%20Taken%3B%20Time,trial%2C%20if%20any%2C%20is%20denied)) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,rendered%20against%20it%20on%20appeal)). The bond must be filed with the JP within that 21 days and approved by the JP. 

    - For a **defendant appealing** a money judgment, the bond amount must equal **twice the amount of the judgment** (to secure the plaintiff against delay) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,rendered%20against%20it%20on%20appeal)). For example, if judgment $5,000, bond needs to be $10,000. It must have at least one surety (someone other than you, often an insurance company or surety company, or a personal surety with property) and be approved by the JP as solvent. Instead of a surety bond, a defendant can deposit **cash in the amount of twice the judgment** with the court in lieu of bond ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,must%20be%20conditioned%20on%20the)). The court then transmits that to the county as the deposit. A **cashier’s check** or money order often needed if doing cash deposit. 

    - For a **plaintiff appealing** (e.g., your case was dismissed or you got less than you think you should, etc.), the rules set a **fixed bond of $500** if the plaintiff is appellant ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,must%20be%20conditioned%20on%20the)). So plaintiffs have a much lower hurdle: just a $500 bond (with surety) or $500 cash deposit. This is independent of judgment size because if plaintiff lost, there’s no money judgment to double; so statute says $500 bond for plaintiff appeals ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,must%20be%20conditioned%20on%20the)). If the plaintiff won but is appealing for more, I believe $500 still covers it, though that scenario is rare. 

    - **Affidavit of Inability to Pay (Pauper’s oath):** If you cannot afford the bond or cash, you can file an affidavit of inability within the 21 days (likely similar to how you filed one to waive initial fees if you did). This triggers a possible contest by the other party. The JP may hold a hearing to see if you truly cannot afford the bond. If the JP denies your pauper status or sets a bond anyway, you must comply or appeal might fail. If the JP approves your inability affidavit (or no one contests within 5 days), the appeal proceeds without bond ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=RULE%20506)) . Note: In eviction cases, an inability affidavit requires also paying one rental period’s rent into court, but that’s eviction-specific. For small claims money cases, just the affidavit can suffice. But be honest – if you have assets or a job that could cover a bond, the other side might contest. If contested, a hearing will be held within 10 days of contest filing. The county court will eventually also look at it and can dismiss an appeal if the affidavit was false or materially insufficient. 

- **Procedure after appeal is filed:** The JP court will prepare a **transcript** of the proceedings (basically the papers: petition, answer, docket entries, judgment, any bond or affidavits) and send it to the county clerk within 15 days after you perfect the appeal (i.e., filed bond) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=match%20at%20L596%20the%20return,notice%20is%20proof%20of%20service)). The case is then docketed as a new case in the county court (or county court at law if your county has one and it has jurisdiction). You’ll get a new case number at county level. The appealing party (now called appellant) must **prosecute the appeal** – meaning follow through at county court. The county court essentially treats it as if you just filed the lawsuit there. They might require you to file a new petition or at least label who was plaintiff/defendant below (the case style often becomes “A.B., Appellant v. C.D., Appellee”). In practice, the parties often file new pleadings: the appellant can file an updated petition or answer, and the appellee can answer or re-answer. The case is tried **de novo** (from scratch) – the county judge (which may be a different judge entirely, e.g., a County Court at Law judge, who is a lawyer-judge) will not be bound by the JP’s ruling or reasoning. It’s as if the JP trial never happened, except the pleadings can carry over. You can present new evidence, new witnesses, etc. *However,* note that if the defendant defaulted in JP, they cannot suddenly introduce new counterclaims on appeal that they never raised below – generally the scope of claims stays the same or narrower, though new defenses and such are allowed (especially if not required to be pled earlier). The county court might allow amendment of pleadings, though, under normal rules.  

- **County court costs:** When the appeal is docketed, you typically have to pay the **county court filing fee** (like a normal new case – often around $300 depending on county). If you filed a pauper’s affidavit, that usually covers the county fees too (the statement of inability filed in JP acts for appeal as well – you may need to file a copy in county to get fees waived). If you don’t pay the county filing fee, the appeal could be dismissed. So check with the county clerk after appeal is filed to see if they need a fee or fee waiver form.  

- **Timeline in county court:** It may take time to schedule – the county courts are generally more formal and busier, so your case might not be heard for months. Also, if it qualifies for **mandatory arbitration or mediation** under local rules, they might send you to ADR first (some large counties require cases under certain amounts to go to non-binding arbitration or mediation). Many small claims appeals get settled or dropped because of the cost/effort of higher court. But if you go through, expect normal civil procedure: possibly exchange of discovery, adherence to rules of evidence strictly, etc. Often, if amount is small (like a few thousand), attorneys might not be involved but the environment is still more formal than JP. You can request a jury at the county level even if none was used in JP (must request within 20 days of appeal being filed in county or as per that court’s rules). If a jury wasn’t demanded in JP, either side can demand in county within proper time after docketing (and pay the jury fee, usually about $40 at county level).  

- **Effect of appeal on judgment:** Filing a proper appeal bond or affidavit **suspends the JP judgment** – meaning it’s not enforceable (no collections) while appeal is pending (the bond sort of supersedes it). If the appeal is perfected, the JP judgment will eventually be replaced by whatever the county court decides. If an appellant fails to follow through (say they drop the appeal or it’s dismissed because they didn’t show or pay fees), the county court will usually dismiss the appeal *and the JP judgment is affirmed* and becomes enforceable again. If the plaintiff was paid by the bond or deposit in the interim (some plaintiff-appellees might motion the county to let them have the deposit if appeal looks stalled), generally that doesn’t happen – the bond funds remain untouched unless final judgment says to use them to pay.  

- **No partial appeal:** You can’t appeal *just* one part of the case – an appeal brings the whole case up. So if defendant won on a counterclaim but lost on plaintiff’s claim, an appeal means both claim and counterclaim will be retried. The county court can render any outcome – including potentially one worse for the appellant than the JP judgment (called the “risk of a **turnaround**”). For example, JP might have awarded plaintiff $2,000 (instead of $5,000 asked). Plaintiff appeals seeking more, but in the new trial the county judge might find for defendant and plaintiff ends up with $0. That is allowed – it’s as if new trial. Similarly, a defendant who appeals could be hit with a judgment equal or greater than in JP (except it can’t exceed justice court jurisdiction $20k – but that’s fine because county court has even higher jurisdiction usually). So consider that risk when deciding to appeal.  

- **De novo means new evidence:** On appeal, you aren’t limited to what was said in JP. *However,* note that if you had a requirement to do something in JP and you didn’t (like you needed to verify an account or plead an affirmative defense and you didn’t), some courts might not let you “mend your hold” on appeal – but usually they do under liberal pleading rules. Practically, come with all evidence/witnesses anew. The result at county will supersede JP’s completely – after appeal, the JP judgment is irrelevant (except possibly for interest calc: sometimes if appeal is delayed, the county judgment might include interest from JP judgment date, but typically interest in small claims isn’t heavy).  

- **No appeal further from county court if under $250:** Texas Government Code §28.053(d) historically barred further appeals from a county court judgment on a de novo small claims if the amount in controversy is ≤$250 (that might be old law for old small claims). Currently (2026), for civil cases generally in county courts, you can appeal to Court of Appeals only if amount in controversy over $250. So if your case is very small, the county court’s decision might be final. But for most small claims (which are often above $250), you could – at least theoretically – appeal to the Court of Appeals on legal errors (not a new trial, but a review of whether any error of law occurred in county trial). This is rarely done for small-dollar cases due to cost of transcript, briefs, etc. But know that the de novo appeal to county is likely the last stop for most cases – you won’t likely go beyond.  

**Appeal example:** You sued for $1,200, JP judge ruled $0 (you lost). You file a $500 bond timely ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,must%20be%20conditioned%20on%20the)). JP approves bond. JP sends case up. In county court, you pay filing fee. You and defendant get trial date. You present case to new judge/jury; maybe you get $1,000 judgment this time. That’s the judgment that counts. If defendant doesn’t pay it, you can enforce via county court execution. If you lost again, you likely stop, or you could appeal to Court of Appeals if amount≥$250.  

**Key takeaways:** Appeals must be **quickly acted on** (21 days) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20How%20Taken%3B%20Time,trial%2C%20if%20any%2C%20is%20denied)) and involve getting a bond or inability affidavit in order. It’s essentially starting over in a higher court with more formality and likely more expense. Many litigants choose not to appeal small JP cases unless principle or significant money is at stake, or if they truly think JP made a clear mistake. But the right is there to ensure a second look by a law-trained judge or jury. If you are the winner in JP and the other side appeals, be prepared to fight again in county. The JP judgment won’t carry weight besides giving you a confidence boost perhaps. And note: the **burden of proof** remains the same in the new trial.  

## 12. Post-judgment collection  
Winning a judgment is often just half the battle – turning that judgment into actual money in hand is the next challenge. Texas unfortunately is known as a “debtor-friendly” state due to strong exemptions (assets a judgment creditor cannot seize) ([texaslegalguide.com](https://texaslegalguide.com/Texas_Constitution%3AArticle_XVI%2C_Section_28#:~:text=Article%20XVI%2C%20Section%2028%20of,2%29%20spousal%20maintenance)) ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/property-code/prop-sect-42-001/#:~:text=,ordered%20child%20support%20payments)). But there are still tools to collect what you’re owed. Here’s a guide to enforcing a small claims judgment in Texas:  

- **Abstract of Judgment & Judgment Lien:** Once you have a final judgment (no appeal filed and time expired, or after appeal if you prevailed), the first thing you should do is record an **Abstract of Judgment** in any county where the debtor owns or might own **non-exempt real property**. An abstract is a summary of the judgment that the county clerk can index in the real property records, creating a **judgment lien** on the debtor’s non-exempt real estate in that county ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/2005/pr/005.00.000052.00.html#:~:text=CHAPTER%2052,2005%20Texas%20Property%20Code)). To get an abstract, go to the justice court (or county court if appealed) and request it (for a small fee, maybe $5-$8). The abstract includes names of parties, date and amount of judgment, etc. Then file that abstract with the **County Clerk** in every county where the debtor has property or you think they may purchase property in the future. The clerk will index it under the debtor’s name. This lien lasts **10 years** from judgment date ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/property-code/title-8/chapter-92/subchapter-b/section-92-0563/#:~:text=,interest%20and%20costs%20of%20court)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/property-code/title-8/chapter-92/subchapter-b/section-92-0563/#:~:text=,interest%20and%20costs%20of%20court)) (assuming abstract properly recorded) and can be renewed by re-abstracting if you revive the judgment before it goes dormant at 10 years ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/property-code/title-8/chapter-92/subchapter-b/section-92-0563/#:~:text=,interest%20and%20costs%20of%20court)) ([tcss.legis.texas.gov](https://tcss.legis.texas.gov/resources/CP/htm/CP.34.htm#:~:text=C,a%20writ%20of%20execution%20is)). The lien attaches to any **non-exempt real property** the debtor owns or acquires in that county ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/property-code/title-8/chapter-92/subchapter-b/section-92-0563/#:~:text=,interest%20and%20costs%20of%20court)). In Texas, a person’s **homestead** (primary residence) is **exempt** from forced sale for debts ([texaslegalguide.com](https://texaslegalguide.com/Texas_Constitution%3AArticle_XVI%2C_Section_28#:~:text=Article%20XVI%2C%20Section%2028%20of,2%29%20spousal%20maintenance)), so the judgment lien won’t force sale of a homestead; however, it still clouds title – if the debtor ever tries to sell that house, title companies may require the judgment be paid before closing, because after sale (if it’s no longer their homestead for them) you could reach proceeds. Also, if they ever lose homestead protection (move out and not get another), the lien might then attach. But essentially, judgment liens mainly affect **non-homestead realty** (like rental properties, vacant land, second houses). If the debtor owns a rental property, your lien would prevent them from selling or refinancing it without dealing with your judgment. If they do nothing, your lien just sits until someday property is sold or debtor dies (then estate has to pay). **Abstracting costs are minimal** – definitely do it because it’s an easy step to secure your judgment.  

- **Writ of Execution:** This is the primary method to collect via seizure of the debtor’s **non-exempt personal property or non-exempt real property**. After the judgment is final (and not superseded by appeal or stay), you can apply to the court for a **Writ of Execution** (Tex. R. Civ. Proc. 621). In justice court, you request it from the JP clerk (some have a form). The fee is small (e.g., $5 issuance plus a sheriff/constable fee ~$200 to execute it). The writ directs the sheriff or constable to attempt to seize non-exempt assets of the debtor, auction them, and apply proceeds to your judgment. **What is non-exempt?** In Texas, the law exempts (protects) a lot: **homestead** (can’t seize the house the debtor lives in) ([texaslegalguide.com](https://texaslegalguide.com/Texas_Constitution%3AArticle_XVI%2C_Section_28#:~:text=Article%20XVI%2C%20Section%2028%20of,2%29%20spousal%20maintenance)); **one vehicle per licensed adult** in the household (so usually can’t take their primary car); **most household items** like furniture, clothes, tools, up to an aggregate fair market value of $100,000 for a family or $50,000 for a single adult ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/property-code/prop-sect-42-001/#:~:text=of%20not%20more%20than%20%24100%2C000%2C,charges%20encumbering%20the%20property%3B%20or)) ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/property-code/prop-sect-42-001/#:~:text=,other%20charges%20encumbering%20the%20property)) (this covers most normal personal property); **tools of trade** (equipment debtor uses in their profession); **qualified retirement accounts, pension plans, life insurance**, etc., are exempt by other laws. **Wages** for personal service are exempt from garnishment by creditors in Texas ([texaslegalguide.com](https://texaslegalguide.com/Texas_Constitution%3AArticle_XVI%2C_Section_28#:~:text=Article%20XVI%2C%20Section%2028%20of,2%29%20spousal%20maintenance)) (except child support, etc.), meaning you cannot garnish their paycheck (see next bullet). So what does that leave? Sometimes not much. **Non-exempt property** that could be seized includes: vacation homes or land (non-homestead realty), a second car or extra vehicles beyond the one exempt (like they have a motorcycle in addition to a car, possibly non-exempt if car already covered; or they have a company vehicle not personally exempt?), luxury items like a boat, ATV, expensive jewelry beyond what’s reasonably exempt (wedding rings are often exempt but not sure if fully, family heirlooms maybe limited by that $100k cap), collectibles or cash in a safe, etc. Also, if the debtor owns a small business that has equipment or inventory in their name personally, those might be reachable (though if under a separate LLC, then you have to enforce against that entity, not the individual’s judgment, unless you did a “pierce the veil” – beyond small claims scope usually). Texas also doesn’t exempt **corporate property** – if your judgment is against a company, none of these personal exemptions apply. The sheriff can seize the company’s assets (inventory, equipment) if the company doesn’t pay voluntarily. So execution is more effective against business debtors or individuals with non-exempt assets. The constable will usually coordinate with you: they often send a letter to debtor first demanding payment (some do this courtesy), then if not paid, they’ll come out and levy. You might have to point them to assets if you know of any (like “Debtor owns a bass boat stored at X address” – they could seize that if it’s not a primary vehicle). If assets are seized, they are sold at a **Sheriff’s auction** (public sale) and the proceeds (minus storage and sale costs) go toward your judgment. Often, auction prices are low (pennies on dollar), so a $5,000 boat might fetch $2,000, which after fees might give you $1,500 for your judgment. If that satisfies, great; if not, you could do another writ for more if other assets discovered (multiple executions can be issued until judgment is paid or becomes dormant). Each writ is a one-time attempt at that period. If no assets found, the officer returns the writ “nulla bona” (nothing found). You can try again later if you suspect they acquire assets.  

- **Writ of Garnishment (bank accounts):** While Texas bans wage garnishment for most debts ([texaslegalguide.com](https://texaslegalguide.com/Texas_Constitution%3AArticle_XVI%2C_Section_28#:~:text=Article%20XVI%2C%20Section%2028%20of,2%29%20spousal%20maintenance)), **bank account garnishment** is allowed. If you know or suspect the debtor’s bank or where they have accounts, you can file an **Application for Writ of Garnishment** against the bank after judgment. This is a separate action but ancillary to your judgment – you (as garnishor) sue the bank (as garnishee) claiming the bank holds funds of the debtor. The bank gets served and must freeze and answer with what funds they hold for the debtor. The debtor gets notified to possibly contest exempt funds. **Important**: Under Texas law, *if the account has any funds that are exempt (like Social Security, unemployment, veterans benefits deposited), those remain exempt even after deposit and the debtor can claim them as exempt from garnishment.* Also, banks often automatically protect certain federal benefit deposits by not releasing them if identified (federal rules require 2 months of SS funds be protected). So garnishment is effective primarily on **non-exempt funds** in accounts – e.g., regular wages that were deposited more than 2 months ago, or savings from non-exempt sources. To do this, you must apply in the court that rendered judgment (justice court can issue garnishments post-judgment, although sometimes they might say go to district court – but they should be able to since it’s enforcement of their judgment and not exceeding jurisdiction). You’ll need to pay a filing fee and likely an additional bond (garnishment requires a bond to cover wrongful garnishment risk; but post-judgment garnishment in Texas sometimes waives bond if judgment already establishes debt – check rules, but to be safe, be prepared for a small bond maybe). If granted, the clerk issues a Writ of Garnishment served on the bank. The bank will then freeze the account up to judgment amount and file an answer disclosing what’s held. Debtor can file a motion to dissolve if funds are exempt or some procedural flaw. If all goes well, after perhaps a hearing or simply after answer, the court can order the bank to pay those funds to you to satisfy judgment (or partial satisfy). Garnishment is very effective if the debtor keeps significant money in a bank and it’s not exempt money. Many debtors know to keep money out of banks or only in exempt forms (like a prepaid debit card might not be easily garnishable, or keep under 2 months of SS in account, etc.). But it’s worth a try if you have banking info.  

- **Turnover Order (Texas Civil Practice & Remedies Code §31.002):** If the debtor has non-exempt property that is difficult to get by normal means, you can ask the court for a **turnover order** requiring the debtor to turn it over to a designated sheriff or receiver. For example, say the debtor owns valuable artwork or an account receivable or owns stock in a company – these might not be straightforward to seize. A turnover order compels the debtor to hand it over or risk contempt. Justice courts historically didn’t have explicit authority to issue turnover (there was debate if it’s considered equitable relief beyond JP jurisdiction). However, some JPs do grant basic turnover under their enforcement powers. To be safe, many creditors apply in district court for turnover after getting a JP judgment. But that’s more cost. Turnover is especially useful to reach **non-exempt assets that can’t easily be levied** (e.g., funds in a secure location, or a company owes the debtor money). Additionally, the court can appoint a **receiver** to locate and liquidate assets – but that’s more common for large judgments due to fees. Unlikely for small claims unless debtor has a business.  

- **Judgment Debtor Exam (Order of Examination):** In Texas, formal “debtor’s exams” (post-judgment deposition or court questioning of debtor about assets) are allowed through TRCP 621a. You can file a Motion to Require Judgment Debtor to Appear and Answer under oath about assets. Typically, this is done by noticing a deposition or sending interrogatories under TRCP 621a (which allows post-judgment discovery same as in pretrial). Because justice courts don’t normally allow discovery pre-judgment, one question was can they for post-judgment? Actually, yes – once you have a judgment, you can use **post-judgment discovery tools** in any court of appropriate jurisdiction. I suspect one can send the debtor written interrogatories or requests for production about assets (but you'd probably file it in the county court if not appeal, or maybe directly under JP cause number – might depend on the JP’s approach). Alternatively, you can subpoena the debtor for a deposition to ask about assets (you’d do that in a district or county with subpoena power, maybe easier if you domesticate the judgment in county for enforcement). Some creditors skip JP and get the judgment abstracted and then apply in district for a post-judgment discovery order. But you could try through the JP as well. If the debtor fails to respond to post-judgment discovery, the remedy is to go to the district court to compel or hold in contempt (JP cannot hold someone in contempt except in narrow circumstances, and likely not for discovery disobedience because that’s beyond their enforcement). So practically, serious post-judgment discovery might be beyond JP’s power to enforce – better to use higher court help.  

- **Dormancy and Revival:** A Texas judgment is valid for **10 years** ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/property-code/title-8/chapter-92/subchapter-b/section-92-0563/#:~:text=,interest%20and%20costs%20of%20court)). Before it expires, you can extend it by **reviving** the judgment (filing a scire facias or an action to revive) to get another 10 years. If you do nothing, after 10 years it becomes dormant and cannot be enforced (though it can be revived within 2 years after dormancy) ([tcss.legis.texas.gov](https://tcss.legis.texas.gov/resources/CP/htm/CP.34.htm#:~:text=C,a%20writ%20of%20execution%20is)). So effectively, you could enforce up to 12 years by reviving at year 10 or so. After 12 years without action, it’s dead permanently. If the debtor is making partial payments, each payment can extend the life because a new payment might reset dormancy countdown in some interpretations – but safer to just keep track of time and renew if needed. Federal law also says if debtor is in bankruptcy or protected by an automatic stay, that period doesn’t count toward dormancy (tolled). But always best to renew anyway timely.  

- **Exempt property – cannot touch:** To emphasize, you *cannot* seize a debtor’s **current wages** (for personal debt) ([texaslegalguide.com](https://texaslegalguide.com/Texas_Constitution%3AArticle_XVI%2C_Section_28#:~:text=Article%20XVI%2C%20Section%2028%20of,2%29%20spousal%20maintenance)). You can’t take their primary **home** or **primary vehicle**, nor tools of trade, nor household essentials within reason ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/property-code/prop-sect-42-001/#:~:text=,ordered%20child%20support%20payments)) ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/property-code/prop-sect-42-001/#:~:text=of%20not%20more%20than%20%24100%2C000%2C,charges%20encumbering%20the%20property%3B%20or)). So if the debtor legitimately has only exempt assets and income, you might be stuck – such a person is often called “judgment-proof.” The judgment stays valid 10 years, so maybe their circumstances improve (they win lottery, or buy non-exempt assets). You keep your lien on any real property possibly. If they inherit land or buy a vacation condo, your lien attaches. If they deposit non-exempt money in a bank, you might snag it. So it’s wise to keep judgments alive and monitor the debtor’s status (some creditors periodically run asset checks or hire private investigators to see if the debtor’s situation changed).  

- **Voluntary compliance:** Before resorting to heavy enforcement, it’s often effective to send the debtor a **post-judgment demand letter** enclosing the judgment, explaining interest is accruing (judgment interest in Texas for consumer debts is 5% per year if not contract rate ([www.barsalou-law.com](https://www.barsalou-law.com/how-much-is-post-judgment-interest-in-texas-understanding-accrual-rates-and-collection-under-texas-law#:~:text=,per%20year)), and for all judgments between 5-15% depending on market – currently likely 7-8% as of 2026), and that if they don’t pay you’ll pursue liens, credit report (note: Texas doesn’t automatically report civil judgments to credit bureaus nowadays, but an unpaid judgment can show up in credit records or public records reviews for 7 years), and seizure. Sometimes, that prompts a payment plan or settlement. Debtors might offer to pay less for a quick settle – that’s your call if partial now is better than maybe nothing later. If you agree, get it in writing and file a **Release of Judgment** once paid to avoid lingering liens.  

- **Credit reporting:** Civil judgments used to appear on credit reports but since 2017, the big credit bureaus removed judgments and tax liens from reports unless they can have full SSN/DOB match, which they ceased collecting due to accuracy issues. So the judgment might not show on standard credit reports. However, it’s still a public record, and many credit checks (like tenant screening or loan underwriting) will search public records for judgments. Also, the abstract ensures it’ll come up in title searches if they try to sell property. So the debtor does have some incentive to resolve it.  

- **Receivership:** If the judgment is fairly large and debtor has a complex asset profile (like maybe interest in an LLC or steady revenue from somewhere), a judgment creditor can apply to a court for a **turnover receivership** – a neutral receiver is appointed to take control of non-exempt assets or claims (like if the debtor is owed money by others, the receiver can sue those on debtor’s behalf and collect). This is probably overkill for small claims (and expensive, since receiver takes a fee often paid from collections). But mention if someone won $15k in small claims and the debtor is egregiously hiding assets but has rental income etc., a receiver could collect those rents. That requires district court involvement typically, and receiver fees could be ~25% of collected funds.  

- **Bankruptcy effect:** If the debtor files for Chapter 7 or 13 bankruptcy after you get your judgment, your collection actions must stop immediately (automatic stay). Unsecured judgments like yours can be **discharged** in bankruptcy (except certain ones like those for willful injury or fraud might be non-dischargeable if you pursue that in bankruptcy court). If you get a notice of debtor’s bankruptcy, you’ll have to cease attempts and likely your judgment will be wiped out at the end of the bankruptcy if it’s dischargeable. Secured status: If you recorded an abstract lien on non-exempt real property before bankruptcy, your judgment lien might survive or give you secured creditor status (though in Chapter 7, if no equity beyond homestead, they could avoid the lien on exempt property). Always review with an attorney if bankruptcy arises.  

- **Enforcement through JP vs others:** A justice court can issue execution and garnishment as discussed. But if you find you need equitable relief like turnover, or to do post-judgment discovery, you might have to go to a higher court. One common tactic: **domesticate** (transfer) the judgment to county or district court. Texas Civil Practice & Rem. Code §31.005 allows you to file a certified copy of a judgment from one court with the clerk of another court of competent jurisdiction to enforce the judgment there. E.g., you could take the JP judgment and file it in the district court as a foreign judgment (though it’s same state, some attorneys simply sue on the judgment in district). But simplest: Texas has **Uniform Enforcement of Foreign Judgments Act** to enforce judgments from other states, and similarly you can file a “certified copy” of an in-state judgment in any county court or district to use their enforcement powers. Then you can use their discovery and turnover processes. There is some legal nuance to that (some debate if a JP judgment can be enforced by district directly via registration – but generally yes, as long as the judgment is final and not dormant).  

- **Costs of enforcement and adding to judgment:** Costs of execution (the sheriff’s fees, storage, auctioneer, etc.) are added to the judgment debt by law (so debtor ends up owing those as well). Post-judgment interest accrues at the rate set in the judgment (if not stated, the legal rate at time of judgment entry) ([texasjudgment.com](https://texasjudgment.com/post-judgment-interest/#:~:text=Post%20Judgment%20Interest%20After%20a,You%20can%20check)). Thus, if the debtor stalls, the amount grows. You cannot, however, claim **attorney’s fees** for your collection efforts unless the original judgment or contract provides for that or you get a turnover receiver (which sometimes can get fees). Usually, your time spent or attorney costs trying to enforce are not recoverable as separate items (except maybe as court costs for new writ fees, etc.).  

**In summary**, to collect your Texas small claims judgment: use easy tools first (demand letter, voluntary payment plan), then lock down assets by abstracting the judgment to create liens on property ([statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/DocViewer.aspx?DocKey=PR%2FPR.52&ExactPhrase=False&HighlightType=1&Phrases=homestead%7Cexemption%7Cfor%7Ctexas&QueryText=homestead+exemption+for+texas#:~:text=,and%20on%20the%20filing%20of)). Next, leverage the sheriff via writ of execution to seize non-exempt assets (if any) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,years%20of%20age%20or%20older)). Garnish any known bank accounts (except wages cannot be garnished, but money in bank after deposit can) to grab funds ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=defendant%20by%20registered%20or%20certified,or%20electronic%20return%20receipt%20requested)). If those standard methods fail because the debtor has protected everything, you may be facing a judgment-proof debtor. You can then renew the judgment lien and wait, or escalate to more intensive measures (post-judgment discovery, turnover orders via higher court) for elusive assets – though for small amounts this might not be cost-effective. Always ensure you act within the dormancy period (keep track of that 10-year mark) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/property-code/title-8/chapter-92/subchapter-b/section-92-0563/#:~:text=,interest%20and%20costs%20of%20court)). And keep copies of all these filings (abstract, writ returns, etc.) with your judgment record. Finally, remember that even a slow collection is something – the judgment accruing interest can motivate a debtor eventually (like when they try to buy a house or business loan and realize they need to clear the judgment). By knowing and using these tools, you maximize your chances of actually getting paid what the court awarded you.  

## 13. Special rules for businesses  
Small claims in Texas are not limited to individuals – businesses (like corporations, LLCs, partnerships) can both sue and be sued in justice court. There are a few special considerations when a business entity is involved:  

- **Representation of a business:** Unlike higher courts where a corporation must appear through an attorney, in Texas justice courts a business can appear **“pro se”** through an owner, partner, or employee. Rule 500.4(b) explicitly allows a corporation or other entity to be represented by **an employee, owner, officer, or partner** who is **not an attorney** in small claims ([www.stcl.edu](https://www.stcl.edu/lib/TexasRulesProject/TRCP474-522/rule500-42013.html#:~:text=,corporation%20or%20other%20entity%20may)) ([www.stcl.edu](https://www.stcl.edu/lib/TexasRulesProject/TRCP474-522/rule500-42013.html#:~:text=,be%20represented%20by%20an%20attorney)). This means your LLC’s office manager or you as the owner can speak for the business in court without hiring a lawyer. For example, if **ABC LLC** is plaintiff, the LLC’s managing member can sign the petition and appear at trial on behalf of the LLC. Or if a corporation is defendant, it can send, say, its regional manager to defend the case. This rule greatly simplifies things for businesses in JP court and saves legal costs. **However**, note that the representative must be **authorized** by the entity. Typically, an employee should have a letter or some proof of their position if it’s not obvious. The court usually doesn’t require formal proof of authority unless contested. Also, the person must have **personal knowledge** of the facts to be a credible witness – sending a random employee with no clue might not go well. Many times the business owner or a knowledgeable staff member comes. If a business prefers, it can still **hire an attorney** to represent it – attorneys are allowed in small claims. But it’s not required as it might be in county/district court. A note: if an entity sends a non-lawyer, that person can do all normal tasks (file pleadings, negotiate, etc.) but they cannot engage in the unauthorized practice of law outside representation of that entity – meaning they represent only the entity, not any other person, which is fine here.  

- **Fictitious business names:** If a business is operating under an **assumed name (“DBA”)**, Texas law requires an assumed name certificate to be on file (Bus. & Com. Code Chapter 71). While this doesn’t bar them from suing, it affects how you name the party. Always sue a business by its **legal name** (and maybe add dba). E.g., if “Sunshine Roofing” is actually Sunshine Roofing, LLC, sue the **LLC**. If “Joe’s Garage” is just Joe Smith sole proprietor, you’d sue “Joe Smith d/b/a Joe’s Garage.” Why it matters: a judgment against a dba alone may be unenforceable if that’s not a legal entity. You want the judgment against the person or legal entity behind the business. Conversely, if a business is plaintiff, ensure they sue in their correct capacity. The JP clerk might ask for clarity if a name looks like a company to ensure the rep can sign.  

- **Number of filings per year limit:** Some states limit how often a business (especially a collection company) can use small claims (e.g., California limited a company to two claims over $2,500/year). Texas **does not impose a numeric limit** on number of cases a business can file in JP. A frequent filer (like a debt buyer or landlord) can file hundreds of suits. The only issue could be if it’s considered harassment of a defendant by splitting claims (not allowed). But number of separate claims is not capped. So a business can be a serial plaintiff in small claims freely.  

- **Attorney requirement or not:** As mentioned, **no attorney requirement** in JP. Even a partnership can send a partner. Even an out-of-state corporation can send an officer. This is a major advantage for small businesses—no need to spend on lawyer if case is simple. Note: If the case goes on appeal to county court, then the rule of needing an attorney might kick in (because in county court, a corporation should be represented by counsel in most cases – Texas case law suggests that in county/district, a corporation must appear through licensed attorney). So if a business wins in JP and the other side appeals to county, the business will likely need counsel for the de novo trial. But at JP level, no.  

- **Registration to do business for out-of-state companies:** If an out-of-state business wants to sue in Texas courts, it generally must be registered to do business in Texas or else risk having the suit abated. Texas law (Tex. Bus. Org. Code §9.051) says a foreign entity transacting business in TX may not maintain a court action unless it’s registered or the transaction is exempt. But being unregistered is an affirmative defense the other party must raise timely – not often raised in JP. If you are suing an out-of-state corporation, they might not be registered, but if their suit arises from business in TX, they should have been. The penalty can be dismissal of their claim until they register (they can then continue). For defendants, being unregistered doesn’t stop them being sued (it’s not a defense to liability). If you want to use it defensively, mention it at trial to possibly block a foreign corporation’s claim until they comply.  

- **Requirement for assumed name certificate in suit:** Under Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 28, if you sue a partnership or unincorporated assoc. under a common name (like a dba), they may answer in their true name and the case continues. They must also file their assumed name certificate if demanded by the plaintiff. So, if you’re suing e.g. “Best Tires” and it’s not clear who owns it, you could request they produce the assumed name certificate to identify the owner. The rule also allows the judgment to be against the entity in its common name and then you can enforce against assets in that name or the underlying owner. But it’s cleanest to amend to the real name once known.  

- **Cap on claims for businesses:** The $20,000 jurisdictional cap is the same regardless of the plaintiff being individual or business ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=)). Unlike some states, Texas doesn’t have a lower cap for company plaintiffs. So a company can sue for the full $20k. They can also be sued up to $20k in JP. If more, must go to higher court.  

- **Suing a proprietorship vs owner:** A sole proprietorship is not separate from the owner. So a judgment should name the owner personally (with dba). If you sued just the trade name and got a judgment, you might have to later amend or get the court to clarify it attaches to the individual. So always identify them. If you aren’t sure of the owner, check county DBA filings, or ask around, or check tax rolls or regulatory licenses.  

- **Business records evidence:** Businesses often want to introduce records (invoices, account statements). Texas evidence law has a “business records affidavit” exception to hearsay. In JP court, formal affidavits might not be required if the custodian is present or if the other side doesn’t object. But if a debt buyer is proving an account, they will often provide a custodian affidavit under Rule of Evidence 803(6) and Tex. Civ. Rem. Code §18. So businesses should gather their records with that in mind. 

- **Pre-trial for business attorneys:** If a business uses an attorney in JP, no formal discovery but the attorney might send a demand for documents to the other side or attempt depositions (needs permission). Rare in small cases but possible if needed.  

- **Registered agent for service:** If a business is defendant, serve their registered agent or an officer. If plaintiff chooses an authorized rep to go to JP, that rep can also accept service for them in that case if necessary. Because as plaintiff they’ll be on record. For business defendants, the citation should be served to a person: e.g., “XYZ Corp by serving its registered agent John Smith at [address].”  

- **Non-resident businesses “doing business” issue:** If an out-of-state company is sued in JP (as defendant), they might try to claim the JP lacks jurisdiction if they don’t have minimum contacts with Texas. That’s a due process argument (personal jurisdiction). In small claims if the transaction happened in Texas or with a Texas resident, likely sufficient contacts exist. It’s rare for a JP to dismiss on that basis but it could if e.g. the business has no presence and the contract was via internet. But normally one sues them where *venue* is proper (likely Texas if transaction happened or plaintiff is here under long-arm).  

- **Filing fees & costs for businesses:** They pay the same as individuals. They can also file a Statement of Inability if truly can’t afford (it’s rare a company would, but theoretically possible – e.g., a small partnership could claim inability). The rule doesn’t forbid it.  

- **Multiple cases and res judicata:** A business often deals with numerous separate claims (like a debt collector with many accounts). Each account or contract is a separate cause of action. They can’t split one claim into many, but they can of course file separate suits for separate contracts or defendants. If a business plaintiff sues on an account and wins or loses, they can’t sue again on that same account. If a business is being serially sued by one person for bits of the same issue, they can raise res judicata too.  

- **Annual filing for debt collectors:** Some states require collectors to file a copy of their collection agency bond or license with small claims court annually. Texas doesn’t require that for court filings, but Texas Finance Code §392.101 requires third-party debt collectors to have a $10,000 surety bond on file with Secretary of State. If they don’t, a defendant could potentially challenge their capacity (though likely it doesn’t void their suit, just a misdemeanor for them, but a savvy defendant’s lawyer might use it to question their standing).  

In short, Texas small claims is user-friendly to businesses: they can send a non-lawyer rep ([www.stcl.edu](https://www.stcl.edu/lib/TexasRulesProject/TRCP474-522/rule500-42013.html#:~:text=,be%20represented%20by%20an%20attorney)), they have the same access to $20k limit, and no numeric caps on use. The main caution is to correctly identify the legal entity in lawsuits and be mindful of any requirement like registering the business in Texas if you’re an out-of-state company bringing suit (to avoid a halt in proceedings).  

## 14. County-level differences  
Texas justice courts are guided by the same state rules, but **procedures and resources can vary by county**. Notably:  

- **Forms and Online Systems:** Some populous counties provide extensive self-help info and custom forms on their websites (e.g., Harris, Dallas, Travis). They may allow email or online submission of answers from defendants, or have local guidelines for e-filing. Rural counties might operate more by traditional paper filing. For example, **Harris County** JP courts have an online case search and sometimes allow setting hearing dates or checking status online. **Dallas County** JPs have PDF forms you can download for small claims and also partake in eFileTexas. **Travis County** JPs highly encourage e-filing and provide a small claims toolkit online. Smaller counties might still expect you to mail or bring documents physically. Also, some counties have **night court** or special dockets to accommodate citizens (e.g., a JP in a busy precinct might hold evening small claims sessions once a month). Check your county’s JP website or call the clerk to learn any local wrinkles.  

- **Number of Precincts and Court Locations:** In large counties, there are multiple JP precincts (Harris has 16 courts (8 precincts with Place 1 and 2 each), Dallas has 5, Bexar (San Antonio) has 4). Each operates independently by an elected JP. While rules are same, local **court culture** can differ: one JP might be stricter on evidence technicalities, another might be more laissez-faire. Some JPs actively encourage mediation (some have court-sponsored mediators who meet with parties before trial on the day of hearing). For instance, **Bexar County** JPs refer many small claims to a Dispute Resolution Center for free mediation before trial. **Tarrant County** (Fort Worth) JPs also often require attempting mediation (their website mentions small claims may be mediated through Tarrant Co. ADR). **Travis County** JP precinct 5 has a standing order referring all small claims to mediation with volunteers on the trial day. So, depending on county, you may spend a morning in mediation prior to seeing the judge. If you settle, great; if not, then you get trial that day or a reset. Local differences also include: whether the JP conducts **pre-trials** (some urban JPs set an initial hearing or “pre-trial” to see if case can settle or to handle motions, especially if jury trial requested; others go straight to trial date).  

- **Jury availability and usage:** In smaller counties, getting enough jurors for a JP jury trial can be cumbersome. Some small counties might push parties to waive jury due to logistical issues. But legally they must provide if requested. Urban counties easily summon jurors (Harris has a large pool). The likelihood of a jury trial might thus differ: urban defendant might actually demand a jury if they think the JP is plaintiff-friendly, whereas in a rural area, both are likely to just let JP decide. It's more of strategy than rule difference, but environment influences it.  

- **Clerks and assistance:** Larger counties have specialized **small claims clerks** or even self-help centers (e.g., Travis County Law Library helps pro se litigants with forms). Smaller counties, the JP clerk might be wearing multiple hats (traffic, small claims, evictions all in one desk). The level of guidance you get may vary. But by law, they can’t give legal advice, only procedural guidance. Many larger JP courts have websites with FAQs (e.g., “How do I file a small claim in Collin County?” etc.).  

- **Language services:** Urban counties often have bilingual staff or easy access to interpreters (Harris JPs frequently have Spanish interpreters ready). Rural counties might require advance notice to get an interpreter from another town. Always notify early anywhere, but in some counties it’s crucial because they might need to budget or arrange for one traveling.  

- **Appeals destination:** In smaller counties without a county court at law, appeals from JP go to the **constitutional county court** (headed by the county judge, who may not be a lawyer; e.g., in rural counties the county judge often handles JP appeals in civil). In larger counties that have county courts at law with civil jurisdiction, those typically hear the JP appeals. E.g., in Harris, a civil JP appeal might go to a Harris County Civil Court at Law. For you, this means the judge on appeal might be more or less legally trained depending on county. Usually, if no law-trained judge at county level, parties might be more likely to stick to JP outcome (just an observation).  

- **Filing fees differences:** We noted base fees are roughly similar (thanks to SB 41 uniform fees), but **local add-ons** cause slight differences. For example: **Dallas** and **Tarrant** JPs charge ~$54, **Harris** we saw an odd table $15 cost + $65 service (but likely now $54 cost + $75 service). Some counties might have a local court building security fee a couple dollars different. But overall not huge variation – maybe $10 swing at most.  

- **Service of process arrangements:** In big counties, constables actively serve process and you typically include their fee on filing. In some very small counties, the JP might hand you the citation and instruct you to have the sheriff serve it and then provide proof. Or a JP might themselves send by certified mail proactively (some JPs do certified mail by default unless you request personal service). It’s minor differences in approach.  

- **Technology in courtroom:** Some JPs (especially in big counties) have **document cameras** or allow a laptop hookup to display evidence on screens. Others are old-school: you just hand paper to judge. If you have, say, a video, in some modern courtrooms you can bring a USB and they have a computer to play it on monitors for all. In others, you must bring your own device and show it to judge privately (so bring at least a laptop or tablet with necessary ports and a charger, plus speakers if audio is important – and always have a transcript in case tech fails or judge can’t discern audio).  

- **Local mediation programs:** For example, **Harris County** precinct 1 place 1 uses volunteer mediators from Dispute Resolution Center (DRC) who often meet with litigants on trial day morning to attempt resolution. If your county has a DRC, likely JPs partner with them. Travis County has DRC mediations available for JPs. Check the local JP website – they often mention if mediation is required or offered. If you strongly want or oppose mediation, you can file a motion to refer or not to refer to it, but usually following local practice is expected.  

- **COVID / remote hearings:** In recent times, some counties conducted JP hearings via Zoom. Some still allow or require remote appearance for certain proceedings. For instance, in 2021–2022 many JPs held small claims trials on Zoom. By 2023–2026, most returned in person, but some might accommodate remote attendance if good cause (distance, etc.). If you’re far away (like you’re in another county or state), you can request to attend via videoconference. Many JPs are amenable if the other side doesn’t object. Each county’s comfort with remote tech differs. Always ask if needed – Texas Supreme Court gave JPs authority to conduct remote proceedings. So a difference would be: a rural JP might say “No remote, come in person,” whereas an urban one might easily set up a Zoom link.  

- **Jury pools temperament:** This is speculation, but local population differences mean if your case goes to jury, e.g., in a plaintiff-friendly county vs a conservative county, outcomes can differ. E.g., a car accident in a big city (with many potential jurors who've had accidents) might yield a more sympathetic jury to plaintiff than a rural county where folks are skeptical of suits. That’s more of a general litigation factor than a formal rule difference.  

Despite minor variations, the **core process** remains uniform due to statewide rules. A savvy litigant will check their county’s JP court website or call the clerk to ask: “Do you have a specific local procedure for x?” Most helpful differences to know: How they handle mediation, interpreter requests, and how they schedule dockets (some smaller counties might only hear civil cases on certain days of the month). For example, **Brazos County JP** might do civil on first Wednesdays only, so your trial date depends on that cycle. Or **El Paso** JPs coordinate with each other for scheduling.  

In summary, **do a bit of local research:** use online resources (many counties have a Justice of the Peace page with local rules or standing orders). Adapt to those differences: bring the right number of copies as usually needed by that court, arrive at correct courtroom (multi-precinct counties have several JP courthouses), and follow any local courtesy rules (like some JPs require motions (like continuance) to be in writing and filed X days ahead; others accept a phone call). The differences won’t typically upend your case – they’re about convenience and procedure. When in doubt, politely ask the clerk: they often volunteer “In our court, we do X...” which can save you trouble.  

## 15. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons  
Many small claims cases get derailed due to avoidable mistakes. Here are frequent pitfalls and how to avoid them:  

- **Suing the wrong defendant or wrong name:** This is a top cause of failure. If you sue the wrong entity or misname the defendant, your judgment might be unenforceable or case dismissed. Examples: You intended to sue “ABC Corp” but you listed “ABC Corp, Inc.” and the real name had no “Inc.” – if the defendant doesn’t show, you might get a default against a non-existent or misnamed entity that can be quashed later. Or you sue a franchise store manager personally when the liable party was the franchise company. **Solution:** Take time to identify the proper legal defendant. For businesses, use Secretary of State records or Assumed Name records to get exact names (including suffix like LLC, LP, Inc.). For individuals, use full legal name if known (John A. Doe instead of just Johnny). If you only know a nickname, at least include “aka”: e.g., “James Smith aka ‘Bubba’ Smith.” If you accidentally sued by a trade name, you can usually amend to the real name once discovered (Tex. R. Civ. Proc. 27 and 28 allow amendment to true name ([www.stcl.edu](https://www.stcl.edu/lib/TexasRulesProject/TRCP474-522/rule500-42013.html#:~:text=,corporation%20or%20other%20entity%20may))). But if you never figure it out, you risk chasing the wrong party. Another angle: If a contract was under “John Doe dba Doe’s Plumbing,” ensure you sue John Doe individually – don’t just sue “Doe’s Plumbing.” Similarly, if suing a married person in Texas, you don’t need to sue their spouse unless it’s community debt (though in some debt cases, both spouses might be liable; but if one spouse wasn’t part of transaction, you generally don’t join them in small claims). Wrong name issues also arise in auto accidents: sometimes the at-fault driver is not the owner of the vehicle. Who to sue? Usually the driver (direct negligence) and optionally the owner if there’s an owner liability theory (like letting an unlicensed child drive – Texas has negligent entrustment but that’s maybe beyond small claims scope). Suing the car is not how – you sue persons. So ensure you have the driver’s correct name (from police report) and any other responsible party. If you’re unsure of an entity’s structure, maybe name multiple: e.g. sue both “ABC LLC” and “ABC LLC d/b/a Super Mart” to cover bases. The court typically allows amendment if you find out “Oops it was actually XYZ LLC who owned that store.” But better get it right the first time.  

- **Lack of jurisdiction/venue:** Filing in the wrong **court** or wrong **county** can waste time. If you mistakenly file in JP when exclusive jurisdiction lies elsewhere, your case gets dismissed. Examples: trying to sue for defamation or seeking an injunction in JP – the judge must dismiss because that’s not allowed in JP ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=a%20suit%20for%20divorce%3B)). Or suing for $25,000 in JP – judge must dismiss for lack of jurisdiction (or possibly suggest you remit it to $20k, but they might just throw it out). Or suing in a county where neither party nor incident has connection – the defendant can transfer it (or if it’s completely wrong and defendant doesn’t show, a default could later be set aside for improper venue if they had no idea they were sued far away). **Solution:** Check Section 1 on what JPs cannot hear ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=a%20suit%20for%20divorce%3B)). Keep demands within $20k principal ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=)). Choose correct venue (the county/precinct where defendant resides or cause arose) – see Section 3 ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,where%20the%20defendant%20resides)). If you realize after filing you picked wrong venue, better to nonsuit and refile properly, or move to transfer quickly.  

- **Statute of limitations expired:** If you file too late (beyond the legal time limit for that claim type), the defendant can raise limitations and get the case dismissed (or judgment in their favor). Many pro se plaintiffs aren’t aware of these deadlines and file old claims – e.g., trying to sue for an unpaid loan from 6 years ago (limit is 4 years ([statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/GetStatute.aspx?Code=CP&Value=16#:~:text=)) – defendant just has to mention it and you likely lose). Or suing for a car wreck that happened 3+ years ago (limit 2 years ([statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/GetStatute.aspx?Code=CP&Value=16#:~:text=trespass%20for%20injury%20to%20the,the%20cause%20of%20action%20accrues)) – case will be dismissed if defendant asserts it). **Solution:** Always check in Section 5 the relevant limitation period, and ensure your case is filed within it. If not, expect the defendant might bring it up and you’ll likely lose unless some tolling applies that you can substantiate (like defendant left state, or it was fraud discovered late). Many defendants in small claims won’t know to raise it, but one shouldn’t count on that – if judgment is by default and limitations had actually run, the defendant can sometimes later attack the judgment as voidable. So time matters. Sue promptly and within limits.  

- **No service or improper service:** If you don’t get the defendant served correctly, the court cannot enter a valid judgment. Cases often stall or get dismissed because the plaintiff didn’t provide a good address or didn’t follow up on service. Example: The constable tries an old address, can’t find them, returns citation unserved – the plaintiff does nothing further, months pass, and the court eventually sets a dismissal docket for want of prosecution because no progress. Or plaintiff tries to serve by certified mail themselves (not allowed; service by certified mail must come from clerk or a disinterested person designated by court), which is improper – so defendant never got notice in the required manner. **Solution:** Take service seriously: after filing, if you haven’t gotten a return of service within a few weeks, check with clerk or constable. If unserved, find a new address or ask the court for alternate service ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28e%29%20Alternative%20Service%20of%20Citation,This%20request%20must%20include)). Don’t just wait indefinitely. If you truly can’t find them, consider hiring a private process server or investigator. Some cases get dismissed at initial hearing because plaintiff can’t show the judge that the defendant was served. Without service or a proper waiver, the case can’t proceed. Also ensure the service paperwork was done right: e.g., if substituted service was ordered, verify the officer did each step (posted and mailed) and filed proof ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=match%20at%20L517%20citation%2C%20the,to%20be%20found%2C%20if%20ascertainable)). Another aspect: sometimes plaintiffs think mailing a copy of petition to defendant is enough – it’s not (unless defendant signs a waiver). Or they hand it to them personally but not through an authorized server – that also doesn’t count legally (a party to the suit cannot effect service ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20Who%20May%20Serve,citations%20may%20be%20served%20by))). Those missteps will lead to dismissal or at least nullification of any default.  

- **Failure to appear (Plaintiff):** If you, as plaintiff, miss your court date (even a scheduled pre-trial or mediation conference if labelled “appearance required”), likely the judge will **dismiss for want of prosecution** (essentially a default against you). That’s the end of your case unless you quickly file a motion to reinstate/new trial within 14 days with a good reason (like “I never got the notice” or “I had a medical emergency”) – and the judge finds it credible. Many pro se plaintiffs unfortunately forget their date or mix up location and end up losing by not showing. **Solution:** Once you file, be vigilant for the court notice. Call the clerk if you don’t get one in a reasonable time. Mark the date and time clearly on your calendar. Aim to arrive early. If you absolutely cannot attend (e.g., illness), notify the court *beforehand* and request a continuance; don’t no-show without word. And remember, if you move or change contact info while case is pending, inform the court in writing – notices go to last address on file and if you miss a notice due to moving, that’s on you.  

- **Failure to appear (Defendant):** If you’re the defendant and you ignore the case (don’t answer and/or don’t go to trial), a **default judgment** will likely be entered against you ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20Default%20Judgment,judgment%20in%20the%20following%20manner)). That’s avoidable by simply filing an answer and showing up to tell your side. Many default because they think “It’s not worth dealing with” or they never check their mail. This puts them in a tough spot, needing to file an appeal or new trial quickly or else the judgment stands. **Solution:** As a defendant, always respond by the answer date and attend. If you disagree with the claim, contest it timely; if you agree you owe something, you might still go to possibly negotiate or at least avoid extra default costs.  

- **Suing for non-monetary relief:** People sometimes file in small claims asking for things like “I want the neighbor to remove his fence” or “I want my job back” or “make the mechanic finish the work.” These are requests for specific performance or injunctive relief which small claims cannot grant ([texas.public.law](https://texas.public.law/statutes/tex._gov%27t_code_section_27.031#:~:text=a%20suit%20for%20divorce%3B)). The JP will likely explain they can only award money (or possibly the judge may treat it as alternative money demand e.g., value of what wasn’t done). If your petition is only for a non-monetary order, the judge will dismiss or require you to re-plead for monetary damages instead. **Solution:** Know that small claims = money. If what you really want is something done, think in terms of what dollar equivalent you could get to then pay for doing it yourself. For instance, instead of “finish my roof,” sue for the cost to hire someone else to finish it. Or if you bought something unique and they won’t deliver, small claims won’t force them to hand it over, but you can sue for its value in cash.

- **Incomplete or unorganized evidence:** Coming to court without key documents or with a jumble of materials can sink your case. If you just tell a story without proof (contract, receipts, photos), the court may find you didn’t meet your burden. Or if evidence is sloppy (e.g., texts on your phone that you can’t properly show the judge), important details might be missed. **Solution:** **Organize your evidence.** Bring printed copies of all critical documents and **present them clearly**. Before trial, review what you must prove and ensure you have evidence for each point (timeline of events, proof of payment or non-payment, cost estimates, etc.). If a witness is essential, make sure they attend or have a proper affidavit if allowed. Think from the judge’s perspective: *What evidence would convince me?* – then gather and present that. Cases are often lost not because the truth wasn’t on someone’s side, but because they lacked **clear evidence** to support it.  

- **Ignoring court instructions or deadlines:** Some litigants miss deadlines (answer, appeal) or ignore specific court orders (like an order to exchange witness lists). This can lead to default judgments, exclusion of evidence, or dismissal. **Solution:** **Read every notice and order from the court carefully**. Diarize all deadlines (answer due, hearing dates, appeal window). If the court orders a pre-trial statement or a mediation session, comply with it. Justice courts are flexible but do expect you to follow basic directions. If you can’t meet a deadline, request an extension *before* it passes, showing good cause. Communicating with the court (via motions or at least calling the clerk with procedural questions) can save you from unintentional default or sanctions.  

- **Arguing emotion over facts/law:** It’s natural to be emotional about your case, but some parties focus on irrelevant grievances or personal attacks (e.g., “The defendant was rude and that’s why I want extra money!”) rather than the legal elements. This can annoy the judge or distract from the core issues. **Solution:** **Stick to relevant facts and the remedy allowed by law.** If the defendant is generally a bad actor but you’re suing for a specific breach, concentrate on that breach and damages from it. Don’t let anger lead you to claim punitive damages or outcomes the court can’t grant (e.g., forcing an apology). Maintain a respectful tone – hostile or disrespectful behavior can undermine your credibility. Judges appreciate parties who are prepared, factual, and courteous, even in tense situations.  

- **Failing to pursue post-judgment remedies:** Sometimes plaintiffs win a judgment and then do nothing, thinking the court will automatically enforce payment. Then the judgment goes unpaid and eventually expires. **Solution:** Remember that **you** must enforce the judgment (Section 12 details methods). After winning, **follow through**: abstract the judgment, seek execution or garnishment if needed ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/texas/2005/pr/005.00.000052.00.html#:~:text=CHAPTER%2052,2005%20Texas%20Property%20Code)) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,years%20of%20age%20or%20older)). Don’t assume the court will mail you a check – if the debtor won’t pay voluntarily, it’s on you to initiate collection processes. Conversely, if you’re a defendant who lost, don’t ignore the judgment – address it via payment, settlement, or appeal. Ignoring a judgment can lead to liens on your property or surprises like bank account garnishment.  

- **Not considering settlement:** Some cases drag on when they could have been settled on reasonable terms. Or a defendant might default when they could have arranged a payment plan. **Solution:** **Evaluate settlement possibilities** at each stage. Judges often encourage settling – it saves time and guarantees a result. If you’re the plaintiff, be open to a fair installment plan or a slightly lower lump-sum if it avoids collection hassle. If you’re the defendant and you owe something, see if you can negotiate to avoid court costs and a judgment on record. Settlement isn’t a pitfall – refusing to even discuss it can be. Many disputes can be resolved by meeting in the middle once both sides see the evidence strength. Use mediation if available – it’s a chance to control the outcome rather than risk the unknown at trial.  

- **Appealing improperly:** If you lose and decide to appeal, mistakes like missing the 21-day deadline, filing bond in the wrong amount, or not paying the county fee can doom your appeal. **Solution:** If appealing, **meticulously follow Section 11 steps**: mark the deadline, arrange the correct appeal bond (twice the judgment for defendants, $500 for plaintiffs) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20How%20Taken%3B%20Time,trial%2C%20if%20any%2C%20is%20denied)) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=,rendered%20against%20it%20on%20appeal)), file it on time with the JP, and pay any required fees to the county court. Many appeals get dismissed because paperwork wasn’t done right. Don’t let a procedural slip cancel your second chance. If unsure, consult an attorney or ask the JP clerk exactly what’s needed to perfect the appeal.  

- **Ignoring a bankruptcy notice:** If the other party declares bankruptcy, the lawsuit or judgment is automatically stayed. Continuing to push collection or the case can violate federal law. **Solution:** If you learn of a bankruptcy, **inform the court** and pause collection. You may need to file a claim in bankruptcy court instead. This is more of a post-judgment pitfall, but worth noting: heed bankruptcy stays to avoid legal penalties.  

By being mindful of these common pitfalls – naming the right party, respecting time limits, properly serving, focusing your evidence, and following procedure – you greatly improve the chances that your small claims case will be heard on the merits and result in a judgment you can enforce. Prepare thoroughly, stay organized, and don’t hesitate to seek clarification from the court on procedural points. This transforms potential pitfalls into manageable aspects of your case.  

## 16. Recent changes (last 3 years)  
Texas small claims procedures have seen a few notable changes and updates in the past few years (as of 2026):  

- **Jurisdictional limit increase:** Perhaps the biggest change – effective **September 1, 2020**, the Legislature raised the civil jurisdiction limit of Justice Courts from $10,000 to **$20,000** (exclusive of interest) ([codes.findlaw.com](https://codes.findlaw.com/tx/government-code/gov-t-sect-27-031#:~:text=,than%20%2420%2C000%2C%20exclusive%20of%20interest)) ([docket.wp.txstate.edu](https://docket.wp.txstate.edu/2020/09/10/jurisdictional-limit-increase-now-in-effect/#:~:text=limit%20increase%20to%20%2420%2C000%20went,regarding%20the%20jurisdictional%20limit%20increase)). This was through Senate Bill 2342 (2019) amending Gov’t Code §27.031. It doubled the amount individuals and businesses can recover in small claims. All the discussions here reflect this higher $20k cap, which is current. If you recall older sources saying $10k, that’s outdated – the $20k limit is firmly in effect since 2020 ([docket.wp.txstate.edu](https://docket.wp.txstate.edu/2020/09/10/jurisdictional-limit-increase-now-in-effect/#:~:text=We%20think%20this%20was%20an,civil%20justice%20court%20cases%2C%20and)). This change allows more cases to be handled inexpensively in JP court (e.g., many car accident claims that were slightly above $10k can now be in small claims).  

- **Uniform filing fees (2022):** House Bill 3774 (2021) and related judicial fee consolidation took effect **January 1, 2022**, standardizing civil filing fees across Texas. Now, Justice Court filing fees are mostly uniform statewide (around $54 for small claims, as discussed) ([www.jp1hillcounty.com](https://www.jp1hillcounty.com/fine-schedule#:~:text=%2454,the%20case%20with%20the%20Court)). In the past, each county had numerous add-on fees; now they’re streamlined into one “Consolidated Court Fee” and a few local fees. This makes it easier to predict costs and was part of a judiciary modernization bill. The exact fee schedule is set by state law now (Gov’t Code §§ 51.317, 133.151). While a minor administrative change, it’s recent and simplifies the cost structure for filers.  

- **E-filing mandate expansion:** Texas has been pushing electronic filing. By 2019, e-filing was required in higher courts; **as of July 2020, e-filing is also mandated for attorneys in Justice Courts in most counties** ([efiletexas.gov](https://efiletexas.gov/#:~:text=e,Filing)). Many Justice Courts have since adopted eFileTexas for all civil filings. Self-represented litigants may still file in person or by mail, but they now have the option to e-file as well. Over the past 3 years, more counties have brought their JP courts online. For example, in 2023 Travis County Justice Courts joined eFileTexas for small claims; Dallas and Harris JPs were already onboard. This change means faster filing and service of process (documents can be served via email to attorneys of record, etc.). If you’re an attorney, you must e-file in JP now (previously, some JPs didn’t have that capability).  

- **COVID-19 procedural adaptations:** The COVID pandemic in 2020-2021 caused temporary changes: the Texas Supreme Court issued emergency orders allowing Justice Courts to conduct hearings via Zoom and extending certain deadlines. For instance, some debt claim cases were abated or remote hearings became common. By 2022-2023, most of those emergency measures ended, but some innovations remain. **Virtual hearings**: Many JPs realized the utility of remote proceedings and have continued offering them in certain situations (especially for status conferences or where parties consent). Also, **electronic evidence submission** became more accepted (emailing PDFs to court pre-trial, etc.). While not a formal rule change, the last 3 years normalized remote appearances – so if needed, you can request a Zoom hearing, citing the successful use during COVID. Each JP might have adopted local practices from that era (check local rules – e.g., some still prefer filings by email for self-reps).  

- **Debt claim rules refinement:** The Supreme Court tweaked Rules for debt claim cases effective **May 2022** to make them clearer. For instance, Rule 508 (debt claims) now explicitly requires attaching a completed “Debt Claim Petition Information” form (including details like account number, charge-off date) – essentially reinforcing what a debt-buying plaintiff must plead ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28b%29%20Debt%20Claim%20Case,Part%20V%2C%20Rule%20508%20applies)) ([www.law.uh.edu](https://www.law.uh.edu/peopleslawyer/legal-topics/smallclaims/texasstatute.html#:~:text=%28a%29%20Small%20Claims%20Case,the%20Rules%20of%20Civil%20Procedure)). This was part of ongoing adjustment to ensure fairness in high-volume debt cases. It’s a minor adjustment most relevant to collection agencies. Also, effective September 1, 2023, legislation (HB 491) addressed **debt buyer attorney’s fees**: limiting when attorneys’ fees can be awarded in debt claims (to prevent awarding high fees on default judgments where minimal work done). This affects small claims: a debt buyer cannot get automatic attorney’s fees on a default unless certain proof of work is shown – encouraging reasonable fees.  

- **Landlord-tenant and eviction-related laws:** While eviction itself isn’t small claims, some new laws indirectly affect small-claims landlord disputes. For example, a 2021 change (HB 531) requires residential landlords to include a notice in leases if there’s no duty to mitigat damages upon tenant early lease breach. If not included, landlord must mitigate. This could come up in a small claims rent suit (landlord suing for remaining rent might be asked if they tried to re-rent). Also, effective 9/1/2021, Texas Property Code §92.109 was amended to increase the **penalty for landlords who improperly retain security deposits** – it clarified bad-faith presumption and perhaps attorney’s fees. Being aware of these tweaks is useful if you’re litigating deposit claims: e.g., **HB 1540 (2021)** made it explicit that if landlord doesn’t sign the itemized deductions list, it’s presumed not provided in good faith (aiding tenants’ cases).  

- **Homestead exemption threshold:** In 2021, Texas voters approved a constitutional amendment raising the homestead exemption for property taxes. Not directly small-claims, but tangentially note that as of 2022 more value is protected as homestead. This doesn’t change judgment collection much (homestead was fully exempt already) but underscores Texas’s continuing strong protection of primary residences ([texaslegalguide.com](https://texaslegalguide.com/Texas_Constitution%3AArticle_XVI%2C_Section_28#:~:text=Article%20XVI%2C%20Section%2028%20of,2%29%20spousal%20maintenance)).  

- **Interest rates:** Post-judgment interest rates have been low (5% floor) for years, but with recent Fed rate hikes, the judgment interest in Texas has crept up (mid-2023 it was ~7.5%). This isn’t a legislative change but a noteworthy economic change – a judgment now accrues a bit more interest than it would have 3 years ago. The statutory floor of 5% ([www.barsalou-law.com](https://www.barsalou-law.com/how-much-is-post-judgment-interest-in-texas-understanding-accrual-rates-and-collection-under-texas-law#:~:text=,per%20year)) remains, but as prime rises, the interest cap of 15% could become relevant if inflation soared. For now, just note your judgment may accumulate more interest than it would have when rates were near zero.  

- **Terminology and rule renumbering:** In 2022, the Texas Supreme Court finalized the rewrite/renumber of certain JP rules (consolidating old “Part V” rules 500-510). Most of the terminology (like “small claims case” vs “debt claim case”) was already in place since 2013, but the past 3 years solidified this usage. Any references to old “Rule 523, 737, etc.” are obsolete – now it’s Rules 500-510. The current rules are the ones used in this guide (as of 2026).  

- **Future changes eyed:** While not yet law as of 2026, keep an eye on proposals such as **raising the jurisdictional limit further** (there was talk of $20k -> $25k, but nothing passed in 2023 session) ([www.sue.com](https://www.sue.com/blog/small-claims-court-limits-by-state-2026#:~:text=Small%20Claims%20Court%20Jurisdictional%20Limits,Here%27s%20the%20current%20table%2C%20updated)) ([capitol.texas.gov](https://capitol.texas.gov/tlodocs/86R/billtext/html/SB02342I.htm#:~:text=86%28R%29%20SB%202342%20,the%20constitutional%20jurisdiction%20of%20the)), or reforms to expedite debt-claim defaults (some advocates want stricter evidence even for default judgments – e.g., requiring original account documents). Also, technology use will likely expand – some counties are experimenting with online dispute resolution for small claims. These aren’t in effect yet, but the trend is toward more accessibility and possibly higher limits. Always check the latest **Texas Government Code §27.031** and **Texas Rules of Civil Procedure Part V** for any amendments if you’re reading this a few years down the line.  

In summary, the last few years brought a **higher claim limit** (making JP courts even more useful) ([docket.wp.txstate.edu](https://docket.wp.txstate.edu/2020/09/10/jurisdictional-limit-increase-now-in-effect/#:~:text=limit%20increase%20to%20%2420%2C000%20went,regarding%20the%20jurisdictional%20limit%20increase)), **streamlined fees**, widespread **e-filing and remote hearing capabilities**, and fine-tuning of debt and landlord-tenant procedures. Texas small claims has become more uniform and user-friendly as a result. Always verify if any new legislation (from the 2025 or 2027 legislature, etc.) affects small claims – but as of 2026, the guide above reflects the current state of the law, incorporating these recent developments.
