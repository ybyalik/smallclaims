## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $7,500 for individuals; $7,500 for businesses [S4].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds $7,500, you cannot split it into multiple cases to fit within the limit. You must either waive the excess amount or file in a higher court [S4].
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S4].

## 2. What you can sue for

### Contracts and money owed

South Carolina allows you 3 years to file claims related to contracts and debts, whether written or oral.

- **Breach of a written contract** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A contractor signed a $5,000 contract to remodel your kitchen, took the deposit, and never completed the work.
  - **Statute:** S.C. Code § 15-3-530. You have 3 years from the date of breach to file. Bring the signed contract, payment records, and any correspondence. [S7]
- **Breach of an oral contract** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You agreed verbally to lend a friend $2,000, which they promised to repay in six months but haven't.
  - **Statute:** S.C. Code § 15-3-530. The statute of limitations is 3 years from the breach. Gather any evidence of the agreement, such as emails or witness statements. [S7]
- **Unpaid personal loan** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You loaned $3,000 to a colleague who agreed to repay in installments but stopped after the first payment.
  - **Statute:** S.C. Code § 15-3-530. File within 3 years from the date the loan was due. Provide documentation of the loan and any payments made. [S7]
- **Default on a promissory note** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A borrower signed a promissory note for $6,000 but failed to make any payments.
  - **Statute:** S.C. Code § 15-3-530. You have 3 years from the date of default to file. Present the original promissory note and records of non-payment. [S7]
- **Unpaid invoice for services rendered** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You provided consulting services worth $4,500, invoiced the client, but haven't received payment.
  - **Statute:** S.C. Code § 15-3-530. The statute of limitations is 3 years from the invoice date. Bring the invoice, service agreement, and any communication regarding payment. [S7]
- **Unpaid balance on an open account** — eligible.

## 14. Post-judgment collection

- **Payment deadline expectation:** 30 days.
- **Post-judgment interest rate:** 8.75% — S.C. Code § 34-31-20. Interest accrues on the judgment amount until paid in full.

### Wage garnishment

Obtain a portion of the debtor's wages until the judgment is satisfied.

File a writ of garnishment with the court, serve the employer, and the employer withholds a portion of the debtor's wages to be applied toward the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $30 plus service fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor is employed and earns above the exemption threshold.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings or 30 times the federal minimum wage, whichever is greater

### Bank levy

Seize funds from the debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, serve it on the debtor's bank, and the bank freezes and turns over funds to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $30 plus service fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor has sufficient funds in the account; may be limited by exemptions.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $5,000 in aggregate value of personal property, including cash and bank accounts

### Property lien

Place a lien on the debtor's real property to secure the judgment.

File the judgment with the county recorder's office to create a lien on the debtor's real property, which must be satisfied upon sale or refinancing.

**Estimated cost:** $25 recording fee  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns real property; may take time to realize payment.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $50,000

### Writ of execution

Seize and sell the debtor's non-exempt property to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, have the sheriff seize non-exempt property, and sell it at public auction to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $50 plus sheriff's fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns valuable non-exempt property; process can be time-consuming.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Various personal property exemptions totaling up to $5,000

- **Wage garnishment cap:** 25% of disposable earnings.
- **Judgment lifespan:** 10 years; renewal: File a motion to renew the judgment before the 10-year period expires; renewal extends the judgment for another 10 years.
- **Debtor's exam form:** SCCA 516 (Motion for Supplemental Proceedings).
- **Exempt assets:**
  - Homestead — $50,000

  - Retirement accounts (federally and state-protected)
  - Social Security and federal benefits
- **Bankruptcy effect:** Filing for bankruptcy imposes an automatic stay on collection efforts; certain judgments may be discharged in bankruptcy.
- **Satisfaction of judgment form:** SCCA 517 (Satisfaction of Judgment).
- **Multiple creditors priority:** Judgment liens are prioritized by the date of recording; earlier liens have higher priority.

## 15. Special rules for businesses

- **Self-representation allowed?** true — Businesses can represent themselves in Magistrate Court without an attorney.
- **Can a business send an employee instead of an attorney?** true.
- **Can a business send an attorney?** true.
- **Form for non-attorney representation:** SCCA 518 (Authorization for Non-Attorney Representation)
- **Insurance adjuster representation:** false.

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Incorrect defendant information

**What goes wrong:** Suing the wrong party or using incorrect names can result in dismissal or unenforceable judgments.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the exact legal name and address of the defendant before filing; for businesses, check with the Secretary of State. [unverified]

### Missing statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations expires leads to automatic dismissal of the case.

**How to avoid it:** Determine the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type and file within that period. [unverified]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** Failure to serve the defendant correctly can delay the case or result in dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Follow the court's rules for service of process precisely, using approved methods and documenting service. [unverified]

### Overlooking counterclaims

**What goes wrong:** Defendants may file counterclaims, potentially turning the tables and resulting in a judgment against you.

**How to avoid it:** Be prepared for possible counterclaims by reviewing the entire dispute and considering the defendant's perspective. [unverified]

### Ignoring court deadlines

**What goes wrong:** Missing filing deadlines or court dates can lead to dismissal or default judgments.

**How to avoid it:** Keep a detailed calendar of all deadlines and court dates, and set reminders to ensure timely compliance. [unverified]

### Insufficient evidence

**What goes wrong:** Lack of adequate evidence can result in losing the case, even if your claim is valid.

**How to avoid it:** Gather all relevant documents, photos, and witness statements to substantiate your claim before filing. [unverified]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in South Carolina Magistrate Court. The process is designed for individuals to represent themselves, though you may choose to hire an attorney if you prefer. [S4]

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: The timeline varies, but most small claims cases in South Carolina are resolved within 30 to 90 days from filing to judgment. [S4]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: In South Carolina Magistrate Court, you can sue for up to $7,500. If your claim exceeds this amount, you must file in a higher court or waive the excess. [S4]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear at the scheduled hearing, the court may grant a default judgment in your favor, provided you present sufficient evidence to support your claim. [S4]

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: Yes, either party can appeal a Magistrate Court decision to the Circuit Court within 30 days of the judgment. The appeal is a trial de novo, meaning the case is heard anew. [S4]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: A judgment in South Carolina is valid for 10 years from the date it is entered. You can renew the judgment before it expires to extend its enforceability. [S4]
