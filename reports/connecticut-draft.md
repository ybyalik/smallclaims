## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $5,000 for individuals; $5,000 for businesses [S1, S12, S4].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds the small claims limit, you cannot split it into multiple cases to fit within the cap. You must either waive the excess amount or file in a higher court. [S4]

- **Title to real estate** — Disputes involving ownership of real estate are beyond the jurisdiction of small claims court. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S1]
- **Defamation** — Claims of libel and slander are excluded from small claims court. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S1]
- **Malpractice** — Professional malpractice claims exceed the jurisdictional limits of small claims court. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S1]
- **Family law matters** — Divorce, child custody, and other family law issues are not handled in small claims court. Where to go instead: Family Court. [S1]
- **Probate matters** — Issues related to wills and estates are outside the jurisdiction of small claims court. Where to go instead: Probate Court. [S1]
- **Claims against the federal government** — Small claims court cannot hear cases against federal entities. Where to go instead: Federal Court. [S1]
- **Class actions** — Class action lawsuits are not permitted in small claims court. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S1]
- **Injunctions or specific performance** — Small claims court cannot issue orders requiring someone to do or stop doing something. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S1]
- **Workers' compensation claims** — These claims are handled by the Workers' Compensation Commission, not small claims court. Where to go instead: Workers' Compensation Commission. [S1]

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File an application for wage execution with the court; upon approval, serve the employer with the execution order; the employer withholds a portion of the debtor's wages and remits to the court.

**Estimated cost:** $50 plus service fees
**Effectiveness:** Most effective when debtor is employed and earning above the exemption threshold.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings or 40 times the federal minimum hourly wage per week, whichever is greater.

### Bank levy

Seize funds from debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

File an application for bank execution with the court; upon approval, serve the bank with the execution order; the bank freezes the debtor's account and remits funds to the court.

**Estimated cost:** $50 plus service fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor has sufficient funds in the account; debtor may claim exemptions.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $1,000 is exempt from execution; additional exemptions may apply for certain benefits.

### Property lien

Place a lien on debtor's real property to secure the judgment.

File a judgment lien certificate with the town clerk where the property is located; the lien encumbers the property until the judgment is satisfied.

**Estimated cost:** $75 plus recording fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor owns real property; lien must be renewed every 20 years.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $75,000.

### Writ of execution

Seize and sell debtor's personal property to satisfy the judgment.

File an application for execution with the court; upon approval, a state marshal seizes the debtor's non-exempt personal property and sells it at public auction.

**Estimated cost:** $75 plus marshal fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor owns valuable non-exempt personal property; process can be time-consuming.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Various personal property exemptions apply; consult statute for details.

- **Wage garnishment cap:** 25% of disposable earnings. [S4]
- **Judgment lifespan:** 20 years; renewal: File a new action on the judgment before the 20-year period expires to renew the judgment. [S4]
- **Debtor's exam form:** JD-CV-23 (Application for Examination of Judgment Debtor). [S4]
- **Exempt assets:**
  - Homestead — $75,000
  - Motor vehicle — $3,500
  - Household furnishings — $1,000
  - Tools of trade — $2,000
  - Social Security and federal benefits
- **Bankruptcy effect:** Filing for bankruptcy may discharge the judgment; certain debts are non-dischargeable. [S4]
- **Satisfaction of judgment form:** JD-CV-52 (Satisfaction of Judgment). [S4]
- **Multiple creditors priority:** Priority among judgment creditors is generally determined by the order in which liens are recorded. [S4]

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Incorrect defendant information

**What goes wrong:** Suing the wrong entity or misspelling the defendant's name can result in dismissal or unenforceable judgments.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the exact legal name and status of the defendant, especially for businesses, through the Secretary of State's records. [S1]

### Exceeding monetary limits

**What goes wrong:** Filing a claim exceeding the small claims limit may lead to dismissal or transfer to a higher court.

**How to avoid it:** Ensure your claim amount, excluding interest and costs, does not exceed $5,000, or $15,000 for home improvement contracts. [S1]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** Failure to serve the defendant correctly can delay proceedings or result in dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Follow the prescribed methods for service, such as certified mail or state marshal service, and file proof with the court. [S1]

### Missing statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations has expired will result in dismissal of your case.

**How to avoid it:** Determine the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type and file within that period. [S4]

### Inadequate evidence

**What goes wrong:** Lack of sufficient evidence can lead to losing your case.

**How to avoid it:** Gather all relevant documents, receipts, contracts, and witness information to support your claim. [S1]

### Ignoring counterclaims

**What goes wrong:** Failing to respond to a defendant's counterclaim can result in a judgment against you.

**How to avoid it:** Review any counterclaims carefully and file a timely response to address them. [S1]

## 18. Recent changes

- **2026-10-01** — **Increase in small claims limit for home improvement contracts**. Effective October 1, 2026, the small claims monetary limit for home improvement contracts increased from $5,000 to $15,000. Conn. Gen. Stat. § 51-15(d). [S4]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in small claims court. The process is designed to be accessible for individuals representing themselves. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: Most small claims cases are resolved within 30 to 90 days, depending on the court's schedule and complexity of the case. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: The maximum amount you can sue for in small claims court is $5,000, except for home improvement contracts, which have a limit of $15,000. [S1]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear or respond, you may request a default judgment. However, you must still provide evidence to support your claim. [S1]

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: Only the defendant has the right to appeal a small claims judgment, and must do so within 15 days by filing a notice of appeal and posting a bond. [S4]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: A small claims judgment is valid for 20 years, during which time you can take steps to collect the judgment. [S4]
