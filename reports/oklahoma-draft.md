## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $10,000 for individuals; $10,000 for businesses [S3, S9].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds $10,000, you cannot split it into multiple small claims cases. You must either waive the excess amount or file in a higher court. [S3]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S3].

## 11. Defendant's response, default, and counterclaims

- **Must file written answer?** No — Defendants are not required to file a written answer but must appear at the hearing to avoid default judgment.
- **Response deadline:** Not specified by statute; defendants must appear at the hearing to avoid default judgment.
- **Default process:** If the defendant fails to appear, the court may grant a default judgment in favor of the plaintiff.
- **Prove-up required for default?** Yes — Even if the defendant defaults, the plaintiff must present evidence to support the claim before a judgment is granted.
- **Motion to vacate default:** form SC-5 (Motion to Vacate Judgment); standard deadline 30 days; lack-of-notice exception 180 days; appeal denial within 30 days. If the motion to vacate is denied, the defendant may appeal the denial within 30 days.
- **Counterclaim:** allowed true; same monetary cap: true; service deadlines 3/5 days. Form: SC-4 (Counterclaim).
- **If counterclaim exceeds the cap:** transfer available: true; If a counterclaim exceeds $10,000, the case may be transferred to the regular civil docket.

## 14. Post-judgment collection

- **Payment deadline expectation:** 30 days.
- **Post-judgment interest rate:** 5.25% — Okla. Stat. tit. 12, § 727.1. Interest accrues from the date of judgment until paid.

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File an application for wage garnishment with the court, serve the employer, and the employer will withhold a portion of the debtor's wages to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees  
**Effectiveness:** Most effective when debtor is W-2 employed at a stable job.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings (federal CCPA floor)

### Bank levy

Seize funds from debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, serve it on the debtor's bank, and the bank will freeze and turn over funds to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $50-100 plus sheriff fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor maintains sufficient funds in bank accounts.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Social Security and federal benefits

### Property lien

Place a lien on debtor's real property to secure the judgment.

File a certified copy of the judgment with the county clerk where the debtor owns property, creating a lien that must be satisfied upon sale or refinancing.

**Estimated cost:** $10-20 filing fee  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor owns real property and intends to sell or refinance.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $75,000

### Writ of execution

Seize and sell debtor's non-exempt personal property to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, have the sheriff seize non-exempt property, and sell it at public auction to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $50-100 plus sheriff fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor owns valuable non-exempt personal property.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Personal property up to $4,000

- **Wage garnishment cap:** 25% of disposable earnings.
- **Judgment lifespan:** 5 years; renewal: File a motion to renew the judgment before it expires; if granted, the judgment is extended for another 5 years.
- **Debtor's exam form:** SC-13 (Application for Debtor's Examination).
- **Exempt assets:**
  - Homestead — $75,000
  - Vehicle — $7,500
  - Retirement accounts (federally and state-protected)
  - Social Security and federal benefits
- **Bankruptcy effect:** Filing for bankruptcy imposes an automatic stay on collection efforts; certain judgments may be discharged in bankruptcy.
- **Satisfaction of judgment form:** SC-14 (Satisfaction of Judgment).
- **Multiple creditors priority:** Priority among multiple judgment creditors is generally determined by the order in which liens are filed.

## 15. Special rules for businesses

- **Self-representation allowed?** true — Businesses can appear without an attorney in small claims court.
- **Can a business send an employee instead of an attorney?** true.
- **Can a business send an attorney?** true.

- **Sole proprietor employee exception:** Sole proprietors may send a designated employee to represent them in court.
- **Out-of-state business plaintiffs:** must register with Secretary of State first: true — Okla. Stat. tit. 18, § 1130.

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Incorrect defendant information

**What goes wrong:** Suing the wrong party or using incorrect names can result in dismissal or unenforceable judgments.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the exact legal name and address of the defendant before filing. [unverified]

### Missing statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations has expired will lead to dismissal of the case.

**How to avoid it:** Confirm the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type before filing. [unverified]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** Failure to properly serve the defendant can delay the case or result in dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Follow Oklahoma's rules for service of process carefully, using authorized methods. [unverified]

### Insufficient evidence

**What goes wrong:** Lack of adequate evidence can lead to losing the case, even if your claim is valid.

**How to avoid it:** Gather all relevant documents, photos, and witness statements to support your claim. [unverified]

### Ignoring counterclaims

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant files a counterclaim and you don't respond, you could lose by default.

**How to avoid it:** Address any counterclaims promptly and prepare a defense. [unverified]

### Failing to collect judgment

**What goes wrong:** Winning a judgment doesn't guarantee payment; you must take steps to collect.

**How to avoid it:** Learn about collection methods like garnishment and liens, and act promptly. [unverified]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in Oklahoma small claims court. The process is designed to be accessible for individuals representing themselves, though you may choose to hire an attorney if you prefer.

**Q: How long does it take?**
A: Small claims cases in Oklahoma are typically scheduled for hearing within 30 to 60 days after filing, providing a relatively quick resolution compared to other court processes.

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**
A: The maximum amount you can sue for in Oklahoma small claims court is $10,000, excluding attorney fees and court costs.

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**
A: If the defendant fails to appear at the hearing, the court may grant a default judgment in your favor. However, you must still present evidence to support your claim.

**Q: Can I appeal?**
A: Yes, either party can appeal a small claims judgment by filing a notice of appeal within 30 days. The appeal results in a new trial (trial de novo) in the district court.

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**
A: A judgment in Oklahoma is valid for 5 years and can be renewed before it expires to extend its enforceability.
