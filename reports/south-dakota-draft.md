## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $12,000 for individuals; $12,000 for businesses [S1, S11].
- **Splitting a claim:** If a claim exceeds $12,000, you cannot split it into multiple claims to fit within the small claims limit. Instead, you must file in a higher court. [S4]

- **Defamation** — Claims involving defamation are not suitable for small claims court due to the complexity and potential damages involved. Where to go instead: Circuit Court. [S5]
- **Malpractice** — Professional malpractice claims exceed the jurisdiction of small claims court and require filing in a higher court. Where to go instead: Circuit Court. [S5]
- **Family law matters** — Issues such as divorce, child custody, and support are handled by family courts, not small claims court. Where to go instead: Family Court. [S5]
- **Probate matters** — Probate issues, including wills and estates, are outside the jurisdiction of small claims court. Where to go instead: Probate Court. [S5]
- **Federal claims** — Claims against the federal government, including bankruptcy, patents, and trademarks, are not handled in small claims court. Where to go instead: Federal Court. [S5]
- **Class actions** — Class action lawsuits are complex and exceed the jurisdiction of small claims court. Where to go instead: Circuit Court. [S5]
- **Injunctions/specific performance** — Small claims court cannot issue injunctions or orders for specific performance; these require higher court intervention. Where to go instead: Circuit Court. [S5]

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File a writ of garnishment with the court, serve the employer, and the employer withholds a portion of the debtor's wages to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees

### Bank levy

Seize funds from debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, serve it on the debtor's bank, and the bank freezes and transfers funds to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor has sufficient funds in the account.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $1,000 in bank account is exempt

### Property lien

Place a lien on debtor's real property to secure the judgment.

File a judgment lien with the county recorder's office, which attaches to the debtor's real property and must be satisfied upon sale or refinancing.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor owns real property; may take time to realize payment.

**What's protected (exemptions):**

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, have the sheriff seize non-exempt property, and sell it at public auction to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $50-100 plus sheriff fees

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Incorrect defendant information

**What goes wrong:** If you sue the wrong person or business entity, the court may dismiss your case.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the exact legal name and address of the defendant before filing. [S1]

### Missing statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations has expired will result in dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Confirm the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type before filing. [S5]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant is not properly served, the case cannot proceed.

**How to avoid it:** Follow the court's guidelines for serving the defendant correctly. [S1]

### Insufficient evidence

**What goes wrong:** Without adequate evidence, the court may rule against you.

**How to avoid it:** Gather all relevant documents, photos, and witness statements to support your claim. [S1]

### Ignoring court deadlines

**What goes wrong:** Missing deadlines can lead to dismissal or default judgment.

**How to avoid it:** Keep track of all court dates and deadlines, and respond promptly. [S1]

### Overestimating claim amount

**What goes wrong:** Claiming more than $12,000 will disqualify your case from small claims court.

**How to avoid it:** Ensure your claim does not exceed the small claims limit, or consider filing in a higher court. [S1]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, small claims court is designed for individuals to represent themselves without a lawyer. However, you may choose to hire one if you prefer. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: The process typically takes between 30 to 90 days from filing to resolution, depending on the court's schedule and case complexity. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: You can sue for up to $12,000 in small claims court in South Dakota. [S1]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear, the court may enter a default judgment in your favor, provided you present sufficient evidence. [S1]

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: Yes, either party can appeal the decision to the Circuit Court within 30 days for a new trial. [S1]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: A judgment in South Dakota is valid for 20 years and can be renewed before expiration. [S5]
