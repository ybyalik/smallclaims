## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $6,500 for individuals; $6,500 for businesses [S4].
- **Splitting a claim:** If a claim exceeds the $6,500 cap, you cannot split it into multiple claims to fit within the small claims limit. You must either waive the excess amount or file in a higher court. [S4]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S4].

## 2. What you can sue for

### Contracts and money owed

Iowa allows you to file claims up to $6,500 for disputes involving contracts and money owed. The statute of limitations varies depending on the type of contract.

- **Breach of a written contract** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A contractor signed a $4,000 contract to remodel your kitchen, took the deposit, and never finished.
  - **Statute:** Iowa Code § 614.1(5). 10-year deadline from breach. Bring the signed contract, payment proof, and any correspondence showing the breach. [S4]
- **Breach of an oral contract** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You agreed verbally to lend a friend $2,000, and they failed to repay it.
  - **Statute:** Iowa Code § 614.1(4). 5-year deadline from breach. Gather any evidence of the agreement, such as emails or witness statements. [S4]
- **Unpaid personal loan** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You loaned $3,000 to a colleague with the understanding it would be repaid in six months, but they haven't paid you back.
  - **Statute:** Iowa Code § 614.1(4). 5-year deadline from breach. Provide documentation of the loan agreement and any repayment attempts. [S4]
- **Default on a promissory note** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A borrower signed a promissory note to repay $5,000 over a year but stopped making payments after two months.
  - **Statute:** Iowa Code § 614.1(5). 10-year deadline from breach. Present the original promissory note and records of payments made. [S4]
- **Unpaid invoice for services rendered** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You provided consulting services worth $4,500 to a client who has not paid the invoice issued three months ago.
  - **Statute:** Iowa Code § 614.1(4). 5-year deadline from breach. Submit the invoice, service agreement, and any communication regarding payment. [S4]
- **Unpaid balance on an open account** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A customer has an outstanding balance of $2,000 on their account with your business and has ignored payment requests.
  - **Statute:** Iowa Code § 614.1(4). 5-year deadline from breach. Provide account statements and records of payment history. [S4]
- **Unpaid rent by tenant** — eligible.
  - **Example:** Your tenant owes $1,500 in unpaid rent and has vacated the property without settling the debt.
  - **Statute:** Iowa Code § 614.1(4). 5-year deadline from breach. Present the lease agreement and records of unpaid rent. [S4]
- **Payment with a bad check** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A customer paid for goods worth $800 with a check that was returned for insufficient funds.
  - **Statute:** Iowa Code § 554.3513. 3-year deadline from dishonor. Provide the bounced check and any bank notices. [S4]
  - **Special remedy:** Check amount plus service charge up to $40 plus civil penalty up to $100 or 2x check amount, whichever is greater.
- **Co-signer seeking repayment** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You co-signed a loan for $5,000, and the primary borrower defaulted, leaving you responsible for the debt.
  - **Statute:** Iowa Code § 614.1(4). 5-year deadline from breach. Provide the loan agreement and records of payments made. [S4]

- **Injunctions/specific performance** — Small claims court cannot issue orders requiring specific actions. Where to go instead: District Court. [unverified]

## 8. Pre-filing requirements

- **Demand letter required?** No — While not required, sending a demand letter can facilitate settlement and demonstrate good faith. [S4]
- **Bad-check notice rule:** A 30-day written notice is required before pursuing statutory penalties for a bad check. [S4]
- **Wage-claim demand rule:** A written demand for unpaid wages is recommended before filing a claim. [S4]
- **Government claim notice:** Not required by statute.
- **Landlord-tenant pre-filing:** Landlords must provide a written itemization of damages within 30 days of lease termination. [S4]
- **Consumer protection pre-filing:** Certain consumer protection claims may require prior notice to the defendant. [S4]
- **Mandatory mediation/ADR before filing:** none [S4]
- **Arbitration clauses in contracts:** courts will enforce — Arbitration clauses in contracts are generally enforced by Iowa courts. [S4]

  Filed by: plaintiff. Required: true. Initiates a small claims action for a money judgment, eviction, return of personal property, recovery of specific personal property, and recovery of property. [S1]

## 10. Service of process

- **Who can serve:**
  - Sheriff
  - Certified mail by clerk
  - Private process server
- **Methods accepted:**
  ### Sheriff personal service
  Sheriff or constable personally hands the papers to the defendant.
  - Pros: Most reliable, Official
  - Cons: May be slower, Additional cost
  ### Certified mail by clerk
  Clerk sends the papers via certified mail to the defendant.
  - Pros: Less expensive, Proof of mailing
  - Cons: Defendant may refuse delivery, Not suitable for all cases
  ### Private process server
  A private individual authorized to serve legal documents delivers the papers to the defendant.
  - Pros: Flexible scheduling, Can be faster
  - Cons: Additional cost, Must ensure proper authorization
- **Timing:** 20 days (in county); 20 days (out of county).
- **Proof of service form:** Form 3.25 (Return of Service); file at least 3 days before the hearing.
- **Business service:** Serve the registered agent or an officer of the corporation; for sole proprietors, serve the individual directly.
- **Out-of-state defendant:** Service must comply with Iowa rules and the rules of the state where the defendant is located.
- **If you can't locate the defendant:** If the defendant cannot be located, service by publication may be permitted with court approval.
- **If the defendant is dodging service:** If the defendant is evading service, document attempts and seek court permission for alternative methods.

## 11. Defendant's response, default, and counterclaims

- **Must file written answer?** No — The defendant must appear at the hearing or risk a default judgment; a written answer is not required but may be filed. [S1]
- **Response deadline:** 20 days from service. [S1]
- **Default process:** If the defendant fails to appear, the court may enter a default judgment in favor of the plaintiff. [S1]
- **Prove-up required for default?** Yes — Even if the defendant defaults, the plaintiff must provide evidence to support the claim. [S1]
- **Motion to vacate default:** form 3.26 (Motion to Set Aside Default Judgment); standard deadline 10 days; lack-of-notice exception 60 days; appeal denial within 20 days. If the motion to vacate is denied, the defendant may appeal within 20 days. [S1]
- **Counterclaim:** allowed true; same monetary cap: true; service deadlines 20/20 days. Form: 3.27 (Counterclaim). [S4]
- **If counterclaim exceeds the cap:** transfer available: true; If the counterclaim exceeds the small claims limit, the case may be transferred to district court. [S4]

## 12. Hearing procedure

- **Lawyers allowed at trial?** Yes — Parties may be represented by attorneys but are not required to do so. [S1]
- **Format:** Bench trial, typically 30-60 minutes per case.; presider: judge. [S1]
- **Burden of proof:** preponderance. [S1]
- **What to bring:**
  - Originals of any contracts
  - Receipts and bank records
  - Photos and videos
  - Names and contact info for witnesses
- **Subpoenas:** form 3.28 (Subpoena); witness fee $10 per day. [S1]
- **Free interpreters:** true — Request an interpreter through the court clerk at least 10 days before the hearing. [S1]
- **Jury option:** false. [S1]
- **Decision timing:** On the spot or mailed within 2 weeks. [S1]
- **On-site mediation:** by county — Some counties offer mediation services on the day of the hearing. [S1]

## 14. Post-judgment collection

- **Payment deadline expectation:** 30 days. [S1]
- **Post-judgment interest rate:** 5% — Iowa Code § 535.3. Interest accrues on unpaid judgments at the statutory rate. [S1]

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File an application for garnishment with the court, serve the employer, and the employer withholds a portion of the debtor's wages to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Most effective when debtor is W-2 employed at a stable job.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings (federal CCPA floor)

### Bank levy

Seize funds from debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, serve the bank, and the bank freezes and turns over funds to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor has sufficient funds in the account.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $1,000 in bank account is exempt

### Property lien

Place a lien on debtor's real property to secure the judgment.

File the judgment with the county recorder to place a lien on the debtor's property, which must be satisfied before the property can be sold or refinanced.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor owns real property and intends to sell or refinance.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption applies

### Writ of execution

Seize and sell debtor's non-exempt property to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, the sheriff seizes non-exempt property, sells it at auction, and applies proceeds to the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $50-100 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor owns valuable non-exempt property.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Various personal property exemptions apply

- **Wage garnishment cap:** 25% of disposable earnings.
- **Judgment lifespan:** 20 years; renewal: File a petition for renewal before the judgment expires; no fee required.
- **Debtor's exam form:** 3.33 (Application for Debtor Examination).
- **Exempt assets:**
  - Homestead — unlimited
  - Vehicle — up to $7,000
  - Retirement accounts — fully exempt
  - Social Security and federal benefits — fully exempt
- **Bankruptcy effect:** Filing for bankruptcy may discharge the judgment; certain debts are non-dischargeable.
- **Satisfaction of judgment form:** 3.34 (Satisfaction of Judgment).
- **Multiple creditors priority:** Priority among creditors is generally determined by the order in which liens are filed. [S1]

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Wrong defendant naming

**What goes wrong:** If you sue 'John's Garage' but the legal entity is 'JG Auto Repair LLC', the judgment may be unenforceable against the LLC's assets.

**How to avoid it:** Search the Secretary of State business records before filing and use the exact registered name; for sole proprietors use the individual's full legal name. [S1]

### Missed statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing one day after the deadline gets the case dismissed with prejudice.

**How to avoid it:** Check the SOL for your specific claim type before filing; for fraud, the discovery rule applies but be prepared to prove when you reasonably could have discovered it. [S4]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant isn't properly served, the case can be delayed or dismissed.

**How to avoid it:** Follow Iowa's service rules carefully; consider using the sheriff for service to ensure compliance. [S1]

### Filing in the wrong venue

**What goes wrong:** The case may be dismissed or transferred, causing delays and additional costs.

**How to avoid it:** File in the county where the defendant resides or where the cause of action arose. [S1]

### Failing to collect judgment

**What goes wrong:** Winning a case doesn't guarantee payment; you may need to take additional steps to collect.

**How to avoid it:** Be prepared to pursue collection methods like garnishment or liens if the defendant doesn't pay voluntarily. [S1]

### Overlooking exemptions in collection

**What goes wrong:** Certain debtor assets are protected from collection, limiting recovery options.

**How to avoid it:** Understand Iowa's exemption laws to assess the feasibility of collection efforts. [S1]

## 18. Recent changes

- **2023-07-01** — **Increase in small claims jurisdictional limit**. The monetary limit for small claims cases increased from $5,000 to $6,500, allowing more cases to be heard in small claims court. Iowa Code § 631.1. [S4]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in small claims court. The process is designed to be accessible for individuals representing themselves, though you may choose to hire an attorney if you prefer. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: Small claims cases are typically resolved within 30 to 90 days from filing, depending on the court's schedule and the complexity of the case. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: As of July 1, 2023, the maximum amount you can sue for in Iowa small claims court is $6,500, excluding interest and court costs. [S4]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear at the hearing, the court may enter a default judgment in your favor, provided you present sufficient evidence to support your claim. [S1]

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: Yes, either party can appeal a small claims decision to the district court within 20 days of the judgment. The appeal will be a new trial (trial de novo) in district court. [S7]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: In Iowa, a judgment is valid for 20 years from the date it is entered. You may need to take steps to renew the judgment before it expires to maintain its enforceability. [S4]
