## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $20,000 for individuals; $20,000 for businesses [S4].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds $20,000, you cannot split it into multiple claims to fit within the small claims limit. You must either reduce your claim to $20,000 or file in a higher court. [S4]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S4].

## 3. What you CANNOT sue for

- **Eviction (unlawful detainer)** — Small claims courts in Utah do not have jurisdiction over eviction proceedings. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]
- **Title to real estate** — Disputes involving the title to real property are beyond the jurisdiction of small claims courts. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]
- **Defamation** — Claims involving defamation, such as libel or slander, are not handled in small claims courts. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]
- **Malpractice** — Professional malpractice claims require more complex proceedings than small claims courts can provide. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]
- **Family law matters** — Issues such as divorce, child custody, and support are outside the jurisdiction of small claims courts. Where to go instead: Family Court. [S1]
- **Probate matters** — Disputes involving wills, estates, and trusts are not handled in small claims courts. Where to go instead: Probate Court. [S1]
- **Claims against the federal government** — Small claims courts do not have jurisdiction over claims against federal entities. Where to go instead: Federal Court. [S1]
- **Class actions** — Class action lawsuits are beyond the scope of small claims courts. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]
- **Injunctions and specific performance** — Small claims courts can only award monetary damages, not equitable relief. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]
- **Workers' compensation claims** — These claims are handled by the Utah Labor Commission, not small claims courts. Where to go instead: Utah Labor Commission. [S1]

## 15. Special rules for businesses

- **Self-representation allowed?** Yes — Businesses can represent themselves in small claims court without an attorney. [S1]
- **Can a business send an employee instead of an attorney?** Yes [S1].
- **Can a business send an attorney?** Yes [S1].
- **Form for non-attorney representation:** SC-13 (Authorization to Appear)
- **Insurance adjuster representation:** Not allowed [S1].
- **Sole proprietor employee exception:** Sole proprietors can send a designated employee to represent them in court. [S1]
- **Out-of-state business plaintiffs:** must register with Secretary of State first: Yes — Utah Code § 16-10a-1501. [S1]

- **Licensing notes:** Unlicensed contractors may face limitations in enforcing contracts in court. [S1]

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Filing in the wrong venue

**What goes wrong:** Filing in the incorrect court can lead to dismissal or transfer, causing delays and additional costs.

**How to avoid it:** Ensure you file in the county where the defendant resides or where the claim arose. [S1]

### Exceeding the monetary limit

**What goes wrong:** Claims exceeding $20,000 are not eligible for small claims court and must be filed in a higher court.

**How to avoid it:** Verify that your claim amount, excluding interest and court costs, does not exceed $20,000. [S4]

### Missing the statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations has expired will result in dismissal of your case.

**How to avoid it:** Be aware of the specific time limits for your type of claim and file accordingly. [S4]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** Failure to properly serve the defendant can delay proceedings or result in dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Follow the court's rules for service of process carefully, using approved methods. [S14]

### Not bringing necessary evidence

**What goes wrong:** Lack of evidence can weaken your case and lead to an unfavorable outcome.

**How to avoid it:** Gather and bring all relevant documents, photos, and witness information to court. [S1]

### Failing to collect on a judgment

**What goes wrong:** Winning a judgment does not guarantee payment; you may need to take additional steps to collect.

**How to avoid it:** Familiarize yourself with post-judgment collection methods and be prepared to use them. [S17]

## 18. Recent changes

- **2025-01-01** — **Increase in small claims monetary limit**. The maximum amount for small claims cases increased from $15,000 to $20,000. Utah Code § 78A-8-102. [S4]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in small claims court. The process is designed to be accessible for individuals representing themselves. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: It typically takes at least 45 days to get a judgment in small claims court, depending on the court's schedule and the specifics of your case. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: As of January 1, 2025, the maximum amount you can sue for in Utah's small claims court is $20,000, excluding interest and court costs. [S4]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear at the scheduled hearing, the court may enter a default judgment in favor of the plaintiff. [S11]

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: Yes, either party can appeal a small claims judgment by filing a notice of appeal within 10 days of the judgment. [S15]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: A small claims judgment in Utah is valid for 8 years and can be renewed before it expires. [S4]
