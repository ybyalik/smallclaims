## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $2,500 for individuals; $2,500 for businesses [S5].
- **Annual filing cap:** Plaintiffs are limited to filing no more than 25 small claims in a calendar year. Ky. Rev. Stat. § 24A.230 [S5].
- **Splitting a claim:** If a claim exceeds $2,500, you must either waive the excess and file in small claims court or file the entire claim in a higher court. Splitting one dispute into multiple claims to stay within the limit is not allowed. [S5]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S5].

## 3. What you CANNOT sue for

- **Eviction (unlawful detainer)** — Small claims court does not handle eviction proceedings; these are filed in the District Court's regular civil division. Where to go instead: District Court Civil Division. [S5]
- **Title to real estate** — Disputes involving the title to real estate are beyond the jurisdiction of small claims court. Where to go instead: Circuit Court. [S5]
- **Defamation (libel and slander)** — Small claims court does not handle defamation cases, including libel and slander. Where to go instead: Circuit Court. [S5]
- **Malpractice** — Professional malpractice claims exceed the jurisdictional limits and complexity suitable for small claims court. Where to go instead: Circuit Court. [S5]
- **Family law matters** — Issues such as divorce, child custody, and support are not handled in small claims court. Where to go instead: Family Court. [S5]
- **Probate matters** — Disputes involving wills, estates, and trusts are outside the jurisdiction of small claims court. Where to go instead: Probate Court. [S5]
- **Claims against the federal government** — Small claims court does not have jurisdiction over claims against federal agencies. Where to go instead: Federal Court. [S5]
- **Class actions** — Class action lawsuits are beyond the scope of small claims court procedures. Where to go instead: Circuit Court. [S5]
- **Injunctions and specific performance** — Small claims court cannot issue orders requiring a party to do or refrain from doing something. Where to go instead: Circuit Court. [S5]
- **Workers' compensation claims** — These claims are handled by the Kentucky Department of Workers' Claims, not small claims court. Where to go instead: Kentucky Department of Workers' Claims. [S5]

## 10. Service of process

- **Who can serve:**
  - Sheriff
  - Certified mail by clerk
  - Private process server

- **Methods accepted:**

  ### Certified mail
  The court clerk sends the summons and complaint to the defendant via certified mail with return receipt requested.
  - Pros: Cost-effective, Provides proof of service
  - Cons: Defendant may refuse delivery, Not suitable if address is unknown

  ### Sheriff personal service
  A sheriff or constable personally delivers the summons and complaint to the defendant.
  - Pros: Reliable, Official service
  - Cons: Higher cost, May take longer

  ### Private process server
  A private individual authorized to serve legal documents delivers the summons and complaint to the defendant.
  - Pros: Flexible scheduling, Can be faster
  - Cons: Additional cost, Must ensure proper authorization

- **Timing:** 7 days (in county); 10 days (out of county).
- **Proof of service form:** AOC-181 (Return of Service); file at least 3 days before the hearing.
- **Business service:** Serve the registered agent or an officer of the corporation; for sole proprietors, serve the individual owner.
- **Out-of-state defendant:** Service must comply with Kentucky's long-arm statute and the laws of the state where the defendant resides.
- **If you can't locate the defendant:** If the defendant cannot be located, service by publication may be permitted with court approval.
- **If the defendant is dodging service:** If the defendant is evading service, you may request alternative service methods from the court.

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of the debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File a garnishment order with the court; serve the employer; employer withholds a portion of wages and remits to the court until the debt is satisfied.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Most effective when debtor is W-2 employed at a stable job.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings (federal CCPA floor)

### Bank levy

Seize funds from the debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court; serve the bank; bank freezes and turns over funds up to the judgment amount.

**Estimated cost:** $50-100 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor has sufficient funds in the account; may be limited by exemptions.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $1,000 in personal property, including bank accounts

### Property lien

Place a lien on the debtor's real property to secure the judgment debt.

File a judgment lien with the county clerk where the property is located; lien must be satisfied before property can be sold or refinanced.

**Estimated cost:** $20-50 filing fee
**Effectiveness:** Effective for securing the debt; payment occurs when property is sold or refinanced.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $5,000

### Writ of execution

Seize and sell the debtor's non-exempt personal property to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court; sheriff seizes property; property is sold at public auction; proceeds applied to judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $50-100 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor owns valuable non-exempt property; process can be time-consuming.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $1,000 in personal property

- **Wage garnishment cap:** 25% of disposable earnings. [S5]
- **Judgment lifespan:** 15 years; renewal: File a motion to revive the judgment before the 15-year period expires; court may grant renewal for an additional period. [S5]
- **Debtor's exam form:** AOC-130 (Order for Examination of Judgment Debtor). [S5]
- **Exempt assets:**
  - Homestead — $5,000
  - Vehicle — $2,500
  - Retirement accounts (federally and state-protected)
  - Social Security and federal benefits
- **Bankruptcy effect:** Filing for bankruptcy imposes an automatic stay on collection efforts; certain judgments may be discharged in bankruptcy. [S5]
- **Satisfaction of judgment form:** AOC-135 (Satisfaction of Judgment). [S5]
- **Multiple creditors priority:** Judgment liens are prioritized by the date of filing; earlier liens have higher priority over later ones. [S5]

## 15. Special rules for businesses

- **Self-representation allowed?** Yes — Businesses can represent themselves in small claims court without an attorney. [S5]
- **Can a business send an employee instead of an attorney?** Yes [S5].
- **Can a business send an attorney?** Yes [S5].
- **Form for non-attorney representation:** AOC-285 (Corporate Authorization to Represent) [S5].
- **Insurance adjuster representation:** Not allowed [S5].
- **Sole proprietor employee exception:** Sole proprietors may authorize an employee to represent them in small claims court. [S5]
- **Out-of-state business plaintiffs:** must register with Secretary of State first: Yes — Ky. Rev. Stat. § 14A.9-010. [S5]
- **Trade-name (DBA) rule:** Businesses operating under a fictitious name must file a Certificate of Assumed Name with the county clerk.; form: AOC-286 (Certificate of Assumed Name) [S5].
- **Licensing notes:** Unlicensed contractors may face limitations in enforcing contracts in court. [S5]

## 16. County-level differences

No material county-level differences; small claims procedure is uniform statewide.

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Wrong defendant naming

**What goes wrong:** If you sue 'John's Garage' but the legal entity is 'JG Auto Repair LLC', the judgment may be unenforceable against the LLC's assets.

**How to avoid it:** Search the Secretary of State business records before filing and use the exact registered name; for sole proprietors, use the individual's full legal name. [S5]

### Missed statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing one day after the deadline gets the case dismissed with prejudice.

**How to avoid it:** Check the statute of limitations for your specific claim type before filing; for fraud, the discovery rule applies but be prepared to prove when you reasonably could have discovered it. [S5]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant is not properly served, the case cannot proceed, and you may have to start over.

**How to avoid it:** Follow Kentucky's rules for service of process carefully; consider using the sheriff for personal service to ensure compliance. [S5]

### Filing in the wrong venue

**What goes wrong:** The case may be dismissed or transferred, causing delays and additional costs.

**How to avoid it:** File in the county where the defendant lives or does business, or where the cause of action arose. [S5]

### Exceeding the claim limit

**What goes wrong:** Claims over $2,500 are not allowed in small claims court and will be dismissed or transferred.

**How to avoid it:** If your claim exceeds $2,500, consider waiving the excess to stay within the limit or file in a higher court. [S5]

### Failing to appear at the hearing

**What goes wrong:** If you don't show up, the case may be dismissed, or a default judgment may be entered against you.

**How to avoid it:** Mark your calendar and make arrangements to attend the hearing; if you have a conflict, request a continuance in advance. [S5]

## 18. Recent changes

- **2024-01-01** — **Increase in small claims filing fees**. Filing fees for small claims cases were increased to $82 for claims up to $1,500 and $102 for claims between $1,501 and $2,500. Ky. Rev. Stat. § 24A.230. [S5]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in small claims court; the process is designed for individuals to represent themselves. [S5]

**Q: How long does it take?**
A: Hearings are typically scheduled within 20 to 40 days after the defendant is served. [S5]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**
A: The maximum amount you can sue for in Kentucky small claims court is $2,500, excluding interest and court costs. [S5]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**
A: If the defendant fails to appear, the court may enter a default judgment in your favor. [S5]

**Q: Can I appeal?**
A: Yes, either party can appeal the decision to the Circuit Court within 10 days of the judgment. [S5]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**
A: A judgment is valid for 15 years and can be renewed before it expires. [S5]
