## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $5,000 for individuals; $5,000 for businesses [S1, S4].
- **Splitting a claim:** If a claim exceeds the $5,000 cap, you cannot split it into multiple claims to fit within the limit. Instead, you must file in a higher court. [S5]

- **Wage garnishment cap:** 25% of disposable earnings.
- **Judgment lifespan:** 20 years; renewal: File a motion to renew the judgment before it expires; consult the court for specific procedures and fees.
- **Debtor's exam form:** Form 12 (Motion for Examination of Judgment Debtor).
- **Exempt assets:**
  - Homestead — $500,000
  - Vehicle — $12,000
  - Retirement accounts (federally and/or state-protected)
  - Social Security and federal benefits
- **Bankruptcy effect:** Filing for bankruptcy may discharge certain judgments; consult an attorney for specific advice.
- **Satisfaction of judgment form:** Form 13 (Satisfaction of Judgment).
- **Multiple creditors priority:** Priority among multiple judgment creditors is generally determined by the order in which liens are recorded.

## 15. Special rules for businesses

- **Self-representation allowed?** Yes — Businesses may represent themselves in small claims court without an attorney. [S1]
- **Can a business send an employee instead of an attorney?** Yes [S1].
- **Can a business send an attorney?** Yes [S1].
- **Form for non-attorney representation:** Form 14 (Authorization for Non-Attorney Representation) [S1].
- **Insurance adjuster representation:** Not allowed [S1].
- **Sole proprietor employee exception:** Sole proprietors may authorize an employee to represent them in small claims court [S1].
- **Out-of-state business plaintiffs:** must register with Secretary of State first: Yes — R.I. Gen. Laws § 7-1.2-1410 [S1].
- **Trade-name (DBA) rule:** Businesses operating under a fictitious name must file a statement with the Secretary of State; form: Form 15 (Fictitious Business Name Statement) [S1].
- **Licensing notes:** Unlicensed contractors may be barred from recovering fees for services requiring a license [S1].

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Waiver of appeal rights

**What goes wrong:** As a plaintiff, you waive your right to appeal the court's decision in small claims cases.

**How to avoid it:** Be certain of your case's strength before filing in small claims court, as you cannot appeal an unfavorable decision. [S12]

### Defendant's short appeal window

**What goes wrong:** Defendants have only 2 days to file an appeal after a judgment is entered.

**How to avoid it:** If you're a defendant, be prepared to act quickly if you intend to appeal a small claims judgment. [S12]

### Service of process errors

**What goes wrong:** Improper service can result in case dismissal or delays.

**How to avoid it:** Ensure you follow the correct procedures for serving the defendant, using authorized methods and personnel. [S1]

### Exceeding monetary limits

**What goes wrong:** Claims exceeding $5,000 are not eligible for small claims court and may be dismissed.

**How to avoid it:** Verify that your claim amount, excluding interest and court costs, does not exceed the $5,000 limit. [S1]

### Filing in the wrong venue

**What goes wrong:** Filing in an incorrect venue can lead to dismissal or transfer, causing delays.

**How to avoid it:** File your claim in the appropriate county based on the defendant's residence or where the cause of action arose. [S1]

### Ignoring statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations has expired will result in dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Confirm the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type before filing. [S6]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in Rhode Island's small claims court. The process is designed to be accessible for individuals representing themselves. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: Most small claims cases in Rhode Island are resolved within 30 to 90 days from filing to judgment. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: The maximum amount you can sue for in Rhode Island's small claims court is $5,000, excluding interest and court costs. [S1]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear, the court may enter a default judgment in favor of the plaintiff, provided the plaintiff presents sufficient evidence to support the claim. [S1]

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: As a plaintiff, you waive your right to appeal in small claims court. Defendants have 2 days to file an appeal after judgment. [S12]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: A judgment in Rhode Island is valid for 20 years and can be renewed before it expires. [S6]
