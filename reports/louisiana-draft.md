## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $5,000 for individuals; $5,000 for businesses [S3, S9].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds $5,000, you cannot split it into multiple claims to fit within the small claims limit. You must either waive the excess amount or file in a higher court. [S3]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S3].

### Contracts and money owed

Louisiana allows you to file claims for breaches of contract and money owed up to $5,000 in small claims court. The statute of limitations varies depending on the type of contract.

- **Breach of a written contract** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A contractor signed a $4,000 contract to remodel your kitchen, took the deposit, and never finished.
  - **Statute:** La. Civ. Code art. 3499. 10-year deadline from breach. Bring the signed contract, payment proof, and any correspondence showing the breach. [S6]

- **Breach of an oral contract** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You agreed verbally to lend someone $2,000, and they failed to repay it.
  - **Statute:** La. Civ. Code art. 3494. 3-year deadline from breach. Provide evidence of the agreement and any partial payments. [S6]

  - **Example:** You loaned a friend $1,500 with the understanding they'd repay in six months, but they haven't.
  - **Statute:** La. Civ. Code art. 3494. 3-year deadline from due date. Document the loan agreement and any communications regarding repayment. [S6]

- **Default on a promissory note** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A borrower signed a promissory note for $3,000 and failed to make the agreed payments.
  - **Statute:** La. Civ. Code art. 3498. 5-year deadline from due date. Present the original promissory note and records of missed payments. [S6]

  - **Example:** You provided $2,500 worth of consulting services, invoiced the client, and they haven't paid.

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Wrong defendant naming

**What goes wrong:** If you sue 'John's Garage' but the legal entity is 'JG Auto Repair LLC', the judgment may be unenforceable against the LLC's assets.

**How to avoid it:** Search the Secretary of State business records before filing and use the exact registered name; for sole proprietors use the individual's full legal name. [S1]

### Missed statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing one day after the deadline gets the case dismissed with prejudice.

**How to avoid it:** Check the statute of limitations for your specific claim type before filing; for fraud, the discovery rule applies but be prepared to prove when you reasonably could have discovered it. [S6]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant isn't properly served, the case can be delayed or dismissed.

**How to avoid it:** Follow Louisiana's service rules carefully; consider using the sheriff for service to ensure compliance. [S10]

### Filing in the wrong venue

**What goes wrong:** The defendant can request the court to transfer or dismiss the case if filed in the wrong venue.

**How to avoid it:** File in the correct parish based on the defendant's residence or where the cause of action arose. [S1]

### Ignoring counterclaims

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant files a counterclaim exceeding the small claims limit, the case may be transferred to a higher court.

**How to avoid it:** Be prepared to address potential counterclaims and understand the implications for your case. [S11]

### Failing to collect judgment

**What goes wrong:** Winning a judgment doesn't guarantee payment; you must take steps to collect.

**How to avoid it:** Familiarize yourself with collection methods like wage garnishment and property liens. [S12]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in Louisiana small claims court. The process is designed to be accessible for individuals representing themselves, though you may choose to hire an attorney if you prefer. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**
A: The typical timeline for a small claims case in Louisiana ranges from 30 to 90 days from filing to resolution, depending on the court's schedule and complexity of the case. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**
A: In Louisiana small claims court, you can sue for up to $5,000, excluding interest, court costs, and attorney fees. [S3]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**
A: If the defendant fails to appear in court, the judge may grant a default judgment in your favor. However, you must still present evidence to support your claim. [S4]

**Q: Can I appeal?**
A: Yes, either party can appeal a small claims judgment to the District Court within 15 days. The appeal is conducted as a new trial (trial de novo). [S11]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**
A: A judgment in Louisiana is valid for 10 years and can be renewed before it expires to extend its enforceability. [S6]
