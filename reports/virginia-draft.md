## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $5,000 for individuals; $5,000 for businesses [S6, S11].
- **Splitting a claim:** If a claim exceeds the $5,000 cap, you cannot split it into multiple claims to fit within the limit. Instead, you must file in the General District Court's regular civil division. [S6]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S6].

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of the debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

Seize funds from the debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a Writ of Fieri Facias from the court, serve it on the debtor's bank, and the bank will freeze and turn over funds to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $25 plus service fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor has sufficient funds in the bank account.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $1,000 in the debtor's bank account is exempt from levy

**Estimated cost:** $10 plus recording fees

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Wrong defendant naming

**What goes wrong:** If you sue 'John's Garage' but the legal entity is 'JG Auto Repair LLC', the judgment may be unenforceable against the LLC's assets.

**How to avoid it:** Search the State Corporation Commission records before filing and use the exact registered name; for sole proprietors, use the individual's full legal name. [unverified]

### Missed statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing one day after the deadline gets the case dismissed with prejudice.

**How to avoid it:** Check the statute of limitations for your specific claim type before filing; for fraud, the discovery rule applies but be prepared to prove when you reasonably could have discovered it. [unverified]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant isn't properly served, the court lacks jurisdiction, and your case may be dismissed.

**How to avoid it:** Ensure service is completed according to Virginia law, using the sheriff, certified mail, or a private process server as appropriate. [unverified]

### Failing to appear in court

**What goes wrong:** If you don't show up, the court may dismiss your case or enter a default judgment against you.

**How to avoid it:** Mark your calendar and set reminders; if an emergency arises, notify the court as soon as possible to request a continuance. [unverified]

### Not bringing necessary evidence

**What goes wrong:** Without proper evidence, you may lose your case even if you're in the right.

**How to avoid it:** Gather all relevant documents, photos, and witness information before the hearing; organize them for easy presentation. [unverified]

### Ignoring post-judgment procedures

**What goes wrong:** Winning a judgment doesn't guarantee payment; you must take steps to collect.

**How to avoid it:** Familiarize yourself with collection methods like garnishment and liens; consult the court clerk for necessary forms and procedures. [unverified]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, attorneys are not permitted to represent parties in Virginia's small claims court; you must represent yourself. [S3]

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: The timeline varies, but cases are typically heard within 30 to 90 days after filing. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: You can sue for up to $5,000 in Virginia's small claims court. [S6]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear, the court may enter a default judgment in your favor, but you must still present evidence to support your claim. [S3]

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: Yes, either party can appeal a small claims court decision to the circuit court within 10 days for a new trial. [S3]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: A judgment in Virginia is valid for 20 years and can be renewed before it expires. [S4]
