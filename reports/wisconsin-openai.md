## 1. Jurisdictional limits — MAXIMUM AMOUNT YOU CAN SUE FOR

**Dollar Cap:** Wisconsin small claims court can handle **claims for money up to $10,000** (for money judgments) ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=,enforce%20judgments%20from%20funds%20owed)). This $10,000 cap applies to most contract and debt claims. **Personal injury or tort claims** have a lower cap: **$5,000** maximum ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=judgments%20from%20funds%20owed%29)). In other words, if you’re suing for bodily injuries or other tort damages, you can only recover up to $5,000 in small claims. Eviction actions are an exception – they can be filed in small claims regardless of the amount of rent owed ([www.wicourts.gov](https://www.wicourts.gov/publications/guides/smallclaimsguide.htm#:~:text=,the%20amount%20financed%20is%20%2425%2C000)), and certain specific actions (like returning earnest money or confirming real estate arbitration awards) are allowed even above $10,000 ([www.wicourts.gov](https://www.wicourts.gov/publications/guides/smallclaimsguide.htm#:~:text=,a%20person%20liable%20for%20that)) (more on these in section 2).

**Plaintiff Type:** The $10,000 monetary limit is the same for **individuals and businesses**; Wisconsin does *not* set a lower cap for corporations or LLCs. Any individual **or any company doing business in Wisconsin** can sue in small claims up to $10,000 ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=Small%20claims%20court%20may%20be,For%20example)). (For torts, the $5,000 cap applies no matter who the plaintiff is ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=judgments%20from%20funds%20owed%29)).) Unlike some states, Wisconsin has no special lower limit or annual filing limit for frequent filers: there is **no state-imposed limit on the number of small claims cases** one plaintiff can file per year. A business (such as a landlord or a credit union) can file multiple small claims, as needed, as long as each case meets the criteria.

**Splitting Claims:** You **cannot split one larger claim into multiple smaller lawsuits** to circumvent the $10,000 cap. Wisconsin law treats this as improper *claim splitting*. If your actual damages exceed the small claims limit, you have two choices: either **waive** (give up) the amount above $10,000 and sue in small claims for $10,000 max, or file as a large claim in regular circuit court to seek the full amount. (Wisconsin courts have held the small-claims cap is a limit on recovery, not a strict jurisdictional bar – a plaintiff may choose to accept $10,000 even if actual damages are higher ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=A%20plaintiff%20may%20elect%20to,1030)). But you cannot recover more by filing multiple suits for the same wrong.) It’s safer to include the entire claim in one case; if you try to sue for $10k now and the rest later, the second suit will likely be dismissed under the rule against splitting a cause of action.

**Special Categories:** Note that the $10,000 cap covers *most* civil money claims (contracts, debts, property damage) ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=,enforce%20judgments%20from%20funds%20owed)). Some specific small-claims case types have different limits or no limit at all: e.g. replevin (return of property) has its own value limits, and evictions and certain real estate disputes have no dollar cap in small claims ([www.wicourts.gov](https://www.wicourts.gov/publications/guides/smallclaimsguide.htm#:~:text=,financed%20is%20%2425%2C000%20or%20less)). These will be detailed in the next section. But for a typical lawsuit seeking monetary compensation, **$10,000 is the maximum** you can demand in Wisconsin small claims (or $5,000 for personal injury/tort claims).

**Key Statute:** The basic authority for Wisconsin’s small claims limits is **Wis. Stat. § 799.01**. It specifies small claims jurisdiction for: (1) money judgments up to $10,000; (2) tort claims up to $5,000; (3) evictions (no limit); (4) return of earnest money (no limit); (5) replevins with value limits, etc ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=%28a%29%20Eviction%20actions,amount%20of%20rent%20claimed%20therein)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=%28d%29%20Other%20civil%20actions,the%20actions%20or%20proceedings%20are)). Always confirm your claim fits one of these categories. If it doesn’t (or exceeds the amount), small claims court cannot hear it.

## 2. What types of cases qualify — EVERYTHING YOU CAN SUE FOR

Wisconsin small claims court may **only be used for certain types of cases** ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=Typical%20small%20claims)). Most common are disputes over money or property within the limits above. Below is an **exhaustive list** of claim types that *can* be filed in Wisconsin small claims, with examples and any special rules or state-specific quirks to know.

### Contract & Money-Owed Disputes

- **Breach of Written Contract:** *Eligible.* If someone fails to perform a contract or pay as agreed, and the amount is $10,000 or less, you can sue in small claims ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=,enforce%20judgments%20from%20funds%20owed)). For example, if you hired a contractor to remodel your kitchen for $8,000 and they never did the work, or a vendor sold you equipment and never delivered, those are written contract breaches you can bring to small claims. **Statute of limitations:** Written contracts in Wisconsin have a 6-year limit (generally) ([wilawlibrary.gov](https://wilawlibrary.gov/topics/justice/civil/judgment.php#:~:text=,Search%20examples%3A%20judgment%20%3B%20judgments)), so file within 6 years of the breach. Be ready to show the written agreement and how it was violated. *No special state quirks* – just note the $10k cap.

- **Breach of Oral/Handshake Agreement:** *Eligible.* Oral contracts are enforceable in Wisconsin (except where the Statute of Frauds requires writing). If you had a verbal agreement – e.g. a friend agreed to sell you a used car for $3,000 and then backed out after you paid, or a neighbor promised to repay a $1,000 loan – you can sue for that breach up to $10,000. Wisconsin small claims hears many informal agreement cases like unpaid services or loans. The challenge is proving the agreement existed: gather any texts, emails, or witnesses to the deal. The same 6-year statute of limitations for contract applies to oral contracts in Wisconsin ([wilawlibrary.gov](https://wilawlibrary.gov/topics/justice/civil/judgment.php#:~:text=,Search%20examples%3A%20judgment%20%3B%20judgments)). There’s no state bar to suing on an oral contract in small claims as long as you can prove it.

- **Unpaid Personal Loans (Friends/Family IOUs):** *Eligible.* If you lent money to someone on a promise and they didn’t pay you back, you can sue for the balance (plus any lawful interest) up to $10,000 in small claims court ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=,enforce%20judgments%20from%20funds%20owed)). Example: You loaned a relative $5,000 to buy a used car and they never repaid a cent – you could file a small claim for $5,000 + agreed interest. It’s essentially an oral contract or implied contract claim. Tip: Bring any proof of the loan (emails, a check copy, or a simple signed IOU if you have one). These cases are common and straightforward.

- **Unpaid Promissory Notes:** *Eligible.* A promissory note is a written promise to pay (like an IOU in writing, often with a repayment schedule). If someone signed a note to repay you money and defaulted, you can sue up to $10k. For instance, a former employee signed a promissory note to repay a $3,000 advance but hasn’t paid – small claims can handle that. The note itself serves as strong proof of the debt. (If the note includes an attorney’s fee clause, be aware that *attorney fees are generally not awarded in small claims* unless specifically allowed by statute or contract ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=The%20%245%2C000%20small%20claims%20limitation,3102)) – in Wisconsin, the court may award certain costs but usually not attorney fees in small claims, except in special cases like rent/deposit disputes or if the note is tied to a statute allowing fees.)

- **Unpaid Commercial Invoices / Accounts Receivable:** *Eligible.* Small businesses often use small claims to collect on unpaid invoices for goods or services. If a customer or client owes you money for delivered products or completed work (and the total is $10,000 or less), you can file a small claim. Example: A freelancer billed a client $2,500 for a project and wasn’t paid, or a wholesaler has an unpaid invoice of $7,000 for supplies delivered – both fit small claims. Attach the invoice, contract, purchase order, or any proof of the sale and non-payment. Wisconsin does not require any special notice before suing on a commercial debt (though sending a demand letter is wise). There’s no annual case limit for businesses – you can file as needed.

- **Unpaid Rent (Residential or Commercial):** *Eligible.* A landlord can use small claims to recover overdue rent or other charges from a tenant, as long as the amount is within $10,000. For **active tenancies**, eviction actions are also handled in small claims (see Landlord–Tenant section below), but here we’re talking about *money judgments*. Example: A tenant moved out and still owes $3,000 in back rent and damages – the landlord can sue for that sum in small claims, separate from any eviction proceeding ([www.wicourts.gov](https://www.wicourts.gov/publications/guides/smallclaimsguide.htm#:~:text=,repossessions%20of%20property)). If the tenancy is ongoing and you also want possession, you’d file an eviction (also a small claim case, but eviction has distinct procedures). Wisconsin allows **eviction actions regardless of amount** ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=%28a%29%20Eviction%20actions,amount%20of%20rent%20claimed%20therein)), so if you combine eviction with rent claim over $10k, the case stays in small claims for the eviction, but **money award** still cannot exceed $10k ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=,enforce%20judgments%20from%20funds%20owed)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=%28d%29%20Other%20civil%20actions,the%20actions%20or%20proceedings%20are)). If a tenant owes more than $10k, a landlord must either forego the excess or file a large claim in circuit court. Also note Wisconsin’s rules on security deposits and damages (discussed below) – landlords must follow those before seeking damages from a tenant.

- **Bounced/Dishonored Checks:** *Eligible.* If someone paid you with a bad check or one that bounced for NSF (non-sufficient funds), you can sue for the amount of the check **and** under Wisconsin’s **worthless check statute** you may recover additional damages. Wisconsin law allows the victim of a bounced check to recover **exemplary (punitive) damages up to 3× the amount of the check**, plus costs and possibly attorney fees ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,checks%20or%20orders%20were%20involved)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,not%20liable%20under%20this%20section)) – *but* the total extra damages + attorney fees is capped at **$500 per check** ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,action%2C%20including%20reasonable%20attorney%20fees)). To get these, you must give the check writer a 20-day notice to pay before suing ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,not%20liable%20under%20this%20section)). Example: Someone writes you a $300 rent check that bounces. You send the required demand letter. If they don’t pay in 20 days, you sue in small claims for $300 + say $50 bank fees as actual loss, plus up to $500 extra. The statute (Wis. Stat. § 943.245) specifically allows up to triple damages and costs for worthless checks ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,checks%20or%20orders%20were%20involved)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,action%2C%20including%20reasonable%20attorney%20fees)), but with the $500 cap, in practice you’ll often just get the face amount plus a few hundred extra. Make sure to attach a copy of the bounced check and proof of your notice letter with your claim.

- **Open Book Accounts/Running Tabs:** *Eligible.* If you have an open account with someone (common in trades or ongoing services) where they periodically owe sums and haven’t paid, you can sue for the balance due (up to $10k). For example, a plumber did multiple small jobs for a client on credit and is owed a total of $1,200 on the account – that can be one small claim. In Wisconsin, an “open account” is essentially treated like a contract debt. The key is to provide an **accounting or statement** of how the balance was reached. There’s a 6-year statute for collection of accounts (treated as contract debt) ([wilawlibrary.gov](https://wilawlibrary.gov/topics/justice/civil/judgment.php#:~:text=,Search%20examples%3A%20judgment%20%3B%20judgments)). Small claims procedure doesn’t have special requirements for an account stated, though detailing the charges helps your case.

- **Co-signer/Guarantor Disputes:** *Eligible.* If you co-signed a loan or guaranteed someone’s debt and ended up having to pay it, you can sue the primary debtor for what you paid (this is **reimbursement** under the contract of guaranty). For example, you co-signed a car loan and the primary borrower defaulted, you paid the lender $5,000, now you want that $5,000 back from the borrower – small claims is an appropriate venue. Likewise, if you were the one owed (say you lent money to X but Y guaranteed it), you could sue the guarantor in small claims if the agreement allows it or after judgment against the primary debtor. Ensure you have copies of the co-signer agreement and proof you paid. Wisconsin doesn’t require any extra steps before suing a guarantor in small claims (assuming the guaranty contract is clear). Keep the claim within $10k.

### Consumer & Service Disputes

- **Refund for Defective Product:** *Eligible.* If you bought a product (e.g. an appliance, electronics, furniture) that turned out to be defective or not as advertised, and the seller refuses to refund or replace it, you can sue for your money back (and possibly related damages) up to $10,000. Example: You purchase a new laptop for $1,200 that never worked, and the store won’t honor a refund – you could sue for a refund plus perhaps any repair costs incurred. Wisconsin’s small claims can handle **breach of warranty** claims like this. If the product had a warranty, bring documentation. Wisconsin also has an Unfair Trade Practices Act (Wis. Stat. § 100.18) prohibiting fraudulent representations in sales; if a seller knowingly misled you, you might additionally claim under that law for double damages (see “False Advertising” below) – but you can certainly claim at least the purchase price. No special notice is required by law before suing for a defective product refund, but sometimes writing a demand letter referencing the warranty helps.

- **Services Not Performed or Done Poorly (Breach of Service Contract):** *Eligible.* If you paid for a service and the provider failed to perform it or did a terrible job, you can sue to recover what you paid and any consequential loss, up to $10k. Example: You pay a photographer $2,000 for a wedding shoot, but they never show up – breach of contract, claim for $2,000 refund. Or you hire a web designer who does half the work and quits; you might sue for the amount you paid minus the value of any partial work. These are treated like contract cases. Wisconsin small claims frequently sees cases against contractors, repairmen, wedding vendors, etc., for not delivering promised services. Document the agreement (even if oral, provide any emails or receipts) and how they breached. If the service was done but poorly, you can claim the cost to fix or the difference in value. For instance, if a house painter’s shoddy work will cost $800 to redo, you might sue for that $800 in damages. There’s no pre-suit requirement like licensing complaints (though if it’s a licensed trade like a contractor, you can also complain to the Dept. of Consumer Protection separately). Small claims is a proper venue for these disputes as long as the money at stake is within the limit.

- **Auto Repair Overcharges or Shoddy Work:** *Eligible.* Auto repair disputes are common small claims. Wisconsin law (Wis. Adm. Code ch. ATCP 132) provides some protections for customers (e.g. written estimates), but in court you’ll generally sue for **breach of contract or negligence**. If a mechanic charged far above estimate or didn’t fix the problem or caused new damage, you can bring a claim. Example: You were billed $900 for car repairs that were not authorized or not actually done – you could sue to get that money back. Or if a $500 brake job was done so poorly that your car was damaged, you might sue for the cost of repair and related losses (though note property damage due to negligence is a tort, which implicates the $5k tort cap if you frame it that way ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=judgments%20from%20funds%20owed%29))). Often you’ll allege both breach of contract (didn’t perform the repair as agreed) and negligence. In Wisconsin, there’s no requirement to go through the DMV or an auto regulator first – you can directly sue. Bring receipts, any warranty info, and perhaps a second mechanic’s statement about the poor work.

- **Home Repair / Contractor Disputes (Incomplete or Defective Work):** *Eligible.* If a home improvement contractor failed to do the work or did it improperly, you can sue for what it costs you (up to $10k). For example, you pay a contractor $5,000 to remodel a bathroom, they do substandard work causing damage – you could sue for the cost to redo the work and any damages, say $5,000 to $10,000. Wisconsin’s Home Improvement Practices Act (Wis. Adm. Code ATCP 110) prohibits certain unfair practices; notably, if a contractor violates those rules (e.g. takes money and doesn’t perform or departs from specs), you might be entitled to double damages and attorney fees under Wis. Stat. § 100.20(5) ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=Landlord%20William%20Waldbillig%20appeals%20a,rent%20an%20apartment%20are%20not)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-20/#:~:text=,including%20a%20reasonable%20attorney%20fee)) (the same law that covers landlord deposits – see below). That means in some contractor fraud cases you could recover twice your loss plus costs. This would be pursued in small claims by pleading the ATCP 110 violation. Even if you don’t go for double damages, you can sue for your actual losses. Document the contract and what went wrong (photos of bad work, etc.). Wisconsin does not require contractors to be given a chance to cure before suing (though it might be practical to demand it). Be aware of the 6-year contract limitation. If damages exceed $10k, you either limit the claim or use large claims court.

- **Appliance/Electronics Warranty Failures:** *Eligible.* If you bought an appliance or electronics item with a warranty and the seller/manufacturer isn’t honoring the warranty, you can sue for the cost of repair or replacement up to $10k. E.g., you buy a $3,000 refrigerator that dies in 6 months and the manufacturer won’t fix/replace it under the warranty – small claims can handle a claim for the cost of a new fridge or refund. Under the federal Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act, small claims courts have jurisdiction to enforce written warranties too, as state contract claims. Wisconsin law doesn’t provide extra damages for warranty (unless the refusal was in bad faith – not usually applicable), so you usually recover the value of the item or cost to fix. Include a copy of the warranty and evidence that you requested service and were denied. These are straightforward contract cases in small claims.

- **Canceled or No-Show Service Providers (e.g. photographers, caterers, movers):** *Eligible.* If a service provider for an event fails to show or cancels last-minute and doesn’t refund your payment, you can sue for what you paid and any additional costs you incurred (within $10k). For instance, a catering company takes a $2,000 deposit for a wedding then never shows up – small claims suit can recover the $2,000 and possibly any extra you paid to arrange last-minute food. Or a moving company no-shows after you paid $500 – sue for the $500 and perhaps any storage fees that resulted. These are breaches of contract. Wisconsin doesn’t require any special notice – file as soon as it’s clear they breached. If the breach caused other monetary loss (consequential damages), you can claim those too (though proving consequential damages can be tricky without receipts). Ensure you mitigate losses (e.g., if a DJ cancels, you tried to find replacement at reasonable cost). The small claims court can award those additional damages if they’re a natural result of the breach and within the cap.

- **Dry Cleaning or Property Loss in Custody (e.g. valet, storage, repair shop):** *Eligible.* If you entrusted your property to someone (a bailee) – like a dry cleaner, a hotel laundry, a valet parking service, a storage facility, a repair shop – and they lost it or damaged it, you can sue for the value (up to $10k). Example: A dry cleaner loses your $500 suit; a car valet returns your car with damage; a storage company’s employee broke your furniture. These claims can be framed as negligence or breach of bailment contract. Since they involve damage to property by another, they could be considered torts (hence $5k cap if strictly tort ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=judgments%20from%20funds%20owed%29))), but often you also argue an implied contract that they’d return the goods in good condition. In practice, small claims judges usually just assess the value and don’t worry about tort vs contract distinctions unless it’s above $5k. Provide receipts or appraisals for the item’s value. Note: Some services (like parking garages) post disclaimers – those might complicate liability but not bar you from suing. No special pre-suit steps required in WI.

- **Gym/Club Membership Cancellations and Refunds:** *Eligible.* If you canceled a gym, spa, or membership service according to the contract and the company won’t issue a refund owed, you can sue for that money. Wisconsin law (Wis. Stat. § 100.177) regulates fitness center contracts, including a 3-day cancellation right and prorated refunds in some cases. If a gym refuses to honor a lawful cancellation, small claims is the venue. Example: You prepaid $800 for a year at a gym, cancel within the allowed window due to moving, but they refuse to refund the unused portion – sue for the unused portion. Provide the contract and proof of cancellation notice. Similarly for cable/telecom or subscription services that owe refunds. Small claims can’t force them to “cancel” the contract (that’s injunctive relief), but by suing for the *money* you effectively enforce the contract’s cancellation terms. If the amount is small (e.g. under $100), consider if it’s worth the filing fee – but technically it’s allowed.

- **Auto Dealer/Used-Car Disputes (Lemon Law, Odometer Fraud, etc.):** *Eligible with caveats.* **New Car Lemon Law:** Wisconsin’s Lemon Law (Wis. Stat. § 218.0171) provides powerful remedies (replacement or double refund) for new vehicles with serious defects. However, Lemon Law cases often exceed $10k (vehicle price), and typically are pursued in regular court or through DOT’s process ([wisconsindot.gov](https://wisconsindot.gov/Pages/dmv/cons-protect/lemon-law/lemonlaw.aspx#:~:text=What%20qualifies%20as%20a%20%E2%80%9Clemon%E2%80%9D%3F)) ([wisconsindot.gov](https://wisconsindot.gov/Pages/dmv/cons-protect/lemon-law/lemonlaw.aspx#:~:text=What%20does%20not%20qualify%20as,a%20%E2%80%9Clemon)). If your new car qualifies as a “lemon,” the manufacturer must provide a full refund or replacement; if they don’t, you could sue – but since new cars cost more than $10k, such a claim doesn’t fit in small claims except perhaps to recover incidental costs. **Used Cars:** If you bought a used car for under $10k and it had undisclosed defects, you can sue the dealer for misrepresentation or breach of warranty. Wisconsin has laws against odometer fraud (Wis. Stat. § 342.155) and a used car must pass emissions if sold with that guarantee, etc. Example: Dealer sells you a car advertised as never in a wreck, but it was salvage – you could sue for fraud or for the diminished value. Under Wis. Stat. § 100.18 (deceptive practices), you could get double damages and attorney fees for intentional fraud in the sale ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/#:~:text=recover%20such%20pecuniary%20loss%2C%20together,of%20a%20violation%20by%20any)). Odometer tampering is also a crime; civilly, you could recover money for that (federal law allows triple damages for odometer fraud). In small claims, stick to the monetary loss (e.g. the difference in car’s value, or the repair costs). Also note, Wisconsin *implied warranty* law: if a used car was sold “as is,” your ability to claim is limited; if sold with some warranty, you can enforce that. No pre-suit notice is needed unless the contract requires it. For **private-party car sales**, small claims is often the only recourse – you can sue a private seller for fraud if they lied (e.g. rolled back odometer or concealed major defects) or for breach of any written terms.

- **False Advertising / Deceptive Trade Practices:** *Eligible.* Wisconsin’s Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA), **Wis. Stat. § 100.18**, allows a consumer to sue a business that made “untrue, deceptive or misleading” statements about goods or services ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/#:~:text=,made%2C%20published%2C%20disseminated%2C%20circulated%2C%20or)). If you were induced to purchase by false advertising and suffered a loss, you can sue in small claims for your damages. Importantly, §100.18 provides that a victim can recover **double the pecuniary loss and attorney’s fees** if they win ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/#:~:text=therefor%20in%20any%20court%20of,in%20real%20estate%20practice%2C%20as)). For example, a homeowner hires a contractor because of false claims in their ads, pays $4,000 and gets shoddy results – they could sue under 100.18 for $4,000 ×2 = $8,000 plus costs and attorney fees (even in small claims) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/#:~:text=recover%20such%20pecuniary%20loss%2C%20together,of%20a%20violation%20by%20any)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/#:~:text=recovered%20by%20the%20defrauded%20party,1198%20%281991)). Another example: a store advertises “all electronics 50% off” but charges you full price – you might sue for the difference. To use §100.18, you must show: a public advertisement with a false statement of fact, your reliance on it, and a monetary loss ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/#:~:text=recovered%20by%20the%20defrauded%20party,1198%20%281991)). Small claims court can hear these claims as long as the actual loss (before doubling) is within $10k. If you intend to seek the **double damages and fees**, cite §100.18 in your complaint and be prepared to show the deceptive act was a significant factor in your decision. No special filing needed beyond that. Wisconsin also forbids certain unfair trade practices via rules (like the Home Improvement practices mentioned) enforceable via §100.20(5), which gives **double damages and attorney fees** for violations of Department of Agriculture, Trade and Consumer Protection (DATCP) regulations ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-20/#:~:text=,including%20a%20reasonable%20attorney%20fee)). This means if a business violated a consumer protection rule and you lost money, you might recover double. Common uses: tenant security deposits (below), home improvement misrepresentations, landlord misrepresentations, etc. These statutory claims can absolutely be brought in small claims as long as the dollar amounts fit.

### Property Damage

- **Vehicle Collision Damage:** *Eligible if under cap.* Small claims can handle minor car accident claims where your damages are within $5,000 (since car accident = typically a tort). Example: Another driver hits your parked car causing $3,500 in damage not covered by insurance – you can sue that driver in small claims for $3,500. If it’s clearly their fault and you have repair estimates, this is a typical small claims case. Keep in mind: if the claim includes personal injury (like whiplash), the $5,000 tort cap applies to the injury portion ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=judgments%20from%20funds%20owed%29)), and most injury claims exceed that. So small claims is mainly used for **property damage only** accidents or very minor injuries. If you have insurance subrogation (your insurer paid and is suing the at-fault party), often the insurer will handle it, but you as an individual can sue for your deductible or uninsured losses. Wisconsin requires exchanging info at accidents, but no extra pre-suit step. Note: if the other driver’s insurance denied your claim, you can still sue the driver (and often the insurer will defend them). Small claims judgments in accident cases can include repair costs, car rental, towing, etc., and statutory 5% interest from the accident date (if liquidated) or judgment date ([wilawlibrary.gov](https://wilawlibrary.gov/topics/justice/civil/judgment.php#:~:text=,Search%20examples%3A%20judgment%20%3B%20judgments)). If your damages are say $7,000, you *cannot* file two cases to skirt the cap – you’d either waive the excess and sue for $5k max, or go to regular court.

- **Damage to Personal Property by Another:** *Eligible.* Any situation where someone negligently or intentionally damages your belongings – if the monetary loss is $5,000 or less (tort cap), you can sue in small claims for reimbursement. Examples: A neighbor accidentally crashes a drone through your window causing $1,000 damage; a friend borrows and breaks your power tools ($300 to replace); a stranger vandalizes your bicycle ($800 damage). These are torts (negligence or property damage), so the **$5,000 limit** is in play ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=judgments%20from%20funds%20owed%29)). Provide proof of repair costs or value (estimates, receipts). If the act was intentional (vandalism, theft), Wisconsin law actually provides a cause of action for property damage due to crime – **Wis. Stat. § 895.446** – allowing *all actual damages, costs of investigation, and up to 3× damages as punitive* ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,caused%20the%20damage%20or%20loss)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,76)). For example, if someone **stole** your property, you can sue under civil theft statute §895.446 for the value and potentially triple damages ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,76)). However, note triple damages under that statute in small claims are still subject to the $10k overall limit for “other civil actions” ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=A%20plaintiff%20may%20elect%20to,1030)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=Civil%20theft%20under%20s,2420)) (and punitive damages in any case might be capped by due process or the statute itself). Usually, for small claims, you’d just ask for your actual loss (or double/triple if explicitly allowed). No pre-suit notice needed except in the worthless check scenario described earlier. If the damage was done by a minor, you can even sue the parents under Wisconsin’s parental liability law (with certain limits, generally parents liable up to $5,000 for a minor’s willful property damage, per Wis. Stat. § 895.035). That fits neatly in small claims.

- **Damage by a Tenant to a Landlord’s Property:** *Eligible.* If a tenant moved out and left excessive damage to your rental unit beyond normal wear and tear, a landlord can sue for those damages (repair costs) in small claims, typically as a counterclaim to a security deposit dispute or a separate action. Example: Tenant punches holes in walls and leaves garbage – $2,000 in repairs – landlord may sue for $2,000. Important: Wisconsin has strict rules on security deposits. A landlord generally must provide an itemized list of damages and costs within **21 days** of the tenant’s surrender of the premises ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/cases/wisconsin/court-of-appeals/1984/84-002-6.html#:~:text=Paulik%20v,Pauliks%20brought%20a%20small%20claims)). If the landlord fails to meet the 21-day requirement or withholds deposit in bad faith, the tenant can actually claim double the deposit (see Landlord–Tenant section). So, as a landlord, ensure compliance with **Wis. Adm. Code ATCP 134.06** regarding security deposits. You can still sue for damages beyond the deposit. Bring photos, inspection reports, and receipts for repairs. As long as each category of damage is reasonably documented, small claims can award those amounts. The $10k cap applies to total sought. If tenant’s damage exceeds that, you may have to limit your claim or sue in large claims. If you already withheld the security deposit, you’d sue for the *additional* amount beyond the deposit (since presumably you’ve applied the deposit to part of it).

- **Damage by a Landlord to a Tenant’s Property:** *Eligible.* If a landlord improperly handles or damages a tenant’s personal belongings (for instance during an illegal eviction or while storing items), the tenant can sue for those losses. Example: Landlord unlawfully removes and throws away your furniture valued at $3,000 – you can sue for $3,000. Under Wisconsin law, after an eviction or abandonment, landlords must follow certain procedures to store or dispose of a tenant’s personal property (Wis. Stat. § 704.05(5)). If they violate those rules or convert your property, small claims is the venue to recover value. Such a claim could be framed as **civil theft or wrongful conversion**; Wis. Stat. § 895.446 (civil theft) would allow potentially 3× damages and attorney fees ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,caused%20the%20damage%20or%20loss)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,76)), which can be very favorable if intentional. But even without that, you get fair market value of items. Document what items were taken/damaged (photos, lists) and their value (original cost and age to estimate depreciated value). No special notice needed, though if this happened in context of an eviction, it might tie into that case. If the landlord’s acts were willful (like throwing property out in the street), pointing out the violation of §704.05 or any local ordinance can bolster your case.

- **Tree/Fence/Boundary Disputes Resulting in Damage:** *Eligible.* If a neighbor’s actions cause damage to your property – e.g., they cut down your tree without permission, or their new fence encroaches and damages your landscaping – you can sue for the damage up to $10k (but if it’s property damage tort, $5k cap; if treated as trespass/property claim possibly $10k as “other civil action” – Wisconsin case law treats civil “timber theft” or conversion as an “other civil action” rather than tort, allowing up to $10k ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=Civil%20theft%20under%20s,cause%20for%20civil%20theft%20provided)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=Civil%20theft%20under%20s,2420))). For instance, unlawful tree cutting can actually entitle you to *double* or *triple damages by statute* in some cases (Wis. Stat. § 26.09 provides double damages for unauthorized timber harvest on another’s land). Ensure to survey where the boundary is if relevant. Small claims can award the cost of repair or diminution in value. Note: If it’s a purely injunctive issue (like you want the fence removed), small claims can’t grant injunctions – but if you paid to move it or repair something, you can claim that cost. Provide any arborist report or estimate if trees were involved. 

- **Pet Damage (Dog Bites or Property Damage by Pets):** *Eligible (limited).* If someone’s pet injured you or your pet, or damaged your property, you can sue the owner for vet bills, medical bills, or repair costs. Pet cases are tricky because if it’s bodily injury (dog bite), that’s a tort claim – $5,000 limit and typically dog bites often exceed that in medical costs. But for small bites or if you only seek the out-of-pocket portion, you could use small claims. Wisconsin has a strict liability dog bite statute (Wis. Stat. § 174.02) making owners liable for full damages, and *double damages* if the dog had known previous biting history ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=The%20%245%2C000%20small%20claims%20limitation,3102)). If, say, a dog bit you causing $2,000 in medical expenses and it had bitten before, you could claim $4,000 (double) under that statute, which is within small claims. Or if a neighbor’s dog chewed up your $500 garden sprinkler system, you sue for $500. Evidence could be photos of injury and medical bills or itemized property damage. Always identify the correct owner of the pet as defendant. Note: If a dog injured your own pet, legally pets are considered property – you can recover vet bills and maybe some value of the animal if deceased, but not “emotional” damages. Those vet bills qualify under the $5k tort cap. No special pre-suit steps (some cities have animal control to report to, but not required for your civil claim).

- **Damage During a Move (by Movers or Friends):** *Eligible.* If you hired movers and they broke or lost items, or even if a friend helping you move accidentally destroyed something, you can file a claim for the value of those items (up to $10k). With professional movers, check your contract: many have arbitration clauses or released liability limits. But you can still attempt a small claim if the amount is modest – sometimes moving companies will pay after you sue. Example: Movers drop and wreck your $2,000 TV and won’t reimburse – sue for $2,000. Provide evidence of the item’s value (receipt, or current market value if used) and proof the movers caused it (photos, any admission on their part, etc.). If a friend caused damage, it’s awkward but legally you have a claim for negligence; possibly their homeowner’s insurance might cover it. Wisconsin doesn’t have any impediment to suing for such casual negligence. Just be mindful of the $5k tort limit if purely property damage tort – although destruction of your personal property could alternatively be seen as property loss where you seek replacement value (which is fine up to $10k as “civil action” – the law is a bit nuanced here on whether all property damage counts as tort or if intentional acts fall under other civil actions up to $10k ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=%28d%29%20Other%20civil%20actions,the%20actions%20or%20proceedings%20are)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=A%20plaintiff%20may%20elect%20to,1030))). In most cases, keep it under $5k to be safe, or argue it as an “other civil action” if over $5k.

### Landlord–Tenant (financial claims only; possession/eviction separate)

*(Note: Eviction actions for possession are also filed in small claims court in Wisconsin ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=%28a%29%20Eviction%20actions,amount%20of%20rent%20claimed%20therein)), but those are handled on an expedited timeline and are beyond the scope of “money recovery” – we focus here on monetary disputes between landlords and tenants.)*

- **Unreturned Security Deposits:** *Eligible and very common.* If you’re a tenant and your landlord failed to return your security deposit or didn’t provide a written statement of deductions within Wisconsin’s required **21 days** after you moved out ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/cases/wisconsin/court-of-appeals/1984/84-002-6.html#:~:text=Paulik%20v,Pauliks%20brought%20a%20small%20claims)), you can sue for your deposit. Wisconsin heavily favors tenants on this: a landlord who improperly withholds a deposit or misses the 21-day deadline can be liable for **double the amount of the deposit, plus tenant’s court costs and reasonable attorney’s fees** ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=withholding%20from%20a%20security%20deposit,rent%20an%20apartment%20are%20not)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-20/#:~:text=,including%20a%20reasonable%20attorney%20fee)). This comes from Wis. Stat. § 100.20(5) and DATCP rule ATCP 134.06. For example, your $800 deposit wasn’t returned or accounted for in 21 days – you can sue for $1,600 (double) plus your court costs ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=Landlord%20William%20Waldbillig%20appeals%20a,rent%20an%20apartment%20are%20not)). Even if the landlord belatedly gives an accounting but wrongfully kept money (e.g., charging for normal wear-and-tear), you may get double the wrongfully withheld portion ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=Landlord%20William%20Waldbillig%20appeals%20a,rent%20an%20apartment%20are%20not)). Small claims is the forum for this (these are often called “security deposit cases” and are a huge portion of small claims filings). Bring a copy of your lease, proof of payment of the deposit, evidence of when you vacated, and any communications. If you sue for double damages under the statute, cite **Wis. Stat. § 100.20(5)** in your complaint. Typically, if you prove the violation, the court *must* award double damages and **shall** award **actual attorney’s fees** if you hired an attorney ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=withholding%20from%20a%20security%20deposit,rent%20an%20apartment%20are%20not)) – though in small claims, most tenants appear without attorney, but you still get costs. The 21-day clock and double-damage penalty apply unless you abandoned the unit without notice or agreed in writing to a longer refund period (rare). **Landlords:** conversely, if you did everything right and the tenant sues, you can defend by showing you complied with ATCP 134 (proper notices, legitimate deductions). But if you didn’t, expect to lose. Security deposit cases are one of the few instances in Wisconsin small claims where the statute mandates awarding attorney fees to the winning tenant ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=withholding%20from%20a%20security%20deposit,rent%20an%20apartment%20are%20not)), to incentivize compliance.

- **Prepaid Rent or Last Month’s Rent Refund:** *Eligible.* Similar to deposits, if a tenant paid last month’s rent in advance or any rent prepayment and then moved out early or had an agreement, but the landlord fails to refund an owed amount, the tenant can sue. Often this overlaps with the security deposit accounting. For example, you gave first and last months’ rent, you properly gave notice and moved, and the landlord had no reason to charge the last month (because a new tenant took over or you didn’t stay) – you can claim that back. While not specifically doubled by statute like a deposit, a wrongful non-refund of prepaid rent could be seen as a violation of ATCP 134.06(2) if not accounted for, potentially invoking double damages too as an “illegal retention of money due under a rental agreement” (some case law has treated improper withholding of any tenant funds as coming under the same double-damages rule). Regardless, you can claim the actual amount and the court can award it. Provide proof you paid and why it should be returned (e.g., you didn’t actually occupy the last month or the lease ended early by agreement).

- **Habitability Issues / Repair-and-Deduct Counterclaims:** *Eligible as tenant claims or defenses.* If the rental had serious habitability problems (no heat, water leaks, pest infestations, etc.) and the landlord didn’t fix them, Wisconsin law allows a tenant to potentially “repair and deduct” or claim a rent abatement. If you spent money to fix something the landlord should have (after giving notice and reasonable time), you can sue to recover those costs. Or if conditions reduced the value of the rental, you might seek a portion of rent back. In small claims, these often arise as **counterclaims** when a landlord sues for rent – the tenant counterclaims for rent abatement due to poor conditions. Example: Landlord sues for $1,000 unpaid rent; tenant counterclaims that the heat was out for 2 months, rendering the place half uninhabitable, so they seek a 50% rent abatement for that period. Wisconsin has an “implied warranty of habitability” (by case law), so a tenant can argue for rent reduction for periods of uninhabitability. There’s no fixed formula, but tenants should document the issues (photos, health department complaints, etc.). There’s no separate statute awarding multiple damages for habitability issues (unless it’s an egregious deceptive trade practice), so typically it’s actual damages or rent reduction. If the tenant did repairs (like paid $200 to exterminate roaches after landlord failed), that $200 can be claimed back. All this is fine in small claims as long as the amounts claimed don’t exceed $10k. These are usually defensive or offsetting claims.

- **Unpaid Rent or Property Damage (Landlord’s claim vs. former tenant):** *Eligible.* After a tenant leaves, if they owe rent or caused damage above the deposit, the landlord can sue in small claims for those amounts. This is very common. Ensure to follow the deposit return process first, as failing to comply can jeopardize your claim (the tenant might get double deposit which could offset what you’re owed). But assuming, for example, the tenant left owing $2,000 rent and $1,500 damage beyond the $1,000 deposit (which you kept), you might sue for the remaining $2,500. Provide the ledger of unpaid rent and invoices for damages. The statute of limitations on written rental agreements is 6 years, so don’t wait too long. Landlords should also be aware if the tenant left no forwarding address, you must send the deposit itemization to their last known address; keeping that proof can help in court. Wisconsin doesn’t require a landlord to first go to collections or anything – you can directly sue when money is due. Just be mindful that if you failed to provide the deposit accounting, the tenant could turn the tables with that double-damage rule even in your suit.

- **Wrongful Retention of Tenant’s Personal Property:** *Eligible.* If a landlord holds onto a tenant’s belongings after move-out and won’t return them, the tenant can sue for return of property or its value. Usually, you want the value (since small claims can’t force the act of return, only money). For instance, you move out and the landlord locked some of your stuff in storage and demands exorbitant fees – you can sue for the *value* of the property or for the statutory penalties. Wisconsin law § 704.05(5) requires landlords to store and notify tenants of left-behind property and allows disposal after notice. If the landlord didn’t follow that and effectively “converted” your property, you have a claim. The measure is typically fair market value of items (unless statute provides a set penalty). Under Wis. Stat. § 895.446 (civil theft/conversion), as mentioned, you could seek up to 3× value and costs for intentional violations ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,caused%20the%20damage%20or%20loss)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,76)). For small claims, you might simply claim the value and let the court decide doubling or not. Bring a list of items and approximate values (used value). If the property’s total value is within $10k, you’re in the right court.

- **Disputes Over Fees (Cleaning fees, late fees, pet fees, etc.):** *Eligible.* Landlords often deduct cleaning or impose fees; tenants often dispute them. Wisconsin allows certain non-standard rental provisions (like pet fees, late fees) only if disclosed in writing. If a landlord charges an unlawful fee or an unreasonable amount, a tenant can sue to get that money back. For example, if your lease had an illegal fee (some fees might violate ATCP rules if not agreed properly) and you paid it, you could sue. Or if the landlord deducted $300 “cleaning” when you left the place clean, you could sue for that $300. These usually fall under the deposit dispute rubric – if the court finds the fee was not valid, it becomes part of the deposit wrongfully withheld and could be doubled under the deposit rule. Wisconsin ATCP 134 forbids deducting for routine cleaning or wear/tear, so those charges are typically invalid. Conversely, if a tenant agreed to a specific fee in the lease (e.g. a $50 late fee for rent), a court will likely enforce a reasonable fee, but not if it’s excessive or not properly disclosed. In any event, small claims will adjudicate whether such fees are owed or must be refunded.

### Employment & Wages

- **Unpaid Final Paycheck or Wages:** *Eligible.* If your employer didn’t pay your last paycheck or other wages you earned, you can sue in small claims for the amount due. Wisconsin employees can also file a wage claim with the state Department of Workforce Development (DWD) ([dwd.wisconsin.gov](https://dwd.wisconsin.gov/er/laborstandards/wages.htm#:~:text=Wage%20Payment%20and%20Collection%20Law,pay%20wages%20earned%20on%20the)), but you have the option to sue directly in court. Wisconsin wage law (Wis. Stat. § 109.03) requires final wages be paid by the next regular pay date after termination. If they’re late or withheld, the law provides **penalty wages**: a court *may* award up to **50% of the unpaid wages** as a penalty (or up to 100% if the state labor department process was invoked first) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=,of%20wages%20due%20and%20unpaid)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=,those%20wages%20due%20and%20unpaid)). Essentially, if you sue on your own immediately, the court has discretion to add up to 50% extra; if you first went through DWD and they tried to resolve, then in court the penalty can be up to 100% (double wages) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=,of%20wages%20due%20and%20unpaid)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=the%20wage%20claim%20under%20sub,those%20wages%20due%20and%20unpaid)). These penalties are not automatic; the judge will consider if the employer’s failure was intentional or in bad faith ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=court%20has%20broad%20discretion%20under,1028)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=Penalties%20under%20sub,1701)). Example: Your employer owes you $1,000 of overtime and doesn’t pay – you can claim $1,000 plus ask the court for up to $500 extra as penalty. The small claims limit is sufficient for most wage claims (because if it’s more than $10k wages, likely you’d do a class action or something bigger). Bring evidence of hours worked and pay rate (timesheets, pay stubs). Wisconsin also mandates employers to pay for unused vacation if company policy treats it as wages. A common situation: an employer simply refuses to pay last paycheck – these are often straightforward in court, and judges may indeed grant the 50% penalty for wages under Wis. Stat. § 109.11(2)(a) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=,of%20wages%20due%20and%20unpaid)) if no good excuse. Note: If you already filed a wage complaint with DWD and they issued a decision or got a judgment, you can’t double-dip in court; but you can withdraw and go to court. Also, certain employees (railroad, government) have special rules. Overdue wages have a 2-year statute if you seek the penalty, or 6 years if just contract. Small claims can also award you **your reasonable attorney’s fees** if you win a wage claim, under Wis. Stat. § 109.03(6), even though generally attorneys fees aren’t given – the wage statutes allow it, so that’s enforceable in small claims (if you hired an attorney). If you win by default, note Wisconsin’s “double costs” rule for wage suits (if employer ignored your wage demand for 7 days, you get double costs) – minor, but an incentive.

- **Unpaid Commissions or Bonuses:** *Eligible.* If you earned a sales commission or bonus under an agreement and your employer refuses to pay (common when leaving a job), you can sue for it. Wisconsin law treats earned commissions as wages (if all conditions are met). For example, you made a big sale pre-termination with commission of $5,000 due, but the company doesn’t pay because you left – you have a strong claim. The same wage laws and possibly penalty wages apply to commissions if they were “due” (the terms of commission plan matter – if it says you must be employed on payout date, that could defeat the claim). For bonuses, if it was discretionary, might not be enforceable; if it was promised for meeting targets, it can be. Provide the commission plan document or emails promising the bonus. No special pre-suit steps aside from possibly a written demand (which is always smart for wage issues; if employer then doesn’t pay within 7 days, you may secure the double costs benefit under §109.11(2)(b) and strengthen case for penalty). The $10k limit applies per claim, but if you have multiple commissions, consider them together. The statute of limitations for wage claims is 2 years (for the 50% penalty) and 6 years otherwise, so don’t delay.

- **Unreimbursed Business Expenses:** *Eligible.* If your employer owes you for expenses you paid (travel, mileage, purchases) and hasn’t reimbursed, you can pursue that in small claims, often as a contract claim. Wisconsin wage law also considers certain reimbursements as “wages” if they’re due under policy, but more commonly it’s just a straightforward debt. Example: You spent $800 on work supplies on your personal card under the agreement you’d be repaid, and they never repaid – sue for $800. Bring receipts and any approval emails. No special statutes here, just your employment agreement or company policy. If it was clearly promised, you should win. If it’s a gray area, it may depend on if the court sees it as an implied contract. Notably, Wisconsin’s wage statutes might not classify expenses as wages subject to penalty, but if the lack of reimbursement effectively reduces your pay below minimum wage, that could be a separate violation. Mostly, treat it as a simple contract debt between you and employer.

- **Misclassification (Independent Contractor vs Employee) – Recoverable Wages:** If you were misclassified and thus not paid overtime or minimum wage, the best route is usually a wage complaint to DWD or a federal Department of Labor complaint. However, you can bring a private action under state and federal law for unpaid overtime or minimum wage. Small claims can handle such an action if the amount is within $10k. For example, you were labeled an “independent contractor” but effectively an employee working 60 hours/week without overtime – you could sue for the unpaid overtime wages up to $10k. Federal FLSA claims can be brought in state court (with liquidated damages equal to the unpaid wages and attorney fees). Wisconsin law (Wis. Stat. § 109 and DWD regs) also covers overtime and allows 50% penalties. These cases can be complex (and often class action territory), but on a small scale (say you’re owed $3,000 in overtime), you can attempt it in small claims. Be prepared to prove you were an employee under the law’s criteria. It’s advisable in such cases to consult an employment lawyer, but individuals have brought small claims for wage differences successfully. If you prevail, the statutes mandate attorney fees and double damages in many instances – a judge can award those even in small claims ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=,of%20wages%20due%20and%20unpaid)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=the%20wage%20claim%20under%20sub,those%20wages%20due%20and%20unpaid)). One caveat: if you file in small claims, you *waive* any amount above $10k even if you might have been entitled, so be sure the uncompensated wages + liquidated damages won’t exceed the cap or you are okay capping it.

- **Better Forum – Labor Commissioner vs Court:** Wisconsin gives employees a choice: file with the **Labor Standards Bureau** of DWD or sue directly. The Labor agency can investigate and even obtain judgments (with a small administrative fee) for you. They’re a good option for straightforward wage claims and there’s no cost to file. However, they might take time and will not pursue penalty wages unless it goes to court. If you want a quick resolution or penalty wages, court might be preferred. Also, if you want to recover attorney fees (say you already hired a lawyer), going to court is necessary. The important thing is you *don’t* get to recover twice – use one route. Certain claims like discrimination, retaliation, etc., must go through administrative steps and are *not* small-claimable (those aren’t just money owed, they involve statutory remedies). But pure wage or hour claims fit in small claims.

### Personal Services & Professional Disputes

- **Hairdresser/Barber/Nail Salon Disputes (Botched service):** *Eligible.* If a personal care service goes awry – e.g., a hairstylist severely damages your hair or a nail salon injures you – and you incurred expenses to fix it or suffered loss, you can sue for money. These can be tricky because minor dissatisfaction (a bad haircut) isn’t usually compensable beyond maybe a refund, but if there was clear negligence (chemical burn on scalp, etc.), you could claim medical bills or costs of correction. Example: A salon’s dye job burned your scalp requiring $300 doctor visit – claim that $300 plus refund of the $150 service. Or a barber seriously cut you leading to a scar – a small personal injury claim could be made, though mindful of $5k tort cap. Often these cases are about getting a refund for a service not provided correctly (which falls under breach of contract). Wisconsin has no specific statute for barbershop liability, so it’s general tort/contract principles. Provide photos of damage and any receipts for subsequent fixes. If an injury is involved, get medical documentation. Generally, you can at least get your money back for the service if it was truly bungled, and potentially more if there were real damages. Most such claims are under a few thousand dollars, well within small claims range.

- **Tutoring/Lessons/Coaching Not Delivered:** *Eligible.* If you paid someone for private lessons or coaching (tutor, music teacher, personal trainer, etc.) and they didn’t deliver the sessions or they were far below promised quality, you can sue for a refund or the value of undelivered sessions. Example: You prepay a tutor $500 for 10 sessions, they only did 2 then disappeared – sue for the $400 for unused sessions. Or you hired a tennis coach guaranteeing to train you to a certain level but they never provided the hours – claim what you lost. These are service contract claims. Provide any written agreement or proof of payment and schedule. There’s no special licensing board for most tutors (except maybe if it’s an academic program). Just treat it as a straightforward breach – small claims handles these regularly.

- **Pet Boarding/Grooming/Veterinary Disputes:** *Eligible.* If you took your pet for boarding or grooming and something went wrong (injury to the pet, or they overcharged, or lost your pet’s belongings, etc.), you can sue for monetary loss. For instance, a dog groomer accidentally injures your dog, incurring $800 vet bills – you can seek those $800. Or a kennel lost your dog’s expensive carrier ($200), sue for that. Veterinary malpractice cases (e.g., a vet’s negligence harms your pet) technically can be brought, but note that pets are property under the law, so damages are typically the vet bills and maybe the value of the pet if deceased, not emotional distress. If a pet died due to negligence, the replacement value (which might be small if it’s a mixed breed, etc.) plus vet costs is recoverable; small claims can’t compensate for sentimental value beyond that. Wisconsin does not have a specific statute for vet malpractice, so it’s plain negligence (with $5k cap if seen purely as tort). Many such cases fall within that. Provide any expert letter if possible to prove malpractice (though in small claims, expert testimony is rare; judges often use common sense). If it’s a simpler contract issue (e.g., you paid for deluxe boarding with playtime and it turns out they neglected the pet, resulting in vet costs), you can argue breach of contract and foreseeability of vet bills as consequential damages. It’s within the court’s equitable power to award reasonable costs to make you whole.

- **Medical Billing Disputes (not insurance-covered):** *Eligible.* Small claims can be used if you were overbilled for a medical procedure or charged for services not rendered, and the amount in dispute is under $10k. However, many medical billing issues are entangled with insurance and aren’t typically resolved in small claims by patients (aside from debt collection actions initiated by providers). But say you paid out-of-pocket for a procedure you didn’t receive or you were double-billed – you could sue the provider for that refund. Example: A dentist charged you $1,000 for a crown that was never actually put in – you can claim that $1,000. If the dispute is that the service was done but poorly (like medical malpractice), that is *not* suitable for small claims because malpractice usually exceeds the cap and requires expert testimony. Minor cases – like being charged for an appointment you canceled in time, or being promised a certain price then charged more – are contract issues you can bring. Wisconsin has an independent review process for some surprise billing, but that’s separate from court. As a small claim, gather any written cost estimates or communications about pricing. Medical providers might negotiate or write off such small disputes once sued.

- **Daycare/Preschool Refund Disputes:** *Eligible.* If you paid a daycare or preschool in advance (e.g., first and last months or a deposit) and then withdrew your child but the facility won’t refund unused fees contrary to the contract, you can sue for that money. Also if the daycare closed suddenly and kept your money, or charged you improper fees. Example: You gave a $600 deposit and one month notice, but the daycare neither provided care nor refunded – that $600 is a claim. Provide the enrollment agreement; many have clauses on refunds (some deposits are non-refundable after a certain date, which the court would enforce if clear). If the daycare violated that or if the contract is silent and they took money for services not rendered, you have a good case under basic contract law or even unjust enrichment. Also, if your child was expelled from a program without cause and no refund, you might claim the unused portion. Wisconsin doesn’t have a specific statute on daycare refunds, so it’s the contract terms. These cases are small-dollar and appropriate for small claims.

### Loans, Debts, and Financial Disputes

- **Money Lent to Friends/Family:** *Eligible.* As noted in “personal loans” above, any informal loan can be recovered in small claims (up to $10k). To reiterate: if you loaned someone money and they haven’t paid you back as promised, that’s a valid small claim. Provide proof of the loan (a check, Venmo record, or a text message “I’ll pay you back next month,” etc.). Even absent a formal note, your testimony plus any circumstantial evidence can establish the loan. There is a 6-year limitation on suing for repayment from the date it was due ([wilawlibrary.gov](https://wilawlibrary.gov/topics/justice/civil/judgment.php#:~:text=,Search%20examples%3A%20judgment%20%3B%20judgments)) (if no specified time, arguably 6 years from the loan date or from your demand). It’s wise to first ask in writing for repayment and give a deadline – it strengthens your case if you show the court you tried to resolve it. These cases often boil down to credibility if the defendant claims it was a “gift.” Be clear in your filing it was a loan. Any interest you charge informally cannot exceed Wisconsin’s usury limit (5% if no agreement, up to 12% if agreed in writing, Wis. Stat. § 138.04), but most personal loans don’t involve interest or just “nice” interest.

- **Disputes Over Jointly-Paid Expenses (Roommates, Ex-Partners, Trip Cost-Sharing):** *Eligible.* If you fronted more than your share of a joint expense expecting reimbursement, you can sue the other party for contribution. Common example: Roommates agreed to split utility bills, but one never paid their half; you paid it all – you can sue that roommate for their half. Or an unmarried couple splits rent but only one’s on lease and paid it all – that person can seek half from the other for the period they lived there (assuming an agreement to share costs). Another: A group of friends went on vacation, one person put the whole Airbnb on their card ($2,000) and others were to pay back $500 each, but two friends never paid – you can sue each for $500. These are typically treated as implied contracts. Wisconsin small claims will entertain such fairness-based claims if you can show an understanding existed. Provide any emails or texts showing the cost-sharing plan or Venmo requests, etc. If nothing was explicit, the court may still find an implied agreement based on conduct (everyone benefited and normally would share costs). The limit is $10k, which covers most scenarios (if it was something huge like a $30k jointly-bought car and one took it, that might exceed small claims). 

- **Disputes Over Wedding/Engagement Gifts and Rings if Engagement Off:** *Eligible (with nuances).* If an engagement is broken, Wisconsin follows the rule that the engagement ring (a conditional gift on marriage) should be returned to the giver if the wedding doesn’t occur, regardless of fault ([wilawlibrary.gov](https://wilawlibrary.gov/topics/justice/civil/judgment.php#:~:text=,Search%20examples%3A%20judgment%20%3B%20judgments)) (case law: *Brown v. Thomas*, 127 Wis.2d 318 (Ct. App. 1985)). So, if you gave an engagement ring and the engagement was called off, you can sue in small claims for return of the ring or its value (often by suing for “replevin” of the ring if you want the item back – small claims can handle replevin for property up to $10k value ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=%2A%20Non,property%20does%20not%20exceed%20%2410%2C000))). Engagement ring cases are actually classic small claims, as the ring value is often within $10k. Similarly, expensive wedding gifts between the couple or funds pooled that now one side holds can be disputed. Example: You and ex-fiancé bought a $5,000 car together for after marriage, now split – if title is in one name but half money was yours, you might claim $2,500 back. That can be framed as an *unjust enrichment* or implied contract claim. Wisconsin courts might apply property law or gift law depending on facts. In small claims, judges often try to reach an equitable result. If it’s gifts from third parties, typically those gifts go to whoever they were addressed to, or if to both and wedding off, they should be returned to giver (but that’s between you and the giver, not subject of your suit). Engagement ring suits are straightforward: sue for the ring (replevin) or its value if it’s gone. Provide proof of value (purchase receipt). Replevin actions in small claims require you describe the item and perhaps post a bond if immediate return is sought (usually not needed if you just want a judgment for return or value). The court may just order the other to hand it over or pay. 

- **Recovery of Cash Deposits / Earnest Money:** *Eligible.* If you put down an earnest money deposit on something (like a real estate purchase, an event venue, or a contractor’s project) and the deal fell through under conditions entitling you to a refund, you can sue for the deposit. In fact, **actions for return of earnest money on real property contracts can be filed in small claims regardless of amount** ([www.wicourts.gov](https://www.wicourts.gov/publications/guides/smallclaimsguide.htm#:~:text=,Eviction%20action%20due%20to%20foreclosure)) – Wisconsin carved out that exception so even a $50,000 home earnest money could be handled as a small claim (likely because these are usually straightforward). For non-real-estate deposits (say a $1,000 catering deposit), the regular $10k limit applies. Example: You gave a $2,000 earnest deposit for a house, the sale contract allowed refund if financing failed; your loan was denied but the seller won’t release the money – you can sue for return of earnest money *even if it was above $10k* ([www.wicourts.gov](https://www.wicourts.gov/publications/guides/smallclaimsguide.htm#:~:text=,Eviction%20action%20due%20to%20foreclosure)) (the statute § 799.01(1)(am) specifically permits “return of earnest money tendered for purchase of real property” in small claims, no cap). For deposits on other services (hall rentals, etc.), you’ll usually be under $10k anyway. Provide the contract and evidence that the condition for refund happened or that the other party breached. Wisconsin’s small claims is a favored venue for earnest money disputes because it’s faster and cheaper than full litigation.

- **Creditors Suing Debtors (Banks, Collection Agencies, etc.):** *Eligible with representation.* Creditors like banks, credit card companies, and collection agencies **can** sue consumers in Wisconsin small claims for debts up to $10,000 (and they frequently do). There is no outright bar in Wisconsin against an assignee of a debt using small claims, but note **who can appear:** A company or collection agency can have a full-time employee represent it in small claims without a lawyer *only if it’s the original creditor*, not an assignee ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.06.html#:~:text=,the%20courts%20of%20this%20state)). **Assignees** (debt buyers) are not allowed to send a non-lawyer agent; they must hire an attorney to prosecute the case ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.06.html#:~:text=attorney%20regularly%20authorized%20to%20practice,the%20courts%20of%20this%20state)). In practice, most large creditors use attorneys anyway. So yes, a collection agency that bought your credit card debt can file a small claim, but they’ll have a lawyer sign the complaint. For the debtor (consumer), these cases are defendable in small claims (you can request proof of debt, etc., and file a counterclaim if any violations of the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act occurred – though FDCPA claims often exceed small claims scope, statutory damages of FDCPA are only up to $1,000 so conceivably you could counterclaim for that within small claims). There’s no prohibition in WI like some states that disallow assignment suits in small claims; Wisconsin explicitly permits it as long as the assignee uses an attorney if not a direct employee. So from the plaintiff perspective: if you are a *creditor* owed money by a consumer or business, you can file in small claims. Just ensure proper standing (if you’re an assignee, have documentation of assignment). Many business-to-business debts also go through small claims.

*(To be clear, the audience for this guide is likely creditors collecting money owed *to them* rather than being sued – this bullet is included for completeness that creditors can use small claims. If you are owed money, whether you are an individual or a business or even a collection agency, small claims is open to you within the cap. Out-of-state creditors note: you may need to be registered to do business in WI if the debt originated from doing business in WI – see Special Rules for Businesses section.)*

### Vehicle-Specific Disputes

- **Accident Damage Where Insurance Won’t Pay:** *Eligible.* If you were in a car accident and the other driver was at fault, typically their insurance should cover your damages. But if insurance denies liability or the at-fault driver is uninsured, you can sue the driver (and owner, if different) directly for your losses, as long as it’s within $5,000 (since it’s a tort). For example, minor fender-bender with $3,000 car repair and $500 car rental – sue for $3,500. If your claim is larger (say $8,000 in damage), small claims can’t award above $5k for property damage tort, so you might limit your claim or go to circuit. You could choose to claim just the $5k max in small claims (waiving the rest). Wisconsin’s comparative negligence rule applies – if it was partly your fault, recovery reduces, but small claims judges handle that too. Provide repair estimates, photos, police report if any. Note: if you already got some payment from your own insurer and are suing for the deductible or un-covered part, clarify that in your evidence so you don’t double recover. Also, you cannot sue the insurance company directly in small claims (Wisconsin requires suing the insured and maybe naming insurer for subrogation if needed, but small claims typically just sees driver as defendant). 

- **Mechanic/Body Shop Disputes:** *Eligible.* Besides overcharge cases addressed above, if a mechanic’s work actually caused damage (e.g., improper repair led to engine failure) or a body shop ruined your paint job, you can sue for the resulting loss. This overlaps with earlier “auto repair shoddy work” which we covered. If a specific part failed because of their negligence, and you had to pay another shop $1,000 to fix it, you seek that $1,000. Keep in mind the tort vs contract distinction: a negligent repair is a tort (limited to $5k), but you often also claim they breached the service contract by not performing competently – contract damages could arguably go to $10k. Many plaintiffs just sue for the amount and courts don’t fuss about cap unless it’s obviously an injury or tort beyond $5k. Provide the invoice from the first shop and the second shop’s report stating what was done wrong. Also, Wisconsin’s Motor Vehicle Repair Trade Practices (ATCP 132) require written estimates approval for extra work, etc. If the mechanic violated those (e.g., did work without authorization), you might have a valid claim to not pay for unauthorized work or to get a refund if you paid. You could also report it to DATCP, but that’s separate. In small claims, mention any such violation as it bolsters your case.

- **Dealership Disputes (outside warranty issues):** *Eligible.* If a car dealer in a sale misrepresented the vehicle or failed to honor a contract term (like a promised repair or accessory), you can sue them. Example: Dealer sells you a “certified” used car with the promise to fix a known issue post-sale but never does – you can sue for the cost of that repair. Or if you paid for an extended warranty that the dealer never actually activated, you could sue for the refund of what you paid. These are contract or misrepresentation issues. As covered under “Auto dealer disputes” in Consumer section, Wisconsin’s special statutes (like §100.18 for misrepresentation, or odometer statutes) may allow doubling damages and fees ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/#:~:text=recover%20such%20pecuniary%20loss%2C%20together,of%20a%20violation%20by%20any)). For moderate money issues (under $10k), small claims is a good venue. Present any “We Owe” documents or written promises from the dealer. If it’s a pure fraud (like they lied about accident history), also reference that. New vehicle lemon law claims typically won’t come here (since those normally exceed $10k remedy). But any *monetary* dispute with a dealer you can quantify is fair game if within limit.

- **Towing/Impound Disputes:** *Eligible.* If your car was wrongly towed or you were overcharged for towing/storage in violation of regulations, you can sue the towing company or whoever ordered the tow for reimbursement. Wisconsin has statutes at local level often, and one at state level (Wis. Stat. § 349.13) governing towing from private lots, requiring proper notice signage, etc. If those weren’t followed, you might recover the towing fee. Example: Your car was towed from an apartment with no proper signage and you paid $300 to recover it – sue tow operator (and possibly lot owner) for $300. Or if an impound lot damaged your car while in custody, sue for the repair cost. Provide photos of lack of required signage or proof of damage. There might also be a complaint process via the city, but small claims is available for your money back or damages. Another scenario: If police ordered a tow after an accident and you believe the tow company charged illegal fees beyond the city rate ordinance, you can sue for the excess. Check local ordinances for tow fee caps. Keep all receipts. These cases are usually under a couple thousand dollars and fine for small claims.

- **Parking Lot Damage – Known Driver:** *Eligible.* If your parked vehicle was hit and you managed to identify the responsible driver (maybe via surveillance or witness) who refuses to pay, you can sue them for the repair cost (up to $5k). For example, someone backed into your car causing $1,200 damage and drove off, but you got their plate and they’re identified – a small claim can hold them liable. This is property damage from a vehicle accident, again limited to $5k. Present any witness statements, video, and repair estimates. If you only suspect someone and have no solid proof they caused it, your case might fail – court needs at least preponderance of evidence they did it. If it’s a hit-and-run and you cannot ID the driver, unfortunately you cannot use small claims (you’d use your own insurance or the uninsured motorist fund if applicable). But if ID’d and they won’t go through insurance, court is the remedy.

### Personal Injury (Minor Cases)

- **Minor Injury Cases (within damages cap):** *Eligible.* Most personal injury cases (like car accidents, slip-and-fall, etc.) are too high in damages for small claims, but if you truly have a small injury – say $3,000 of medical bills, a few days of discomfort, no lasting issues – you could choose to sue in small claims rather than get a lawyer for a larger case. The **maximum for personal injury recovery in small claims is $5,000** total ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=judgments%20from%20funds%20owed%29)). That includes things like medical expenses, lost wages, and pain & suffering (though pain & suffering awards in small claims tend to be modest or sometimes not entertained if unsupported). Example: You slipped on a store’s wet floor, sprained your ankle, had $1,200 in urgent care and therapy bills and a $300 income loss – that’s $1,500 out-of-pocket; you might ask for maybe $3,000 total including some pain and suffering. That is permissible. Wisconsin has no separate small claims procedure for injury – it’s just a regular small claim case, often you vs the business or person. Liability needs proving – if clear (like they admitted fault or there’s an incident report), you have a decent shot pro se. If it’s contested, note you *can* have a lawyer in small claims, but lawyers might not take a very small case on contingency. If you do it yourself, gather your medical records, proof of bills, and evidence of fault (photos of the hazard, witness statements). No expert testimony is required for simple injuries. Also note, if you initially file in small claims and later realize your damages are more, you can’t then get more than $5k unless you dismiss and refile in larger court (preferable to assess correctly from start). *Caution:* If there’s any chance your injuries are more serious or could worsen, don’t undersell yourself to stay in small claims – consult an attorney first. But for genuinely minor harm, small claims is a quick way to get compensation. The same applies for minor dog bites or minor property damage with slight personal injury: keep total under $5k. Wisconsin’s comparative negligence (if you were partially at fault, your award reduces proportionally) is applied even in small claims.

### Specific Statutory Claims

Beyond the general categories above, Wisconsin law provides some **statutory causes of action with enhanced damages** that you can bring in small claims when the dollar amounts fit:

- **Bad Check Claims:** *Covered earlier.* Wis. Stat. § 943.245 allows up to 3× the check amount as exemplary damages (max $500 extra) plus attorneys fees and costs ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,checks%20or%20orders%20were%20involved)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,action%2C%20including%20reasonable%20attorney%20fees)). Use this statute if you’re suing for a bounced check. Remember the 20-day notice prerequisite ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,not%20liable%20under%20this%20section)). This is a common specific claim type in small claims. For instance, if someone wrote you a $2,000 bad check for a used item, you gave notice, no payment – you sue for $2,000 + up to $500 penalty + costs ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,action%2C%20including%20reasonable%20attorney%20fees)). Cite §943.245 in your complaint to alert the court to these extra damages.

- **Consumer Protection (Wisconsin DTPA – Wis. Stat. § 100.18):** If you were defrauded by unfair or deceptive business practices (false advertising, etc.), you can sue under §100.18 as discussed for double damages and attorney fees ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/#:~:text=therefor%20in%20any%20court%20of,in%20real%20estate%20practice%2C%20as)). This small claims suit essentially is a “DTPA claim.” Example: A contractor deliberately deceived you about being licensed and you incurred loss due to their shoddy work, you could make it a §100.18 claim and double your damages. Just ensure the actual loss is ≤ $10k, because the doubling still must fit the small claims jurisdiction of $10k per “other civil action” ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=A%20plaintiff%20may%20elect%20to,1030)) (some argue the double is a statutory penalty and doesn’t count toward cap, but most likely the total judgment including double cannot exceed $10k in small claims court). These claims help when a merchant’s conduct was egregious or widespread (could even support a class action in big court, but as an individual you do it here). If you win, the court *shall* award you costs and reasonable attorney’s fee – that can exceed the $10k cap technically, but typically fees are handled separately. The main double damages remedy is directly within small claims power ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/#:~:text=therefor%20in%20any%20court%20of,in%20real%20estate%20practice%2C%20as)). Statute of limitations for §100.18 is 3 years, so act timely.

- **Lemon Law (New Car Lemon – Wis. Stat. § 218.0171):** As noted, this gives a consumer of a defective new vehicle the right to a refund or replacement, and if manufacturer fails, you can get **double the vehicle’s purchase price plus attorney fees**. However, because new vehicles are expensive, Lemon Law cases usually exceed small claims. The minimum remedy (double price) will almost always top $10k (e.g., a $25k car becomes a $50k claim doubled). Therefore, lemon law suits are handled in regular court (and often the manufacturer resolves it before trial, given the stiff penalty). If you have a cheap new motorcycle or something under $10k, theoretically you could try small claims lemon law – but practically, it’s not common. So, while lemon law is a statutory claim with enhanced damages, it doesn’t align with small claims’ dollar limit except in edge cases. If your vehicle qualifies as a lemon, you’re better off using the DOT’s arbitration or hiring a lemon law attorney (who will recover fees from manufacturer if you prevail). 

- **Identity Theft / Credit Fraud:** Wisconsin law (Wis. Stat. § 895.446 covers property loss from crime including identity theft under §§ 943.201, .203) allows victims to sue the perpetrator for **actual damages, costs of repair (like credit monitoring, attorney fees spent clearing it), and up to 3× damages as punitive** ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,caused%20the%20damage%20or%20loss)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,76)). If someone opened accounts in your name or stole your identity, you can sue them under this statute. Often, though, the perpetrator is unknown or judgment-proof. But say a known person (relative, etc.) did it – you could attempt to recover the money you had to pay or spent resolving it. Federal law also allows victims of identity theft to recover some statutory damages under specific acts (like Fair Credit Reporting Act if a credit agency failed in obligations – those are typically federal court or big court matters, but if just $1,000 statutory, small claims can hear it). In practice, identity theft victims more often rely on law enforcement and credit report fixes rather than small claims suits, but the option exists. The small claims cap of $10k applies – so if losses were e.g. $15k drained from your account, small claims can’t award full amount. You might choose to sue for $10k cap if the thief has some money and you want a quicker judgment. Provide evidence linking the defendant to the fraud and documentation of your loss. If successful, you might get up to 3× your loss (capped by the $10k limit, presumably). 

- **Wage/Hour Statutory Damages:** We discussed these in Employment, but to recap as statutory: Wis. Stat. § 109.11 allows up to 50% or 100% penalty on unpaid wages ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=,of%20wages%20due%20and%20unpaid)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=the%20wage%20claim%20under%20sub,those%20wages%20due%20and%20unpaid)); Wis. Stat. § 109.03(6) mandates costs and fees to a worker who wins a wage claim. Federal Fair Labor Standards Act allows double back-pay and fees for overtime/minimum wage violations. These extra damages and fees *are recoverable in small claims*. The court won’t shy from awarding them just because they’re “penalties” – they are part of the statutory right. Just ensure your base wage claim is ≤ $10k. If, say, you’re owed $8k wages and asking for $4k penalty = $12k total, that breaches the limit – you might only claim $6k wages + $3k penalty to stay at $9k total, or go to large court. Determine your strategy accordingly.

- **Civil Theft (Wis. Stat. § 895.446):** Already referenced – if someone committed a crime like theft, fraud, criminal damage, you can sue under this for actual plus 3× exemplary damages and fees ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,caused%20the%20damage%20or%20loss)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,76)). It’s broader than identity theft – includes any intentional property crime. If your **ex-roommate stole your expensive laptop** ($2,000), you could sue for $2k actual + potentially up to $6k punitive = $8k (within limit). Provide evidence of the theft (police report, etc.). If proven, courts often give some punitive damages (maybe not full triple in all cases, but statute allows up to triple without needing separate proof of malice ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,76))). 

- **DATCP Rule Violations (via Wis. Stat. § 100.20(5)):** Many specific consumer protections come via DATCP administrative rules: e.g. retail installment contracts, price gouging, membership club practices, etc. If a business violates one of those rules and it caused you a monetary loss, §100.20(5) lets you sue for **double the loss plus attorneys fees** ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-20/#:~:text=,including%20a%20reasonable%20attorney%20fee)). We already saw this with tenant security deposits and home improvement, but it also covers things like: *automatic renewal* violations (not clearly obtaining consent for auto-renewing subscriptions as required by ATCP 127 – though that rule doesn’t have a private double-damages remedy except maybe under 100.20(5) if interpreted as an “order” – not entirely settled law), *refund policy violations*, *ticket reselling* scams, etc. The key is checking if a particular DATCP rule states that violation is an unfair trade practice enforceable under 100.20. If yes, you get double damages. Example: ATCP 121 requires proper disclosure of dating service contract rights – if a dating club scammed you out of $2,000 contrary to rules, you can sue for $4,000 plus fees. These are niche but mentionable for thoroughness: Wisconsin gives many avenues to double damages for consumer issues.

### Other State-Specific Claim Types

*(Additional claim types a Wisconsin non-lawyer might bring in small claims include:)*

- **HOA/Neighborhood Association Disputes:** Small claims can resolve some financial disputes with your homeowners’ association or condo association. For example, if your HOA improperly fines you and you pay it under protest, you could sue to recover the fine. Or if you provided a service to the HOA and they didn’t pay, or vice versa (HOA sues owner for unpaid dues – but that’s the HOA as plaintiff, which they often do in small claims if under $10k). Wisconsin has no special court for HOA issues, so small claims handles money claims like unpaid assessments, reimbursement for property damage common element, etc. Note: If an HOA lien is involved, they might foreclose in big court for large sums, but small sums they might just do a personal judgment. If you’re a homeowner suing the HOA, you usually must follow any internal dispute resolution processes if required by bylaws first. But legally, you can bring a claim for monetary relief (not injunction) in small claims. Example: Condo association charged you $300 for a rule violation that you dispute – you pay to avoid trouble, then sue for $300 back. It’s a long shot unless you show the fine was not per rules, but small claims will hear it. Or HOA hired you (an owner) to maintain a lawn and never paid you $1,000 – clear contract claim.

- **Agricultural/Farm Disputes:** Wisconsin being agricultural, a farmer might use small claims for, say, a neighbor’s cattle eating crops (property damage), or a failed sale of a cow, etc. These fall under general contract or tort rules we’ve covered. One specific statute: Wisconsin’s fence law (Ch. 90) where neighbors share fence costs – if your neighbor refuses to pay their share of a partition fence after notice, you could sue for reimbursement under Wis. Stat. § 90.05. The amounts are typically small enough for small claims. Another: if buying/selling hay, feed, livestock, and someone doesn’t pay or the product was bad, those are contract cases suitable for small claims. Some farm matters may have DATCP mediation (e.g., farm mediate credit issues) but it’s optional. Nothing bars these from small claims if purely monetary.

- **Fishing/Hunting License or Equipment Disputes:** Not common, but if, say, you paid a guide for a hunting trip that was never taken, or bought a boat that turned out to have liens, etc., those are regular contract fraud cases. If you bought a used boat for $8k and the seller lied about it being free of liens, you might sue for rescission or damages (like cost to clear lien) – under $10k, that’s fine in small claims (though if rescission, you want money back and return boat, which effectively is done by ordering money judgment and you offering boat back). Or you paid a deposit for a guided fishing tour $500 and it was canceled with no refund – small claim that deposit. No special agencies to go through first.

- **Rideshare/Gig Economy Disputes:** If you’re a gig worker (Uber driver, etc.) and the company or a rider owes you money (like a cleaning fee that wasn’t paid out), or as a customer if you were wrongly charged, you can theoretically sue for those amounts. However, many gig companies have arbitration clauses in their terms that might bar court. If arbitration isn’t invoked, small claims can handle it. E.g., you’re a delivery driver owed $200 in tips you can prove weren’t paid – you could sue the delivery company. They’d likely compel arbitration, but if not, the court could decide it as a wage claim. Similarly, if you loaned your car to a friend for Uber and they damaged it, we covered that in property damage. Or if a rideshare driver caused a minor collision you have a claim as above.

- **Cell Phone Early Termination Fees (ETF) or Billing Errors:** If a mobile carrier charged you an improper fee or kept charging after cancellation and you paid, you could sue to get that money back. Many telecom contracts have arbitration clauses, but if not enforced, you can go to small claims. For instance, you canceled service per contract but they charged a $300 ETF incorrectly – sue for $300. Or you returned a phone within the trial period but were billed $800 – small claims. The Wisconsin Department of Ag, Trade & Consumer Protection would also take complaints on telecom billing, but they don’t adjudicate money – they mediate. So court is fine. Federal law caps some termination fees and provides remedies for unauthorized charges (FCC rules), but those typically funnel through FCC or class actions, which is beyond small claims. For an individual billing dispute, small claims works.

- **Ticket Scalping / Event Refunds:** If you bought event tickets from someone and they turned out fraudulent, you can sue that seller for your money back (assuming you can find them). Or if an event promoter canceled and never refunded tickets you bought directly, you could sue (though often class actions handle big cases). Small claims is appropriate if, say, a local concert was canceled and promoter refuses refunds of $100 each – you individually can file. Wisconsin has a “Ticket Buyer Bill of Rights” but enforcement is usually through DATCP or the AG. Still, contractually, a seller owes you a refund for a canceled event unless terms say otherwise (force majeure cases like pandemic cancellations got tricky – sometimes credits were offered). If a rescheduled event doesn’t work for you, some states mandated refunds; Wisconsin followed general contract principles. You could argue frustration of purpose to get refund if not provided. These cases may or may not succeed, but small claims gives you a shot at a fair hearing quickly.

- **Airbnb or Short-Term Rental Deposits:** If you rented a vacation home and the host wrongfully withheld your security deposit or charged bogus damage fees, you can sue for that amount. Airbnb’s platform has dispute resolution, but that might not bar you from suing in court if unsatisfied (Airbnb’s terms do have arbitration, but applying it to a host-guest deposit dispute can be murky). Wisconsin small claims would treat it like any landlord-tenant security deposit if it’s in Wisconsin (for a rental < 30 days, ATCP 134 might not technically apply, but the concept of returning deposit still is contractual). Example: Host keeps $500 saying you damaged a table which you didn’t – you can sue host (if you can find their real identity; Airbnb won’t be a party since they’re just an intermediary typically). Many out-of-state guests might not want to come back to WI for a small claims hearing unless $ is substantial. But a local person might. On the flip side, if you’re a host and guest broke something beyond deposit, you could sue guest for additional damages (though often not worth it unless major). This is possible in small claims as a simple property damage claim by the host.

- **Subrogation claims (insurance reimbursements):** Insurance companies often file small claims as subrogee of their insured for small amounts – e.g., your insurance paid $3,000 for your fender-bender caused by someone, the insurer might sue the at-fault in small claims to recover that $3,000. This is allowed (they stand in your shoes), but as an individual audience, you wouldn’t be filing those (just know if you’re sued by “State Farm as subrogee of John Doe,” it’s a valid small claim scenario). The insurance company does need an attorney (because they are not the original party but an assignee of the claim by subrogation, and under WI law an assignee must appear by attorney unless a full-time employee who is an attorney) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.06.html#:~:text=attorney%20regularly%20authorized%20to%20practice,the%20courts%20of%20this%20state)). Typically they do have counsel. I mention this only because it’s a distinct category of small claims plaintiff out there.

- **Government Fines or Taxes (municipal claims):** As a citizen, you usually can’t sue the government in small claims except for things like refunds of taxes or fees that were wrongfully collected, but those often require filing a claim with the agency first. Conversely, **cities or counties can sue individuals in small claims for things like unpaid taxes (if under $10k) or ordinance violation fees** ([www.wicourts.gov](https://www.wicourts.gov/publications/guides/smallclaimsguide.htm#:~:text=arbitration%20award%20where%20arbitration%20was,Eviction%20action%20due%20to%20foreclosure)). For instance, a city might use small claims to collect a $2,000 property tax arrearage or a health code fine. Those are not claims you as a plaintiff bring, but be aware small claims covers them. If you needed to *recover* a fee you paid (say the city wrongly assessed you for something and you want a refund), you’d typically file a claim with the city under Wis. Stat. § 893.80 first (notice of claim) and then could sue if denied. That would likely be a large claim or a specialized proceeding if it’s a tax. Small claims might not be the venue if it’s essentially an appeal of a tax assessment. But if it’s straightforward like “I paid $300 for a permit that I never received; the city won’t refund” – you could try small claims after giving the required notice to municipality. Wisconsin requires a notice of claim to the appropriate clerk and either disallowance or 120 days pass, *before suing a county or city* for money ([wilawlibrary.gov](https://wilawlibrary.gov/topics/justice/civil/judgment.php#:~:text=,Search%20examples%3A%20judgment%20%3B%20judgments)). So do that to avoid dismissal. State entities have similar requirements and often sovereign immunity issues (e.g., you can’t sue the state in small claims for a refund unless a statute lets you).

*(The above list is not exhaustive but covers the vast majority of small-claimable situations in Wisconsin. Essentially, **if your dispute is for money and doesn’t fall into a special exclusion (like family matters, government immunity, etc.), and is ≤ $10,000 ($5,000 for PI torts), you can likely file it in small claims**.)*

### Counterclaims in Small Claims

- **Defendant Counterclaims:** If you’re sued in small claims, you have the right to file a counterclaim against the plaintiff *in the same case*. The counterclaim can be for any related claim you have **against the plaintiff**. In Wisconsin, if your counterclaim **“arises out of the same transaction or occurrence”** as the plaintiff’s claim and it **exceeds the small claims limits**, **the entire case will be removed to regular circuit court** (the small claims procedure no longer applies) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.02.html#:~:text=,06%20%282)). For example, plaintiff sues you for $3,000 in damages, and you counterclaim for $15,000 in related damages – you can do so, but you’ll have to pay an additional filing fee (the difference between a large and small claim fee) and the case converts to a formal civil case with full procedure ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.02.html#:~:text=,06%20%282)). If your counterclaim over the limit is *not* from the same transaction (unrelated), the court will **dismiss** that counterclaim as not allowed in this small claims case ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.02.html#:~:text=,and%20proceed%20under%20this%20chapter)) (you’d have to sue separately) – so you can’t tag on an unrelated big claim. If the counterclaim is within $10k (or $5k for tort), it stays in small claims and will be heard together.

- **Dollar Limit on Counterclaims:** Generally, the counterclaim is subject to the same $10k/$5k limits if you want to remain in small claims. You *may* counter-sue for more than that, but as noted, it triggers removal to circuit court if it’s same occurrence ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.02.html#:~:text=,06%20%282)). If you file a counterclaim for more than limit and *don’t* want removal, you can **voluntarily cap/waive the amount over the limit** and keep it in small claims (e.g., counterclaim for “$10,000 of my $15,000 damage, waiving excess”). If you do nothing, by law the clerk will treat it as a request to bump the case up to circuit (with fee) or dismiss if not same occurrence. 

- **Procedure to Counterclaim:** In Wisconsin small claims, you may state your counterclaim orally at the return date or file it in writing before or on the return date ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.20.html#:~:text=,otherwise%20respond%20to%20the%20complaint)). Many counties provide a form **“SC-5200V Answer and Counterclaim”** for defendants ([courts.danecounty.gov](https://courts.danecounty.gov/Resources/Forms/small-claims-forms#:~:text=GF)). By filling that, you can write your counterclaim on it. Dane County lists **SC-5200V** for this purpose ([courts.danecounty.gov](https://courts.danecounty.gov/Resources/Forms/small-claims-forms#:~:text=Small%20Claims%20Eviction%20Stipulation)) ([courts.danecounty.gov](https://courts.danecounty.gov/Resources/Forms/small-claims-forms#:~:text=GF)). If you appear in court on return date, the commissioner or judge will ask if you have a claim against the plaintiff ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.20.html#:~:text=,is%20subject%20to%20immediate%20dismissal)). If you do, you should say so and ideally have it written out. The counterclaim doesn’t need to be elaborate – a brief statement of what you’re seeking and why. The court will then typically schedule a trial for both the claim and counterclaim together. You must **mail a copy** of any written counterclaim to the plaintiff or their attorney within 60 days of filing ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-02/#:~:text=,within%2060%20days%20after%20filing)) (requirement in §799.02), which practically means immediately after you file if not earlier. In some counties like Milwaukee, initial hearings are before a commissioner and if any party contests or counterclaims, it gets set for trial later. 

- **If Counterclaim Exceeds Limit:** As mentioned, if you do file one that’s above $10k and same facts, the clerk will charge you a fee to convert the case to an $**ordinary civil case** ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.02.html#:~:text=,06%20%282)). For example, if you counterclaim $20,000, you pay the fee difference (~$102 currently, since small claim fee $94.50 vs large $197.50, see Waukesha fees) ([www.waukeshacounty.gov](https://www.waukeshacounty.gov/circuit-courts/court-fees/#:~:text=Garnishment%20%20,%24164.50)) ([www.waukeshacounty.gov](https://www.waukeshacounty.gov/circuit-courts/court-fees/#:~:text=Occupational%20Driver%27s%20License%20Petition%20,%24164.50)), and then both claims proceed with formal discovery, etc., not under small claims rules. If you don’t pay, likely your counterclaim may not be accepted beyond limit or the case stays small claims and you tacitly waived the excess (the statute says “shall be tried under ch. 801-847” if fee paid; if fee not paid, possibly the counterclaim could be dismissed or court may order it anyway – best to pay if you want that route). If your counterclaim is unrelated and over limit, the court will dismiss it from this case altogether ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.02.html#:~:text=,and%20proceed%20under%20this%20chapter)).

- **What if Counterclaim Exceeds Plaintiff’s Claim?** That’s fine as long as it’s within small claims cap or you accept removal if not. E.g., Plaintiff sues for $1,000, you counterclaim for $5,000 – this is allowed (same deal or maybe you claim some other damage from same event). You could end up “winning” money from the plaintiff if your counterclaim outweighs their claim. Small claims judges can net them out or award you the difference if you both have valid points.

- **Form/Name of Filing:** The official form SC-5200V (Answer and Counterclaim) is used to both respond and assert your counterclaim ([courts.danecounty.gov](https://courts.danecounty.gov/Resources/Forms/small-claims-forms#:~:text=GF)). If you already answered, you can still file a counterclaim by the return date. Always include the case caption and title it “Counterclaim” if typing your own. State “Defendant counterclaims against Plaintiff for [brief description and amount].” No summons needed for a counterclaim.

- **Counterclaim Filing Fee:** There’s typically no separate fee if your counterclaim remains in small claims limits; the initial plaintiff paid the filing fee already. Only if you exceed the limit, §799.02(1) requires you to pay the fee equal to difference between regular civil and small claims fee ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.02.html#:~:text=,06%20%282)). So if you counterclaim $15k arising out of same event, expect to pay ~ $55 (per current fees) to clerk. If you don’t, they might stick with small claims rules but then arguably you can’t recover above $10k.

- **Deadlines:** It’s best to file any counterclaim by the **return date** (the first court date). In many counties, written answer/counter is required by that date (e.g., Waukesha’s local rule 6.2 requires defendant file a written answer by return date) ([www.waukeshacounty.gov](https://www.waukeshacounty.gov/circuit-courts/civil-court/small-claims/#:~:text=Small%20Claims%20,LOCAL%20RULE)). If you show up and orally state it, they often accept that and set a later date for you to file something formal if needed. But don’t wait beyond the return date unless the judge gives permission – you could lose the chance. If you discover a counterclaim later, you might still ask the court’s leave to file it, but it’s discretionary.

### What You CANNOT Sue for in Small Claims (Wisconsin-specific exclusions)

Small claims is **limited to money judgments and certain specific actions.** You generally **cannot** use small claims court for cases seeking *non-monetary* relief or falling under specialized jurisdictions. Here are key things you **cannot sue for in Wisconsin small claims:**

- **Evictions / Unlawful Detainer (for possession):** Wait, this is tricky – **evictions are handled in small claims court** in Wisconsin, but they are a special category with separate procedures ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=%28a%29%20Eviction%20actions,amount%20of%20rent%20claimed%20therein)). So you *do* file evictions as small claims (regardless of rent amount). However, an eviction case seeks possession of property (and possibly rent owed). The question is phrased “cannot sue for possession – separate docket.” In Wisconsin, it’s not a separate court, it’s within small claims but expedited. If you are a landlord wanting to evict, you absolutely file in small claims (with an “Eviction” case type). But **if you are a tenant wanting to force a landlord to let you back in or stop an eviction,** you cannot initiate that in small claims – you’d have to defend in their eviction case or seek an injunction in regular court. Small claims doesn’t issue injunctions to, say, **prevent** an eviction or enforce housing rights beyond money. So, **possession of real estate** is only granted in small claims in the context of a landlord’s eviction action or a foreclosure-related eviction ([www.wicourts.gov](https://www.wicourts.gov/publications/guides/smallclaimsguide.htm#:~:text=,financed%20is%20%2425%2C000%20or%20less)). A private person cannot sue in small claims for an injunction or specific performance like “make my landlord give me keys” or “stop my neighbor from trespassing” – that’s equitable relief not available. Summary: **Landlords do evictions as small claims; tenants or others cannot sue for possession in small claims except to defend or counterclaim in an eviction case.**

- **Family Law Matters:** Small claims won’t hear cases involving divorce, child support, child custody, alimony, adoption, or anything under family court. Those are exclusively handled by family court (circuit court family division). You cannot sue your ex-spouse in small claims to enforce divorce decree payments – you’d go back to family court for contempt or modification. You also can’t sue for emotional distress or pain & suffering resulting from family disputes in small claims (those would be tort claims often exceeding cap and also possibly barred by inter-spousal immunity if during marriage). *Exception:* If after a divorce, one party owes the other money as per a property settlement and hasn’t paid, sometimes that is collected via contempt in divorce case, but potentially one could try a separate civil judgment; however, courts typically fold that into the divorce enforcement rather than new case. So consider family-related debts out-of-bounds for small claims. Also, paternity and parental rights actions are not small claims.

- **Defamation (Libel/Slander) above $5k:** Libel or slander is a tort – often claimed damages are intangible (harm to reputation) and typically exceed $5k or are hard to quantify. Small claims in theory could hear a defamation case if the plaintiff limits the claim to $5,000 or less (since it’s a tort) ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=judgments%20from%20funds%20owed%29)). But slander cases usually need evidence of actual damage like lost income, etc. They are complex and require careful evidence of false statements and damages – not really suitable for the relaxed small claims process. Also, Wisconsin requires defamation plaintiffs to itemize special damages unless statements are defamatory per se. In practice, you will almost never see a defamation claim in small claims – they belong in regular court because of complexity and often need for discovery. So it’s **highly discouraged** to attempt defamation suits in small claims. If someone tries and demands, say, $5,000 for insults someone said, the court might hear it but likely dismiss if no concrete proof of $ loss. It’s safer to consider defamation as *not for small claims*, especially if the harm is more than $5k. Additionally, if you want injunctive relief (stop someone from speaking), small claims cannot provide that – it’s money only.

- **Title to Real Estate:** Small claims cannot determine ownership of real estate or resolve title disputes. Those must go to circuit court large-claim. For instance, you cannot sue in small claims to quiet title on a property or to enforce a real estate contract via specific performance (forcing a sale). If you tried to fit it as a money claim (like suing for the down payment back – that is fine, as we discussed “earnest money return” is allowed in small claims ([www.wicourts.gov](https://www.wicourts.gov/publications/guides/smallclaimsguide.htm#:~:text=,claimed%20is%20%2410%2C000%20or%20less))). But if it’s about who owns land or whether a deed is valid, that’s outside small claims. Similarly, partition of property or foreclosure actions are not small claims (foreclosures are large-claim regardless of amount involved, with separate procedures). So anything involving actual title or possession of *real property* beyond an eviction scenario – small claims is not the forum.

- **Malpractice (Medical, Legal, etc.):** Professional malpractice claims typically far exceed $5k and also require expert testimony to prove deviation from standard of care, which small claims is not suited for (expert witnesses often require formal discovery, depositions, etc.). You cannot realistically litigate a med mal case in small claims – plus med mal in Wisconsin has procedural prerequisites (mediation, etc.) for claims >$1,000. Even a small malpractice (like a dentist pulled the wrong tooth and cost to fix is $2,000) – you might think to file in small claims. But by Wisconsin law, any action against a healthcare provider for malpractice likely triggers Chapter 655 rules (if it’s against a physician/hospital) including mediation requirement first. So those carve it out of small claims track. Legal malpractice similarly often involves complex issues of proof and almost always more than $5k damage. So practically, **malpractice claims are not handled in small claims**.

- **Federal Claims or Causes of Action:** Small claims is a state court proceeding, so certain matters that fall under exclusive federal jurisdiction or complex federal statutes are not suitable. For example, you cannot sue someone in small claims for federal civil rights violations (42 U.S.C. §1983) – even if your damages are small, those cases belong in regular court (state or federal) because they involve constitutional questions and attorney fees shifting, etc. Bankruptcy-related matters: if someone owes you money but filed bankruptcy, you can’t pursue them in small claims due to the automatic stay (and ultimately the discharge). Any claim *against the federal government* – e.g., you think USPS lost your package and owe you $200 – that must go through federal administrative claim process, not small claims. ERISA-governed disputes (like if a health plan improperly denied $1,000 benefit) – those must go to federal court typically, not small claims. So, **federal jurisdiction cases (bankruptcy, patents, federal tax, etc.) are off-limits.** Another example: you cannot bring a **federal bankruptcy adversary claim** in small claims – those must be in bankruptcy court. Nor can you sue someone who’s in an active bankruptcy due to the stay.

- **Class Actions or Mass Tort Actions:** Small claims is for individual claims. You cannot file a class action via small claims procedure. Class actions must be in regular circuit court and meet class certification rules. So even if each class member’s claim is small, you can’t collectively bring it as one small claim case on behalf of many – it doesn’t allow representative actions. (Exception: certain *consolidation* of related cases by court is possible, but not a class per se). So no class suits in small claims.

- **Specific Performance / Injunctive Relief:** Small claims = money. The court will not issue orders forcing someone to do or not do something (other than to pay money or return specific personal property via replevin). For instance, you cannot ask small claims court to order a contractor “finish the job properly” – you can only sue for the cost of hiring someone else to finish. You cannot ask the court to order your neighbor to remove a fence – you can sue for damages that fence caused if any, but not removal. No restraining orders – those are under separate harassment or family statutes in regular court (though note, injunctions like harassment restraining orders are filed in civil court but not as small claims; they have no fee for certain types and a different process). So if you want anything other than a money judgment (or return of personal property), small claims is not the correct forum. The one quasi-exception is replevin, which results in an order to return property (or pay its value) – small claims can do that for personal property valued ≤ $10k ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=%2A%20Non,property%20does%20not%20exceed%20%2410%2C000)). But beyond that, no equitable relief like injunctions or declaratory judgments.

- **Suing the State of Wisconsin or the United States:** Generally, you can’t sue the State in small claims without it consenting. Certain small claims actions *by the state* are allowed (like state tax collection under $10k might be done in small claims). But if the state owed you money (like a tax refund) you’d have to follow the specific statute (Court of Claims for state or file a claim with Department of Administration). So small claims is not available against state agencies. Likewise, you can’t sue the federal government or an arm of the federal government in state small claims – those claims (if allowed at all under Federal Tort Claims Act or such) must go through federal channels. 

- **Workers’ Compensation:** If you’re injured at work, that goes to the Department of Workforce Development’s worker’s comp division, not civil court. You cannot sue your employer in small claims for an on-the-job injury or unpaid medical bills from such – exclusive remedy is via worker’s comp system (except in very rare scenario employer didn’t carry insurance, then you sue under tort but that’d exceed small claims anyway). So anything under the jurisdiction of the Labor and Industry Review Commission (LIRC) like worker’s comp, unemployment comp appeals, equal rights complaints – those cannot be started in small claims.

- **Intellectual Property (Patent/Trademark/Copyright):** Those are exclusively federal for patent/trademark, and for copyright you must sue in federal court after registration if seeking damages. You cannot, for example, sue in small claims alleging someone infringed your patent or your trademark – federal jurisdiction preempts. (You *could* have a contract case about IP if small enough, but not infringement per se.) The U.S. does have a new “Copyright small claims board” at federal level (not state court) for small copyright disputes, but that’s separate. So any true IP infringement claim is out of bounds in Wisconsin small claims.

- **Cases Against Immune Parties:** You cannot sue certain parties due to immunity. For example, you can’t sue the State of Wisconsin or a Wisconsin state agency without permission (so not in small claims). You also cannot sue a foreign country or a Native American tribe in state small claims if they have sovereign immunity unwaived – those would be dismissed. If someone is protected by an **automatic bankruptcy stay** or discharge, the case similarly cannot proceed (the court will dismiss it if they learn of it). Bankruptcy stay is an injunction from federal court – state small claims must abide. So if you file a small claim and defendant shows they filed bankruptcy, the small claim will be halted or dismissed (you’d have to go to bankruptcy court to ask relief from stay, etc., which is beyond small claims scope). 

- **Criminal Matters & Fines:** You cannot use small claims to bring criminal charges or enforce criminal fines. If someone owes criminal restitution to you, you don’t sue – the DA/courts handle that (you can convert an unpaid restitution into a civil judgment but via criminal case). You also cannot sue to impose a fine or penalty except as allowed by statutes we discussed (and that still results in a civil judgment, not a criminal conviction). So anything punitive outside the civil statutory multipliers we discussed is not directly available.

- **Relief Already Granted Elsewhere:** If the matter belongs in probate (estate issues), juvenile court, or tax court – those are separate. For instance, a dispute over inheritance or a will – go to probate court, not small claims. A dispute in a divorce over property division – go to family court. Essentially, small claims is not a shortcut to circumvent another specialized process.

To summarize exclusions: small claims is meant for simpler, private disputes over money or property. It **cannot** do evictions for tenants (only landlords can evict through it), cannot alter marital or parental status, cannot issue injunctions, and cannot decide rights in real property (except rental possession in eviction or return of earnest money). It’s also not for high-stakes or complex litigation requiring extensive evidence or class-wide relief. Wisconsin Stat. § 799.01 and related statutes list what’s within small claims – anything outside that list is excluded ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=%28a%29%20Eviction%20actions,amount%20of%20rent%20claimed%20therein)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=%28d%29%20Other%20civil%20actions,the%20actions%20or%20proceedings%20are)). If you file a wrong-type case, it will be dismissed or transferred.

*(When in doubt about whether a case is allowed in small claims, check Chapter 799 of the statutes and consult an attorney. Filing in the wrong court can waste time and fees.)*

### Damages – What You Can and Can’t Recover

Small claims in Wisconsin can award various types of **compensatory damages and certain statutory penalties**, but it has limits on **punitive damages** and other categories:

- **Compensatory Damages (Actual out-of-pocket loss):** This is the core of small claims – you can recover money to compensate your actual losses: unpaid money owed, the cost to repair or replace damaged property, medical bills, lost wages, etc. The idea is to make you whole up to the jurisdictional cap. As claimant, you should be prepared to prove the dollar amount of your loss with evidence like bills, receipts, estimates, or testimony. The court can award up to $10,000 (or $5,000 in tort cases) in compensatory damages plus allowable costs ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=,enforce%20judgments%20from%20funds%20owed)). If your proven loss exceeds the cap, the max you’ll get is the cap (unless a statutory multiplier or interest pushes it slightly over – usually the principal judgement will be capped and things like interest run separately). 

- **Punitive/Exemplary Damages:** Generally, **punitive damages are not awarded in small claims** unless specifically authorized by statute in the context of your claim. Wisconsin case law notes small claims judges *can’t* issue injunctions or other equitable relief ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=This%20section%20does%20not%20authorize,2d%20129%20%281980)), and by extension, they typically don’t handle common-law punitive damages because those often require a separate phase and are discretionary beyond compensatory. However, many of the statutes we discussed (double damages for deposits, triple for civil theft, etc.) are essentially punitive or exemplary in nature and **are** allowed because the statute authorizes them ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=A%20plaintiff%20may%20elect%20to,1030)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,76)). But *pure* punitive damages for, say, a tort like assault (if you wanted to punish the defendant beyond medical costs) – small claims judges are unlikely to entertain that. They’ll limit you to actual damages unless a statute clearly says otherwise. So if you have a case where punitive damages might be warranted, it’s probably beyond small claims’ scope (and likely over the cap anyway). In sum: **No, punitive damages aren’t available** in small claims except for those statutory multiple-damage provisions (which technically are considered additional compensatory or exemplary as defined by statute). One exception: Wis. Stat. § 799.02(2) implies small claims can’t handle *punitive damages claims that exceed the cap*, since the cap itself would limit them. If you ask for punitive, it could count toward the $5k/$10k limit, and courts might strike it. Better to just not seek punitive in a small claim filing.

- **Statutory Multipliers/Penalties:** These *are* recoverable if your case falls under a statute that provides them. We’ve gone over many: double deposit, double consumer damages, triple theft damages, 50% wage penalties, etc. Small claims judges *will* award these if proven, and they do not consider them outside their jurisdiction, as long as the total doesn’t blow past $10k in a way that violates §799.01. For instance, if you have $5,000 actual and a law says double, that’s $10k – okay. If it says triple, that’s $15k – not okay in small claims. Actually, interestingly Wis. Stat. §799.01(1)(d) says small claims covers other civil actions up to $10,000 ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=%28d%29%20Other%20civil%20actions,the%20actions%20or%20proceedings%20are)). A 2017 WI Supreme Court case (*Miller v. Storey*, cited in the notes ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=A%20plaintiff%20may%20elect%20to,1030))) clarified that a statutory civil theft claim ($10k actual stolen) is an “other civil action” not a tort, so $10k was allowed (and indeed awarded) even though treble would have been $30k – the court allowed up to $10k because that’s the cap and saw it as an “other civil action” scenario. They didn’t allow going to $30k in small claims. So the pattern: if statutory multipliers push the claim above $10k, the maximum you’ll get is $10k (unless you had separate counts or separate defendants possibly). Always check if the statute has its own cap (like worthless check extra $500 cap ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,action%2C%20including%20reasonable%20attorney%20fees)) or dog bite double only if known prior bite). In those cases, the smaller cap or formula will apply and likely be within small claims limit.

- **Attorney’s Fees and Court Costs:** Usually, in American courts each side pays their own attorney fees unless a contract or statute says otherwise. In Wisconsin small claims, **court costs** (the filing fee, service fees, etc.) are typically awarded to the **prevailing party** as a matter of course ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.24.html#:~:text=,the%20judgment%20and%20lien%20docket)) (just like in larger cases). So if you win, you can ask for your filing fee, service fee, perhaps postage or statutory fees. Under Wis. Stat. § 799.25, allowable costs in small claims include the filing fee, service fees, witness fees, etc., and in some cases a prevailing plaintiff gets an additional attorney fee of $10 or $25 (a nominal amount) if they had an attorney – but in practice, because attorneys aren’t common in small claims, this is minor. Now, **actual attorney’s fees** (beyond that token) are not recoverable *unless* a statute or contract specifically allows it. We noted several statutes: security deposit double-damages law mandates the court to award reasonable attorney’s fees to the tenant who prevails ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=withholding%20from%20a%20security%20deposit,rent%20an%20apartment%20are%20not)); the fraudulent representation law (§100.18) and other consumer laws also allow full attorney fees ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/#:~:text=recover%20such%20pecuniary%20loss%2C%20together,of%20a%20violation%20by%20any)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/#:~:text=therefor%20in%20any%20court%20of,in%20real%20estate%20practice%2C%20as)); wage claims allow fees ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=,of%20wages%20due%20and%20unpaid)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=Penalties%20under%20sub,1701)). If you sue under those in small claims and have hired a lawyer, the court will include those fees in the judgment (the judge might require an affidavit of hours/cost to decide the amount). There is no $10k cap on attorney fees awarded by statute, as fees are considered “costs” technically – § 799.01’s limit is on the **damages**. For example, if a tenant wins a $500 deposit case, they get $1,000 doubled plus maybe $1,500 in attorney fees = $2,500 total, which small claims can award (courts treat the fee as an adjunct, not counting toward $10k cap ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/#:~:text=This%20section%20does%20not%20provide,idea%20that%20the%20award%20of)) perhaps). But absent such statute or agreement, you cannot ask the court to make the other party pay your lawyer. If you had a contract that says “loser pays attorney fees,” Wisconsin courts do enforce that typically. In small claims, a contractual fees clause should be honored (though a judge might scrutinize reasonableness). However, note that for consumer contracts, sometimes such clauses might be void or one-sided (e.g., many leases say if landlord sues tenant, tenant pays fees – Wisconsin courts will enforce that if provided in lease and not unconscionable, but because of ATCP deposit rules, often the only scenario it comes up is landlord being able to recover fees if lease says so *and* if they win). So in summary: **you can recover actual attorney fees only if a law or contract specifically entitles you to them.** Otherwise you just get filing/service costs and maybe a token statutory attorney fee ($10–$50 depending on judgment size per §799.25) if you had a lawyer. Most self-represented get just the costs. 

- **Interest (Pre- and Post-Judgment):** Yes, you can recover **interest** on your claim. Pre-judgment interest is typically allowed on liquidated sums from the date due to date of judgment at the legal rate of 5% ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=The%20%245%2C000%20small%20claims%20limitation,3102)), unless a contract specifies a different lawful rate. Wisconsin’s statutory interest on overdue accounts or contracts is 5% simple interest per annum when not otherwise agreed ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-20/#:~:text=person%20of%20s,including%20a%20reasonable%20attorney%20fee)) (or 1% over prime for judgments after 2011, see next). If your claim is for a set amount that was due on a certain date (like a loan or unpaid invoice), you can calculate interest to the filing or judgment date and ask the court for that as part of damages. Often small claims forms don’t have a space, but you can include “plus interest at 5% from [date]” in your complaint. The court may or may not add it explicitly in judgment, but by law, **post-judgment interest** accrues on Wisconsin judgments at a variable rate: currently it’s **1% above the federal prime rate** as of the judgment date ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-20/#:~:text=,including%20a%20reasonable%20attorney%20fee)) (this changed with 2011 Act 69; before that it was a flat 12%). The Director of State Courts publishes the annual judgment interest rate – for example, in 2022–2023 it’s around 5.25% (prime ~4.25% +1) and it adjusts each year if prime moves ([wilawlibrary.gov](https://wilawlibrary.gov/topics/justice/civil/judgment.php#:~:text=Access%29%20,on%20Judgments%20in%20Civil%20Actions)). For **consumer judgments** (consumer transactions <= $25k), the rate is fixed at **12%** per § 814.04(4) for pre-2011 cases; but that changed – now all judgments post-2011 use prime+1 except tax and family. Actually, correction: 2011 Act 69 set interest on **consumer transactions** (where customer is a party) at **12%** simple, and non-consumer at prime+1%. Wait, double-check: Act 69 (2011) amended §814.04(4) to 1% over prime *except* for consumer transactions (like credit cards) which remain 12%. Yes, the Wisconsin Court System’s interest rate chart shows separate categories. So: if your small claim arises from a consumer transaction (most will, unless it’s say business vs business), the post-judgment interest by statute is a **fixed 12% per year** ([wilawlibrary.gov](https://wilawlibrary.gov/topics/justice/civil/judgment.php#:~:text=,on%20Judgments%20in%20Civil%20Actions)). If it’s not a consumer transaction, it’s prime+1 (which in recent years has been lower than 12%). The judgment itself will typically state the rate or just cite the statute. Either way, you get interest until paid. Pre-judgment and post-judgment interest combined cannot push you over the cap at the time of judgment – the cap pertains to the principal amount awarded, interest is ancillary. So, yes, expect interest to be awarded on any judgment, and you can request specific pre-verdict interest if applicable. 

- **Court Costs & Service Fees:** As mentioned, winning party gets to add the **filing fee, service of process fees, and certain other costs** to the judgment ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.24.html#:~:text=,the%20judgment%20and%20lien%20docket)). In Wisconsin, small claims court costs are governed by §799.25 and §814.62. Typically, if you win, the judgment will say “plus $ costs” which include the $94.50 filing fee, the service fees (sheriff or private process server, e.g. $12 + mileage per defendant served ([www.womenslaw.org](https://www.womenslaw.org/laws/wi/statutes/81470-fees-sheriffs#:~:text=%281%29%20Service%20of%20process,123%20if)) ([www.womenslaw.org](https://www.womenslaw.org/laws/wi/statutes/81470-fees-sheriffs#:~:text=,mile%20actually%20and%20necessarily%20traveled))), and even $5-7 mailing fees if any. If you had a witness subpoenaed, the fee you paid (e.g. $16 witness fee + mileage) can be taxed as cost if that witness’s testimony was relevant. If a party prevailed and had an attorney, they might get a statutory attorney fee of say $100 (just pulling from memory – in small claims it might be $10 for under $500, $25 for $500-$1000, $50 for over $1k – check §799.25 or §814.04(1)). But again, these are small add-ons. The important part: you **do get reimbursed for filing and serving** if you win. If both parties win something (like plaintiff wins on claim, defendant on counterclaim and they offset), the court might offset costs or each bear their own – up to judge’s fairness. If the case is dismissed (defendant wins entirely), typically the defendant can get costs from the plaintiff (though if it was non-payment of rent where tenant won on technicality, sometimes courts might not assess costs – but generally yes, prevailing defendant gets costs).

- **Exemplary “Double/Triple” Damages:** Covered above, but to reiterate, these are recoverable when statute allows. They are intended to make it worthwhile for individuals to sue for small amounts by upping the award (like double deposit, etc.). Small claims judges are aware of these and will award them if you prove the criteria (for deposit, just show no statement in 21 days, then court must double ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=withholding%20from%20a%20security%20deposit,rent%20an%20apartment%20are%20not)); for consumer misrep, show the violation and actual loss, they’ll double by statute ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/#:~:text=therefor%20in%20any%20court%20of,in%20real%20estate%20practice%2C%20as))). They won’t consider those prohibited “punitive damages,” because they are statutory remedies.

- **No Pain & Suffering Without Proof:** If your case isn’t an injury/tort case, don’t expect any money for emotional distress or pain. Small claims won’t give you money for “inconvenience” or “time spent” (except maybe as a modest factor if very relevant). Example: you sue a contractor for breach – you can’t tack on $500 for “stress” – court will limit to economic loss. If it’s a tort like minor injury, you can claim some general damages, but you need to justify it. Realistically, small claims tends to focus on out-of-pocket losses. Emotional distress or punitive aspects of a wrong (like humiliation by a scam) are beyond small claims unless a statute multiplies the damages to cover that in a sense.

In closing: **Small claims can award** you money up to the limits, cover your costs, grant any statutory double/triple damages and mandated attorney fees or interest, and order return of specific personal property (via a replevin judgment). It **cannot** give you non-monetary relief or make the other side “apologize” or anything intangible. No jail or criminal penalties either – just civil money judgments (if the person doesn’t pay, you use collection methods in section 12). No huge punitive awards outside the statutory framework. In Wisconsin, any interest and costs are added on top of the base damages in the judgment ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.24.html#:~:text=,the%20judgment%20and%20lien%20docket)), and judgments accrue interest until paid at the specified rate. 

That covers what you can expect to recover (dollars) – the main goal is to compensate your loss, with some statutes boosting the recovery to cover attorney fees or deter bad behavior.

## 3. Where to File

**Court Name:** Small claims cases in Wisconsin are handled by the **Circuit Court** of the county, specifically in the **civil division** as “small claims.” Each of Wisconsin’s 72 counties has a circuit court, and small claims is a subset of its civil jurisdiction. Often it’s informally called “Small Claims Court,” but it’s not a separate court – it’s a **procedure within the circuit court** ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=Any%20individual%20or%20corporation%20doing,799%20governs%20small%20claims%20actions)). For filing, you go to the **Clerk of Circuit Court** in the county where you’re filing, and specify it’s a small claims case. The case will be given a “SC” case type code. Many counties have a designated small claims clerk or intake window.

**Venue (Which County):** Wisconsin venue rules for small claims are laid out in Wis. Stat. § 801.50 and specifically § 799.11 for small claims. In general, you should file in the **county where at least one defendant resides**, or where the claim arose. Some common venue guidelines:

- **If the defendant is an individual (person):** file in the county where they **live** (their residence) or where the **claim arose** (e.g., where the contract was signed or performed, or where the accident happened) ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=Filing%20the%20lawsuit)) ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=For%20personal%20injury%20or%20property,where%20the%20other%20party%20lives)). For instance, if someone who lives in Milwaukee County owes you money but the deal/accident happened in Dane County, either Milwaukee or Dane could be proper. Typically, plaintiff chooses defendant’s home county or county of dispute. If the defendant has multiple residences or moves, use last known address and that county.

- **If the defendant is a business:** 
  - **Wisconsin corporation or LLC:** venue is proper where the company’s *principal place of business* is located ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=For%20claims%20based%20on%20contract,business%20and%20maintains%20an%20address)), or where the claim arose. You can find a corporation’s main office address via the Dept. of Financial Institutions. Example: You’re suing XYZ LLC that is based in Brown County – file there (or where the events happened if different).
  - **Out-of-state corporation (foreign company):** you can sue in any Wisconsin county where that company **does substantial business and has an address** ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=corporation%2C%20file%20the%20lawsuit%20in,business%20and%20maintains%20an%20address)). That implies if, say, an Illinois contractor did work in Wisconsin, and has no physical office but did the work in Kenosha County, Kenosha might be considered where they did business. Or if suing a big out-of-state company (like a bank) that operates statewide, you technically can choose any county where they have a branch or agent. The DATCP guide says for an out-of-state corporation, *any county where they do substantial business and maintain an address* is fine ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=corporation%2C%20file%20the%20lawsuit%20in,business%20and%20maintains%20an%20address)). It’s wise to pick where the transaction occurred or where they have a registered agent address. If they are required to have a registered agent in WI (most foreign corps do if doing business here), that agent’s county could be seen as venue.
  - **Sole Proprietor or Partnership:** treat like an individual – where that owner resides or does business.
  - **Municipality or County as defendant:** there are special venue provisions; generally, if suing a city or county, it should be in that county (also notice of claim needed). But if a city is suing you, they file in their own county’s circuit.
  
- **Where the contract was signed or service performed:** Under Wis. Stat. § 801.50, generally a claim may be brought where a **contract was entered or to be performed** or where a **tort occurred**. For small claims, Section 799.11 echoes that – e.g., for *rent* claims, the county where rental property is located is also proper venue.

The **Wisconsin DATCP** guidance states simply: for contract disagreements, sue in the county where the person you’re suing lives (if a Wisconsin corp, where its main office is; if out-of-state corp, any county where it does substantial business and has an address) ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=Filing%20the%20lawsuit)). For injury or property damage claims, sue in the county where the injury took place or where the defendant lives ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=For%20personal%20injury%20or%20property,where%20the%20other%20party%20lives)). So that covers most cases:
- If you’re not sure, default to defendant’s home county.
- If multiple defendants in different counties, you can sue in any county where at least one defendant resides ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=If%20there%20are%20several%20people,and%20its%20agents%2C%20contact%20the)). Typically, you’d choose where one with assets or main person is.
- For **evictions:** file in county where property is (since that court will have jurisdiction to order eviction).
- For **return of earnest money (real estate):** statute says that is proper in the county where the property is, but it actually says “regardless of amount.” In practice, often filed where property is or where one party is – but safer to do where property is because the original transaction related to that county.

If you file in the wrong county, what happens? The defendant can object to venue. If venue is indeed improper (not just inconvenient), the court will likely dismiss or transfer. Wisconsin’s venue statutes allow transfer if justice requires. Small claims judges often will transfer the case to the proper county rather than dismiss (Wis. Stat. § 799.11(1)(b) technically says if a case is filed in wrong county and defendant raises it by return date, the court **shall** dismiss without prejudice, but the plaintiff can then refile in correct county; sometimes judges have discretion to transfer rather than require refile). It’s best to get it right the first time to avoid delay and paying another fee.

**Venue vs Jurisdiction:** Venue is about the correct location. *Jurisdiction* is statewide for these claims – any circuit court has jurisdiction over a resident or someone with sufficient contacts. But if you file in a county with no tie to the parties or event, it’s improper venue and can be contested. If the defendant doesn’t object, the case can technically proceed in the wrong county (failure to object waives venue).

**Online Filing (E-Filing):** Yes, Wisconsin has **mandatory e-filing** for attorneys and optional for self-represented litigants, available in all counties for small claims. As of July 2017, attorneys must e-file small claims. As a self-represented individual, you *may* file online through the **Wisconsin eFiling system** (accessible via wicourts.gov). Alternatively, non-attorneys can still file in paper at the clerk’s office. E-filing requires creating an account on the court’s **eFile (Wisconsin Courts eFiling System)**, then following prompts to initiate a case, upload the Summons & Complaint form, and pay the fee by credit card. The **Wisconsin Court System** website provides a **self-help Small Claims forms assistant** and **eFiling instructions** ([www.wicourts.gov](https://www.wicourts.gov/forms1/circuit/ccform.jsp?FormNumber=SC-5200V#:~:text=Wisconsin%20Court%20System%20,are%20available%20in%20Microsoft%20Word)) ([www.waukeshacounty.gov](https://www.waukeshacounty.gov/circuit-courts/civil-court/small-claims/small-claims-matters-post-judgment/#:~:text=In%20All%20Cases%20,pursue%20the%20money%20and%2For%20property)), including a step-by-step on how to e-file. In short, you can do it all online without visiting the courthouse, except you’ll still need to arrange service of the summons. The eFiling site will issue an electronic authenticated summons/complaint after you submit, which you can then print and have served.

For those who prefer paper: go to the Clerk of Court’s office in the appropriate county, ask for the small claims form (or bring your completed SC-500 form), file it, and pay the fee. Some counties (like Dane) have local versions or cover sheets. Check the county court’s website for any specific local forms or rules.

**Improper Venue – Dismissal or Transfer:** Wisconsin small claims law (Wis. Stat. § 799.11) requires the clerk to set a return date and summons when filed, even if venue might be wrong – it’s on the defendant to object. If a defendant timely objects to venue in writing or at the return date, the court should dismiss for improper venue (without prejudice) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.29.html#:~:text=of%20deposit)). Some judges might allow a transfer if both parties agree, but by statute, dismissal is the remedy if a venue objection is raised ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.29.html#:~:text=of%20deposit)) (the statute used to allow change of venue on motion with additional fee under §799.12 but that pertains to service by mail issues, not exactly the same). As plaintiff, if you realize you picked wrong venue after filing, you can file a motion to change venue sooner to save time. But the cleanest is to refile in correct county (since filing fee is often lower in correct venue if your initial county had a high fee – but fee differences are small). It’s better to start fresh than confuse matters.

**Special Venue Situations:** 
- If the defendant is in the military stationed elsewhere – you could still sue in their Wisconsin home county or where incident occurred, but Service Members Civil Relief Act may allow them to delay the case if they can’t appear due to service.
- If defendant moves out of state after the incident – you can still sue in WI where it happened, and serve them out of state (Wisconsin allows out-of-state service for someone who had contacts here). Court will have personal jurisdiction if the claim arises in WI or they lived here at time; venue would be the Wisconsin county of the event or their last known residence here (if none, maybe where event occurred). The small claims pamphlet suggests if out-of-state defendant has no WI business or address, “any county where an address for them is available” might suffice ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=For%20claims%20based%20on%20contract,business%20and%20maintains%20an%20address)) but that’s a gray area. Usually, you’d use the county of the incident as default in that case.

**Venue for Specific Cases Recap:**
- *Landlord sues tenant:* where rental property is located (since that’s where tenancy exists) or where tenant resides now (property county is safest for eviction).
- *Security deposit suit by tenant:* could be where rental was (property location) or where landlord resides or does business. The DATCP guide suggests filing where landlord lives for contract issues ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=Filing%20the%20lawsuit)), but many tenants file in the county of the rental property since evidence/witnesses often there, and typically landlord owns property there. Strictly, venue is also proper where claim arose – failing to return deposit arguably arose where the property is or where lease was. Many counties see those routinely where property is.
- *Auto accident:* where accident occurred or defendant’s home.
- *Contract signed by mail/internet:* you might use your county if you’re plaintiff and the other party did business with you there, but the rule is less clear. Could argue the claim arose where you received the breach impact (like where payment was due to you). But safer to choose their county.

**If filed in wrong venue and defendant doesn’t show up:** The court might still question venue before giving default. Some judges will check that and either transfer or require letter showing venue basis. If they miss it, you might get a default judgment, but the defendant could later move to vacate alleging improper venue if they have a reason for not appearing (some might vacate, some might say venue isn’t jurisdiction – but it’s not jurisdictional, so if no timely objection, it’s waived, default stands).

So pick the right county to avoid these complexities. The **Clerk of Courts** offices can often confirm if your chosen county is appropriate for the type of claim, but they can’t give legal advice – they may at least tell you “if defendant lives in X county, you should probably file there.”

In summary: **File in the county where the defendant resides or does business, or where the underlying events happened.** Use the **Circuit Court (Small Claims Division)** of that county. Wisconsin provides **statewide e-filing**, and you can initiate the case online or in person. Filing in the wrong county may lead to dismissal, so choose venue carefully.

## 4. Filing Fees

Wisconsin small claims filing fees are set by statute and can vary slightly with surcharges, but here’s the breakdown as of 2023 (they haven’t changed in the last couple years):

- **Basic Filing Fee:** For a standard small claims case (money claim ≤ $10,000, replevin, eviction, etc.), the **filing fee is $94.50** in most counties ([www.waukeshacounty.gov](https://www.waukeshacounty.gov/circuit-courts/court-fees/#:~:text=Occupational%20Driver%27s%20License%20Petition%20,%24164.50)). This includes a base fee of $22.00 per §814.62(3)(a) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2010/814/814.62.html#:~:text=814)) plus various surcharges (court support services fee $20, justice information fee $21.50, law library fee, mediation fee in some counties, etc.). For example, Waukesha County’s schedule lists “Small Claim action ≤ $10,000: $94.50” ([www.waukeshacounty.gov](https://www.waukeshacounty.gov/circuit-courts/court-fees/#:~:text=Occupational%20Driver%27s%20License%20Petition%20,%24164.50)). Milwaukee, Dane, etc., are the same or within a dollar (some counties tack on a tiny library fee). You pay this when you file the Summons & Complaint.

- **Filing Fee for *Garnishment* or *Eviction*:** There are specific fees:
  - An **earnings garnishment** filing (post-judgment) is $92.50 if the judgment was ≤ $10k ([www.waukeshacounty.gov](https://www.waukeshacounty.gov/circuit-courts/court-fees/#:~:text=Foreclosure%20%20,%24164.50)).
  - An **eviction** action is considered a small claim too. The fee for an eviction (which often includes a rent claim) is also $94.50 in many counties (some might list it separately, but it’s generally the same small claims filing fee).
  - If you’re filing a **replevin (return of property)**, that’s also the $94.50 if value ≤ $10k ([www.waukeshacounty.gov](https://www.waukeshacounty.gov/circuit-courts/court-fees/#:~:text=Occupational%20Driver%27s%20License%20Petition%20,%24164.50)). Consumer replevin (if it falls under the $25k financed category allowed) might ironically require a higher fee if above $10k value, but since small claims allows it regardless of amount financed for certain cases, I believe they still charge small claims fee for that because it’s in small claims scope.

- **Fee for counterclaim exceeding small claims limit:** If you file a counterclaim that bumps the case out of small claims, you must pay a fee equal to the difference between the normal civil filing ($~265.50 for >$10k) and small claims fee. According to Waukesha: *“Small Claim Counterclaim – Action over $10,000: $125.50”* ([www.waukeshacounty.gov](https://www.waukeshacounty.gov/circuit-courts/court-fees/#:~:text=Occupational%20Driver%27s%20License%20Petition%20,%24164.50)). That suggests if you counterclaim above $10k, you pay $125.50 (which is $31 more than $94.50, aligning with the large claim fee of $147.50 for ≤$10k or $265.50 for >$10k pro-rated – actually, to parse Waukesha: *Large Claim ≤ $10k: $147.50; Large Claim > $10k: $265.50* ([www.waukeshacounty.gov](https://www.waukeshacounty.gov/circuit-courts/court-fees/#:~:text=Large%20Claim%20%20,%24164.50)), interestingly they have a mid-tier for exactly 10k or less in large claim context). But essentially, if converting to a large claim, you pay the extra.

- **Service of Process Fees:** The cost to serve the summons and complaint on each defendant is separate from filing. If you use the **Sheriff** in Wisconsin: 
  - Statutory fee is **$12 per service attempt per defendant** (plus **$6 for each additional person at same address**) ([www.womenslaw.org](https://www.womenslaw.org/laws/wi/statutes/81470-fees-sheriffs#:~:text=%281%29%20Service%20of%20process,123%20if)), **plus mileage**. For mileage, in counties <500k pop, it’s $0.25 per mile round-trip for the deputy ([www.womenslaw.org](https://www.womenslaw.org/laws/wi/statutes/81470-fees-sheriffs#:~:text=,mile%20actually%20and%20necessarily%20traveled)); in Milwaukee County (pop >500k), they charge a flat **$4 per service for travel** instead of per mile ([www.womenslaw.org](https://www.womenslaw.org/laws/wi/statutes/81470-fees-sheriffs#:~:text=,mile%20actually%20and%20necessarily%20traveled)). So, if defendant is local, you might pay about $12 + maybe a couple dollars mileage. Many sheriffs now just charge a flat rate around $40-$60 for out-of-county serves (including mileage). Actually, by statute, Milwaukee Sheriff could charge $12 + $4 = $16 for one person at one address, which is low. Some counties have enacted higher fees under Wis. Stat. § 814.705 (which lets them set higher by county board). Check with the Sheriff’s civil process unit: e.g., Milwaukee Sheriff’s fee might be around $35 nowadays due to county-set increase. But stat default is $12. 
  - If you hire a **private process server**, their fees vary. Typically $50-$75 per serve in metro areas. This cost can be recovered if you win (with a receipt).
  - **Certified mail by clerk:** Wisconsin allows service by mail in small claims in some counties by local rule ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.12.html#:~:text=,11)), except evictions. If authorized, the clerk will mail summons to defendant for a fee of **$2 per defendant plus postage** (somewhere around $7 total). If the mail is not returned and defendant doesn’t show, it’s considered served. Many counties (especially smaller ones) do this. The fee for mail service is listed as $2 + certified mail cost (about $8) in statute 814.62(4). For instance, Dunn County might do it for ~$10. If defendant doesn’t sign green card and letter isn’t returned, it's considered served after 7 days of mailing in small claims. But note, Milwaukee, Dane likely require personal or substitute service by process server for most cases. Always check if your county has authorized mail service under §799.12(2) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.12.html#:~:text=,11)). Some like Winnebago, Rock do, I believe.
  - If service by mail fails (envelope returned), you have to do personal service and likely pay that cost later.
  
  In summary, plan ~$50 per defendant for service if using sheriff or private, though statutory minimum is lower. These fees are added to judgment if you win.

- **Fee Waiver (In Forma Pauperis):** If you cannot afford the filing fee or service costs, you can petition the court for a **fee waiver**. File a form CV-410A (Petition for Waiver of Fees/Costs Affidavit of Indigency). If you are on public assistance (Medicaid, food stamps, etc.) or have extremely low income, the court may waive the fees ([www.wicourts.gov](https://www.wicourts.gov/forms1/circuit/ccform.jsp?FormNumber=SC-5200V#:~:text=Circuit%20court%20forms%20Official%20forms,are%20available%20in%20Microsoft%20Word)). The judge or court commissioner reviews your affidavit. If granted, you don’t pay the filing fee or service (the sheriff will serve without charge, or the clerk will mail at court expense). If partial waiver or denial, you’d have to pay. Each county’s process is similar; often you file the waiver request simultaneously with your complaint. The **criteria**: income under 150% of poverty or receiving means-tested aid is presumptive eligibility, or other hardship factors. This is at the court’s discretion but generally granted to those truly unable to pay. 

- **Recoverability of Fees:** If you paid the fees and win, the judgment will include those amounts so the defendant effectively reimburses you ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.24.html#:~:text=court%20shall%20immediately%20enter%20it,the%20judgment%20and%20lien%20docket)). If you got a fee waiver and win, the state might not charge the defendant those waived fees unless statute provides (I think the court can order the losing defendant to pay the waived fees to the clerk as part of costs). Typically yes, costs can be imposed on the non-indigent party.

- **Other Fees:** 
  - **Jury Demand Fee:** If a party demands a jury trial in a small claims case, there’s an additional fee of **$94 (or $89 in some schedules)** to cover the 6-person jury cost ([www.waukeshacounty.gov](https://www.waukeshacounty.gov/circuit-courts/court-fees/#:~:text=Small%20Claim%20%20,%24134.50)). Waukesha lists “Small Claim Demand for Jury Trial (6-person) – $89.00” ([www.waukeshacounty.gov](https://www.waukeshacounty.gov/circuit-courts/court-fees/#:~:text=Small%20Claim%20%20,%24134.50)). The statute §814.61(4) sets $72 for a 6-person jury fee and some additional charges. The demand must be accompanied by paying that fee within the timeframe (which is: a party must demand jury within 20 days after joining issue, and pay fee or it’s waived ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.21.html#:~:text=799)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.21.html#:~:text=by%20jury%20is%20waived%20forever,trial%20by%20jury%20is%20filed))). If no fee is paid, no jury.
  - **Publication fee:** If you have to serve by publication (because defendant couldn’t be found and court orders service by publishing notice in the newspaper), you’ll incur newspaper charges ($40-$100). This you pay to the newspaper but can add to costs. Many small claims default notices are one-time Class 1 notices, costs vary by paper length.
  - **Transcript or Docketing Fees:** If you win and want to docket the judgment in the lien docket (to lien real estate or pursue garnishment), there’s a $5 **docketing fee** payable to clerk ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.24.html#:~:text=court%20shall%20immediately%20enter%20it,the%20judgment%20and%20lien%20docket)). Actually, reading §799.24 ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.24.html#:~:text=court%20shall%20immediately%20enter%20it,the%20judgment%20and%20lien%20docket)): after judgment, if you pay $5, the clerk enters it into the statewide lien docket for property lien ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.24.html#:~:text=court%2C%20except%20in%20municipal%20and,the%20judgment%20and%20lien%20docket)). So that’s optional but common for large judgments. If ordering transcripts of judgment for other counties, each transcript might cost $5 as well.
  - **Garnishment Filing Fees:** If you need to file an **earnings garnishment** against debtor’s employer post-judgment, the filing fee is $ garnishment case fee ($ garnishment under $10k: $92.50 as listed) ([www.waukeshacounty.gov](https://www.waukeshacounty.gov/circuit-courts/court-fees/#:~:text=Foreclosure%20%20,%24164.50)). If you do **non-earnings garnishment** (bank or third party), small claims also $92.50 in many counties (some might slightly differ but it’s the same category).
  - **Motion to Reopen Fee:** If a default judgment is entered and the defendant later wants to reopen, they must pay a $50 reopen fee if judgment was $0-$5k, or $100 if >$5k (that’s set by §814.07 and §814.61(7)). For plaintiffs wanting to vacate their own dismissal or something, similar fees apply. But for initial filing perspective, not needed.

- **Fee for Replevin Bonds:** If you seek immediate return of property before final judgment (pre-judgment replevin writ), there’s a bond that might be required – that is not a fee but a surety amount. Rare in small claims, usually property is held until trial.

To illustrate a typical cost: Suppose you file a small claim for $2,000. You pay $94.50 to file. You pay say $60 for sheriff or private service. Total $154.50 out of pocket. If you win, judgment might read $2,000 damages + ~$154.50 costs + perhaps interest. You’d get those costs back upon collection. If you lose, you eat the costs (and possibly have to pay defendant’s costs e.g. their $60 attorney fee or lost wages if judge awards costs, but in small claims, defendants’ recoverable costs are similarly filing fee if any, and token attorney fee if applicable).

**Important:** Always check the current fee schedule on your county Clerk of Courts website or the state law library site before filing, as fees can change by legislature (though they’ve been stable since 2011’s Act 69 adjustments). As of 2026, the amounts given are expected to still hold (no new legislation known increasing small claim fees).

## 5. Statute of Limitations

The statute of limitations (SoL) is the legal deadline by which you must file your lawsuit. In Wisconsin, the limitation periods depend on the type of claim, but they are the same whether you file in small claims or larger court. Here are the key limitations for common claim types (current as of 2026, per Wisconsin Statutes Chapter 893):

- **Written Contracts:** **6 years** from the breach/date the claim accrues ([wilawlibrary.gov](https://wilawlibrary.gov/topics/justice/civil/judgment.php#:~:text=,Search%20examples%3A%20judgment%20%3B%20judgments)). (Wis. Stat. § 893.43(1) sets 6 years for an action on a contract or obligation, whether written or oral now – previously written was 6 and oral 6, now both 6 due to changes in 2019; actually the statute was amended in 2019 to 6 years for all contracts, having been briefly 3 for some written contracts between 2013-2015). So if someone breached a written agreement, you have 6 years from the breach to sue. Example: you loaned money via a signed IOU due 1/1/2020 – you must sue by 1/1/2026.

- **Oral Contracts:** **6 years** (same statute §893.43) ([wilawlibrary.gov](https://wilawlibrary.gov/topics/justice/civil/judgment.php#:~:text=,Search%20examples%3A%20judgment%20%3B%20judgments)). In the past, Wisconsin had 6 years for written, 3 for oral, but that changed; now it’s uniformly 6 years for “an action upon any contract” made after March 2016. For older contracts, transitional rules might apply but for safety assume 6. So handshake deals, informal promises – 6-year window.

- **Open Accounts / Credit card debt:** **6 years** generally. Wisconsin considers credit card or open credit accounts as contracts (non-written if no signed agreement, but still 6 now). There is also Wis. Stat. §893.40 (20-year for court judgments) but not relevant until after you have judgment. For a revolving account, the clock might reset at each charge or last charge; typically it runs from when the balance was due or account closed.

- **Promissory Notes (written, for payment of money):** Also 6 years, as they fall under written contracts. If a note had a specific due date or an acceleration clause, time runs from due date (or acceleration date if invoked). If it’s an installment note with payments, a missed payment causes partial breach – each missed payment might have its own mini-limit or some rules treat it as on each installment. In any case, usually within 6 years of last due date or last payment.

- **Property Damage (to personal property):** **6 years** if based on negligence or intentional tort (Wis. Stat. §893.52 is 6 years for injury to property) ([wilawlibrary.gov](https://wilawlibrary.gov/topics/justice/civil/judgment.php#:~:text=,Search%20examples%3A%20judgment%20%3B%20judgments)). Example: someone damaged your car on Jan 1, 2020 – you have until Jan 1, 2026. (This used to be 6, got changed to 3 for a short time, then back to 6 in 2018 – so current is 6). 

- **Personal Injury (like bodily injury in accident):** **3 years** from the injury or from when reasonably discovered (for latent issues) (Wis. Stat. §893.54) ([wilawlibrary.gov](https://wilawlibrary.gov/topics/justice/civil/judgment.php#:~:text=,Search%20examples%3A%20judgment%20%3B%20judgments)). For example, a car accident on Jan 1, 2023 injuring you – deadline Jan 1, 2026. Wrongful death is also 3 years from death. So any lawsuit for personal injuries must be started within 3 years, even if you plan to limit to small claims $5k, that doesn’t extend time. (Note: If the injured party was a minor or legally incompetent, the period can be tolled until they turn 18 or competence regained, then 2 years or the regular period, whichever is longer.)

- **Libel/Slander (Defamation):** **2 years** (Wis. Stat. §893.57, which covers intentional torts like defamation, fraud, etc., is 3 years since 2018; actually check: previously intentional torts were 2, now 3 – yes, 2018 Act 235 changed 893.57 to 3 years for intentional torts). Correction: Actually as of April 5, 2018, §893.57 is 3 years for intentional torts including defamation. So currently **3 years** for defamation ([wilawlibrary.gov](https://wilawlibrary.gov/topics/justice/civil/judgment.php#:~:text=,Search%20examples%3A%20judgment%20%3B%20judgments)). Just a note, many sources might still mention 2; the update extended it to 3. But double-check current statute: I see 893.57 (2022) says 3 years for “an action to recover damages for libel, slander, assault, battery, invasion of privacy, false imprisonment or other intentional tort.” So small claims defamation (if attempted) must be within 3 years of the defamatory statement publication.

- **Fraud (common law fraud/misrepresentation):** considered an intentional tort, so also **3 years** under §893.57 (used to be 6 under catch-all, but since change, intentional misrepresentation likely is seen as “other intentional tort” or “fraud” which falls under either 893.57 or maybe discovery rule extension if not discovered immediately). If it’s under the Deceptive Trade Practices Act (100.18), that statute has its own limitation of **3 years** from occurrence (100.18(11)(b)3). So either way, 3 years for fraud.

- **Consumer Protection claims:** 
  - Wis. Stat. **§100.18** (false advertising) – **3 years** (explicit in statute) ([wilawlibrary.gov](https://wilawlibrary.gov/topics/justice/civil/judgment.php#:~:text=Search%20example%3A%20judgments%20,contain%20relevant%20information%20about%20judgments)).
  - Wis. Stat. **§895.446** (civil theft) – by reference to criminal statutes, but actually 895.446(2) says you have to prove the crime occurred; 895.446(3)5 said limitations same as for the crime. However, separate note: There’s case law that says the civil theft (conversion) is an “other civil action” in small claims context, but for limitations, likely 6 years if treated as injury to property, or maybe 3 if “intentional tort.” Actually, **civil theft** specifically is often analogized to conversion (6 years), but since they made intentional torts 3, it’s ambiguous. The safe approach: for any intentional property loss, better within 3 years if one could argue it’s an intentional tort. But I think conversion (a property tort) historically had 6 years (it’s not enumerated in 893.57, conversion might be covered by 893.52’s 6-year property damage). If it’s framed as fraud or theft by misrepresentation, then 3 years. 
  - **Security deposit double-damages** – That’s not explicitly stated, but since it’s essentially a statutory forfeiture/damages, likely falls under either 1 year for penalties or 2 years for liabilities created by statute where not otherwise provided (some argue deposit claims maybe fall under 893.93(2)(a) for statutory actions which was 2 years but might now be 3 after changes). Actually, the case *Pierce v. Norick* (Wis. App 1987) held deposit claims under ATCP 134 (via 100.20(5)) are subject to 2-year limit as a “forfeiture” or “penalty” under §893.93(2) (which was 2 years) ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=Property%20%E2%80%94%20landlord,Wisconsin%20Court%20of%20Appeals%20Civil)). That might still be good law (except now 893.93(2) is 2 years for “forfeiture or penalty” and 893.93(1) is 6 for liabilities created by statute). There’s an argument deposit double damage is a “penalty” – which is 2-year. Many tenant attorneys assume a **2-year limit** from when tenancy ended for double-damages deposit claims. To be safe, tenants should bring deposit suits within 2 years of lease end. (They could try up to 6 arguing it’s damage under contract, but best not to risk dismissal – file within 2).  
  - **Other consumer statutes** usually fall under either the specific statute's limit or the general 3-year for fraud or 2-year for penalty. Eg. home improvement code violations via 100.20(5) might be considered a “penalty” with 2-year. 

- **Judgments:** Once you get a judgment, it’s valid for **10 years** and can be renewed for another 10 before expiration by filing a new action on the judgment (Wis. Stat. §806.15 and §893.40: a judgment is presumed paid after 20 years) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2017/chapter-806/section-806.15/#:~:text=Statutes%20%3A%3A%20U,person%20against%20whom%20the%20judgment)). But that’s post-case. If someone already has a judgment and wants to collect via small claims, they wouldn’t file new case, they use enforcement steps in same case.

- **Unpaid Wages:** The limitations depends. For a straightforward wage claim, Wis. Stat. §893.44 says **2 years** for wages (from date due) when seeking the 50% penalty (this is referencing the administrative remedy context, but per Johnson v. Roma II, a case said 2-year if penalty sought, 6 if not – but wait, that was old law?). Actually, historically, 2 years for wage claims in circuit if plaintiff seeks that additional 50% penalty (because it’s a penalty). If just normal contract, 6 years as breach of contract. Many wage claims just use 2 to be safe for the penalty portion. 
  Meanwhile, the federal FLSA is 2 years (3 if willful). So advisable to treat **unpaid wage claims as 2-year** (or 3 if willful) to seek full remedies. The plaintiff could still sue up to 6 for breach of contract wages, but then you lose the automatic penalty segment. For final pay violations specifically, Wis. Stat. §109.11 says the penalty wages cannot be awarded for wages due more than 2 years before the action ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=commencement%20of%20an%20action%20in,%282%29%20%28b)). So yes, you must file within 2 years of separation to get waiting time penalty. 
  So, summary: **2 years for wage claims** if you want penalties; 6 years if you’re only claiming base wages as contract.

- **Utility or Medical Bills, Debt Collections:** Treated as contract or open account – so 6 years after last payment or default. Credit card debts often considered 6 (some argue 6 from each purchase or last payment – but effectively 6 from charge-off).

- **Government (State/City) as Defendant:** You must file a **notice of claim within 120 days** (for cities) or typically **within 3 years** (for state) of the incident and then lawsuit within e.g. 6 months of claim disallowance, depending on entity (Wis. Stat. §893.80 for local governments requires notice of injury within 120 days, and notice of claim with itemized relief and then they have 120 days to respond) – plus underlying statute of limitations still applies (usually 3 years if injury, etc.). So if you have a claim against a city for a car damage by a city vehicle, you must give written notice to city within 120 days of the accident or you might be barred, even though you’d have 3-year base limit. Then if the city formally denies your claim or time passes, you have the normal statute 3 years (bodily) or 6 (property) to sue, I think. Actually, 893.80(1d) tolls the limit for up to 120 days during claim process. So essentially, for small claims vs government: Always file a timely notice, then file suit after denial but within the original statute period.

- **Indefinite Tolling:** Certain events toll limitations:
  - If the defendant is out of state and not reachable for service for a period, the clock might toll under some circumstances (Wis. Stat. §893.19 historically – but note WI eliminated tolling for absence in many tort cases).
  - Minor or legally insane plaintiff: statute doesn’t run until disability removed (with cap of adult age plus extension).
  - Fraudulent concealment by defendant of a claim can toll until discovered.

For most small claims users: stick to the main time frames:
**6 years** for most contract and property damage; **3 years** for personal injury and certain statutory/fraud claims; **2 years** for intentional torts pre-2018 (3 now) and wages (for penalty part); **120-day notice for local government** issues.

If you file after the limitations period, the defendant can raise statute of limitations as an affirmative defense, and if proven, the court will dismiss your case as time-barred (with prejudice). The statute is usually counted from the date the cause of action “accrued” – i.e., when you first could sue (when a breach happened or when harm was or should have been known). For installment or continuing contracts, each breach might have its own accrual.

**Examples:** 
- You loaned money due on Jan 1, 2017 – SoL expired Jan 1, 2023 on a contract claim (6 years).
- Car accident on June 1, 2020 – SoL ends June 1, 2023 for personal injury; property damage from it would be June 1, 2026 if property and 2018 law considered conversion, might say 6 year, but default to 6 for property.
- Security deposit withheld from tenancy ending Aug 31, 2021 – best to sue by Aug 31, 2023 (2-year) to get double damages. If you waited until 2024, landlord could argue it’s a penalty and time-barred. Some cases allowed tenant to recover the deposit itself as breach of contract in 6-year but not double. But prudent to treat as 2.

**Tolling for Discovery Rule:** Wisconsin follows a discovery rule for latent injuries - e.g. if you didn’t know you were harmed immediately (like found out of fraud much later), the limitation may start when you discovered or should’ve discovered the injury and cause. This mainly applies in e.g. medical malpractice (which is not in small claims) or latent property damage. For general fraud, courts often say SoL starts when the fraud was discovered or could have been. (Wis. Stat. §893.93(1)(b) for fraud is 6 years from discovery, I recall old rule, but since 2018 it’s 3 years from when discovered (since moved to intentional tort category). There is also a specific statutory scheme for credit card fraud (but you’d handle via federal law).

**Important:** The statute of limitations clock stops once you *file* your case (not when it’s served). So as long as you file the Summons & Complaint by the deadline, you’ve met the SoL. But you must then diligently attempt service; if you intentionally delay serving for months or years, the court might dismiss for failure to prosecute even if limitations was tolled by filing. So file in time and serve promptly.

If you’re very near the deadline, be sure to file by end of business that day (e-filing by 11:59pm might count as same day). 

Finally, if a claim is filed late and the defendant doesn’t raise the statute of limitations defense, the court might consider it waived (SoL is an affirmative defense that must be raised in the answer or at trial). But most defendants or judges will notice. It’s safer to presume you cannot succeed if it’s clearly expired.

In summary:
- **6 years:** Most contract and debt claims, property damage.
- **3 years:** Personal injury; fraud/deceptive practices; many intentional torts (since 2018).
- **2 years:** Certain statutory penalties (e.g., some wage penalties, double deposit as interpreted, etc.); personal injury prior to 2018 changes (no longer applicable now except older incidents).
- **120 days notice + the above** for claims vs local government.
- **No time limit**: Actually, Wisconsin removed some older tolling for crimes and such, but for completeness, there's a 10-year limit on certain official bonds, 2-year on actions against estates after death.

When in doubt, file sooner rather than later. If your claim is borderline time-barred, prepare to explain if any discovery rule tolling applies. Otherwise, the judge will likely dismiss if the filed date is beyond the statutory period.

## 6. Pre-filing Requirements

Wisconsin generally does not mandate formal pre-filing steps for small claims plaintiffs, but it’s often **recommended** or required by specific statutes or contracts to take certain actions before suing. Here are pre-filing considerations:

- **Demand Letter (Recommended):** While not legally required in most cases, it’s a good practice to send a **written demand letter** to the potential defendant before filing. The Wisconsin small claims court guide itself suggests trying to settle first ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=Try%20to%20settle%20first)). A polite but firm letter stating the amount owed or the remedy you seek and giving, say, 10-14 days to respond can sometimes resolve the issue. It also documents that you attempted to resolve it, which judges appreciate. In some scenarios, a demand letter is effectively required to trigger enhanced damages:
  - For **worthless checks**, you *must* give the 15-day written notice demanding payment before you can sue for treble damages ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,not%20liable%20under%20this%20section)).
  - For certain consumer protection claims (like under ATCP rules or lemon law), you often have to give the seller/manufacturer an opportunity to cure or a notice. E.g., Wisconsin’s Lemon Law requires you to send the manufacturer a written demand for refund/replacement and give them 30 days before filing suit ([wisconsindot.gov](https://wisconsindot.gov/Pages/dmv/cons-protect/lemon-law/lemonlaw.aspx#:~:text=What%20qualifies%20as%20a%20%E2%80%9Clemon%E2%80%9D%3F)) ([wisconsindot.gov](https://wisconsindot.gov/Pages/dmv/cons-protect/lemon-law/lemonlaw.aspx#:~:text=,or%20more%20due%20to%20defects)).
  - For **labor wage claims**, not strictly required to send a demand to employer before suing, but if you do and they still don’t pay within 7 days, you might be entitled to certain double costs ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=at%20the%20time%20an%20enforcement,1701)) (plus it bolsters that employer was in bad faith for waiting time penalty).
  - For **security deposit**, not required since the law puts the onus on landlord to do itemization. Tenants often request their deposit after 21 days if not received, but not required. 
  - Also, many counties have small claims mediation programs (like Waukesha’s mandatory mediation for contested claims) ([www.waukeshacounty.gov](https://www.waukeshacounty.gov/mediation#:~:text=Mandatory%20Mediation%20Section%20Menu%20Pursuant,the%20default%20mediators%20for%20small)). While not pre-filing, you’ll likely be required to try mediation after filing if the county has that local rule (e.g., Waukesha Local Rule 6.4).
  
  So, highly recommended: send a certified or tracked letter or even an email (and keep copies) to show you gave the other party a chance. This can sometimes avoid court or at least make them look worse if they ignored a reasonable demand.

- **Mandatory Notice or Cure Period (Contractual):** Check your **contract** if any. Some contracts require disputes be subject to arbitration or notice to the other party and an opportunity to cure. For example, home improvement contracts often have a provision that the contractor must be given written notice of any defects and a chance to fix them. If you signed something like that, you should comply before suing, or defendant might argue the suit is premature. Similarly, some leases say tenant must request deposit return in writing (Wisconsin law doesn’t require that by statute though). If contract has an **alternative dispute resolution clause (mediation or arbitration)**: 
  - **Arbitration clause:** If valid and covers the dispute, the defendant can move to compel arbitration and dismiss the small claim. Courts generally enforce arbitration agreements even in small claims. So if you know there’s an arbitration clause (common in credit card agreements, cellphone contracts, etc.), anticipate you might not get to litigate in small claims unless they waive it. You might still file and see if they raise it; if they do, court will likely stay/dismiss in favor of arbitration. If you prefer arbitration or it’s required (some credit card contracts specifically carve out small claims as an exception – many do allow small claims despite overall arbitration for larger disputes).
  - **Mediation clause:** If a contract mandates mediation before litigation (some service contracts have this), a judge could enforce that and either dismiss or stay the case until mediation is attempted. In practice for small claims, this is rare, but possible.
  
  In summary, if your contract says “party must notify the other of breach and allow 30 days to cure,” do that letter and allow 30 days before suing. If it says “all disputes to arbitration”, be aware your suit could be halted if the other side pushes it.

- **Special Statutory Notices:** 
  - **Home improvement (contractors):** Before suing under the Home Improvement Practices Act (ATCP 110), not a formal requirement to notify DATCP or anything, but if you plan on claiming double damages via Wis. Stat. 100.20(5), you just show violation and loss; no pre-suit demand needed specifically (though a demand might be good evidence of their willful refusal).
  - **Automobile Repair:** Wisconsin’s Auto Repair Code (ATCP 132) doesn’t require pre-suit notice to the shop, but you might consider letting the shop know you’re unsatisfied and intend to sue if not resolved – again, for courtesy and possibly evidence of reasonableness.
  - **Medical malpractice:** If it were small enough (not likely in small claims), Wisconsin law requires a patient to notify the healthcare provider of a claim and go through mediation before suing (Wis. Stat. Chapter 655). But practically, med mal is beyond small claims.
  - **Suing State of WI or municipality:** As mentioned under SoL, you must serve a **Notice of Claim** to the appropriate office (usually via certified mail to the clerk or attorney) and either wait for it to be denied or 120 days pass before filing a lawsuit (Wis. Stat. §893.80) ([www.wicourts.gov](https://www.wicourts.gov/ca/opinion/DisplayDocument.html?content=html&seqNo=96519#:~:text=,Cir)). For example, if you’re going to sue the City for damage to your car from a pothole, you have to send them notice describing the incident and amount of claim within 120 days of the incident. If you don’t, your claim can be barred, no matter how valid. The lawsuit itself must then be after they disallow or by operation of time. This is crucial: the court will dismiss if notice of claim not satisfied for suits vs government. There is also a **$50,000 cap** on damages against municipal governments (which small claims is under anyway) and generally no punitive damages allowed against government. If your claim is against the State (e.g., state agency owes you a refund), the procedure is slightly different (file a claim with the State Claims Board or particular agency depending, and if denied, some have to go to Circuit Court or some exclusively in Court of Claims). You’d need to research that specifically, but generally you cannot just small-claims sue the State of Wisconsin without going through its process.

- **Mandatory Mediation Programs (Local):** Some counties require you to attempt **mediation after filing but before trial** in certain case types (often in small claims between individuals). For instance, in Waukesha County, **all contested small claims (except evictions & replevins)** go to a mediation session by rule ([www.waukeshacounty.gov](https://www.waukeshacounty.gov/mediation#:~:text=Mandatory%20Mediation%20Section%20Menu%20Pursuant,the%20default%20mediators%20for%20small)). Milwaukee County has a **Marquette Volunteer Legal Clinic mediation** program onsite on return date for many small claims. Dane County encourages mediation through the Dispute Resolution Center. These aren’t strictly pre-filing, but as soon as you file and the defendant appears to contest, the court may order mediation. You should be prepared to engage in that in good faith. If you absolutely refuse, the court might not look kindly (some courts may even dismiss for failure to mediate if it’s mandated by rule). However, mediation results are non-binding – if it fails, you still get your trial. It’s free or low-cost. So while not “pre-filing”, it is part of initial process. It might be effectively pre-trial.

- **Eviction cases – notice to quit:** This is important: If you are filing an **eviction (small claims)**, you must have served the tenant a proper **termination notice** (e.g., 5-day or 14-day notice depending on situation) as required by Chapter 704 **before** filing the eviction ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=Typical%20small%20claims)). If you don’t, the eviction will be dismissed. That notice is a prerequisite but is part of landlord-tenant law, not a small claims rule per se – still absolutely needed. For nonpayment, usually a 5-day pay or quit; for nuisance or breach, curable 5-day or non-curable 14-day, etc., per Wis. Stat. §704.17. Also, if it’s a month-to-month tenancy, a 28-day notice to end tenancy may be required. So landlords must ensure they gave correct notice and it expired before filing on the return date. Similarly for land contracts (not exactly small claims if foreclosure needed, but maybe an eviction after land contract – need proper default notice etc.). The court at eviction first hearing will ask if notice was given and attached.

- **Uniform Dwelling Code or Other Pre-suit Notice:** If you’re suing a home builder for construction defects, Wis. Stat. §895.07 (Right to Cure law) required you to give the contractor written notice of defect and 90 days to cure before suing. But that law was repealed in 2015, so currently no such requirement in Wisconsin. So no, not now.

- **Labor disputes – administrative remedy:** For unpaid wages, as discussed, you have the option to file with the Department of Workforce Dev (DWD) wage claim bureau. You are not required to – you can go straight to court. But if you do file with DWD first, they might pursue it and if they can’t resolve, you can still sue later (the limitations for wages might be tolled during that process). There’s no exhaustion requirement (except in certain employment like discrimination claims where you must go through EEOC/ERD first for discrimination, but those aren’t typically small claims money-only matters). For pure wage or commission, no need to exhaust administrative remedy, though you can’t double recover (if DWD already got a judgment, you can’t re-sue; if DWD claim is pending, they may drop it if you sue independently).

- **Certificate of Authority for out-of-state business plaintiff:** If you as a business from out-of-state want to sue in WI courts, technically you should have a certificate of authority if you are "doing business" in Wisconsin (Wis. Stat. §180.1502 for corporations: a foreign corp transacting business in WI without certificate cannot maintain a lawsuit in WI until it obtains one). So an out-of-state company plaintiff should register with Dept. of Financial Institutions to avoid a defense that you lack capacity. If you're just an individual from elsewhere, no issue. But businesses should check: if not registered to do business, the defendant could motion to abate the case until you register (which you can typically do quickly by paying a fee to DFI). This isn't exactly pre-filing required by court, but a legal prerequisite to maintain the action if contested.

In sum, **Wisconsin small claims doesn’t impose broad mandatory pre-filing steps** like some states (which might require a demand letter or mediation first). But specific types of claims or defendants do: 
- **Always attempt a demand** as a best practice.
- **If suing government**, follow notice of claim procedures.
- **If claim is based on a statute that has conditions (like worthless check or lemon law)**, fulfill those conditions (notice to issuer or manufacturer).
- **Landlord/tenant**: ensure proper termination notices and wait periods were given for evictions, and measure deposit timing (21 days).
- **Arbitration agreements**: consider them, maybe attempt that route or prepare for them.
- **Mediation**: know your county’s approach; you often can’t skip it if court orders it after filing.

One more note: 
- **For replevin (return of property)**, if it’s a consumer credit transaction, Wis. Stat. §425.205 requires the creditor to send a **“right to cure” notice** 15 days before filing a replevin (for collateral under consumer credit). If they don’t, the case can be dismissed. That falls under the Wisconsin Consumer Act. So e.g., if a car loan company wants to repossess, they must give notice of default and right to cure first. As an individual suing for return of property, no such requirement (rare scenario, usually it’s businesses reclaiming property).
- **For foreclosure of a mortgage** (not small claims anyway if real property, but in case someone wonders: that has own notice if owner-occupied 1-4 family – a mediation or help notice, but foreclosure is in large claim civil).

**Bottom line:** Check any law governing your type of dispute for preconditions. If none explicitly, at least send a **demand letter**. You might write something like:
“According to Wis. Stat. [some chapter], you owe me X. If I do not receive payment within 10 days, I intend to file a lawsuit in small claims court to recover the amount plus allowable costs and damages.” This sometimes spurs payment or settlement.

Even though not required, a demand letter can sometimes entitle you to attorneys fees under certain statutes if they then ignore and you win – e.g., under certain fee-shifting rules, judges like to see you gave them a chance.

Therefore, **no blanket pre-filing requirement** in Wisconsin small claims, but *demand letters and statutory notices are highly advisable whenever applicable*. 

## 7. Forms Required

To file a small claims case in Wisconsin, you will need the appropriate **forms.** The exact forms can vary slightly by county (some counties have custom versions or require cover sheets), but generally the process uses standardized state forms available on the Wisconsin Court System website. Here are the key forms and documents:

- **Summons and Complaint (SC-500)** – This is the primary form to start a small claims lawsuit. It serves both as the *Summons* (ordering the defendant to appear or answer) and the *Complaint* (where you state your claim). Wisconsin has a standard form **SC-500** (for regular small claims) and SC-500i (Spanish version). You can obtain it online or from the clerk. On this form, you fill in:
  - Your name and address (plaintiff).
  - Defendant’s name(s) and address(es). Make sure to use exact legal names – e.g., “John Doe” or business name with entity type (“ABC, Inc.” or “John Doe d/b/a John’s Plumbing”).
  - The amount you are claiming (including any specific relief like return of property or eviction).
  - A brief statement of the claim (e.g., “Defendant failed to repay a loan of $___ made on [date]” or “Security deposit of $___ wrongfully withheld after lease end on [date]”).
  - For **evictions or replevin** there are boxes to check and additional details (like property address for eviction, or description of property for replevin).
  - The Summons part will list the **return date** or hearing date/time when the defendant must appear or answer. The clerk typically assigns that when you file (some counties have you leave it blank and clerk fills it; others have specific date schedules).
  
  **Where to get SC-500:** On wicourts.gov under forms -> circuit court -> small claims, you’ll find **SC-500** “Summons and Complaint (Small Claims).” It’s available as a fillable PDF or Word doc. For reference, the form number and revision date are usually at bottom; as of now, SC-500 (English) was revised 10/10/19 last。 Example direct link: `wicourts.gov/formdisplay/SC-5000?formNumber=SC-500&formType=Form&formatId=2&language=en` (the official site might generate the PDF).
  
  Many counties have **eFiling**, in which case you don’t physically fill SC-500; instead you enter info in the eFiling system which generates an electronic summons/complaint. But it’s essentially the same info.

- **Electronic Filing Notice (if applicable):** If you file on paper and the defendant is *not* evading, and you want to use eFiling for the case, you might include an **Notice of Availability of Electronic Filing** (some counties might generate this). Actually, by rule, when you file a paper small claim and one party is eFiling-eligible, the clerk might issue an eFiling Notice with the case. Typically, attorneys are required to eFile, pro se can choose. If you file by paper, the clerk often gives you a form that explains to the defendant how they can eFile a response if desired. That’s **Notice of Mandatory/Optional eFiling** (CV-410 for general civil; for small claims it might be included in summons?). This might not be something you fill; the clerk may handle it. For completeness: new rules say if one side eFiles, case converts to eFile, and the other side gets a notice how to opt in. But initial paper filing in small claims by a self-rep plaintiff doesn’t require including any eFile notice – the clerk will issue a **small claims information sheet** to defendant usually, and maybe an opt-in form.

- **Proof of Service form (SC-512 or Affidavit of Service):** After you serve the Summons & Complaint, you must prove to the court that service was done. If a **Sheriff** serves, they will typically file a **Certificate of Service** or return that document to you (some counties the sheriff files it directly with clerk, others give to plaintiff to file). If a **private process server** or any non-sheriff adult serves, that person must complete a signed **Affidavit of Service** (or **Affidavit of Non-Service** if they couldn’t serve). Wisconsin has a form **SC-512** (or GN-312) “Affidavit of Service” you can use, or many process servers use their own formats which is fine if it has needed details (server’s name, date/time/place/method of service, and who was served). The small claims Summons (SC-500) has a section on the back for the sheriff’s use to note service, but if someone else serves, you need a separate affidavit. This must be filed with the court by the return date or presented at hearing to show defendant was properly served. If using **certified mail** by clerk, the clerk will note on file if the green card came back signed or the envelope returned – and you’d maybe file an **Affidavit of Mailing** if using certain methods.

## 1. Jurisdictional limits — MAXIMUM AMOUNT YOU CAN SUE FOR

**Dollar Cap:** In Wisconsin small claims court, you can sue for up to **$10,000** in money damages (for claims seeking a judgment for payment of money) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=%28d%29%20Other%20civil%20actions,the%20actions%20or%20proceedings%20are)) ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=,enforce%20judgments%20from%20funds%20owed)). This $10,000 limit applies to most regular civil claims like debts, unpaid loans, contract breaches, and property damage. There is a lower cap for certain *tort* claims: if your case is for **personal injury or other tort (wrongful act) damages**, the cap is **$5,000** ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=judgments%20from%20funds%20owed%29)). For example, a claim for injuries from a minor car accident is capped at $5,000 in small claims. 

Certain case types have **no dollar limit** in small claims: **eviction actions** (actions for possession of rental property) can be filed in small claims regardless of the amount of rent due ([www.wicourts.gov](https://www.wicourts.gov/publications/guides/smallclaimsguide.htm#:~:text=,financed%20is%20%2425%2C000%20or%20less)), and **return of earnest money** in a real estate purchase has no limit ([www.wicourts.gov](https://www.wicourts.gov/publications/guides/smallclaimsguide.htm#:~:text=,claimed%20is%20%2410%2C000%20or%20less)). Also, **replevin** (recovery of property) has separate limits: if it’s a non-consumer credit replevin, the property value must be $10,000 or less; if it’s a consumer credit transaction replevin (e.g. car loan default), the amount financed must be $25,000 or less ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=%2A%20Non,property%20does%20not%20exceed%20%2410%2C000)). These are statutory exceptions allowing certain cases in small claims even above $10,000 principal, though any money judgment resulting usually cannot exceed $10,000 (aside from allowable costs/interest) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=A%20plaintiff%20may%20elect%20to,1030)). 

**No Different Limits by Plaintiff Type:** The $10,000/$5,000 caps are the same whether the plaintiff is an individual, a corporation, an LLC, a sole proprietor, or a government entity. Any **person or corporation doing business in Wisconsin** can sue or be sued in small claims court under these limits ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=Any%20individual%20or%20corporation%20doing,799%20governs%20small%20claims%20actions)) ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=,enforce%20judgments%20from%20funds%20owed)). Unlike some states, Wisconsin does *not* set a special lower cap for businesses or frequent filers – the $10,000 is uniform. (One minor exception: a **municipality** suing to recover a tax or forfeiture is limited to $10,000 in small claims by statute ([www.wicourts.gov](https://www.wicourts.gov/publications/guides/smallclaimsguide.htm#:~:text=arbitration%20award%20where%20arbitration%20was,Eviction%20action%20due%20to%20foreclosure)), which falls under the normal cap anyway.)

**Multiple Claims and Splitting:** You cannot circumvent the limits by splitting one large claim into several small claims cases. Wisconsin law expects you to bring **one action per claim** – if your total damages exceed $10,000, you generally should file as a regular civil case. If you sue for $10,000 in small claims and try to sue for the remainder later, the second suit will likely be dismissed as *claim splitting* (all related claims against the same defendant should be joined). **However, you may choose to waive any amount above $10,000** and sue in small claims for the $10,000 maximum ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=A%20plaintiff%20may%20elect%20to,1030)). In other words, you can voluntarily limit your recovery to the cap in order to use the simpler small claims process. For example, if you’re owed $12,000, you could opt to sue for $10,000 in small claims (forgiving $2,000) – the court will not award more than $10,000 plus allowable costs. Once you do that, you generally can’t later sue for the leftover $2,000; it’s considered given up. **Note:** Wisconsin courts have held that the small claims limit is a limit on recovery, not on the court’s jurisdiction to hear the case ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=A%20plaintiff%20may%20elect%20to,1030)). This means if you accidentally filed for *more* than $10,000 in small claims, the court wouldn’t automatically toss the case – but it **cannot award** more than the statutory cap, and the excess would be disregarded or require transfer to large claims. 

**Multiple Plaintiffs or Defendants:** The $10,000 cap is per case, not per defendant. If two plaintiffs each have a claim, they typically need to file separately (joint filing is possible but the total combined still should not exceed $10,000 in one suit). If you have multiple defendants responsible for one harm, you still only recover up to $10,000 total from them in that case (the judgment could be joint and several for up to $10k). **Frequent Filers:** Wisconsin does not impose a limit on how many small claims cases you can file per year or any higher fee after a certain number of filings (some states do for more than 10 cases/year – Wisconsin does not). So a business or individual can file numerous small claims as long as each fits the criteria.

In summary, **$10,000** is the maximum for monetary judgments (except $5,000 for personal injury torts) in Wisconsin small claims ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=,enforce%20judgments%20from%20funds%20owed)). Ensure your claim is at or below that – if it’s higher and you don’t want to waive the excess, you must go to regular circuit court. Evictions and specific property return cases are allowed regardless of amount, but those cases generally seek non-monetary relief or limited money (e.g. any amount of unpaid rent can be claimed with an eviction in small claims, though **money** judgments for rent are still capped at $10k if pursued separately) ([www.wicourts.gov](https://www.wicourts.gov/publications/guides/smallclaimsguide.htm#:~:text=,financed%20is%20%2425%2C000%20or%20less)) ([www.wicourts.gov](https://www.wicourts.gov/publications/guides/smallclaimsguide.htm#:~:text=arbitration%20award%20where%20arbitration%20was,Eviction%20action%20due%20to%20foreclosure)). 

Finally, note these limits are for the *claim amount*. On top of a $10,000 principal, the court can award certain additional amounts like court costs, attorney’s fees if authorized, and statutory interest even if it bumps the total slightly over $10k ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,action%2C%20including%20reasonable%20attorney%20fees)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/#:~:text=therefor%20in%20any%20court%20of,in%20real%20estate%20practice%2C%20as)). Those are considered collateral to the judgment and not counted against the cap.

## 2. What types of cases qualify — EVERYTHING YOU CAN SUE FOR

Wisconsin small claims court may only be used for certain types of cases authorized by statute. The idea is to handle relatively simple, lower-value civil disputes. Below is an exhaustive list of case types you **can** file in Wisconsin small claims, by category, with examples and any special rules or requirements. If a case type isn’t mentioned here, it likely belongs in regular circuit court or an administrative forum. (We’ll also cover non-qualifying cases in a later section.)

### Contract & Money-Owed Disputes

- **Breach of Contract (Goods or Services Not Provided / Violated Terms):** *Eligible.* If someone failed to perform their obligations under a contract or broke the terms, and it caused you financial loss, you can sue for damages (up to $10,000) in small claims ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=,enforce%20judgments%20from%20funds%20owed)). This covers written contracts, signed agreements, and also oral contracts (since oral contracts are enforceable in Wisconsin, though proving the terms may be trickier). Examples: A contractor was paid to remodel your kitchen but never did the work; a caterer for your event breached a cancellation policy and kept your deposit without cause; or you paid for a product that wasn’t delivered as promised. **Statute of limitations:** 6 years for written or oral contract actions in Wisconsin ([wilawlibrary.gov](https://wilawlibrary.gov/topics/justice/civil/judgment.php#:~:text=,Search%20examples%3A%20judgment%20%3B%20judgments)). No special pre-suit steps required (though sending a demand letter is wise). Bring a copy of the contract if written, and evidence of the breach (texts, emails, photos of unfinished work, etc.). Small claims judges regularly handle contract disputes such as home repairs, event services, sales of goods, and so on. As long as the amount you seek is ≤ $10,000, contract claims fit here. If the contract included an attorney fee clause, note that small claims courts can award those fees if you prevail (since it’s based on contract) – though any award of fees + damages still can’t exceed the $10k cap except possibly as costs ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=The%20%245%2C000%20small%20claims%20limitation,3102)).

- **Loans and Unpaid Debts (Promissory Notes, Personal Loans):** *Eligible.* If you lent someone money or extended credit and they didn’t pay you back, you can sue in small claims for the balance due (plus any interest if agreed, subject to interest rate limits). This includes formal **promissory notes** or informal loans to friends/family. Example: You gave your friend a $2,000 personal loan and they never repaid per your verbal agreement – you can sue for $2,000. Or a more formal example: you sold your car on a payment plan with a signed note, and the buyer defaulted, you can sue for the remaining payments or repossession (replevin) of the car if under $10k value. Wisconsin treats these as contract actions. No special notice required beyond perhaps a polite reminder/demand. **Bad check loans:** If the person gave you a check that bounced, see the “bounced checks” item below – you get extra remedies. For plain unpaid loans, provide any IOU document or at least testimony of the loan’s existence and non-payment. Often personal loans are unenforced by paperwork, but if you have bank records or texts acknowledging the debt, bring those. The statute of limitations on an unsecured loan is 6 years from the date payment was due or last made ([wilawlibrary.gov](https://wilawlibrary.gov/topics/justice/civil/judgment.php#:~:text=,Search%20examples%3A%20judgment%20%3B%20judgments)). (If it’s an **open-ended credit** like credit card debt, also typically treated as 6 years in Wisconsin for breach of contract on a credit agreement.)

- **Open Accounts and Unpaid Invoices (Business-to-Business or Business-to-Consumer Debts):** *Eligible.* If someone owes money on an account, such as an unpaid bill for goods delivered or services rendered, you can sue for the amount due (up to the cap). Examples: A freelance graphic designer completed work for a client who never paid the $3,000 invoice; a supplier sold materials to a contractor on credit and is owed $8,000; a medical office is owed a patient’s deductible portion of $1,200 that hasn’t been paid. These are essentially contract debts. In Wisconsin an “open account” or running account is actionable in small claims – you should attach or itemize the account statement or unpaid invoices and any agreement on payment terms. Collection agencies or creditors frequently use small claims to sue on credit card debts, utility bills, etc., as long as the balance is ≤ $10,000 (if more, they sue in large claims, or they might waive excess) ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=,enforce%20judgments%20from%20funds%20owed)). If you’re an **assignee** (collection agency) suing a consumer, note you must have proper documentation of the assignment and the underlying debt. Also, **if you have filed 20+ consumer collection cases in a year, you should be aware of Wis. Stat. §814.04(6) which limits your recoverable attorney fees to $35 – but that’s a detail for high-volume filers). Regular businesses and individuals suing for unpaid accounts don’t have special pre-suit requirements aside from possibly sending a final bill or demand, which is just good practice.

- **Rent Owed (Landlord suing Former Tenant for Unpaid Rent or Tenant suing Landlord for Prepaid Rent):** *Eligible.* Monetary disputes arising from leases can be handled in small claims, with $10k limit applying to money claims. If you are a **landlord seeking unpaid rent** after a tenant move-out (or additional damages beyond the security deposit), you can use small claims. Example: Tenant moved owing $2,500 in rent; you already applied the $1,000 security deposit, leaving $1,500 still unpaid – you can sue for $1,500. (Often this comes coupled with property damage claims or part of an eviction case – see Landlord–Tenant section and security deposit item below for special rules.) If you already obtained a judgment for eviction that included rent through the date of eviction, you wouldn’t double sue for that – but you could sue for any remaining rent owed that wasn’t included. Conversely, if you’re a **tenant who paid last month’s rent or other prepaid rent** and didn’t get credit or refund for it (for instance, you paid first and last month up front, then moved early or the lease was broken and last month wasn’t used), you can sue the landlord for that amount. Many times these get wrapped into security deposit issues. Wisconsin requires landlords to return any **prepaid rent** not earned and any prorated rent if tenant moved out early and unit re-rented, etc. Those would be contract damages if not returned. No special notice beyond the statutory 21-day requirement for deposit (covered later). Landlords should be aware of the **$10k cap** – if a tenant skipped out owing $12k, you’d likely just sue for $10k and either forgive the rest or pursue in large claims (though large claims might not be worth it if tenant is judgment-proof). Landlords cannot split unpaid rent by month into multiple suits; it should be one suit for the total owed. Landlords also should comply with any **security deposit accounting** obligations before suing (or you risk losing deposit-related claims). We’ll discuss deposit rules separately (essentially, you must provide an itemized list of damages or rent withheld from the deposit within 21 days, or you could forfeit the right to claim those items) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2010/704/ATCP%20134.html#:~:text=Wisconsin%20Code%20%3A%3A%20Wisconsin%20Code,View%20All%20Versions)) ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=Landlord%20William%20Waldbillig%20appeals%20a,rent%20an%20apartment%20are%20not)). If a tenant is suing a landlord for **wrongful rent charges** or **illegal fees**, that’s also allowed (under $10k). E.g., a landlord illegally charged $300 in late fees beyond what the lease or law allows – tenant could sue for a refund.

- **Dishonored/Bounced Checks:** *Eligible.* If someone paid you with a check that **bounced** (non-sufficient funds, closed account, etc.), you have a specific remedy under Wisconsin law. You can sue for the amount of the check *plus* certain penalties. Wisconsin’s worthless check statute (Wis. Stat. §943.245) allows you to recover the face value of the check, any incurred bank fees, and **exemplary (punitive) damages of up to 3 times the amount** (but **capped at $500** extra) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,checks%20or%20orders%20were%20involved)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,action%2C%20including%20reasonable%20attorney%20fees)), as well as your actual costs including reasonable attorney fees ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,adult%20or%20emancipated%20minor%20who)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,action%2C%20including%20reasonable%20attorney%20fees)). To get these extra damages, you must first give the check writer a **written notice** at least 15 days before suing, demanding payment of the amount and stating that failure to pay will make them liable for additional damages ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,not%20liable%20under%20this%20section)). (Send this by regular mail to their last known address and keep an **affidavit of mailing** ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,not%20liable%20under%20this%20section))). If they still don’t pay, you can file a small claim. For example: John gives you a $500 check that bounces. You send the statutory notice. He doesn’t pay within 15 days. You sue in small claims for $500 + say $30 bank fee + up to $500 penalty (since triple $530 would be $1,590, but statute caps penalty at $500) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,action%2C%20including%20reasonable%20attorney%20fees)). The court can award, for instance, $500 actual + $30 fee + $500 exemplary = $1,030, plus court costs. Note that the total (here $1,030 + costs) is under $10k, so fine. Even if triple damages would push over $10k, you would be limited by small claims cap and by the statute’s $500 limit on exemplary damages per check ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-943/section-943-245/#:~:text=,action%2C%20including%20reasonable%20attorney%20fees)). Make sure to bring a copy of the bounced check and the 15-day notice letter (and an affidavit of service of that letter) to court. The judge will likely ask if proper notice was given as required by law. Bounced check cases are common in small claims – often merchants or landlords use them. Under Wisconsin law, writing a bad check can also lead to criminal charges or a citation, but those are separate; your civil remedy is as described. If the check writer pays after you’ve filed but before judgment, you can dismiss the case (perhaps recover your filing fee as part of settlement).

- **Co-signer or Guarantor Liability:** *Eligible.* If you co-signed a loan or guaranteed someone’s obligation and ended up having to pay it, you can sue the primary debtor for reimbursement in small claims (assuming the amount is within the limit). For instance, you co-signed an apartment lease and the tenant skipped out leaving $4,000 in damages/rent that you paid – you can sue the tenant to recover that $4,000. Or you guaranteed a bank loan that defaulted and you paid $6,000 to settle it – you can pursue the original borrower for $6,000. This is basically enforcing your rights of *indemnity* or *contribution* under contract/common law. Wisconsin recognizes that a surety who pays has a right to recover from the principal. Provide evidence you paid (receipts, bank statements) and the agreement that you were a co-signer (like the lease or promissory note). Ensure you sue within the appropriate limitation (likely 6 years from the date you paid, as it’s effectively a contract claim for reimbursement). No special procedure aside from perhaps notifying the person that you will seek reimbursement. Sometimes the co-signer agreement might even explicitly allow recovery of costs. As long as the underlying debt is not extremely large (if it was, you’d likely be in large claims). But often co-signer claims are a few thousand, perfect for small claims.

**Note:** Many of these contract and debt claims, if involving consumer transactions, could also potentially fall under Wisconsin consumer protection statutes if there were deceptive practices (see “False Advertising/Deceptive Practices” in Consumer section). But the core breach or non-payment is adequately addressed as a straightforward contract claim in small claims.

### Consumer Disputes

- **Defective Products (Refund or Repair Costs):** *Eligible.* If you bought a product (new or used) that turned out to be defective or not as advertised, and the seller/manufacturer refuses to refund or repair it, you can sue for the diminished value or cost of repair (or purchase price) up to $10k. Examples: You purchase a new appliance that never works and the store won’t take it back – sue for a refund. Or you buy a used car “as-is” but later discover the seller hid a known major defect – you might sue for the cost to fix it or the difference in value (be aware “as-is” limits some claims, but misrepresentation is actionable). If a product came with a warranty that the company isn’t honoring, that’s essentially a breach of warranty (contract) – small claims can handle that as long as the remedy sought (often the cost of the item or repair) is under the limit. **Consumer products in WI** also fall under the UCC implied warranties, and a suit for breach of implied warranty is allowed in small claims (e.g., a farmer’s $8,000 tractor engine blows within a week – implied warranty of merchantability, sue dealer for repair cost). No special pre-suit requirement except if it’s a regulated item (for instance, new car under Lemon Law – see below). But generally, you might give the seller/manufacturer a chance to cure the defect by sending a written notice or going through their warranty claims process. If they still don’t fix it, you can proceed to court. Provide evidence of the defect (photos, expert mechanic statement, etc.) and any communications where they refused to remedy. Wisconsin’s Deceptive Trade Practices Act (Wis. Stat. §100.18) might also apply if they made false claims about the product – if so, you could get double damages and attorney fees under that law ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/#:~:text=recover%20such%20pecuniary%20loss%2C%20together,of%20a%20violation%20by%20any)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/#:~:text=therefor%20in%20any%20court%20of,in%20real%20estate%20practice%2C%20as)), as discussed later. But even without invoking that, you can recover your out-of-pocket losses through a small claim.

- **Services Not Performed or Performed Poorly:** *Eligible.* If you paid for personal services that were not provided, only partially provided, or done in a shoddy manner, you can sue to get your money back or the cost to fix the poor work (within $10k). Common examples: a contractor or handyman takes a deposit but never shows up to do the work; a wedding photographer you paid fails to appear at the wedding; a roofing contractor did the job but it leaks horribly and you had to pay someone else $5,000 to repair their mistakes. These are breaches of service contracts or possibly negligence. Wisconsin small claims routinely handles these disputes (often under breach of contract or misrepresentation theories). There’s no requirement to give them a formal chance to “cure” the work, but practically and ethically, it’s good to complain and allow them to fix it before suing. If they refuse or fail, then sue. For licensed trades (plumbers, electricians), you can also complain to the licensing board, but that’s separate from getting your money back. Document the issue: contracts, receipts, photographs of bad work, expert letters on what’s wrong if possible. If you claim the work was worthless, you might seek a full refund; if partially done, you might seek the difference in value or cost to complete/repair. **Note:** Wisconsin has a Home Improvement Consumer Protection law (ATCP 110) that, if violated by a contractor (say they took money and did nothing), allows you to recover double damages and attorney fees via §100.20(5) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-20/#:~:text=,including%20a%20reasonable%20attorney%20fee)) ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=Landlord%20William%20Waldbillig%20appeals%20a,rent%20an%20apartment%20are%20not)). That can be invoked in small claims too (though there’s a two-year limit on the double-damages remedy) – see “Statutory claims” section. But even without that, you can use plain contract law to get compensation. If you do invoke ATCP 110, no special demand required beyond showing the violation. Summarily, unsatisfactory services are fair game for small claims.

- **Refunds for Services Not Received (No-show or Canceled Vendors):** *Eligible.* Similar to above, if a service provider simply **did not show up or canceled on you** and kept your deposit or prepayment, you can sue to recover that money. Examples: You paid a DJ $800 for your event but they never came or canceled last-minute and didn’t refund you; you reserved a banquet hall with a $2,000 deposit and they double-booked and canceled your reservation without refund. Clear breach – sue for the deposit and any extra costs you incurred due to their failure (like if you had to find replacement service at higher cost, you might claim the difference as consequential damages, although some courts might say consequential damages need to be within the cap and foreseeable). There’s no statute forcing such vendors to settle (though you might also report them to BBB or DATCP). Just ensure to have the contract and proof of your payment and their cancellation. Wisconsin, again, has a general Unfair Trade Practices law that could consider such behavior (especially if they do it to multiple people) fraudulent – which could allow double damages, etc. But typically a straightforward small claims suit for the lost money will suffice.

- **Auto Repair Disputes (Overcharges or Faulty Repairs):** *Eligible.* If an auto mechanic or body shop charged you for work that wasn’t actually done, or did the repair improperly causing damage, you can sue them for the overcharge or the cost to fix their mistakes. Example: A mechanic billed you $900 for parts and services that you later find out were never replaced – you could sue for that $900. Or they performed a brake job negligently leading to an accident or requiring re-doing by another shop for $500 – you sue for $500 (and possibly any related damages up to $5,000 if it’s property damage or minor injury due to negligence). Wisconsin’s Motor Vehicle Repair Trade Practices (Wis. Adm. Code ch. ATCP 132) require detailed estimates and customer authorization for extras. If the shop violated those rules (e.g., performed work you didn’t authorize and demands payment), you can use that as a defense to not pay or as a claim if you paid under protest. You should first attempt to resolve with the shop manager – often they will adjust the bill or correct the error if confronted, since they know about DATCP regulations. If they don’t, small claims is the venue. Provide the original invoice, any second-opinion statements (e.g., another mechanic’s letter that “Old parts were still on vehicle, evidently not replaced as billed”), and timeline. The law doesn’t require a pre-suit notice to the shop, but doing so (writing “I believe I was overcharged – please refund the difference or I will take further action”) can strengthen your case and possibly prompt a settlement. If a specific law was breached (like *no written estimate given* or *fraudulent billing*), you could also claim it under Wis. Stat. §100.20(5) for double damages ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-20/#:~:text=,including%20a%20reasonable%20attorney%20fee)), but usually just claim the amount of overcharge or cost to fix. For repair work that physically damaged your car (like they installed something wrong and it broke the engine), that might be treated as property damage (tort) and subject to $5k cap – but you could also frame as contract breach (failing to perform skillfully) to argue for $10k cap. In small claims, that distinction rarely comes up explicitly, as most auto repair disputes are under $5k anyway.

- **Home Improvement & Contractor Disputes:** *Eligible.* (We touched on this in services and auto, but to emphasize separately): If you hired a contractor for home improvements (remodeling, roofing, etc.) and they *abandoned the job* or performed substandard work, you can use small claims to recover what you paid (if job never finished) or the cost to correct the defects. Example: You paid a roofer a $4,000 down payment, they did half the work and disappeared – you sue for the $4,000 plus any cost to hire someone to finish beyond the original contract price. Or they finished but left significant problems that cost $1,500 to repair – sue for $1,500. Wisconsin’s **Home Improvement Practices** regulations (ATCP 110) require contractors to use written contracts and not misrepresent, etc. If your contractor violated those (say took money before any materials delivered or misrepresented their credentials), you have the option to seek double damages and fees under §100.20(5) ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=Landlord%20William%20Waldbillig%20appeals%20a,rent%20an%20apartment%20are%20not)). In small claims, homeowners frequently sue for unfinished or bad work. *Pre-suit note:* If the contractor is licensed (like electrician, plumber), you can also complain to state licensing boards; if they’re simply bad but not licensed, you can complain to DATCP. But these are parallel actions. For the lawsuit, ensure to provide copies of the contract, cancelled checks, photos of incomplete work, etc. Also have any communications (texts “I’ll be back next week” then never came, etc.). As mentioned, Wisconsin gives extra remedies for some of these via the consumer statutes – you may cite those in your complaint to potentially get attorney fees or double damages ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=Landlord%20William%20Waldbillig%20appeals%20a,rent%20an%20apartment%20are%20not)), which can be especially helpful if the actual loss is small but you had big hassle. However, note the **statute of limitations** for invoking the ATCP 110 double-damage remedy is 2 years ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=Property%20%E2%80%94%20landlord,Wisconsin%20Court%20of%20Appeals%20Civil)), so if it’s been over 2 years, you’d just sue for actual loss under contract law (6-year limit). Often though, these disputes arise immediately or during the project. Summing up: yes, small claims is the proper venue for most homeowner vs. contractor money disputes under $10k.

- **Appliance/Electronics Repair or Warranty Failures:** *Eligible.* If you paid for appliance or electronics repair and it wasn’t done properly (e.g., you paid a TV repair shop $300 and the TV still doesn’t work and they refuse further service), you can sue for the amount paid or cost to have someone else fix it. If an item was under warranty and the company isn’t honoring the warranty (say your phone warranty claim was denied wrongfully and you had to pay $200 for a fix), you can sue for that $200. These are essentially contract issues (warranty is a contract). Example: a customer buys a refrigerator with a 1-year warranty, it fails in 6 months, manufacturer drags feet and won’t repair or replace, you pay $800 for a new fridge – you could sue manufacturer for $800 (the Lemon Law covers vehicles specifically, but not appliances – so you rely on general warranty law). **No pre-suit requirement** beyond using the warranty claim process (which you did, and they refused). Provide documentation of warranty and communications. This category is broad – basically if a service or product you paid for didn’t deliver the promised benefit or quality, small claims can likely provide a remedy in dollars.

- **Gym Memberships / Subscription Services (failure to cancel or refund):** *Eligible.* If you canceled a membership or service per the contract but the company keeps charging you or refuses a refund, you can sue to enforce the cancellation terms and get your money back. Example: You properly canceled a gym membership and have proof, but they continued auto-billing your card for $200 – sue for $200. Wisconsin has laws on fitness center contracts (Wis. Stat. §100.177) that allow cancellation in certain circumstances – if the gym violates those, DATCP can assist, but you can also sue. Or if you followed the company’s procedure to cancel a cable subscription or online service but they ignore it – sue for the unused portion they charged. Always read the contract’s cancellation terms carefully; if you complied and they didn’t, you have a case. If you didn’t follow it exactly, do so before suing (for instance, some require written notice). Good evidence: certified mail of your cancellation letter, or email confirmations. Many of these disputes can be resolved by disputing charges with your credit card too (that’s another route), but small claims is an option if that fails. The amounts are often relatively small (under $500), but principle matters and fees can be awarded under consumer statutes if it was an unfair trade practice. 

- **Dry Cleaning / Bailee Issues (Property lost or damaged):** *Eligible.* If you entrusted property to someone for service (dry cleaner, valet parking, storage unit, repair shop) and they lost it or damaged it, you can sue for the value of the property (up to the cap). Example: Dry cleaner lost your $300 suit – you can claim $300. A car valet returned your car with a big scratch and refuses to pay for repaint – if the repaint costs $800, sue for $800. These situations are governed by “bailment” law: the bailee (service provider) had a duty to care for your item. If they fail, they owe the value. Many dry cleaners post limited liability signs (like “not responsible for items over $…”) but those are not absolute protections if they were negligent. Wisconsin does not require a special demand before suing, but you should of course ask the business to compensate you first. If they refuse, small claims is appropriate. Provide any receipts or tickets that show you left the item there, and evidence of its value (purchase receipt or current market price). For damage, get a repair estimate. In some cases, such disputes might be handled by the business’s insurer if you push (e.g., valet’s garagekeeper insurance might cover damage). But if they stonewall, court is fine. Note: if the defendant is a **hotel or innkeeper**, they have some statutory limits on liability for property in some circumstances (Wis. Stat. ch. 134) – but those mostly apply if no negligence. In any event, small claims can sort that out. For vehicles in parking garages, Wis. Stat. §779.43 provides that they can’t disclaim liability for their own negligence even if signage says “park at your own risk.” So don’t be deterred by disclaimers if you believe the loss was their fault.

- **False Advertising / Deceptive Sales Practices:** *Eligible.* If you were induced to buy a product or service by false or misleading claims and suffered a financial loss, you can sue under Wisconsin’s Deceptive Trade Practices Act (Wis. Stat. §100.18) for your damages. We’ll detail this more in “Specific statutory claims,” but to note here: such a claim (e.g., a store advertised a TV as 4K resolution and you paid $500, but it turns out not to be 4K) is something you can bring in small claims. Under §100.18, you could recover your purchase price or difference in value, and that law entitles you to **double damages and reasonable attorney’s fees** if you win ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/#:~:text=therefor%20in%20any%20court%20of,in%20real%20estate%20practice%2C%20as)). The limit is still $10k on the actual loss (and it doubles it). You must file within 3 years of the false advertisement ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2016/chapter-100/section-100.18/#:~:text=100,18%20Annotation)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/#:~:text=recovered%20by%20the%20defrauded%20party,1198%20%281991)). Also, you have to prove the ad was untrue or misleading, you believed it, and it materially caused your purchase. If so, the judge can award double your loss and attorney fees (which the defendant would have to pay) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/#:~:text=recover%20such%20pecuniary%20loss%2C%20together,of%20a%20violation%20by%20any)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/#:~:text=recovered%20by%20the%20defrauded%20party,1198%20%281991)). No pre-suit notice is required by that statute, but often a strongly worded letter referencing §100.18 might get a business to resolve it to avoid the penalty. Example scenario: A timeshare company lied about what you’d get, you paid $5,000 – you could sue for $5,000, potentially doubled to $10,000 (which fits the cap) plus your costs. We’ll see more statutory claims later, but yes, you can pursue those in small claims as long as the base damages are within the limit.

*(The above list covers virtually all scenarios a typical individual or small business might have: if it involves **someone owing you money, failing to pay for something, or you paying for something you didn’t get or that was not as represented**, it likely qualifies for small claims in Wisconsin. Now we’ll move to more specific categories like property damage, landlord-tenant, etc.)*

### Property Damage

- **Vehicle Damage from an Accident (Minor Collisions):** *Eligible.* If another person damaged your car or other personal property (and it’s within small claims limit), you can sue them for the repair costs or fair market value loss. Commonly, this is used for vehicle accidents where the damage is not fully covered by insurance or the at-fault driver is uninsured. Example: A fender-bender caused $3,000 of damage to your car and the other driver’s insurance denied the claim – you can sue the other driver for $3,000 in small claims. Or your neighbor backed into your motorcycle causing $1,200 damage – sue the neighbor. Such claims are essentially **negligence** claims (tort), which means the small claims $5,000 limit for torts applies ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=judgments%20from%20funds%20owed%29)). But if the damage is, say, $4,500, it fits. Wisconsin requires that a car accident injury claim usually goes to large claims because injuries often exceed $5k, but for *property-only* damage it often is under $5k and fine for small claims. If your vehicle damage exceeds $5k, you either limit your claim to $5k or file as a large claim; you can’t split it into multiple suits. Also note, if you already received some insurance payout and are suing for the **deductible** or remaining loss, make that clear (e.g., “Seeking $1,000 deductible not covered by insurer”). Provide an estimate or invoice for repairs, and any evidence of the other driver’s fault (police report, photos of scene, etc.). Usually these cases are straightforward if liability is clear. If liability is disputed, the court will weigh evidence (e.g., each driver’s testimony, any witness). If both parties are partly at fault, Wisconsin follows comparative negligence – your award is reduced by your percentage of fault. The court can handle that in small claims (e.g., judge might say “I find defendant 75% at fault, plaintiff 25% at fault, so of $4,000 damage, plaintiff recovers $3,000”). Note that if it’s exactly 50/50 or uncertain, you might get nothing as plaintiff must prove more likely than not the other’s negligence caused the loss. But small claims judges are used to parsing accident responsibility. No special pre-suit actions (though exchanging insurance info is required by law at accidents). If the defendant is uninsured and you have your own collision coverage, you might have had your insurer pay and they could subrogate – often the insurer will handle it (and possibly file a small claim in your name “as subrogee” – which they can do). But if it’s on you, small claims is the place.

- **Intentional Property Damage or Vandalism:** *Eligible.* If someone intentionally damaged your property (broke your window, slashed your tires, etc.), you can sue them for the repair or replacement cost. These are intentional torts – the $5,000 limit applies. However, Wisconsin has a statute for “Civil liability for property damage or loss caused by crime” (Wis. Stat. §895.446) which allows a victim to recover **actual damages, costs of investigation, and possibly 3× damages as punitive** for deliberate property damage or theft ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,76)). For example, if someone **stole** your property or vandalized it, you could sue for the value and ask for up to triple as exemplary damages. That statute requires proving the person violated a criminal statute (like criminal damage to property) by a preponderance of evidence ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=occurs%20on%20or%20after%20November,caused%20the%20damage%20or%20loss)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,76)). If you succeed, you could get up to triple damages and potentially attorney fees. In small claims, your total still capped at $10k (so if actual damage was $4k, triple would be $12k, you’d likely be limited to $10k) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=A%20plaintiff%20may%20elect%20to,1030)). But double damages ($8k) would be within cap. Regardless, straightforward approach: sue for the cost to fix or replace what they damaged. If it was an act of vandalism, hopefully you filed a police report – bring that or at least reference it. If the defendant was criminally charged and convicted, that conviction can be used as evidence in your civil case (they can’t relitigate the facts easily – it’s collateral estoppel if same issues). Also, you might be entitled to restitution in the criminal case; if so, you can’t double recover via civil. Usually you choose one route or the other. People use small claims often when restitution isn’t ordered or fully paid. Note: if the culprit was a minor, Wisconsin allows you to also hold parents liable up to $5,000 for a minor’s willful property damage (Wis. Stat. §895.035). So you could sue the minor’s parent in small claims for up to $5k plus costs. No special notice required beyond maybe a demand or contacting their insurance if any (for example, some homeowners insurance cover certain liability for minors). Provide documentation of the repairs or value. Because it’s intentional, you could cite §895.446 for potential treble – but in practice many small claims judges will just give actual damages unless you make a strong case for punitive. Still, mention it if applicable (like “pursuant to Wis. Stat. 895.446, plaintiff seeks 3x damages for intentional harm”).

- **Damage by Tenant to Rental Property:** *Eligible.* If you’re a landlord and a tenant caused damage beyond normal wear-and-tear, you can sue for the cost of repairs (often this is part of claiming the security deposit or additional charges). Typically, you would first deduct from the security deposit and send the tenant an itemized statement within 21 days of move-out, as required ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/cases/wisconsin/court-of-appeals/1984/84-002-6.html#:~:text=Paulik%20v,Pauliks%20brought%20a%20small%20claims)). If the damages exceed the deposit, you then demand the balance from the tenant. If they don’t pay, you may sue in small claims for those damages (up to $10k). Example: Tenant left big holes in walls and ruined the carpet, costing $2,000 to fix, and deposit was $800 – you sue for $1,200 remaining. You **must** have complied with Wis. Admin. Code ATCP 134.06(2) & (4) which require the 21-day refund or accounting with a written list of damages ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2010/704/ATCP%20134.html#:~:text=ATCP%20134%20%2C%20Wis,View%20All%20Versions)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/cases/wisconsin/court-of-appeals/1984/84-002-6.html#:~:text=Paulik%20v,Pauliks%20brought%20a%20small%20claims)). If you failed to send that list or were late, the tenant can counterclaim to get double the deposit back as a penalty ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=withholding%20from%20a%20security%20deposit,rent%20an%20apartment%20are%20not)) (see Landlord–Tenant section below). So be sure you followed the rules. As long as you did, you can pursue the actual damages. Bring copies of the move-in/move-out inspection checklists, photos of damage, invoices for repairs/replacements, and the deposit accounting letter you sent. Landlords often bundle this claim with unpaid rent in one suit (that’s fine). If the tenant disputes these damages, the burden is on landlord to prove they were beyond ordinary wear and caused by tenant’s negligence or misuse. The tenant might argue some items were already old (like you had 10-year-old carpet – might not get full cost). The court will assess reasonableness. **Note**: If you did *not* send the required damage list in time, the tenant can actually recover the **full deposit plus possibly 2× deposit as penalty and attorney fees** ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=Landlord%20William%20Waldbillig%20appeals%20a,rent%20an%20apartment%20are%20not)) – so in that scenario it might be the tenant suing you instead, or you get your claim dismissed and owe them a penalty. So landlords, follow the procedure to preserve your claim. Provided you did, small claims is the right venue for property damage by tenants. (Large damages above $10k are rare in normal apartments; if it’s a huge issue like a fire, it likely goes to insurance and maybe larger court if needed).

- **Damage by Landlord to Tenant’s Property:** *Eligible.* If a landlord or their agent wrongfully handles or damages a tenant’s personal belongings – for example, during an improper eviction or unlawful entry – the tenant can sue for the value of those belongings. Say a landlord illegally dispossessed you and put your stuff on the curb, and some items were stolen or ruined, you could sue for those losses. Another example: landlord’s negligence (like they were doing maintenance and caused a flood that damaged your furniture). These are property damage claims as well. Many times, such issues overlap with housing law (e.g., “self-help” evictions are illegal and you might have separate remedies under Wis. Stat. §704.05 or §799.40). But for the pure money aspect – value of your property – small claims works. There is also a specific Wisconsin statute: if a landlord **fails to return stored personal property** after an eviction or doesn’t follow the statutory procedure for abandoned property, the tenant can recover actual damages and possibly double value if it was a willful violation (some courts analogize that to theft). The safe route: sue for the fair market value of items not returned or destroyed. Provide evidence (photos, list of items and estimated values, maybe receipts if high-value). If the landlord blatantly disregarded the law, mention that in your claim – e.g., Wis. Stat. §704.05(5) provides that a landlord who *willfully* violates rules about a tenant’s personal property is liable for twice the actual damages ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,caused%20the%20damage%20or%20loss)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,76)). That could bump your claim but still within cap (e.g., $2,000 actual becomes $4,000 recoverable). No required pre-suit notice, besides perhaps a demand to return the items or compensate (which likely happened or is futile if relations are bad). So tenants absolutely can use small claims to get compensation for personal property losses caused by the landlord’s unlawful actions.

- **Boundary/Neighbor Disputes (Tree Damage, Fence Damage, etc.):** *Eligible.* If a neighbor’s actions physically damage your property, you can sue for the cost to repair or value lost. Examples: Neighbor’s tree falls on your shed (if due to their neglect of a dead tree, you could claim their negligence), or they deliberately cut down your tree that was on your side – you can sue for the tree’s value. Wisconsin has a specific statute for wrongful timber cutting: Wis. Stat. §26.09 allows the tree owner to recover *double* the tree’s reasonable market value (or sometimes 3x if trespass was willful) plus costs. That is enforceable in small claims if the amounts are within the $10k (two or three times tree’s value, often trees aren’t huge value unless timber harvest; but ornamental trees do have value, sometimes surprising but seldom exceed $5k each). Another scenario: neighbor builds a fence on your land damaging your garden – you could sue for the cost to relocate the fence and garden restoration. Often these disputes might also seek an injunction (e.g., remove the fence) – note that *injunctive relief* (forcing someone to do or stop something) is **not** available in small claims court ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=This%20section%20does%20not%20authorize,2d%20129%20%281980)). Small claims can only award money. So if your main issue is the encroachment itself, you’d need to go to regular court for an injunction. But if you simply want compensation (e.g., you’ll accept money for the loss of use of that strip or cost to adjust), you can pursue that in small claims. Many tree and fence disputes get resolved by each neighbor’s homeowners insurance or by amicable agreement, but if not, small claims is appropriate for the damage portion. Provide survey or evidence if boundary is disputed, pictures, and any estimates for repairs to your side. 

- **Pet-Related Damage:** *Eligible.* If someone’s pet caused damage to your property or injured your pet, you can sue the owner for vet bills or repair costs. For example, the neighbor’s dog chewed up your $500 lawn furniture, or their dog attacked yours resulting in $800 vet bill – you can claim those costs. Wisconsin has a strict liability **Dog Bite** law (Wis. Stat. §174.02) which makes an owner liable for full damages caused by their dog biting a person, domestic animal, or property, and if the dog had a known previous bite, the owner can be liable for **2× damages** as a penalty ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=,those%20wages%20due%20and%20unpaid)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=the%20wage%20claim%20under%20sub,amount%20of%20those%20wages%20due)). That law applies in small claims too (with the double damages often pushing the total near the cap if injuries were in the thousands). But for minor injuries or property damage by animals, it fits easily. If your claim is for a **dog bite to you** (personal injury), that’s subject to $5k cap in small claims – many dog bites result in more medical costs, so they often go to large court unless very minor. However, claims for injury to *another pet* or livestock would be considered property damage (the law views pets as property in civil cases). So if a dog killed your chickens worth $300 or injured your $1,000 show-dog incurring $400 vet bills, small claims can handle that. Provide vet receipts or proof of item value. Also note, Wisconsin allows if someone’s dog kills or injures a domestic animal, the court *shall* award full damages and **may** award up to 2× that as punishment, regardless of prior bites ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,caused%20the%20damage%20or%20loss)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,76)) (some interplay between §174.02 and §895.446 possibly – point is, owner can be penalized for dog’s aggression). No special steps needed besides perhaps reporting the incident to animal control (which is wise both for safety and evidence documentation). You should attempt to get the owner to pay voluntarily; if they refuse or ignore, proceed to court. As always with pet cases, keep emotions in check: the court can only give money, not punish the owner beyond statutes, and not order the pet be removed or anything (that’s up to authorities via dangerous dog proceedings, not small claims). So focus on the financial loss.

- **Damage During Moving/Transport:** *Eligible.* If you hired movers or entrusted your belongings to someone and they got damaged, you can sue for the damage under breach of contract or negligence. Example: A moving company dropped your piano, causing $2,000 damage – you can claim repair costs or diminution in value. Movers often have a contract limiting liability (e.g., “$0.60 per pound per item”) – those terms can complicate full recovery. But you can still bring the case, and the court will decide if that limitation is enforceable (intrastate moves in WI often allow limitations if you agreed; interstate moves are governed by federal law where $0.60/lb is standard if no additional insurance). If you feel the limitation is unfair or not clearly agreed, argue that. If it is clearly in contract, you may be stuck with that limit. But even then, you could get the limited amount. Many moving disputes indeed end up in small claims when the moving company’s offered compensation is too low. Provide the contract and evidence of the damage (photos, repair estimates). Similarly, if a friend was helping you move and they accidentally smashed your TV, you could sue the friend; though you might consider it was a favor – legally they’d be liable for negligence, but practically you might not want to sue a friend unless they refuse to take responsibility. If you do, treat it like any property damage case. No special requirements. Another variant: shipping damage (if you shipped furniture via a shipping company and it arrived broken, you may have signed a contract with declared value – again, small claims for up to that value if they don’t pay). Car transport is similar – small claims if car got scratched or lost in transport. Many of these companies do eventually pay claims (or their insurer does), but small claims is your backup if they delay or deny.

*(Essentially, any scenario where someone’s action (or inaction) caused physical damage or loss to your property and the amount is within the limit can be brought in small claims. Just be mindful of whether it’s considered a tort (then $5k cap) or contract (possibly $10k cap). In practice, if it’s clearly from negligence or an accident, courts use the $5k cap. If it’s tied to a contract (like hired services), you might argue it’s contract breach for the $10k cap – but since $5k vs $10k only matters if your damages are between 5–10k, that’s a narrow band. Most pure property damage from accidents is resolved under $5k or goes to regular court via insurance subrogation if higher.*)

### Landlord–Tenant (financial only — possession/eviction is separate)

*(Small claims **does** handle evictions, but because eviction suits also involve returning possession of property (a form of non-monetary relief), they have some different rules and aren’t “money judgments” – we focus here on the *money* disputes between landlords and tenants. Eviction procedures and possession are noted in section 10 and what small claims cannot do).*

- **Unreturned Security Deposits:** *Eligible and very common.* If you are a tenant and your landlord failed to return your security deposit, or made wrongful deductions, you can use small claims to get your deposit back. Wisconsin law is *strongly* pro-tenant on deposits: a landlord must either return the full deposit or send a written itemized list of deductions within **21 days** after you vacate ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/cases/wisconsin/court-of-appeals/1984/84-002-6.html#:~:text=Paulik%20v,Pauliks%20brought%20a%20small%20claims)). If they violate this requirement – by missing the 21-day deadline or by withholding deposit for damages or fees not allowed – the tenant is entitled to the deposit’s return and can also seek a **penalty of double the amount wrongfully withheld**, plus court costs and reasonable attorney’s fees ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=Landlord%20William%20Waldbillig%20appeals%20a,rent%20an%20apartment%20are%20not)). That comes from Wis. Stat. §100.20(5) and ATCP §134.06(4) ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=Landlord%20William%20Waldbillig%20appeals%20a,rent%20an%20apartment%20are%20not)). In small claims, tenants routinely sue for their deposit and this double-damage remedy. Example: You paid a $1,000 deposit, moved out on August 31, and gave a forwarding address. As of September 22 (22 days later) you got nothing – that’s a violation (21-day rule). You can sue for $1,000 *and it will be doubled to $2,000* plus your costs ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=withholding%20from%20a%20security%20deposit,rent%20an%20apartment%20are%20not)). Or say your landlord did send a letter but improperly withheld, e.g., $200 for “routine cleaning” (which is not a valid reason under WI law) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2010/704/ATCP%20134.html#:~:text=Wisconsin%20Code%20%3A%3A%20Wisconsin%20Code,View%20All%20Versions)). That $200 is wrongfully withheld – if you sue, you’d get $200 doubled to $400, plus possibly the $800 that was fine if there were valid deductions (if you’re only disputing part, you get double of the wrongful part). Also, if the landlord withheld for damages but never provided the required list in 21 days, they forfeit the right to withhold anything – you get full deposit double ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=Landlord%20William%20Waldbillig%20appeals%20a,rent%20an%20apartment%20are%20not)). Because of these strong penalties, many landlords will pay up when confronted. But if not, small claims judge will enforce this. **Tenant’s pre-suit step:** You don’t legally have to give any notice beyond having provided a forwarding address at move-out. Many tenants do send a reminder letter like “It’s been 21 days, where’s my deposit? If not returned, I’ll sue.” That’s good practice but not required. In court, you as tenant should show: tenancy ended, you gave proper notice/address, you left the place in good condition (if landlord claims damage, they have burden to prove it and that they complied with rules), and that landlord failed to comply with 21-day rule or made illegal deductions. Bring your lease, proof of date you vacated, any photos of move-out condition, and copies of any correspondence (especially the landlord’s itemized list if they sent one – show why it’s wrong or not sent timely). If you win, the law says the court *shall* award double damages and *shall* award your reasonable attorney’s fees ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=withholding%20from%20a%20security%20deposit,rent%20an%20apartment%20are%20not)) – meaning even if you hired a lawyer and spent $500, the landlord might have to pay that back to you (attorney fees are not usually awarded in small claims except by statute – this is one of those statutes). If you’re self-represented, you obviously don’t get attorney fees, but you get costs. This is probably the #1 small claims case type volume-wise – it’s straightforward thanks to clear regulations. 

- **Prepaid Rent or Unearned Rent Refunds:** *Eligible.* If you (tenant) had paid, for example, last month’s rent or a prorated amount that ended up not being used, and the landlord didn’t refund it, you can claim that. Often this folds into the deposit dispute or lease-break negotiations. For instance, you gave first and last month’s rent at move-in; you properly gave notice and moved, so the last month’s rent you prepaid should cover the final month. If the landlord also collected rent from a new tenant for that month (double-dipping) or otherwise owes you for unused period, you can recover that. Wisconsin doesn’t allow double rent collection. Or if you vacated early and the landlord re-rented the unit before your paid period ended, the landlord must return any overlapping rent (landlord can’t collect rent twice for same period). If they don’t volunteer that refund, you can sue. This scenario is less common, but just know any **unearned rent** is treated like a deposit under WI law – it should be returned in 21 days as well ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/cases/wisconsin/court-of-appeals/1984/84-002-6.html#:~:text=,Pauliks%20brought%20a%20small%20claims)). If a landlord kept your last month’s rent payment saying “that’s your security deposit now,” that’s not allowed unless specified as deposit in lease. You could treat that as a deposit and use the same double-damage remedy to get it back. On the flip side, if you as a landlord had a tenant who left early without paying the last month (and you didn’t have a deposit to cover it), that’s just unpaid rent – you’d sue under contract as discussed above. The difference is: a tenant suing for prepaid rent doesn’t have a special double rule (except if considered part of deposit). But often it is part of deposit or at least analogous. If it was clearly labeled as “last month’s rent” and not a deposit, the statutes don’t explicitly give double for it, but a court might see withholding it without basis as an “unfair trade practice” anyway. Regardless, you get it back if it wasn’t used. Provide proof of payment and evidence it wasn’t applied or earned (like new tenant’s lease showing overlap). If it’s small, many landlords just forget to refund until asked; a letter might resolve it. If not, small claims will.

- **Rent Abatement or Repair Costs (Habitability issues):** *Eligible as counterclaims or separate claims.* If you’re a tenant and had serious habitability problems (no heat, water leaks, vermin, etc.) and the landlord failed to fix them, you might be entitled to a reduction in rent (rent abatement) or reimbursement for repairs you paid for. Usually, tenants raise this as a defense or counterclaim when a landlord sues for unpaid rent – “Yes, I withheld rent because the furnace was out for 2 months.” Wisconsin’s law implies a warranty of habitability in residential leases. A tenant can’t usually get *punitive* damages for bad conditions, but can get compensation: either the difference between rent paid and value received, or actual costs incurred (like $200 space heaters, or $500 plumber bill you paid after landlord refused). Small claims court can certainly adjudicate these issues, but typically you won’t see a tenant file an affirmative suit for, say, “My landlord didn’t fix stuff, I want $1,000 back.” You *can*, but often tenants prefer to use “repair and deduct” (fix and subtract from rent) or move out and use the security deposit remedy or negotiate. However, if the tenancy is ongoing and you haven’t paid rent due to conditions, often the landlord will sue you – then you *counterclaim* for rent abatement. So practically, habitability issues show up as defenses/counterclaims in an eviction or rent suit. For completeness: if you as a tenant went ahead and *made repairs yourself after giving notice and landlord didn’t act*, you can sue the landlord to recover those repair costs (or more likely, deduct and then landlord might sue you, and you defend by showing why you deducted). The court will evaluate if the repair was necessary and cost reasonable. There’s no statutory double-damage for general repair issues (unlike deposits). But if conditions violated local housing codes, you might also report them to building inspectors (again separate process). Summation: Yes, you can get money back for serious uninhabitable conditions (lack of heat, water, or other material violations). Provide evidence: photos, correspondence complaining, maybe health department records. The measure is typically the percentage of rent that represented the reduced value (like no heat for 1 month = substantial abatement). The judge might say “conditions were 50% unlivable, so tenant gets half that month’s rent back.” Or if you actually moved due to breach, you might get moving costs and difference in new rent, etc. It’s a bit discretionary. Since these are usually raised in response to a landlord’s claim, a tenant could indeed initiate a small claim after the fact (e.g., you moved out early because of awful conditions and want last month’s rent back plus damages). That could be framed as constructive eviction damages. It’s legally allowed in small claims if under $10k. Note: not every minor issue qualifies – it has to be significant enough that you didn’t get what you paid for.

- **Wrongful Eviction / Lockout (financial aspects):** *Eligible.* If a landlord illegally locked you out or disposed of your property (discussed above), aside from property damages you might have other financial losses – e.g., cost of temporary lodging, etc. You can claim those as well. Those often accompany a claim for property or deposit. There’s no separate tort recognized as “wrongful eviction” in WI beyond contract and property damage elements arguably, but one could argue it as an intentional tort (maybe $5k cap). Realistically, you’d bundle those losses under the above categories – e.g., lockout caused you $500 hotel bill and $300 missing property – sue for $800. And as noted, double damages are possible for willful misconduct by landlord regarding property ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,caused%20the%20damage%20or%20loss)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-895/section-895-446/#:~:text=,76)).

- **Fees and Charges Disputes:** *Eligible.* If there’s a dispute over lease charges – like late fees, utility charges, cleaning fees – either party might bring it in small claims. Landlord might sue for unpaid late fees (though note, WI caps late fees to reasonable estimate of costs, and if not in lease can’t charge; many judges disallow excessive late fees). A tenant might sue because the landlord charged them improper fees (like $50 “administrative fee” not in lease or $100 for carpet cleaning when it was normal wear). If a fee is taken out of deposit, it falls under deposit rules (so tenant gets double that amount back if not allowed) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2010/704/ATCP%20134.html#:~:text=Wisconsin%20Code%20%3A%3A%20Wisconsin%20Code,View%20All%20Versions)) ([wislawjournal.com](https://wislawjournal.com/2013/05/09/property-landlord-tenant-security-deposits-damages/#:~:text=withholding%20from%20a%20security%20deposit,rent%20an%20apartment%20are%20not)). If it was charged separately, tenant can still challenge it as an “illegal lease provision” and recover it under Wis. Admin. Code ATCP 134. You wouldn’t get double for an illegal provision itself unless it results in withholding deposit or is considered an unfair trade practice (some are, e.g., unlawful late fees might be an unfair trade practice? Probably just violation of admin code giving damage = fee amount). But definitely small claims can resolve these monetary disagreements. Example: Lease says $25 fee if you don’t clean oven; tenant disputes paying it because they did clean – small claims to recover if deducted. It’s small beans usually, but sometimes principle. If you’re the tenant and it’s under $100, consider if it’s worth the filing fee (maybe if you’re also suing for deposit or bigger issue, you include it). If you’re landlord and the fee is minor, think similarly – though some landlords sue on principle for multiple small fees (rarely worth it alone). 

*(In summary, landlord-tenant money disputes often involve deposits, rent, and damages – all of which small claims covers extensively, with special tenant protections in place (double damages for deposit, etc.). Possession cases (evictions) are also small claims but follow a rapid schedule and separate process, and are discussed in section 10 under what small claims can’t do beyond money.)*

### Employment & Wages

- **Unpaid Wages or Final Paycheck:** *Eligible.* If your employer didn’t pay you for work you’ve done – whether it’s your final paycheck, overtime, regular wages, or vacation pay owed – you can sue in small claims. Wisconsin law requires employers to pay all earned wages by the regular payday after separation or the next payday (depending on circumstances) ([dwd.wisconsin.gov](https://dwd.wisconsin.gov/er/laborstandards/wages.htm#:~:text=Wage%20Payment%20and%20Collection%20Law,pay%20wages%20earned%20on%20the)). If they fail to pay on time, and you file a claim, the court can award not only the wages but also **50% up to 100% extra as a penalty** for the late payment ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=,of%20wages%20due%20and%20unpaid)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=the%20wage%20claim%20under%20sub,those%20wages%20due%20and%20unpaid)). Specifically, under Wis. Stat. §109.11(2), if you sue before the Department of Workforce Development process is completed, a circuit court *may* award up to 50% of unpaid wages as a penalty; if you went through the DWD process first and then sue, the court *may* award up to 100% (double wages) as a penalty ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=,of%20wages%20due%20and%20unpaid)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=the%20wage%20claim%20under%20sub,those%20wages%20due%20and%20unpaid)). In any case, the core amount is the unpaid wages themselves (which is what you are certainly owed). Example: Your final paycheck of $800 was never given. You sue in small claims for $800 + ask for 50% penalty = $400 (so total $1,200) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=,of%20wages%20due%20and%20unpaid)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=Penalties%20under%20sub,1701)). The court has discretion on the penalty – it’s not automatic like the tenant deposit double (with wages, the statute says “may” award, and it depends on if the failure was intentional or employer had a good faith dispute) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=court%20has%20broad%20discretion%20under,1028)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=Penalties%20under%20sub,1701)). But if it was willful, likely you get it. Wisconsin wage law also mandates the court to award you **your attorney’s fees and costs** if you win a wage claim ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=,of%20wages%20due%20and%20unpaid)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=Penalties%20under%20sub,1701)). In small claims, as a self-rep you only get costs, but if you hired a lawyer, the employer would have to pay that fee (this is to encourage attorneys to take wage cases, though in small claims the amounts might be small so often people go to DWD instead). **Pre-filing:** Wisconsin gives you the option to file a wage claim with the **Department of Workforce Development (Labor Standards Bureau)** and let them attempt collection (they often send a letter or hold an administrative hearing, and can even get judgment). You are not required to do that; you can go straight to court if you prefer ([dwd.wisconsin.gov](https://dwd.wisconsin.gov/er/laborstandards/wages.htm#:~:text=Wage%20Payment%20and%20Collection%20Law,pay%20wages%20earned%20on%20the)). Some choose DWD because it’s simpler and free, but it can be slow. If you use DWD, and they find in your favor, sometimes the employer pays, or DWD might refer to DA for violation. But to get the penalty wages beyond what DWD can do, you may need the court. The statute of limitations on wage claims for the 50% penalty is 2 years ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=at%20the%20time%20an%20enforcement,1701)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=the%20wage%20claim%20under%20sub,those%20wages%20due%20and%20unpaid)) (for the base wages it’s 6 years as a contract, but to get the additional “waiting time” penalty, you must act within 2 years). So don’t delay. Provide evidence of hours worked and pay rate (pay stubs, time sheets, etc.). If you quit or were fired, provide evidence of last day and that payday passed. Often, just showing you weren’t paid and you tried contacting them is enough; the employer then must justify non-payment (like “you damaged property so I withheld pay” – which is usually illegal without consent or court judgment, so you’d likely win). **Note:** This includes **unpaid commissions** or bonuses that were earned and due. Those are considered wages if the conditions for earning them were met. If an employer refuses to pay a commission you clearly earned, same process – sue for it (within 2 years if you want penalty). If they claim you didn’t meet conditions, that’s the dispute for court. Also, if an employer made illegal deductions from pay (e.g., deducted cash register shortages without written agreement), you can sue to recover those amounts – that’s treated as unpaid wages too (and subject to penalty) because WI law prohibits certain deductions without authorization. All such wage cases are very appropriate for small claims as long as the amount is ≤ $10k. Many are just a few hundred or a couple thousand dollars (e.g., unpaid overtime $1,000). If it’s more (like a big sales commission $15k), you either waive to $10k or file as large claim; often those get settled or handled by attorneys anyway. So small claims sees many straightforward paycheck claims (like final paycheck, last week of work, etc.). 

- **Unpaid Overtime or Minimum Wage (Wage/Hour Violations):** *Eligible.* This is a subset of unpaid wages but worth noting: if your employer didn’t pay required overtime (time-and-a-half for hours over 40 in a week for non-exempt employees) or paid below minimum wage, you can claim those under both state and federal law. Wisconsin’s overtime and minimum wage law aligns largely with federal (FLSA). The FLSA allows you to recover unpaid overtime going back 2 years (3 if willful) and an equal amount in “liquidated damages” (basically double) plus attorney fees. You can bring an FLSA claim in state court small claims (concurrent jurisdiction) for amounts up to $10k. The state law also allows the 50% or 100% penalty wages as above. If it’s a straightforward calculation (like they misclassified you and you’re owed $2,000 overtime), small claims can award that plus the penalty (50% = $1,000). These can be complicated if employer disputes you were entitled to overtime or the hours – but small claims can handle relatively simple disputes like "I worked 50 hours a week, employer only paid straight time for 10 overtime hours." You show timesheets, etc. The employer might say "You were exempt/salaried" – that’s a legal issue the court may have to decide quickly. If it’s complex with multiple employees or large sums, those often become bigger lawsuits. But one worker under $10k is fine. You may also file a complaint with the Wisconsin DWD Equal Rights Division for wage/hour – not required before suing, that’s more for an administrative remedy. Often the threat of attorney fees and double damages spurs employers to settle these once confronted. 

- **Unpaid Bonuses, Commissions, or Benefits:** *Eligible.* If you had a contract or policy promising a **bonus or commission** and you met the criteria but the employer refuses to pay, you can seek that in small claims. Example: contract says "If sales exceed $100k, rep gets $5k bonus" and you did but they didn’t pay – sue for $5k. Or you earned commissions on sales completed before leaving but they didn’t pay after you left – if those commissions were earned under the compensation plan, you can claim them. Wisconsin’s wage payment law covers commissions and some benefits as wages (e.g., earned vacation pay is considered wages that must be paid out upon separation if promised by company policy) ([dwd.wisconsin.gov](https://dwd.wisconsin.gov/er/laborstandards/wages.htm#:~:text=Wage%20Payment%20and%20Collection%20Law,pay%20wages%20earned%20on%20the)). So if they don’t pay out your accrued vacation per policy, you could claim that too. The same penalty wage provisions apply to these as to normal wages (some employers wrongly think they can withhold a bonus or accrued vacation – if company policy gave it, they can’t after you did what’s required). Provide the written plan or policy and evidence you achieved the goal or accrued X hours. The time limit and penalty are like other wages (2 years for penalty). If it’s discretionary bonus (“maybe we’ll give you something”), you can’t force it. But if it was a clear promise or part of contract, you can. 

- **Unreimbursed Expenses:** *Eligible.* If your job required you to pay certain business expenses out-of-pocket with the understanding you’d be reimbursed (mileage, travel expenses, buying equipment) and the employer didn’t reimburse you, you can sue for those amounts. Wisconsin includes certain reimbursements as part of wage claims if an employer is contractually obligated to pay them. For example, you traveled to a conference for work and spent $500 on airfare that employer said they’d cover, but after you submit expense report they never pay – that’s a contract claim. In small claims, you’d present the expense report and any policy or email that those expenses are reimbursable. Note: If not in writing, you’ll have to testify it was agreed. Wisconsin doesn’t have a specific statute mandating expense reimbursement (some states do for certain items), but if failing to reimburse effectively lowers your pay below minimum wage, that’s an issue. Assuming that’s not the case, it’s just a straightforward debt the employer owes you. The 6-year contract SOL would apply (no shorter limit since it’s not exactly “wages” – though some could argue it’s covered as wage under §109, but safer to file within 2 years if possibly considered wage). In any event, small claims is fine for it. The amount should be net of any advance they gave you. If an employer provided a per diem or partial reimburse and you’re claiming extra, ensure it was approved. The court will want to see that the expense was legitimately work-related and agreed to be paid by employer. 

- **Misclassification (employee vs. contractor disputes):** This is more of a defense – if you were treated as an independent contractor and not paid overtime or minimum wage, you can still sue for those wage/hour underpayment as above, arguing you were in fact an employee. That’s essentially an overtime case as discussed. If you were a contractor and the hiring firm just didn’t pay your invoice, that’s a contract claim (we covered under breach of contract – you’d sue for unpaid invoice like any B2B debt). If you are trying to get a legal determination of status, small claims probably isn’t where to do it except as needed to get your money. For other misclassification issues (like benefit eligibility), that goes beyond small claims into administrative law or big cases.

**Better forum - Labor Dept vs. Court:** As mentioned, Wisconsin’s DWD Labor Standards Bureau and the U.S. Department of Labor’s Wage & Hour Division are avenues to recover wages without a lawyer. The state’s process is to file a wage claim (which you can do online or by mail) and they’ll investigate and try to collect ([dwd.wisconsin.gov](https://dwd.wisconsin.gov/er/laborstandards/wages.htm#:~:text=Wage%20Payment%20and%20Collection%20Law,pay%20wages%20earned%20on%20the)). They actually can issue a determination and even refer to district attorney if wages aren’t paid (because failure to pay wages is a statutory offense). Many people choose DWD for small wage amounts because it’s free and they sometimes have more leverage (threat of criminal charges). However, DWD cannot award the 50% penalty itself (they can only try to get your base wages). Only a court can impose the 50%/100% penalty and attorneys fees. So some employees, especially if they get a lawyer, go straight to court to get all remedies. It’s up to you. Using the administrative route does *not* preclude you from later suing if not satisfied (unless you signed a settlement). Just note the 2-year clock for penalties keeps running unless DWD’s involvement might toll it (there’s some case law that filing with DWD might toll – but not guaranteed, so safer to sue within 2 years). If you do file with DWD first, and the employer still doesn’t pay after DWD orders, you can get a wage judgment from DWD (filed with clerk) and then enforce that or still sue for penalty. But that’s more complex – likely you’d just stick with one route or the other.

In summary, **small claims court is a powerful tool for employees to recover relatively small amounts of unpaid wages** (and for contractors to recover agreed payments). The key is that the amount must fall within the $10,000 limit (most individual wage claims do). Wisconsin law provides for **50%–100% additional damages** and **mandatory attorney fees** in these cases to encourage employers to pay up and to make you whole ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=,of%20wages%20due%20and%20unpaid)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-109/section-109-11/#:~:text=Penalties%20under%20sub,1701)). Small claims judges will enforce those statutes as long as you prove you were not paid what you’re owed. Always try a demand letter first (sometimes a letter on law firm letterhead citing §109.11 and that you’ll seek 50% penalty if not paid in 7 days gets rapid results). If not, filing a small claim should get their attention via official summons, and many employers will pay once served to avoid a judgment and penalties. If they still contest, you have the law on your side in most clear wage cases, and the court will likely award wages plus penalty if the non-payment was wrongful.

Next, let’s note what types of cases you **cannot** bring in small claims or that belong elsewhere, and then procedural things like service, hearing, etc.)*

### Personal services / professional disputes

*(This appears partially overlapping with Consumer disputes above, but we’ll ensure all listed examples are addressed. Many were already covered under defective services. We’ll briefly mention a few specific niches the prompt listed.)*

- **Personal Care Services (Hair, Nails, Spa) – Botched Service:** *Eligible.* If a stylist or salon does a poor job that requires you to spend money to fix, you could sue for that cost. For example, a hairdresser severely damaged your hair with the wrong chemical, forcing you to pay $200 for treatments to correct it – small claims can handle that. It’s essentially negligence or breach of contract (they didn’t provide the service skillfully). As discussed, these typically aren’t large amounts (and sometimes the salon will offer a refund or free fix – but if not, you have recourse). Document the damage (photos of the botched hair, a statement from another stylist about what had to be done to fix it). No special pre-suit steps besides maybe complaining to the salon manager (which you likely did). There’s no specific statute for hair/nail services, but general consumer protection applies. If it was really extreme (e.g., hair treatment caused scalp burns and medical treatment), that becomes a personal injury case likely beyond $5k small claims cap – but minor damage or cost to redo hair is fine. Another example: manicure at a nail salon that caused an infection – you could claim the medical expense (if under $5k, as it’s personal injury). Or a spray tan that stained your expensive dress – claim cleaning or replacement cost. Judges will assess if the provider failed to use reasonable care. Many of these places have customers sign waivers (“not responsible for…”) – those waivers are not ironclad if there’s negligence, but can be considered. In small claims, often the judge just tries to reach a fair outcome.

- **Tutors, Coaches, Trainers – Paid and Not Delivered:** *Eligible.* If you paid for lessons or sessions that were not given, you can sue for a refund. E.g., you paid a private tutor $500 for 10 sessions and they only did 3 then quit – you can claim the unused $350. Or you hired a personal trainer for a package of 20 sessions for $1,000 and they disappeared after 5 – sue for the value of the remaining 15. Provide proof of payment and the agreement (often a text or receipt stating number of sessions). If they partially delivered, it’s basically a pro-rated refund of what wasn’t delivered. No unique rules; it’s a straightforward breach of contract. Similarly, if the quality was abysmal – e.g., music teacher took your money but never actually taught anything – you could argue for some or all money back due to failure of consideration. But it’s easier when they simply did not show up for scheduled lessons. If it’s an ongoing monthly service (like you paid monthly and got a bad service one month), you might just stop paying – but if you paid in advance or a lump sum, that’s when you’d sue if they don’t fulfill it.

- **Boarding, Grooming, and Training of Pets – Issues:** *Eligible.* If you paid a pet boarder or trainer and something went wrong (they didn’t provide the service or your pet was harmed), you can sue for financial loss. For instance, you pre-paid a dog trainer $600 for a multi-week program and they only did one week – get remaining $400 back. Or you took your dog to a groomer who injured the dog, incuring $200 vet – sue for vet bill (and possibly refund of grooming fee). Pet services typically have you sign some waivers too (“not responsible for vet bills up to $x”), but if they clearly were negligent (cut the dog’s skin badly), you can claim actual costs. There’s no special statute for pet grooming, it falls under normal negligence/bailment. If your pet died due to their negligence, note that in civil law pets are property – you’d get market value, not “emotional distress” (courts won’t award that). Market value of a pet is often not much (except specialized breeds/training). But you could get vet bills and maybe burial costs. These might exceed $5k if it was something like a show horse – in that case, beyond small claims. But typical dog or cat issues are under the cap. If the provider had you sign an agreement limiting liability to say the cost of service, that might come into play – courts sometimes enforce those unless gross negligence. Usually though, small claims will at least give you your vet bills if the groomer clearly caused harm. Similarly, if a boarding kennel lost your pet or it died due to their negligence, you could seek the pet’s value (and vet bills if it was injured). It’s a sad scenario but legally it’s a property claim. If they breached any contract terms (like “we provide 24/7 supervision” and they didn’t, leading to loss), that strengthens your case. Provide any contract, and evidence of harm (vet reports, etc.). And you likely should have confronted the business – maybe they offered to pay the vet yet perhaps not enough. If they offered some settlement, you can still sue for the difference if you think you deserve more (though weigh time/cost of doing so).

- **Medical or Professional Service Billing Disputes:** *Eligible (with caveats).* If you are overcharged or charged for services you didn’t receive by, say, a dentist, chiropractor, or other professional, you could dispute it via small claims. Example: dentist charged you for an expensive procedure you never agreed to – you could sue to get that money back if you paid, or if you haven’t paid, you could defend if they sue you. Note that true **malpractice** (negligence causing injury) typically exceeds small claims and also requires expert witnesses, so those cases do not belong in small claims. But small claims can handle simpler disputes like a **billing error or minor service failure**. If a doctor’s office charged you for a treatment you declined, or double-charged, you can ask for a refund in small claims (assuming you tried to resolve with their billing dept first). If a provider failed to deliver what was promised under some service package (like a med spa sold you 5 laser sessions and you only got 2), you can sue for the unused portion. For health care, often there’s an arbitration agreement or at least an internal dispute process – check what you signed. If arbitration is mandated, the provider might move to compel arbitration instead of court. But often for small billing disputes, they might just settle or let it go to small claims. So yes, those kinds of professional service fee disputes can be resolved in small claims. Just not the substantive malpractice aspects – those require circuit court (and typically exceed $5k easily).

- **Daycare / Preschool Fee Disputes:** *Eligible.* If you paid a daycare or preschool and they didn’t provide the care (e.g., facility closed and didn’t refund prepaid tuition), you can pursue your money. Or if you gave a deposit or supplies fee that wasn’t used and not returned. Example: pre-paid $1,200 for three months of daycare, daycare shuts down after one month with no refund – sue for $800. These are contract breaches. Possibly an event like COVID closure triggered issues – in 2020 some daycares didn’t refund, claiming hardship; legally, if the service wasn’t provided, you’d argue frustration of purpose or unjust enrichment to get your money back. They might claim it was impossible to perform (which could excuse them contractually if truly outside their control – that was untested area in courts). But likely small claims amicably resolved many COVID fee disputes. If not, one could file. Provide proof of payment and non-receipt of service (attendance records, closure announcement). Another: if a daycare expelled your child unjustly and refused to refund unused days, you have a case for the unused portion. Or if you gave two weeks notice and they still charged beyond your last day contrary to contract. All those fit as small claims.

*(Essentially, any one-on-one service arrangement where you paid and didn’t get what you paid for, or were harmed by the service, can be translated into a dollar claim and brought to small claims – as long as it’s within the value cap and not requiring complex evidence. The common thread: breach of contract or negligence causing monetary loss, which is what small claims addresses.)*

### Loans, Debts, and Financial

*(We covered personal loans and promissory notes in Contract disputes. This section likely intends things like shared expenses, engagements, breakups – we touched on those in contract, but let’s address each bullet explicitly.)*

- **Money Lent to Friend/Family:** *Eligible.* (Already discussed under Loans above.) If you lent $ to someone with the expectation of repayment, it’s enforceable whether or not you had a written note. It’s considered an **oral contract** for repayment. You can absolutely sue for it in small claims up to $10k ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=,enforce%20judgments%20from%20funds%20owed)). Be prepared to prove it’s not a gift – evidence like text messages “I’ll pay you back next month” or them making a partial payment, or a memo line on a check “loan” – all help. Without any evidence, it becomes your word vs theirs; judges often may trust consistent testimony, but it’s better to have something. The statute of limitations on an oral loan is 6 years from the date it was supposed to be repaid or from demand if no set time ([wilawlibrary.gov](https://wilawlibrary.gov/topics/justice/civil/judgment.php#:~:text=,Search%20examples%3A%20judgment%20%3B%20judgments)). Usually personal loans are awkward, but small claims gives a formal avenue to resolve it. No special steps beyond asking them to pay (which you likely did many times). If they promised in writing to repay (even an email “I will pay you $100/mo”), that’s even stronger. Summarized: yes, sue friends or family at peril to relationship, but legally you’ll likely win if you can show it was a loan.

- **Disputes Over Joint Expenses (Roommates, Couples splitting bills):** *Eligible.* If you and someone agreed to split costs (rent, utilities, vacation costs, etc.) and one person didn’t pay their share, you can sue for contribution. For instance, roommates both on lease – one skipped out and you paid the full rent of $1,000 for last month; you can sue the roommate for their $500 half. Or you and a friend went on a trip and agreed to split lodging, you fronted the whole $800 and friend didn’t pay you back $400 – that’s enforceable as an **implied contract**. Provide any proof of the agreement (text “we’ll split 50/50,” or at least evidence you requested it and they acknowledged). Even without writing, courts often find an implied promise to share common expenses if that was the clear understanding. Another scenario: splitting a **cell phone family plan** – if one party agreed to pay you $50/mo and stopped, you can claim the unpaid portion. These often rely on informal understandings, but small claims judges hear them a lot (especially ex-roommates and ex-couples dividing bills). If it’s an ex-romantic partner situation, keep it framed financially, not emotional – e.g., “We each agreed to pay half of rent and utilities; they failed to pay for three months after break-up, so I paid all $X – I want half.” Judges will enforce fair sharing if credible. Just ensure you can show you actually paid those bills (bank statements, receipts) and that the defendant benefited from them. Usually the defense is “I never agreed to pay that” – the judge will evaluate credibility. In absence of clear writing, they might split difference or lean on who has proofs. But by law, if two cohabitants share expenses by practice, an agreement to split can be inferred. So yes, small claims is correct venue for these personal contribution claims. The limit (6 years for oral agreements) likely not an issue as these are recent by nature. One caution: if it’s about **child support or something** disguised as expense splitting – that actually belongs in family court if it’s for a child or arising from a divorce (the small claims court cannot override a divorce order or decide child expenses – that must go to family court). But things like a couple bought furniture together and one kept it without paying share – that you can do in small claims (property division of unmarried couples is allowed for small personal property division or reimbursements – though if it’s essentially a domestic property dispute or palimony, those can get complex, but small claims can handle simpler “I paid for the couch, they kept it, I want money” scenarios).

- **Wedding / Engagement Money and Property:** *Eligible (with some nuances).* If an engagement is broken off, Wisconsin follows basically that the **engagement ring** should be returned to the giver (because it’s a gift conditional on marriage) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=A%20plaintiff%20may%20elect%20to,1030)). If the person who has the ring refuses to return it, the giver can sue for return of the ring or its value. Typically, they sue for “replevin” of the ring (property return) in small claims – the value of many rings is under $10k (if above, you can still do replevin in small claims because earnest money and engagement ring actions are often allowed as “other actions” regardless of amount in some states; in WI an action for return of a specific personal property up to $10k value is allowed ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov/Pages/Publications/SmallClaimsCourt173.aspx#:~:text=%2A%20Non,property%20does%20not%20exceed%20%2410%2C000))). So yes, you can demand the ring back via small claims. Bring proof of purchase or testimony the ring was given. Most Wisconsin courts will enforce that rule: ring goes back to purchaser if no marriage occurred, regardless of who broke it off (Wisconsin sees it as an “conditional gift” not to be penalized by fault) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-01/#:~:text=Civil%20theft%20under%20s,cause%20for%20civil%20theft%20provided)). For other wedding-related disputes: if you paid deposits to vendors and the wedding was canceled, you as the person who paid may try to get those deposits back. Many vendor contracts say “non-refundable” – if it was canceled close to date, probably vendor keeps it. If far in advance and they rebooked the date, you could argue for some refund. That would be a contract interpretation in small claims. If it’s an issue between the engaged couple – e.g., they jointly paid for a venue deposit and relationship ended, and one ended up absorbing all costs – that’s a joint expense scenario (they might sue each other to split the lost deposits). Harder if no explicit agreement beyond “we share everything.” But small claims can equitably decide if fairness dictates splitting. They can’t order emotional damages or who was at fault in break-up – purely the money. So if, for example, the bride’s parents paid $5,000 to caterer and contract non-refundable, and groom’s family promised to share cost but now groom’s side refuses, that’s somewhat unenforceable because typically such promises in contemplation of marriage are conditional too. Wisconsin doesn’t have a specific law on other pre-marriage expenses, but the ring rule is clear. For other gifts exchanged during engagement, if specifically given conditional on marriage (like “here’s a $2,000 check to help with house after wedding”), those might be treated similarly to the ring – conditional. If marriage didn’t occur, they should arguably be returned. Small claims can decide on a case-by-case equitable basis. Often these cases are messy and maybe not ideal for self-represented folks because emotions. But legally, yes, one can attempt to retrieve money or property given in contemplation of marriage that fell through. Actually, Wisconsin case law (e.g., *Brown v. Thomas* for rings) only deals directly with the ring. For expenses, usually each bears their own unless an agreement to share costs. So if you and ex-fiancé each paid half the canceled venue deposit and lost it, typically each loses own half. If one person paid it all, absent agreement from the other to reimburse part, you might not get the other to pay. Because being engaged doesn’t automatically create liability to split wedding costs if it breaks off. So you’d need an actual contract – which couples rarely have. But if you do (we’ve seen some scrap of writing like “I’ll pay you back for half of [specific expense] if we cancel”), then you could enforce that. So those cases can be attempted in small claims but success depends on evidence of a promise or unjust enrichment. The court could find unjust enrichment if one party solely paid a huge expense that directly benefited the other in some way and fairness demands sharing. It’s not guaranteed. But small claims can at least adjudicate it without huge costs.

- **Jointly Owned Property After Break-up:** *Eligible (depending on what it is).* If an unmarried couple or friends bought an item together (TV, car, furniture) and then parted ways, you can resolve who gets it or value via small claims if under $10k. If one person kept the item, the other can sue for the value of their share. For example, you and partner co-bought a $6,000 car (each paid $3k), car is titled only in partner’s name and they keep it after break-up – you could sue for $3,000 as your equitable share. Or if both names on title, you could sue for partition, but small claims cannot transfer title (that’s equitable relief beyond its scope). Instead, small claims could award money to one party to “buy out” the other’s interest. Often, folks settle those by one paying the other voluntarily. If not, small claims can effectively do the same via judgment. Another example: you jointly purchased household items – small claims can’t physically divide them (it can’t issue an order “give them the couch” because that’s like specific performance – though a replevin action is possible for specific items if one party clearly is rightful owner of particular item). More typically, one party sues for value of items they didn’t get. Provide receipts or testimony of contributions. Wisconsin doesn’t recognize “common law marriage” or automatic property rights for unmarried couples, but it does allow equitable claims for contributions (like *Watts v. Watts* case in WI allows an unmarried cohabitant to seek compensation for contributions to property or assets acquired during relationship under unjust enrichment). Such a *Watts* claim often exceeds $10k and is filed in circuit court. But smaller discrete property disputes are fine in small claims – e.g. “I paid $2,000 towards a boat titled in ex’s name, we broke up, they have boat, I want my $2k back.” That’s precisely a small claim scenario. No special steps beyond demand letter. Summing up: small claims can sort out simple joint property and expense issues after a friendship or relationship ends, by awarding money to offset contributions, as long as the claim isn’t entangled with needing injunctions or declaratory relief.

- **Security Deposits / Earnest Money in non-real-estate context:** Real estate earnest money was covered (it’s allowed regardless of amount in small claims by statute) ([www.wicourts.gov](https://www.wicourts.gov/publications/guides/smallclaimsguide.htm#:~:text=,claimed%20is%20%2410%2C000%20or%20less)). For other contexts, like you put down a deposit for a *purchase* (e.g., $500 deposit on a custom furniture order, then seller never delivered and won’t refund), those are simply contract issues and yes, you’d get your deposit back via small claims. We did cover those in defective products possibly.

### Counterclaims

If you are sued in small claims as a defendant, you have the right to file a **counterclaim** against the plaintiff for any claim you have that arises out of the same transaction or occurrence. A **counterclaim** is essentially you suing them back in the same case. Wisconsin allows counterclaims in small claims, subject to the same jurisdictional limits (generally $10,000) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.02.html#:~:text=,06%20%282)):

- **Filing a Counterclaim:** You should state your counterclaim in writing or orally at the return date. It’s best to use the Wisconsin small claims **Answer and Counterclaim (SC-5200V)** form to ensure it’s properly recorded ([courts.danecounty.gov](https://courts.danecounty.gov/Resources/Forms/small-claims-forms#:~:text=GF)). You must file it with the clerk and mail a copy to the plaintiff (or their attorney) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-02/#:~:text=,within%2060%20days%20after%20filing)). In small claims, you can also simply tell the court at the initial hearing, “I have a claim against the plaintiff too,” and the commissioner will typically schedule the matter so you can file a written statement of that claim after. The counterclaim can be up to $10,000 (or $5,000 if it’s a tort claim), just like a regular claim ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.02.html#:~:text=,06%20%282)). If your counterclaim would exceed the small claims limit and it arises from the same facts, Wisconsin law says the case may be transferred to large-claim court if you pay the fee difference ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.02.html#:~:text=,06%20%282)). Specifically, if a counterclaim > $10k is “compulsory” (same transaction), the *entire matter shall be tried under regular circuit court procedure* (not small claims), meaning it effectively bumps it out of small claims if you pursue the full amount ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.02.html#:~:text=,06%20%282)). To do that, you’d have to pay an additional filing fee (the clerk will inform you of the amount) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.02.html#:~:text=,06%20%282)). If you don’t pay the fee for transfer, the court will likely dismiss your counterclaim beyond the $10k. In practice, most counterclaims in small claims are also under $10k, so it stays in small claims process. If your counterclaim is unrelated to the plaintiff’s claim, the court will not hear it in this case – they’ll require you to file a separate lawsuit ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.02.html#:~:text=,and%20proceed%20under%20this%20chapter)). (Small claims doesn’t allow adding totally unrelated grievances as a countercomplaint; it must be either from the same transaction or at least within the jurisdiction’s typical scope for joining. The statute §799.02(2) says an unrelated counterclaim beyond small claims limits *shall be dismissed* ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.02.html#:~:text=,and%20proceed%20under%20this%20chapter)), whereas a same-transaction one beyond limit can bump the case to big court). Example: Plaintiff sues you for a credit card debt; you have an unrelated claim that the plaintiff (say a bank) damaged your property in a different incident. The court would not let you litigate that in this case – it’s unrelated, file separately.

- **Counterclaim Scenarios:** Common counterclaims: 
  - In a landlord’s rent suit, tenant counterclaims for security deposit or repairs (so landlord sues for $1,000 rent, tenant counterclaims $1,200 deposit – the court can offset and perhaps rule tenant actually is owed something after offset). 
  - In a contractor’s suit for unpaid invoice, homeowner counterclaims for defective work costs. 
  - In a collections case for a purchase, consumer counterclaims that the product was defective or the contract violated the law, seeking damages or cancellation. 
  - Essentially, any defense that not only defeats plaintiff’s claim but entitles you to affirmative relief can be a counterclaim.

- **Service of Counterclaim:** If you file a written counterclaim, the law requires you to “serve” (mail or deliver) a copy to the plaintiff (or their lawyer) within 60 days of filing ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-799/section-799-02/#:~:text=,within%2060%20days%20after%20filing)). It’s best to do it immediately by mail with a simple certificate of mailing. The clerk may forward it electronically if plaintiff is e-filing. But to be safe, send a copy yourself. If you state it orally at the hearing, the court will typically order that a written version be mailed anyway. 

- **If Counterclaim Exceeds Cap:** As noted, if you *have* a valid counterclaim above $10k that’s closely related, you have a choice: either limit it to $10k to keep it in small claims, or pay to move the case to circuit court and not limit it ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.02.html#:~:text=,06%20%282)). For example, if plaintiff sues for $5k, and you believe they actually breached contract causing you $15k damages, you might decide to waive the amount over $10k and counterclaim for $10k in small claims (simpler, no lawyer needed perhaps). Or if it’s worth it, you pay the extra fee (~$125 or so difference) and the case will be handled as a regular civil case (which might necessitate hiring a lawyer, formal discovery, etc.). In small claims, it’s common that defendants just keep their counterclaims within the limit to avoid the hassle of transfer, unless the excess is very large. 

- **Default on Counterclaim:** If you counterclaim and then the plaintiff (original) fails to appear at trial, the court can enter a default judgment in your favor on the counterclaim (just like if a plaintiff no-shows, their claim can be dismissed and the counter-plaintiff can win by default) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.22.html#:~:text=,or%20on%20its%20own%20motion)). Conversely, if you file a counterclaim and then don’t show up, the court may dismiss your counterclaim and still possibly give judgment to plaintiff on their claim if they prove it. Each claim is treated separately for default if one side or the other doesn’t appear. The safest practice: show up if you filed something, or risk losing it.

- **Filing Fee for Counterclaim:** If the case remains in small claims, there’s technically no additional fee to file a counterclaim up to $10k (you already paid or the plaintiff did initially). If it triggers a transfer (counterclaim beyond limit), you must pay the difference in filing fees between small and large claims ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/2011/799/799.02.html#:~:text=,06%20%282)). For instance, if plaintiff paid $94.50 to file small claim, and your counter is >$10k, you might have to pay around $170 (the difference up to the $265.50 large claim fee) ([www.waukeshacounty.gov](https://www.waukeshacounty.gov/circuit-courts/court-fees/#:~:text=Large%20Claim%20%20,Habitual%20traffic%20offenders%20and%20appeals)) ([www.waukeshacounty.gov](https://www.waukeshacounty.gov/circuit-courts/court-fees/#:~:text=Small%20Claim%20%20,%24117.50)). Check with clerk; Waukesha’s schedule explicitly says “Counterclaim over $10,000: $125.50” ([www.waukeshacounty.gov](https://www.waukeshacounty.gov/circuit-courts/court-fees/#:~:text=Occupational%20Driver%27s%20License%20Petition%20,%24164.50)). That aligns with paying the fee difference. 

- **Timeline for Counterclaims:** Ideally bring it up at the **first hearing (return date)**. Per Wis. Stat. §799.02, a counterclaim should be filed by the time of the answer/return date, or if discovered later, as soon as possible. If you attempt to counterclaim on the trial date out of the blue, the court may refuse as it could prejudice plaintiff without notice (small claims is a bit looser, but fairness still applies). The safe route: raise it from the beginning so scheduling and mediation can account for it.
