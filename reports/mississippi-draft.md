## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $3,500 for individuals; $3,500 for businesses [S1].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds $3,500, you must either waive the excess amount to file in Justice Court or file in a higher court. Splitting one claim into multiple cases to fit under the cap is not permitted. [S3]

## 3. What you CANNOT sue for

- **Title to real estate** — Justice Courts do not have jurisdiction over disputes involving the title to real property. Where to go instead: Chancery Court. [S1]
- **Defamation** — Defamation cases typically exceed the monetary limit and involve complex legal issues unsuitable for small claims court. Where to go instead: Circuit Court. [S1]
- **Malpractice** — Professional malpractice claims involve complex legal standards and typically exceed the small claims monetary limit. Where to go instead: Circuit Court. [S1]
- **Family law and probate** — Matters such as divorce, child custody, and probate are outside the jurisdiction of Justice Courts. Where to go instead: Chancery Court. [S1]
- **Claims against the federal government** — Justice Courts do not have jurisdiction over claims against federal entities. Where to go instead: Federal Court. [S1]
- **Workers' compensation** — Workers' compensation claims are handled by the Mississippi Workers' Compensation Commission. Where to go instead: Mississippi Workers' Compensation Commission. [S1]
- **Class actions** — Class action lawsuits are complex and exceed the procedural scope of small claims court. Where to go instead: Circuit Court. [S1]
- **Injunctions and specific performance** — Justice Courts can only award monetary damages and cannot issue injunctions or orders for specific performance. Where to go instead: Chancery Court. [S1]
- **Bankruptcy** — Bankruptcy matters are exclusively handled by federal bankruptcy courts. Where to go instead: Federal Bankruptcy Court. [S1]

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of the debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File a writ of garnishment with the court, serve the employer, and the employer withholds a portion of the debtor's wages to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Most effective when the debtor is employed and earns above the exemption threshold.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings (federal CCPA floor)

### Bank levy

Seize funds from the debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution, serve it on the debtor's bank, and the bank freezes and transfers funds to the court.

**Estimated cost:** $50-100 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor has sufficient funds in the account; may be limited by exemptions.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $10,000 in personal property, including bank accounts

### Property lien

Place a lien on the debtor's real property to secure the judgment.

File the judgment with the county clerk to create a lien on the debtor's property, which must be satisfied upon sale or refinancing.

**Estimated cost:** $25-50
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns real property; may take time to realize payment.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $75,000

Seize and sell the debtor's non-exempt property to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, and the sheriff seizes and sells the debtor's property at auction.

**Estimated cost:** $50-100 plus sheriff fees

**What's protected (exemptions):**

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Wrong defendant naming

**What goes wrong:** If you sue 'John's Garage' but the legal entity is 'JG Auto Repair LLC', the judgment may be unenforceable against the LLC's assets.

**How to avoid it:** Search the Secretary of State business records before filing and use the exact registered name; for sole proprietors, use the individual's full legal name. [S1]

### Missed statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing one day after the deadline gets the case dismissed with prejudice.

**How to avoid it:** Check the statute of limitations for your specific claim type before filing; for fraud, the discovery rule applies but be prepared to prove when you reasonably could have discovered it. [S1]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant is not properly served, the case may be dismissed or delayed.

**How to avoid it:** Follow the court's rules for service carefully, and consider using the sheriff or a professional process server. [S1]

### Filing in the wrong venue

**What goes wrong:** Filing in the wrong county can result in dismissal or transfer, causing delays and additional fees.

**How to avoid it:** File in the county where the defendant resides or where the cause of action arose. [S1]

### Failing to collect judgment

**What goes wrong:** Winning a judgment does not guarantee payment; you may need to take additional steps to collect.

**How to avoid it:** Familiarize yourself with collection methods like garnishment and liens, and be prepared to pursue them if necessary. [S1]

### Overlooking exemptions in collection

**What goes wrong:** Certain debtor assets are exempt from collection, which can limit your ability to satisfy the judgment.

**How to avoid it:** Understand the exemptions under Mississippi law to assess the feasibility of collection efforts. [S1]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in Justice Court; the process is designed for self-representation, but you may hire one if you choose. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**
A: The typical timeline from filing to hearing is 30 to 90 days, depending on the court's schedule and the complexity of the case. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**
A: The maximum amount you can sue for in Mississippi Justice Court is $3,500. [S1]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**
A: If the defendant fails to appear, the court may enter a default judgment in your favor, but you must still present evidence to support your claim. [S1]

**Q: Can I appeal?**
A: Yes, either party can appeal a judgment to the County or Circuit Court within 10 days of the decision. [S1]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**
A: A judgment in Mississippi is valid for 7 years and can be renewed before it expires to extend its enforceability. [S1]
