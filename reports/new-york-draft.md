## 3. What you CANNOT sue for

- **Eviction (unlawful detainer)** — Small Claims Court cannot grant possession of property; eviction proceedings must be filed in Housing Court. Where to go instead: Housing Court. [S1]
- **Defamation (libel and slander)** — Claims for defamation are not permitted in Small Claims Court as they do not involve monetary damages only. Where to go instead: Supreme Court. [S1]
- **Malpractice** — Professional malpractice claims exceed the jurisdiction of Small Claims Court due to complexity and potential damages. Where to go instead: Supreme Court. [S1]
- **Family law matters** — Issues like divorce, child support, and custody are outside the jurisdiction of Small Claims Court. Where to go instead: Family Court. [S1]
- **Claims against the federal government** — Small Claims Court lacks jurisdiction over federal entities; such claims must be filed in federal court. Where to go instead: Federal Court. [S1]
- **Class actions** — Class action lawsuits are beyond the scope of Small Claims Court and must be filed in a higher court. Where to go instead: Supreme Court. [S1]
- **Injunctions and specific performance** — Small Claims Court can only award monetary damages, not compel actions or inactions. Where to go instead: Supreme Court. [S1]
- **Title to real estate** — Disputes involving property ownership are too complex for Small Claims Court. Where to go instead: Supreme Court. [S1]
- **Probate matters** — Issues related to wills and estates are handled by Surrogate's Court, not Small Claims Court. Where to go instead: Surrogate's Court. [S1]
- **Workers' compensation claims** — Claims for workplace injuries are under the jurisdiction of the Workers' Compensation Board. Where to go instead: Workers' Compensation Board. [S1]

## 10. Service of process

- **Who can serve:**
  - Court clerk via certified mail
  - Process server
  - Sheriff

- **Methods accepted:**

  ### Certified mail by court clerk
  The court attempts to serve the defendant by sending the notice via certified mail.
  - Pros: Included in filing fee, Handled by court
  - Cons: May fail if defendant doesn't accept mail

  ### Personal service by process server
  A professional process server delivers the notice directly to the defendant.
  - Pros: Higher success rate, Proof of service provided
  - Cons: Additional cost, Must arrange independently

  ### Personal service by sheriff
  The sheriff's office delivers the notice to the defendant.
  - Pros: Official service, Proof of service provided
  - Cons: Additional cost, May take longer

- **Timing:** 15 days (in county); 20 days (out of county).
- **Proof of service form:** SC-2 (Affidavit of Service); file at least 5 days before the hearing.
- **Business service:** Serve the registered agent or an officer of the corporation; for sole proprietors, serve the individual directly.
- **Out-of-state defendant:** Service must comply with the laws of the state where the defendant is located.
- **If you can't locate the defendant:** If the defendant cannot be located, you may request permission from the court for alternative service methods.
- **If the defendant is dodging service:** If the defendant is evading service, document your attempts and request alternative service from the court.

- **Sole proprietor employee exception:** Sole proprietors may represent themselves but cannot send an employee in their place. [S1]
- **Out-of-state business plaintiffs:** must register with Secretary of State first: Yes — N.Y. Business Corporation Law § 1301. [S1]
- **Trade-name (DBA) rule:** Businesses must sue and be sued under their legal names; trade names are insufficient. [S1]
- **Licensing notes:** Unlicensed contractors may be barred from suing for unpaid work if a license is required. [S1]

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Incorrect defendant information

**What goes wrong:** If you sue the wrong party or use incorrect information, your case may be dismissed or the judgment unenforceable.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the defendant's legal name and address before filing; for businesses, check the Department of State's records. [S1]

### Exceeding monetary limits

**What goes wrong:** Filing a claim exceeding the court's monetary limit can result in dismissal or the need to waive the excess amount.

**How to avoid it:** Ensure your claim amount is within the appropriate limit for the court where you're filing. [S1]

### Missing statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations has expired will lead to dismissal of your case.

**How to avoid it:** Confirm the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type before filing. [S1]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant isn't properly served, the court may not hear your case.

**How to avoid it:** Follow the court's rules for serving the defendant, and ensure proof of service is filed. [S1]

### Lack of evidence

**What goes wrong:** Without sufficient evidence, you may lose your case even if you're in the right.

**How to avoid it:** Gather all relevant documents, photos, and witness statements before the hearing. [S1]

### Ignoring counterclaims

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant files a counterclaim and you don't prepare, you could owe them money.

**How to avoid it:** Review any counterclaims carefully and prepare your defense accordingly. [S1]

## 18. Recent changes

- **2024-01-01** — **Increase in monetary limit for NYC Small Claims Court**. The monetary limit for Small Claims Court in New York City increased from $5,000 to $10,000, allowing for higher-value claims to be filed. N.Y. C.P.L.R. § 1801. [S5]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**
A: No, you do not need a lawyer to file or defend a case in Small Claims Court; the process is designed for individuals to represent themselves. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**
A: The timeline varies, but most cases are resolved within 30 to 90 days from filing to judgment. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**
A: In New York City, the maximum is $10,000; in City Courts outside NYC, it's $5,000; and in Town and Village Courts, it's $3,000. [S1]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**
A: If the defendant fails to appear, the court may grant a default judgment in your favor, but you must still prove your claim. [S1]

**Q: Can I appeal?**
A: Yes, either party can appeal the court's decision within 30 days by filing a notice of appeal and paying the required fee. [S1]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**
A: A judgment is valid for 20 years in New York, and you can take steps to enforce it during that time. [S1]
