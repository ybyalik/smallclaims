## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $10,000 for individuals; $10,000 for businesses [S1, S5, S14].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds $10,000, you cannot split it into multiple claims to fit within the small claims limit. You must either waive the excess amount or file in the Civil Division. [S5]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S5].

- Promissory note: File within 3 years from the date of default on the note.
- Open account / credit card debt: Claims must be filed within 3 years from the last activity on the account.

Have a portion of the debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

Place a lien on the debtor's real property, preventing its sale until the judgment is satisfied.

File a judgment lien with the Recorder of Deeds, which attaches to the debtor's real property.

**Estimated cost:** $20 plus recording fees

Obtain a writ of execution, have the U.S. Marshal seize the debtor's property, and sell it at public auction.

**Estimated cost:** $20 plus service and storage fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns valuable personal property.

**What's protected (exemptions):**

- **Multiple creditors priority:** Priority among multiple judgment creditors is generally determined by the order in which liens are recorded.

- **Self-representation allowed?** No — Businesses must be represented by an attorney in small claims court. [S1]
- **Can a business send an employee instead of an attorney?** No [S1].
- **Can a business send an attorney?** Yes [S1].
- **Insurance adjuster representation:** No [S1].
- **Sole proprietor employee exception:** Sole proprietors may represent themselves but cannot send an employee in their place. [S1]
- **Out-of-state business plaintiffs:** must register with Secretary of State first: Yes — D.C. Code § 29-105.02. [S1]
- **Trade-name (DBA) rule:** Businesses must use their legal entity name when filing or defending a claim. [S1]
- **Licensing notes:** Unlicensed contractors may be barred from recovering fees for services requiring a license. [S1]

No material county-level differences; small claims procedure is uniform statewide.

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Incorrect defendant information

**What goes wrong:** Suing the wrong party or using incorrect names can result in dismissal or unenforceable judgments.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the exact legal name and address of the defendant before filing. [unverified]

### Missing statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations expires leads to automatic dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Confirm the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type and file within the allowed period. [unverified]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** Failure to serve the defendant correctly can delay the case or lead to dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Follow the court's rules for service of process precisely, using approved methods. [unverified]

### Overlooking counterclaims

**What goes wrong:** Defendants may file counterclaims that could offset or exceed your claim.

**How to avoid it:** Be prepared to address potential counterclaims and gather evidence to support your position. [unverified]

### Inadequate evidence

**What goes wrong:** Lack of sufficient evidence can result in losing the case.

**How to avoid it:** Collect and organize all relevant documents, photos, and witness statements before the hearing. [unverified]

### Ignoring post-judgment procedures

**What goes wrong:** Winning a judgment doesn't guarantee payment; failure to follow up can leave you unpaid.

**How to avoid it:** Learn about and utilize post-judgment collection methods to enforce the judgment. [unverified]

## 18. Recent changes

- **2017-01-03** — **Increase in small claims jurisdictional limit**. The jurisdictional limit for small claims cases increased from $5,000 to $10,000, allowing more cases to be heard in the Small Claims Branch. D.C. Code § 11–1321. [S1]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, individuals are not required to have a lawyer in small claims court; however, businesses must be represented by an attorney. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: Small claims cases typically resolve within 30 to 90 days, depending on the complexity and court schedule. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: The maximum amount you can sue for in D.C. small claims court is $10,000, excluding interest, attorney fees, and costs. [S1]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear, the court may enter a default judgment in favor of the plaintiff. [S1]

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: Yes, either party can appeal a small claims decision by filing a motion for a new trial within 3 days of the judgment. [S12]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: A judgment in D.C. is enforceable for 12 years from the date of entry and can be renewed before expiration. [S5]
