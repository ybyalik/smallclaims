## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $7,000 for individuals; $7,000 for businesses [S1].
- **Splitting a claim:** If a claim exceeds the $7,000 limit, you may waive the excess and file in small claims court, or file the entire claim in a higher court. Splitting one dispute into multiple claims to stay within the limit is not allowed. [S3]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S3].

## 3. What you CANNOT sue for

- **Eviction (unlawful detainer)** — Eviction cases are not handled in small claims court and must be filed in the appropriate housing or district court. Where to go instead: Housing Court or District Court. [S1]
- **Defamation (libel and slander)** — Defamation cases are excluded from small claims court due to their complexity and the need for formal procedures. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S1]
- **Malpractice claims** — Professional malpractice claims require expert testimony and are not suitable for small claims court. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S1]
- **Family law matters** — Divorce, child custody, and support cases are handled by family courts, not small claims court. Where to go instead: Probate and Family Court. [S1]
- **Claims against the federal government** — Claims against federal agencies must be filed in federal court, not state small claims court. Where to go instead: U.S. District Court. [S1]
- **Class actions** — Class action lawsuits are complex and exceed the jurisdiction of small claims court. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S1]
- **Injunctions and specific performance** — Small claims court cannot issue orders requiring someone to do or stop doing something. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S1]
- **Workers' compensation claims** — These claims are handled by the Department of Industrial Accidents, not small claims court. Where to go instead: Department of Industrial Accidents. [S1]
- **Probate matters** — Issues related to wills and estates are handled by probate courts, not small claims court. Where to go instead: Probate and Family Court. [S1]
- **Title to real estate** — Disputes over property ownership are beyond the jurisdiction of small claims court. Where to go instead: Land Court or Superior Court. [S1]

## 10. Service of process

  A sheriff or constable personally delivers the court documents to the defendant.

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of the debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File a writ of execution with the court, serve it on the debtor's employer, and the employer will withhold a portion of the debtor's wages to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $5 plus service fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor is employed and earns above the exemption threshold.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings or 50 times the federal minimum hourly wage per week, whichever is greater.

### Bank levy

Seize funds from the debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution, serve it on the debtor's bank, and the bank will freeze and turn over funds to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $5 plus service fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor has sufficient funds in the bank account.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $2,500 is exempt from levy.

### Property lien

Record the judgment with the county registry of deeds to place a lien on the debtor's property, which must be satisfied before the property can be sold or refinanced.

**Estimated cost:** Varies by county
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns real property.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $500,000.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, and the sheriff will seize and sell the debtor's non-exempt property to satisfy the judgment.

- **Bankruptcy effect:** Filing for bankruptcy may discharge the judgment, depending on the type of debt. [S6]
- **Satisfaction of judgment form:** CIV-115 (Satisfaction of Judgment). [S6]
- **Multiple creditors priority:** Priority among creditors is generally determined by the order in which liens are recorded. [S6]

## 15. Special rules for businesses

- **Self-representation allowed?** Yes — Businesses may represent themselves in small claims court without an attorney. [S3]
- **Can a business send an employee instead of an attorney?** Yes [S3].
- **Can a business send an attorney?** Yes [S3].
- **Form for non-attorney representation:** CIV-116 (Authorization to Appear)
- **Insurance adjuster representation:** Not allowed [S3].
- **Sole proprietor employee exception:** A sole proprietor may authorize an employee to represent the business in court [S3].
- **Out-of-state business plaintiffs:** must register with Secretary of State first: Yes — Mass. Gen. Laws ch. 156D, § 15.01 [S3].
- **Trade-name (DBA) rule:** Businesses operating under a fictitious name must file a business certificate with the city or town clerk; form: CIV-117 (Business Certificate).
- **Licensing notes:** Unlicensed contractors may be barred from recovering fees for services requiring a license [S3].

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Incorrect defendant information

**What goes wrong:** If you sue the wrong party or use incorrect information, the case may be dismissed or the judgment unenforceable.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the exact legal name and address of the defendant before filing. For businesses, check with the Secretary of State's office. [S1]

### Missing statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations has expired will result in dismissal of your case.

**How to avoid it:** Determine the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type and file within that period. [S3]

### Insufficient evidence

**What goes wrong:** Without adequate evidence, you may lose your case even if your claim is valid.

**How to avoid it:** Gather all relevant documents, photos, and witness statements to support your claim. [S1]

### Failure to appear in court

**What goes wrong:** If you don't show up for your hearing, your case may be dismissed or you could lose by default.

**How to avoid it:** Mark your calendar and ensure you attend the scheduled hearing. [S1]

### Ignoring court procedures

**What goes wrong:** Not following court rules can lead to delays or dismissal of your case.

**How to avoid it:** Familiarize yourself with the court's procedures and comply with all requirements. [S1]

### Overestimating claim amount

**What goes wrong:** Claiming more than the allowable limit can result in dismissal or transfer to a higher court.

**How to avoid it:** Ensure your claim does not exceed the $7,000 limit, or waive the excess to stay within small claims jurisdiction. [S1]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in small claims court. The process is designed to be accessible for individuals representing themselves. However, you may choose to hire an attorney if you prefer. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: The timeline varies, but most small claims cases are scheduled for a hearing within 30 to 70 days after filing. The exact duration depends on the court's schedule and the specifics of your case. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: In Massachusetts, the maximum amount you can sue for in small claims court is $7,000. Exceptions apply for certain cases, such as property damage from automobile accidents, where higher amounts may be claimed. [S1]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear at the scheduled hearing, the court may enter a default judgment in your favor. You will still need to provide evidence to support your claim before the judgment is granted. [S1]

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: In Massachusetts, only the defendant has the right to appeal a small claims judgment. The appeal must be filed within 10 days of the judgment and results in a new trial before a judge or jury. [S1]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: A judgment in Massachusetts is valid for 20 years. During this time, you can take steps to collect the judgment, such as wage garnishment or placing a lien on the debtor's property. [S3]
