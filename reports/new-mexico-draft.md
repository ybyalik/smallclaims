## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $10,000 for individuals; $10,000 for businesses [S4].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds $10,000, you cannot split it into multiple cases to fit under the cap. You must either waive the excess amount or file in a higher court. [S4]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S4].

New Mexico allows you 6 years to file a claim for breach of a written contract and 4 years for an oral contract.

## 10. Service of process

- **Who can serve:**
  - Sheriff
  - Certified mail by clerk
  - Private process server

- **Methods accepted:**

  ### Sheriff personal service
  Sheriff or constable personally hands the papers to the defendant.
  - Pros: Reliable, Official
  - Cons: May incur additional fees, Potential delays

  ### Certified mail by clerk
  Court clerk sends the documents via certified mail to the defendant.
  - Pros: Cost-effective, Provides proof of service
  - Cons: Defendant may refuse delivery, Not suitable for all cases

  ### Private process server
  A licensed individual delivers the documents to the defendant.
  - Pros: Flexible scheduling, Can locate evasive defendants
  - Cons: Additional cost, Must ensure proper licensing

- **Timing:** 15 days (in county); 20 days (out of county).
- **Proof of service form:** 4-226 NMRA (Return of Service); file at least 5 days before the hearing.
- **Business service:** Serve the registered agent; if none, serve an officer or managing agent.
- **Out-of-state defendant:** Service must comply with New Mexico rules and the laws of the state where service is made.
- **If you can't locate the defendant:** If the defendant cannot be located, service by publication may be permitted with court approval.
- **If the defendant is dodging service:** If the defendant is evading service, document attempts and seek court permission for alternative methods.

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of the debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File a writ of garnishment with the court, serve the employer, and the employer will withhold a portion of the debtor's wages to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $30–$50 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Most effective when the debtor is employed and earns a regular wage.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings (federal CCPA floor)

### Bank levy

Seize funds from the debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, serve the bank, and the bank will freeze and turn over funds from the debtor's account.

**Estimated cost:** $30–$50 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor has sufficient funds in the account; may require multiple attempts.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $2,000 in a personal bank account

### Property lien

Place a lien on the debtor's real property to secure the judgment amount.

File a transcript of judgment with the county clerk where the property is located, creating a lien that must be satisfied before the property can be sold or refinanced.

**Estimated cost:** $25–$50
**Effectiveness:** Effective for securing payment but may take time until the property is sold or refinanced.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $60,000

### Writ of execution

Authorize the seizure and sale of the debtor's non-exempt property.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, and the sheriff will seize and sell the debtor's non-exempt property to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $50–$100 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns valuable non-exempt property; may require additional time and expense.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Personal property up to $5,000
- Tools of the trade up to $1,500

- **Wage garnishment cap:** 25% of disposable earnings.
- **Judgment lifespan:** 14 years; renewal: File a motion to renew the judgment before the 14-year period expires; no specific form required.
- **Debtor's exam form:** 4-234 NMRA (Order for Examination of Judgment Debtor).
- **Exempt assets:**
  - Homestead — $60,000
  - Vehicle — $4,000
  - Retirement accounts (federally and state-protected)
  - Social Security and federal benefits
- **Bankruptcy effect:** Filing for bankruptcy may discharge certain judgments; consult an attorney for specifics.
- **Satisfaction of judgment form:** 4-227 NMRA (Satisfaction of Judgment).
- **Multiple creditors priority:** Priority among multiple judgment creditors is generally determined by the order in which liens are filed. [S15]

## 15. Special rules for businesses

- **Self-representation allowed?** Yes — Businesses may represent themselves in small claims court without an attorney. [S4]
- **Can a business send an employee instead of an attorney?** Yes [S4].
- **Can a business send an attorney?** Yes [S4].
- **Form for non-attorney representation:** 4-235 NMRA (Authorization for Non-Attorney Representation)
- **Insurance adjuster representation:** Not allowed [S4].
- **Sole proprietor employee exception:** Sole proprietors may authorize an employee to represent them in court. [S4]
- **Out-of-state business plaintiffs:** must register with Secretary of State first: Yes — N.M. Stat. Ann. § 53-17-1 [S4].
- **Trade-name (DBA) rule:** Businesses operating under a trade name must file a Certificate of Trade Name with the county clerk.; form: 4-236 NMRA (Certificate of Trade Name).
- **Licensing notes:** Unlicensed contractors may face limitations in enforcing contracts; consult an attorney for specifics. [S4]

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Incorrect defendant information

**What goes wrong:** Suing the wrong party or using incorrect names can result in dismissal or unenforceable judgments.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the defendant's legal name and address before filing; for businesses, check the Secretary of State's records. [unverified]

### Missing statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations expires will lead to dismissal of the case.

**How to avoid it:** Confirm the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type before filing. [unverified]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** Failure to serve the defendant correctly can delay the case or lead to dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Follow the court's rules for service of process carefully; consider using a professional process server. [unverified]

### Insufficient evidence

**What goes wrong:** Lack of evidence can result in losing the case, even if your claim is valid.

**How to avoid it:** Gather all relevant documents, photos, and witness statements to support your claim. [unverified]

### Ignoring court deadlines

**What goes wrong:** Missing filing or response deadlines can lead to default judgments or dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Keep track of all court deadlines and respond promptly to any court notices. [unverified]

### Overlooking counterclaims

**What goes wrong:** Failing to file a counterclaim may waive your right to seek damages related to the same dispute.

**How to avoid it:** Consider any potential claims against the plaintiff and file a counterclaim if appropriate. [unverified]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in New Mexico small claims court. The process is designed to be accessible for individuals representing themselves. However, you may choose to hire an attorney if you prefer. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: Most small claims cases in New Mexico are resolved within 30 to 90 days from filing, depending on the court's schedule and the complexity of the case. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: In New Mexico, the maximum amount you can sue for in small claims court is $10,000, excluding interest and court costs. [S4]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear at the hearing, the court may enter a default judgment in your favor. You will still need to provide evidence to support your claim. [S8]

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: Yes, either party can appeal a small claims court decision to the district court within 15 days. The district court will hear the case anew in a trial de novo. [S6]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: In New Mexico, a judgment is valid for 14 years. You can renew the judgment before it expires to extend its enforceability. [S4]
