## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $6,000 for individuals; $6,000 for businesses [S3].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds $6,000, you must either waive the excess amount to file in small claims court or file in a higher court. Splitting one claim into multiple cases to stay within the limit is not permitted. [S3].
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S3].

## 3. What you CANNOT sue for

- **Eviction (unlawful detainer)** — Small claims court cannot handle eviction proceedings; these must be filed in the regular division of the municipal or county court. Where to go instead: Municipal or County Court. [S3]
- **Title to real estate** — Disputes involving the title to real estate are beyond the jurisdiction of small claims court. Where to go instead: Court of Common Pleas. [S3]
- **Libel and slander** — Claims for libel or slander are not permitted in small claims court. Where to go instead: Municipal or County Court. [S3]
- **Malicious prosecution** — Small claims court does not handle cases of malicious prosecution. Where to go instead: Municipal or County Court. [S3]
- **Punitive damages** — Claims seeking punitive or exemplary damages are not allowed in small claims court. Where to go instead: Municipal or County Court. [S3]
- **Family law matters** — Divorce, child custody, and other family law matters are outside the jurisdiction of small claims court. Where to go instead: Domestic Relations Court. [S3]
- **Probate matters** — Issues related to wills, estates, and guardianships are not handled in small claims court. Where to go instead: Probate Court. [S3]
- **Claims against the state or federal government** — Small claims court cannot hear cases against the State of Ohio or the United States government. Where to go instead: Ohio Court of Claims or Federal Court. [S3]
- **Class actions** — Class action lawsuits are beyond the scope of small claims court. Where to go instead: Municipal or County Court. [S3]
- **Injunctions or specific performance** — Small claims court cannot order someone to do or stop doing something; it can only award monetary damages. Where to go instead: Municipal or County Court. [S3]

- **Bankruptcy effect:** Filing for bankruptcy may discharge the judgment; certain debts are non-dischargeable. [S3]
- **Satisfaction of judgment form:**  (Satisfaction of Judgment). [S3]
- **Multiple creditors priority:** Priority among creditors is generally determined by the order of filing or recording liens. [S3]

## 15. Special rules for businesses

- **Self-representation allowed?** Yes — Businesses may appear without an attorney in small claims court. [S3]
- **Can a business send an employee instead of an attorney?** Yes [S3].
- **Can a business send an attorney?** Yes [S3].
- **Insurance adjuster representation:** Not allowed [S3].
- **Sole proprietor employee exception:** Sole proprietors may represent themselves or send an employee [S3].
- **Out-of-state business plaintiffs:** must register with Secretary of State first: Yes — Ohio Rev. Code § 1703.03 [S3].
- **Trade-name (DBA) rule:** Businesses must use their legal entity name; trade names alone are insufficient. [S3]
- **Licensing notes:** Unlicensed contractors may face challenges in enforcing contracts. [S3]

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Incorrect defendant information

**What goes wrong:** If you sue the wrong party or provide incorrect information, the court may dismiss your case or you may be unable to collect a judgment.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the defendant's legal name and address before filing; for businesses, check the Ohio Secretary of State's records. [S3]

### Missing the statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations has expired will result in dismissal of your case.

**How to avoid it:** Determine the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type and file within that period. [S5]

### Insufficient evidence

**What goes wrong:** Without adequate evidence, you may lose your case even if your claim is valid.

**How to avoid it:** Gather all relevant documents, photos, and witness statements to support your claim. [S3]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant is not properly served, the case cannot proceed.

**How to avoid it:** Follow the court's rules for service of process carefully and use reliable methods. [S3]

### Failing to appear in court

**What goes wrong:** Missing your court date can result in dismissal of your case or a default judgment against you.

**How to avoid it:** Mark your calendar and set reminders; if you cannot attend, notify the court in advance. [S3]

### Overlooking counterclaims

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant files a counterclaim and you are unprepared, you could lose more than you anticipated.

**How to avoid it:** Be prepared to address potential counterclaims and gather evidence to defend against them. [S3]

## 18. Recent changes

- **2016-09-28** — **Increase in small claims monetary limit**. The jurisdictional limit for small claims court was increased from $3,000 to $6,000, allowing more cases to be heard in this streamlined forum. Ohio Rev. Code § 1925.02. [S3]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in small claims court. The process is designed to be accessible for individuals representing themselves. [S3]

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: Most small claims cases are resolved within 30 to 90 days from filing, depending on the court's schedule and complexity of the case. [S3]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: The maximum amount you can sue for in Ohio small claims court is $6,000, excluding interest and court costs. [S3]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear, the court may enter a default judgment in your favor, provided you present sufficient evidence to support your claim. [S3]

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: Yes, either party can appeal a small claims judgment to the Court of Appeals within 30 days of the decision. [S3]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: A judgment in Ohio is valid for 21 years and can be enforced during that period. [S5]
