## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $10,000 for individuals; $10,000 for businesses [S5].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds $10,000, you cannot split it into multiple claims to fit within the small claims limit. You must either waive the excess amount or file in a higher court. [S5]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S5].

## 14. Post-judgment collection

- **Payment deadline expectation:** 30 days.

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File a motion for proceedings supplemental, serve the employer with a garnishment order, and the employer withholds a portion of the debtor's wages to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Most effective when debtor is W-2 employed at a stable job.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings (federal CCPA floor)

### Bank levy

Seize funds from debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a court order, serve it on the debtor's bank, and the bank freezes and transfers funds to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus bank fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor has sufficient funds in the account.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Social Security and federal benefits
- Certain retirement accounts

### Property lien

Place a lien on debtor's real property to secure the judgment.

File the judgment with the county recorder's office to create a lien on the debtor's real property, which must be satisfied upon sale or refinancing.

**Estimated cost:** $20-40 recording fee
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor owns real property and intends to sell or refinance.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $19,300

### Writ of execution

Seize and sell debtor's personal property to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, have the sheriff seize debtor's non-exempt personal property, and sell it at public auction.

**Estimated cost:** $50-100 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor owns valuable non-exempt personal property.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Personal property up to $10,250

- **Out-of-state business plaintiffs:** must register with Secretary of State first: Yes — Ind. Code § 23-0.5-4-1 [S1].
- **Trade-name (DBA) rule:** Businesses operating under a fictitious name must file a Certificate of Assumed Business Name with the county recorder; form: Certificate of Assumed Business Name [S1].
- **Licensing notes:** Unlicensed contractors may face challenges in enforcing contracts in court; ensure proper licensing before filing a claim [S1].

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Incorrect venue selection

**What goes wrong:** Filing in the wrong venue can lead to dismissal or transfer of your case, causing delays and additional costs.

**How to avoid it:** Ensure you file in the correct county based on the defendant's residence, employment, or where the incident occurred. [S1]

### Exceeding the monetary limit

**What goes wrong:** Claims exceeding $10,000 are not eligible for small claims court and must be filed in a higher court.

**How to avoid it:** If your claim exceeds the limit, consider waiving the excess amount or filing in the appropriate higher court. [S1]

### Failure to serve defendant properly

**What goes wrong:** Improper service can result in delays or dismissal of your case.

**How to avoid it:** Follow Indiana's rules for service of process carefully, using certified mail, sheriff, or a private process server as appropriate. [S1]

### Missing the statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations has expired will result in dismissal of your case.

**How to avoid it:** Be aware of the specific statute of limitations for your claim type and file within the required timeframe. [S6]

### Insufficient evidence

**What goes wrong:** Lack of evidence can lead to losing your case, even if your claim is valid.

**How to avoid it:** Gather all relevant documents, photos, and witness information to support your claim before filing. [S1]

### Ignoring counterclaims

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant files a counterclaim and you are unprepared, you could lose and owe money.

**How to avoid it:** Be prepared to address any counterclaims by gathering evidence and understanding the defendant's potential arguments. [S1]

## 18. Recent changes

- **2025-01-01** — **Increase in small claims court monetary limit**. The jurisdictional limit for small claims court increased from $6,000 to $10,000, allowing for higher-value disputes to be resolved in small claims court. Ind. Code § 33-28-3-4. [S5]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in Indiana small claims court. The process is designed to be accessible for individuals representing themselves. However, you may choose to hire an attorney if you prefer.

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: Most small claims cases in Indiana are resolved within 30 to 90 days from filing, depending on the court's schedule and the complexity of the case.

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: As of 2026, the maximum amount you can sue for in Indiana small claims court is $10,000, not including interest and court costs.

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear at the scheduled hearing, the court may enter a default judgment in your favor. You may still need to present evidence to support your claim.

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: Yes, either party can appeal a small claims court decision to the Indiana Court of Appeals within 30 days of the judgment.

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: A judgment in Indiana is valid for 10 years and can be renewed for an additional 10 years if necessary.
