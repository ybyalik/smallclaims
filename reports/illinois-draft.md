## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $10,000 for individuals; $10,000 for businesses [S1].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds $10,000, you cannot split it into multiple small claims cases. You must either waive the amount over $10,000 to file in small claims court or file your case in a higher court. [S1]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S1].

### Contracts and Money Owed

Illinois allows you 10 years to file a lawsuit for breach of a written contract and 5 years for an oral contract. Ensure you file within these timeframes to preserve your claim.

- **Breach of a Written Contract** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A contractor signed a $4,000 contract to remodel your kitchen, took the deposit, and never finished.

- **Breach of an Oral Contract** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You agreed verbally to lend someone $2,000, and they failed to repay as promised.
  - **Statute:** 735 ILCS 5/13-205. You have 5 years from the date of breach to file your claim. Gather any evidence of the agreement, such as emails, texts, or witness statements. [S4]

## 10. Service of process

- **Who can serve:**
  - Sheriff
  - Certified mail by clerk
  - Private process server

- **Methods accepted:**

  ### Sheriff personal service
  A sheriff or deputy personally delivers the summons and complaint to the defendant.
  - Pros: Official and reliable, Proof of service provided
  - Cons: May be slower than private service, Additional fees apply

  ### Certified mail
  The court clerk sends the summons and complaint to the defendant via certified mail with return receipt requested.
  - Pros: Cost-effective, Proof of mailing provided
  - Cons: Defendant may refuse delivery, Not suitable for all cases

  ### Private process server
  A licensed private individual delivers the summons and complaint to the defendant.
  - Pros: Faster service, Flexible scheduling
  - Cons: Higher cost, Must ensure server is licensed

- **Timing:** 3 days (in county); 7 days (out of county).
- **Proof of service form:** SC-500.2 (Affidavit of Service); file at least 1 days before the hearing.
- **Business service:** Serve the registered agent of the corporation or, if none, an officer or agent of the corporation. For sole proprietors, serve the individual owner.
- **Out-of-state defendant:** Service must comply with Illinois law and the laws of the state where the defendant is located.
- **If you can't locate the defendant:** If the defendant cannot be located after diligent efforts, service by publication may be permitted with court approval.
- **If the defendant is dodging service:** If the defendant is evading service, document all attempts and seek court approval for alternative methods.

- **Decision timing:** On the spot or mailed within 7 days. [S8]
- **On-site mediation:** by county — Some counties offer mediation services on the day of the hearing; check with the local court. [S8]

## 13. Appeals

- **Who can appeal:** either. [S15]
- **Deadline:** 30 days. [S15]
- **Fee:** $50. [S12]
- **Type:** on the record — The appellate court reviews the record of the small claims court proceedings; no new evidence is presented. [S15]
- **Attorneys allowed on appeal:** true. [S15]
- **Bond required:** false. [S15]
- **Stay of enforcement automatic on appeal?** false. [S15]
- **Default judgment appealable?** true — A default judgment can be appealed, but it is often more effective to file a motion to vacate the judgment first. [S15]
- **Notice of appeal form:** SC-500.8 (Notice of Appeal). [S11]
- **Frivolous appeal penalty:** Not specified by statute

## 14. Post-judgment collection

- **Payment deadline expectation:** 30 days. [S18]
- **Post-judgment interest rate:** 9% — 735 ILCS 5/2-1303. Interest accrues at 9% per annum on judgments. [S18]

### Wage Garnishment

Have a portion of the debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File a wage deduction order with the court, serve the employer, and the employer will withhold a portion of the debtor's wages to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor is employed and earns above the exemption threshold.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- The lesser of 15% of gross weekly wages or the amount by which disposable earnings exceed 45 times the federal minimum wage per week. [S17]

### Bank Levy

Seize funds from the debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a non-wage garnishment order from the court, serve the bank, and the bank will freeze and turn over funds from the debtor's account.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor has sufficient funds in the account; timing is crucial to prevent withdrawals.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $4,000 in personal property, including bank accounts, is exempt from collection. [S18]

### Property Lien

Place a lien on the debtor's real property, preventing its sale or refinancing until the judgment is satisfied.

Record the judgment with the county recorder's office to create a lien on the debtor's real estate.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 recording fee
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns real estate; may take time to realize payment, as the lien is satisfied upon sale or refinancing.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $15,000 for individuals, $30,000 for married couples. [S18]

### Writ of Execution

Authorize the sheriff to seize and sell the debtor's non-exempt property to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, and the sheriff will seize and auction the debtor's non-exempt assets.

**Estimated cost:** $50-100 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns valuable non-exempt property; process can be time-consuming and may not yield full payment.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $4,000 in personal property is exempt; specific items like necessary clothing and tools of the trade are also exempt. [S18]

- **Wage garnishment cap:** 15% of disposable earnings.
- **Judgment lifespan:** 7 years; renewal: File a petition to revive the judgment before the 7-year period expires to extend its enforceability.
- **Debtor's exam form:** SC-500.15 (Citation to Discover Assets).
- **Exempt assets:**
  - Homestead — $15,000 for individuals, $30,000 for married couples
  - Motor vehicle — up to $2,400
  - Personal property — up to $4,000
  - Retirement accounts — fully exempt
  - Social Security and federal benefits — fully exempt
- **Bankruptcy effect:** Filing for bankruptcy imposes an automatic stay on collection efforts; certain judgments may be discharged, while others, like those for fraud, may survive.
- **Satisfaction of judgment form:** SC-500.7 (Satisfaction of Judgment).
- **Multiple creditors priority:** Judgment liens are prioritized based on the date of recording; earlier liens have higher priority over later ones. [S18]

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Incorrect defendant information

**What goes wrong:** Suing the wrong party or using incorrect names can result in dismissal or unenforceable judgments.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the exact legal name and address of the defendant before filing; for businesses, check the Secretary of State's records. [S1]

### Missing statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations expires leads to automatic dismissal of the case.

**How to avoid it:** Confirm the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type and file within the allowed time frame. [S4]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** Failure to serve the defendant correctly can delay the case or result in dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Follow Illinois rules for service of process carefully, using authorized methods and ensuring timely service. [S13]

### Insufficient evidence

**What goes wrong:** Lack of adequate evidence can lead to losing the case, even if your claim is valid.

**How to avoid it:** Gather and organize all relevant documents, photos, and witness statements before the hearing. [S1]

### Ignoring court deadlines

**What goes wrong:** Missing filing deadlines or court dates can result in default judgments or case dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Keep track of all deadlines and court dates, and ensure timely submissions and appearances. [S1]

### Overlooking counterclaims

**What goes wrong:** Failing to file a counterclaim may forfeit your right to seek damages related to the same dispute.

**How to avoid it:** If you have a claim against the plaintiff, file a counterclaim within the allowed time frame. [S1]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in Illinois small claims court. The process is designed to be accessible for individuals representing themselves. However, you may choose to hire an attorney if you prefer. [S8]

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: The timeline varies by county, but most small claims cases are resolved within 30 to 90 days from filing to judgment. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: In Illinois small claims court, you can sue for up to $10,000, excluding interest and court costs. [S1]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear in court, the judge may enter a default judgment in your favor. You may still need to provide evidence to support your claim before the judgment is finalized. [S10]

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: Yes, either party can appeal a small claims court decision. The appeal must be filed within 30 days of the judgment, and the appellate court will review the record of the original proceedings. [S15]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: In Illinois, a judgment is enforceable for 7 years. You can petition the court to revive the judgment before it expires to extend its enforceability. [S4]
