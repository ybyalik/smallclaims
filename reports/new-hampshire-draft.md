## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $6,000 for individuals; $6,000 for businesses [S1, S4].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds $6,000, you must either waive the excess to proceed in small claims or file in a higher court. You cannot split a single dispute into multiple small claims to circumvent the cap. [S4].
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S4].

## 2. What you can sue for

### Contracts and money owed

New Hampshire allows most contract and debt claims up to $6,000 in small claims court. The statute of limitations is generally 3 years for written and oral contracts.

- **Breach of a written contract** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A roofer signed a $5,000 contract to replace your roof, took a $2,000 deposit, and never started the work.
  - **Statute:** N.H. Rev. Stat. § 508:4. You have 3 years from the breach to file. Bring the signed contract, payment records, and any communications. [S1]
- **Breach of an oral contract** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You agreed verbally to pay a neighbor $500 to paint your fence, but they never showed up after you paid.
  - **Statute:** N.H. Rev. Stat. § 508:4. 3-year deadline from breach. Bring proof of payment and any witnesses to the agreement. [S1]
- **Unpaid personal loan** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You loaned a friend $2,000 with the promise of repayment in 6 months, but they never paid you back.
  - **Statute:** N.H. Rev. Stat. § 508:4. 3-year deadline from when repayment was due. Bring evidence of the loan and any repayment attempts. [S1]
- **Unpaid promissory note** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A borrower signed a $3,000 promissory note and defaulted on payments.
  - **Statute:** N.H. Rev. Stat. § 508:4. 3-year deadline from default. Bring the original note and payment history. [S1]
- **Unpaid invoice for goods/services** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You provided $1,200 in landscaping services and the client never paid your invoice.
  - **Statute:** N.H. Rev. Stat. § 508:4. 3-year deadline from when payment was due. Bring the invoice and proof of service. [S1]
- **Open account or credit card debt** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A customer owes $4,500 on a store credit account and stopped making payments.
  - **Statute:** N.H. Rev. Stat. § 508:4. 3-year deadline from last payment or charge. Bring account statements and payment records. [S1]
- **Unpaid rent (money only)** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A tenant owes $2,000 in back rent after moving out.
  - **Statute:** N.H. Rev. Stat. § 508:4. 3-year deadline from when rent was due. Bring the lease and payment records. [S1]
- **Bad check (NSF or closed account)** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A customer paid you $800 with a check that bounced.
  - **Statute:** N.H. Rev. Stat. § 638:4. You must give a 14-day written notice before suing. Bring the returned check and notice proof. [S20]
  - **Special remedy:** You may recover the check amount plus a statutory penalty.
- **Co-signer liability for unpaid debt** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You co-signed a $3,000 loan and the primary borrower defaulted.
  - **Statute:** N.H. Rev. Stat. § 508:4. 3-year deadline from default. Bring the loan agreement and payment records. [S1]

### Landlord-tenant (money only)

Small claims court covers money disputes between landlords and tenants, such as security deposits and unpaid rent. Evictions are excluded.

  - **Statute:** N.H. Rev. Stat. § 540-A:6. Landlord must return deposit or itemize deductions within 30 days. Bring lease, move-out photos, and correspondence. [S21]
  - **Special remedy:** Tenant may recover double the amount wrongfully withheld.
- **Deposit deductions not itemized** — eligible.
  - **Example:** Landlord deducted $400 from your deposit but gave no itemized list.
  - **Statute:** N.H. Rev. Stat. § 540-A:6. Landlord must provide itemized statement within 30 days. Bring lease and move-out documentation. [S21]

- **Uninhabitable rental (money only)** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You paid $1,200 for a month in an apartment with no heat or water.
  - **Statute:** N.H. Rev. Stat. § 540-A:6. Bring lease, photos, and repair requests. Only money damages, not repairs or injunctions. [S21]

  - **Statute:** N.H. Rev. Stat. § 540-A:6. Bring lease, move-out documentation, and proof of property value. [S21]

  - **Statute:** N.H. Rev. Stat. § 540:13-a. Bring lease, police report, and proof of damages. Only money damages. [S22]

## 3. What you CANNOT sue for

- **Eviction (unlawful detainer)** — Eviction actions are not allowed in small claims court; only money damages can be sought. Where to go instead: District Court ejectment process. [S1]
- **Title to real estate** — Small claims court cannot decide ownership or title to real property. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S1]
- **Defamation** — Claims for libel or slander are excluded from small claims court. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S1]
- **Malpractice** — Professional malpractice claims are not allowed in small claims court. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S1]
- **Family law and probate** — Divorce, child custody, and probate matters are excluded. Where to go instead: Family Division or Probate Court. [S1]
- **Federal claims** — Bankruptcy, patents, trademarks, and other federal claims are not allowed. Where to go instead: Federal Court. [S1]
- **Class actions** — Class actions are not permitted in small claims court. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S1]
- **Injunctions/specific performance** — Small claims court cannot order someone to do or stop doing something. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S1]
- **Workers' compensation** — Workers' compensation claims are handled by the Department of Labor. Where to go instead: Department of Labor. [S1]
- **Suing a deceased person** — Claims against deceased persons must be filed in probate court. Where to go instead: Probate Court. [S1]


## 4. Damages and recovery

- **Compensatory damages:** yes.
- **Punitive damages:** no — Punitive damages are not allowed, but statutory penalties and multipliers may apply for certain claims such as bad checks or consumer fraud.
- **Statutory multipliers:**  
  ### Bad check (after 14-day notice)
  Check amount plus a civil penalty of either $100 or twice the check amount, whichever is greater, after proper notice. [S20]  
  Statute: N.H. Rev. Stat. § 638:4.

  ### Security deposit wrongfully withheld
  Tenant may recover double the amount wrongfully withheld if landlord fails to comply with deposit return rules. [S21]  
  Statute: N.H. Rev. Stat. § 540-A:6.

  ### Consumer Protection Act violation
  Court may award up to three times actual damages for willful or knowing violations. [S25]  
  Statute: N.H. Rev. Stat. § 358-A:10.
- **Attorney's fees recoverable:** sometimes — Attorney fees may be awarded for Consumer Protection Act claims and certain statutory violations, but not for ordinary contract or debt claims.
- **Pre-judgment interest:** Not specified by statute.
- **Post-judgment interest:** 2% (simple) — N.H. Rev. Stat. § 524:6-a. Interest accrues at 2% per year on judgments.
- **Filing/service fees recoverable from defendant if you win:** yes.

## 8. Pre-filing requirements

- **Government claim notice:** Not specified by statute

- **Consumer protection pre-filing:** No special pre-filing notice required for Consumer Protection Act claims. [S17]
- **Mandatory mediation/ADR before filing:** none

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File a writ of execution with the court, serve the employer, and the employer withholds a portion of wages and remits to the court until the judgment is satisfied.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Most effective if the debtor is steadily employed.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings (federal CCPA floor)

### Bank account levy

Freeze and seize funds from debtor's bank account.

Obtain a writ of execution, serve the bank, and the bank freezes and pays out available funds up to the judgment amount.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if you know where the debtor banks.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Social Security and federal benefits

### Lien on real property

Record a lien against debtor's real estate.

Record the judgment with the county registry of deeds to create a lien on any real property owned by the debtor.

**Estimated cost:** $25-50 recording fee
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor owns real estate; paid when property is sold or refinanced.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Wrong defendant naming

**What goes wrong:** If you sue the wrong legal entity, your judgment may be unenforceable.

**How to avoid it:** Check Secretary of State records and use the exact legal name. [S1]

### Missed statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the deadline results in dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Check the SOL for your claim type before filing. [S1]

### Insufficient proof of damages

**What goes wrong:** You may win liability but get only nominal damages.

**How to avoid it:** Bring receipts, estimates, and photos to court. [S1]

### Improper service

**What goes wrong:** Case may be dismissed or delayed if service is not done correctly.

**How to avoid it:** Follow court rules for service and file proof promptly. [S13]

### Suing for more than $6,000

**What goes wrong:** You must waive the excess or file in a higher court.

**How to avoid it:** Limit your claim to $6,000 or pursue the full amount elsewhere. [S1, S4]

### Not bringing all evidence

**What goes wrong:** The judge may rule against you if you lack documentation.

**How to avoid it:** Prepare all relevant documents and witnesses before the hearing. [S1]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**
A: No, you do not need a lawyer in New Hampshire small claims court. Parties may represent themselves, and lawyers are allowed but not required. Many people handle their own cases. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**
A: Most small claims cases are resolved within 30 to 90 days from filing to judgment, depending on court scheduling and whether the defendant appears. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**
A: The maximum amount you can claim in New Hampshire small claims court is $6,000, not including interest and court costs. [S1, S4]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**
A: If the defendant fails to appear, the court may enter a default judgment for the plaintiff, but the plaintiff must still prove their damages. [S1, S10]

**Q: Can I appeal?**
A: Either party can appeal a small claims judgment within 14 days by filing in Superior Court. The appeal is a new trial (trial de novo). [S6, S14]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**
A: A small claims judgment in New Hampshire is valid for 20 years and can be renewed before expiration. [S16]
