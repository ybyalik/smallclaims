## 3. What you CANNOT sue for

- **Eviction (unlawful detainer)** — Small claims court does not handle eviction cases; these must be filed in county court. Where to go instead: County Court eviction process. [S1]
- **Defamation (libel or slander)** — Claims involving defamation are excluded from small claims court jurisdiction. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]
- **Injunctions or specific performance** — Small claims court cannot issue orders requiring someone to do or stop doing something. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]
- **Family law matters** — Divorce, custody, and related family law issues are not handled in small claims court. Where to go instead: Family Court. [S1]
- **Probate matters** — Issues related to wills and estates are outside the jurisdiction of small claims court. Where to go instead: Probate Court. [S1]
- **Claims against the federal government** — Small claims court cannot hear cases against federal entities. Where to go instead: Federal Court. [S1]
- **Class actions** — Class action lawsuits are not permitted in small claims court. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]
- **Malpractice claims** — Professional malpractice cases exceed the jurisdiction of small claims court. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]
- **Workers' compensation claims** — These claims are handled by the Division of Workers' Compensation, not small claims court. Where to go instead: Division of Workers' Compensation. [S1]
- **Bankruptcy matters** — Bankruptcy cases are under federal jurisdiction and not handled in small claims court. Where to go instead: Federal Bankruptcy Court. [S1]

## 10. Service of process

  - Certified mail by clerk

- **Methods accepted:**

  ### Sheriff personal service
  Sheriff or constable personally hands the papers to the defendant.
  - Pros: Reliable, Official
  - Cons: May incur fees, Timing depends on sheriff's schedule

  ### Certified mail by clerk
  Court clerk sends documents via certified mail to the defendant.

  - Cons: Defendant may refuse delivery, Less personal

  ### Private process server
  A licensed individual delivers the documents to the defendant.
  - Pros: Flexible timing, Can locate hard-to-find defendants
  - Cons: More expensive, Requires hiring a professional

- **Business service:** Serve the registered agent; if unavailable, serve an officer or manager.
- **Out-of-state defendant:** Service must comply with Colorado rules and the rules of the state where the defendant is located.
- **If you can't locate the defendant:** If the defendant cannot be located, you may request permission for alternative service methods from the court.
- **If the defendant is dodging service:** If the defendant is evading service, document attempts and seek court approval for alternative methods.

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File a writ of garnishment with the court, serve the employer, and the employer withholds a portion of the debtor's wages to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Most effective when debtor is W-2 employed at a stable job.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings (federal CCPA floor)

### Bank levy

Seize funds from debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of garnishment, serve the bank, and the bank freezes and turns over funds from the debtor's account.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor maintains sufficient funds in bank accounts.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $2,500 exemption for personal bank accounts

### Property lien

Place a lien on debtor's real property to secure the judgment.

File a transcript of judgment with the county recorder to place a lien on the debtor's real property.

**Estimated cost:** $20-30 recording fee
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor owns real property; lien must be satisfied upon sale or refinancing.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $75,000

### Writ of execution

Seize and sell debtor's non-exempt personal property to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, have the sheriff seize non-exempt property, and sell it at public auction.

**Estimated cost:** $50-100 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor owns valuable non-exempt personal property.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Household goods up to $3,000
- Vehicle up to $7,500

- **Wage garnishment cap:** 25% of disposable earnings.
- **Judgment lifespan:** 6 years; renewal: File a motion to revive the judgment before expiration; personal service on debtor required.
- **Debtor's exam form:** JDF 111 (Motion and Order for Hearing on Asset Disclosure).
- **Exempt assets:**
  - Homestead — $75,000
  - Vehicle — $7,500
  - Household goods — $3,000
  - Retirement accounts (federally and state-protected)
  - Social Security and federal benefits
- **Bankruptcy effect:** Filing for bankruptcy may discharge the judgment; certain debts are non-dischargeable.
- **Satisfaction of judgment form:** JDF 111 (Satisfaction of Judgment).
- **Multiple creditors priority:** Priority among multiple judgment creditors is generally determined by the order of filing or recording liens. [S1]

## 15. Special rules for businesses

- **Self-representation allowed?** Yes — Businesses can appear without an attorney; a full-time officer or employee may represent the business. [S1]
- **Can a business send an employee instead of an attorney?** Yes [S1].
- **Can a business send an attorney?** Yes [S1].
- **Form for non-attorney representation:** JDF 250 (Notice, Claim, and Summons to Appear for Trial) [S1].
- **Insurance adjuster representation:** Not allowed [S1].
- **Sole proprietor employee exception:** Sole proprietors may represent themselves or have a full-time employee represent them. [S1]
- **Out-of-state business plaintiffs:** must register with Secretary of State first: Yes — Colo. Rev. Stat. § 7-90-801. [S1]
- **Trade-name (DBA) rule:** Businesses must use their legal entity name; trade names (DBAs) are not sufficient. Form: JDF 250 (Notice, Claim, and Summons to Appear for Trial) [S1].
- **Licensing notes:** Unlicensed contractors may face challenges in enforcing contracts; courts may deny recovery. [S1]

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Exceeding the claim limit

**What goes wrong:** If your claim exceeds $7,500, you must waive the excess to proceed in small claims court; otherwise, the case may be dismissed or transferred.

**How to avoid it:** Ensure your claim, excluding interest and costs, does not exceed $7,500 or be prepared to waive the excess amount. [S1]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** Failure to properly serve the defendant can result in delays or dismissal of your case.

**How to avoid it:** Follow the court's rules for service carefully, using approved methods and completing service within required timeframes. [S1]

### Missing the statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations has expired will lead to dismissal of your case.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type and file within the allowed period. [S5]

### Failing to appear at trial

**What goes wrong:** If you do not appear at the scheduled trial, the court may dismiss your case or enter a default judgment against you.

**How to avoid it:** Mark your calendar and ensure you attend the trial; if you cannot, notify the court in advance to request a reschedule. [S1]

### Not collecting the judgment

**What goes wrong:** Winning a judgment does not guarantee payment; you are responsible for collection efforts.

**How to avoid it:** Familiarize yourself with collection methods and be prepared to take necessary steps to enforce the judgment. [S1]

### Ignoring counterclaims

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant files a counterclaim and you are unprepared, you could lose your case or owe money.

**How to avoid it:** Review any counterclaims carefully and prepare your defense; consider seeking legal advice if needed. [S1]

## 18. Recent changes

- **2024-01-01** — **Increase in small claims court limit**. The jurisdictional limit for small claims court increased from $7,500 to $10,000, allowing for higher-value claims to be filed. Colo. Rev. Stat. § 13-6-403. [S5]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**
A: In Colorado's small claims court, attorneys are generally not allowed unless the defendant hires one; then the plaintiff may also hire one.

**Q: How long does it take?**
A: The typical timeline for a small claims case in Colorado ranges from 30 to 90 days from filing to resolution.

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**
A: You can sue for up to $7,500 in Colorado's small claims court.

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**
A: If the defendant fails to appear at the trial, the court may enter a default judgment in favor of the plaintiff.

**Q: Can I appeal?**
A: Yes, either party can appeal a small claims court decision within 14 days by filing a Notice of Appeal.

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**
A: A judgment in Colorado is valid for 6 years and can be revived before expiration.
