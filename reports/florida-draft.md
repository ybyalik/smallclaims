## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $8,000 for individuals; $8,000 for businesses [S5].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds $8,000, you cannot split it into multiple small claims cases. You must either waive the excess amount or file in a higher court. [S5]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S5].

### Contracts and money owed

Florida allows you 5 years to file a claim for breach of a written contract and 4 years for an oral contract. Ensure you have all relevant documents and evidence to support your claim.

- **Breach of a written contract** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A contractor signed a $6,000 contract to remodel your kitchen, took the deposit, and never completed the work.

- **Breach of an oral contract** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You agreed verbally to lend a friend $3,000, which they promised to repay in 6 months, but they failed to do so.
  - **Statute:** Fla. Stat. § 95.11(3)(k). You have 4 years from the date of breach to file. Gather any evidence of the agreement, such as emails or witness statements. [S6]

- **Default on a promissory note** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A borrower signed a promissory note for $7,500 but failed to make any payments.
  - **Statute:** Fla. Stat. § 95.11(2)(b). You have 5 years from the date of default to file. Present the original promissory note and records of non-payment. [S6]

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Personal property up to $1,000 is exempt

- **Wage garnishment cap:** 25% of disposable earnings. [S15]
- **Judgment lifespan:** 20 years; renewal: File a motion to renew the judgment before the 20-year period expires. [S7]
- **Debtor's exam form:** 7.343 (Order to Appear for Examination). [S10]
- **Exempt assets:**
  - Homestead — unlimited
  - Personal property — $1,000
  - Vehicle — $1,000
  - Retirement accounts — exempt
  - Social Security and federal benefits — exempt
- **Bankruptcy effect:** Filing for bankruptcy may discharge the judgment; certain debts are non-dischargeable. [S7, S15, S16]
- **Satisfaction of judgment form:** 7.350 (Satisfaction of Judgment). [S10]
- **Multiple creditors priority:** Judgment liens are prioritized by the date of recording; earlier liens have higher priority. [S7]

## 15. Special rules for businesses

- **Self-representation allowed?** Yes — A business may be represented by an officer or authorized employee in small claims court [S8].
- **Can a business send an employee instead of an attorney?** Yes [S8].
- **Can a business send an attorney?** Yes [S8].
- **Form for non-attorney representation:** 7.310 (Authorization of Representative) [S8].
- **Insurance adjuster representation:** Not allowed [S8].
- **Sole proprietor employee exception:** A sole proprietor may authorize an employee to represent the business in small claims court [S8].
- **Out-of-state business plaintiffs:** must register with Secretary of State first: Yes — Fla. Stat. § 607.1501 [S8].
- **Trade-name (DBA) rule:** Businesses operating under a fictitious name must register with the state and may need to provide proof in court; form: DBA-1 (Fictitious Name Registration) [S8].
- **Licensing notes:** Unlicensed contractors may be barred from suing to recover fees for unlicensed work [S8].

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Filing in the wrong venue

**What goes wrong:** Filing in the incorrect county can result in dismissal or transfer, causing delays and additional costs.

**How to avoid it:** Ensure you file in the county where the defendant resides or where the cause of action occurred. [S8]

### Exceeding the monetary limit

**What goes wrong:** Claims exceeding $8,000 are outside small claims jurisdiction and may be dismissed.

**How to avoid it:** If your claim exceeds $8,000, consider waiving the excess or filing in a higher court. [S5]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** Failure to serve the defendant correctly can delay the case or lead to dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Follow Florida's rules for service of process, using authorized methods and ensuring timely service. [S13]

### Missing the statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations expires results in automatic dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type and file within the allowed time frame. [S6]

### Not bringing necessary evidence

**What goes wrong:** Lack of evidence can lead to losing the case, even if your claim is valid.

**How to avoid it:** Bring all relevant documents, receipts, contracts, and witness information to support your claim. [S8]

### Ignoring counterclaims

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant files a counterclaim and you don't respond, you could lose by default.

**How to avoid it:** Address any counterclaims promptly and prepare your defense accordingly. [S8]

## 18. Recent changes

- **2020-01-01** — **Increase in small claims monetary limit**. The jurisdictional limit for small claims court increased from $5,000 to $8,000, allowing more cases to be heard in this forum. Fla. Stat. § 34.01. [S5]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in small claims court. The process is designed for individuals to represent themselves, though you may choose to hire an attorney if you prefer. [S8]

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: Small claims cases typically resolve within 30 to 60 days from filing to judgment, depending on the court's schedule and complexity of the case. [S8]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: The maximum amount you can sue for in Florida small claims court is $8,000, excluding costs, interest, and attorney's fees. [S5]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant fails to appear at the pretrial conference or trial, the court may enter a default judgment in your favor. [S8]

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: Yes, either party can appeal a small claims judgment to the circuit court within 30 days of the judgment date. [S14]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: A judgment in Florida is valid for 20 years and can be renewed before it expires. [S6]
