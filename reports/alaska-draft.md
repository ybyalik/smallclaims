## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $10,000 for individuals; $10,000 for businesses [S4].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds $10,000, you can still file in small claims court but must waive the right to collect any amount over $10,000. Splitting one dispute into multiple claims to stay within the limit is not allowed. [S4]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S4].

## 3. What you CANNOT sue for

- **Eviction (unlawful detainer)** — Small claims court cannot be used for eviction proceedings; these must be filed in the district court's regular civil division. Where to go instead: District Court civil division. [S1]
- **Title to real estate** — Disputes involving the title to real property are outside the jurisdiction of small claims court. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S1]
- **Injunctive relief** — Small claims court cannot issue orders requiring a party to do or refrain from doing a specific act. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S1]
- **Family law matters** — Divorce, child custody, and other family law issues are not handled in small claims court. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S1]
- **Claims against the government** — Small claims court does not have jurisdiction over claims against the State of Alaska or the United States government. Where to go instead: Appropriate administrative agency or court. [S1]
- **Class actions** — Class action lawsuits are beyond the scope of small claims court procedures. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S1]
- **Malpractice claims** — Professional malpractice claims require complex evidence and are not suitable for small claims court. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S1]
- **Probate matters** — Issues related to wills and estates are handled by the probate division, not small claims court. Where to go instead: Superior Court probate division. [S1]
- **Workers' compensation claims** — Disputes over workers' compensation benefits are handled by the Workers' Compensation Board. Where to go instead: Alaska Workers' Compensation Board. [S1]
- **Bankruptcy proceedings** — Bankruptcy cases are under federal jurisdiction and not handled by state small claims courts. Where to go instead: U.S. Bankruptcy Court. [S1]

## 7. Statute of limitations

| Claim type                       | Years | Clock starts                          | Statute         |
|-----------------------------------|-------|---------------------------------------|-----------------|
| Written contract                  | 3     | Date of breach                        | AS 09.10.053 [S4] |
| Oral contract                     | 3     | Date of breach                        | AS 09.10.053 [S4] |
| Sale of goods (UCC)               | 4     | Date of breach                        | AS 45.02.725 [S4] |

| Personal injury                   | 2     | Date of injury                        | AS 09.10.070 [S4] |
| Property damage                   | 2     | Date of damage                        | AS 09.10.070 [S4] |

| Judgment enforcement              | 10    | Date judgment was entered             | AS 09.35.020 [S8] |

- Written contract: Ensure to file within 3 years from the date the contract was breached.
- Oral contract: Oral contracts also have a 3-year limitation period from the date of breach.
- Sale of goods (UCC): Under the Uniform Commercial Code, actions for breach of contract for sale must be commenced within 4 years.
- Promissory note: File within 3 years from the date the borrower defaulted on the note.
- Open account / credit card debt: The 3-year period starts from the last payment or charge on the account.
- Personal injury: Personal injury claims must be filed within 2 years from the date of injury.
- Property damage: File within 2 years from the date the property was damaged.
- Fraud: The 2-year period begins when the fraud is discovered or reasonably should have been discovered.
- Bad check: File within 3 years from the date the check was dishonored by the bank.
- Unpaid wages: Claims for unpaid wages must be filed within 2 years from the date the wages were due.
- Security deposit: File within 1 year from the date the landlord was required to return the deposit.
- Judgment enforcement: Judgments can be enforced within 10 years from the date they were entered.

**Discovery rule:** The discovery rule applies to fraud claims, starting the limitation period when the fraud is discovered or reasonably should have been discovered.

**Tolling:** The statute of limitations may be tolled if the defendant is out of state, the plaintiff is a minor, or in cases of bankruptcy stays.

## 10. Service of process

- **Who can serve:**
  - Certified mail by clerk
  - Process server
  - Peace officer

- **Methods accepted:**

  ### Certified mail by clerk
  The court clerk sends the summons and complaint to the defendant via certified mail with return receipt requested.
  - Pros: Cost-effective, Handled by the court
  - Cons: Defendant may refuse delivery, Delivery confirmation may be delayed

  ### Process server
  A private process server personally delivers the summons and complaint to the defendant.
  - Pros: Professional service, Proof of service provided
  - Cons: More expensive, May require coordination

  ### Peace officer (personal service)
  A peace officer, such as a sheriff or police officer, personally serves the defendant.
  - Pros: Official service, Proof of service provided
  - Cons: Fees may vary, Availability may be limited

- **Timing:** 10 days (in county); 20 days (out of county).
- **Proof of service form:** SC-104 (Proof of Service); file at least 5 days before the hearing.
- **Business service:** Serve the registered agent of the corporation or, if none, an officer or managing agent. For sole proprietors, serve the individual owner.
- **Out-of-state defendant:** Service must comply with Alaska rules and the laws of the state where the defendant is served.
- **If you can't locate the defendant:** If the defendant cannot be located, you may request the court's permission for alternative service methods, such as publication.
- **If the defendant is dodging service:** If the defendant is evading service, document your attempts and request the court's permission for alternative service.

- **Post-judgment interest rate:** 3.75% — AS 09.30.070. Interest accrues on unpaid judgments at the rate specified by statute. [S1]

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of the debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File a writ of garnishment with the court, serve the employer, and the employer will withhold a portion of the debtor's wages to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $25 filing fee plus service costs
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor is employed and earns sufficient wages.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings or 40 times the federal minimum hourly wage per week, whichever is greater

### Bank levy

Seize funds from the debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, serve it on the debtor's bank, and the bank will freeze and turn over funds to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $25 filing fee plus service costs
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor has sufficient funds in the bank account.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $1,820 of deposits are exempt from execution

### Property lien

Place a lien on the debtor's real property to secure the judgment amount.

Record the judgment with the recorder's office in the county where the property is located to create a lien.

**Estimated cost:** $20 recording fee
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns real property; the lien must be satisfied before the property can be sold or refinanced.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $72,900

### Writ of execution

Authorize a peace officer to seize and sell the debtor's non-exempt property to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, and a peace officer will seize and sell the debtor's non-exempt property.

**Estimated cost:** $25 filing fee plus service costs
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns valuable non-exempt personal property.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Various personal property exemptions apply

- **Wage garnishment cap:** 25% of disposable earnings.
- **Judgment lifespan:** 10 years; renewal: File a motion to renew the judgment before the 10-year period expires; the court may grant a renewal for an additional 10 years.
- **Debtor's exam form:** SC-12 (Motion for Judgment Debtor Examination).
- **Exempt assets:**
  - Homestead — up to $72,900
  - Vehicle — up to $4,050
  - Personal property — up to $3,750
  - Wages — 75% of disposable earnings or 40 times the federal minimum hourly wage per week, whichever is greater
  - Retirement accounts — generally exempt
  - Public assistance benefits — fully exempt
- **Bankruptcy effect:** Filing for bankruptcy may discharge the judgment debt; however, certain debts are non-dischargeable.
- **Satisfaction of judgment form:** SC-6 (Satisfaction of Judgment).
- **Multiple creditors priority:** Judgment liens are prioritized based on the date of recording; earlier liens have higher priority. [S1]

## 15. Special rules for businesses

- **Self-representation allowed?** true — Businesses may represent themselves in small claims court without an attorney. [S1]
- **Can a business send an employee instead of an attorney?** true.
- **Can a business send an attorney?** true.
- **Form for non-attorney representation:** SC-13 (Authorization to Appear)
- **Insurance adjuster representation:** false.
- **Sole proprietor employee exception:** Sole proprietors may authorize an employee to represent them in small claims court. [S1]
- **Out-of-state business plaintiffs:** must register with Secretary of State first: true — AS 10.06.150. [S1]

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Filing in the wrong venue

**What goes wrong:** Filing in the incorrect district may result in dismissal or transfer, causing delays and additional costs.

**How to avoid it:** Ensure you file in the correct district where the defendant resides or where the claim arose. [S1]

### Exceeding the monetary limit

**What goes wrong:** Claims exceeding $10,000 cannot be fully recovered in small claims court; you must waive the excess amount.

**How to avoid it:** If your claim exceeds $10,000 and you do not wish to waive the excess, file in the district court's regular civil division. [S1]

### Missing the statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations has expired will result in dismissal of your case.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type and file within the required timeframe. [S4]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** Failure to properly serve the defendant can delay proceedings or result in dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Follow the court's rules for service of process carefully, using approved methods and completing service within required timeframes. [S1]

### Not bringing necessary evidence

**What goes wrong:** Lack of evidence can weaken your case and lead to an unfavorable outcome.

**How to avoid it:** Gather and bring all relevant documents, photos, and witness information to support your claim. [S1]

### Ignoring post-judgment procedures

**What goes wrong:** Winning a judgment does not guarantee payment; you must take steps to enforce it.

**How to avoid it:** Familiarize yourself with collection methods such as wage garnishment and property liens to enforce the judgment. [S1]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**
A: No, you do not need a lawyer to file or defend a case in Alaska's small claims court. The process is designed to be accessible for individuals representing themselves, though you may choose to hire an attorney if you prefer. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**
A: The timeline varies, but most small claims cases in Alaska are resolved within 30 to 90 days from filing to judgment, depending on court schedules and the complexity of the case. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**
A: In Alaska's small claims court, you can sue for up to $10,000. If your claim exceeds this amount, you must waive the excess to proceed in small claims court or file in the district court's regular civil division. [S4]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**
A: If the defendant fails to appear after being properly served, the court may enter a default judgment in your favor. You may still need to provide evidence to support your claim before the judgment is granted. [S1]

**Q: Can I appeal?**
A: Yes, either party can appeal a small claims court decision to the Superior Court within 30 days of the judgment. The appeal will be a new trial (trial de novo) where both parties can present their cases again. [S1]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**
A: A judgment in Alaska is valid for 10 years from the date it is entered. You can renew the judgment before it expires to extend its enforceability for another 10 years. [S8]
