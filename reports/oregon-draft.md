## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $10,000 for individuals; $10,000 for businesses [S1, S4].

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File a writ of garnishment with the court, serve the employer, and the employer withholds a portion of the debtor's wages to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $50 plus service fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor is employed and earns above the exemption threshold.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings or 40 times the federal minimum hourly wage per week, whichever is greater.

### Bank account levy

Seize funds from debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of garnishment, serve it on the debtor's bank, and the bank freezes and turns over funds to the court.

**Estimated cost:** $50 plus service fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor has sufficient funds in the account; some funds may be exempt.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $2,500 in a single account is exempt from garnishment.

### Property lien

Place a lien on debtor's real property to secure the judgment.

Record the judgment with the county recorder's office to create a lien on the debtor's real property, which must be satisfied upon sale or refinancing.

**Estimated cost:** $20 recording fee
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns real property; may take time to realize payment.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $40,000 for individuals, $50,000 for joint owners.

### Writ of execution

Seize and sell debtor's personal property to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, have the sheriff seize the debtor's non-exempt personal property, and sell it at auction.

**Estimated cost:** $50 plus sheriff's fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns valuable non-exempt personal property.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Household goods up to $3,000, vehicle up to $3,000, tools of trade up to $5,000.

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Failing to attempt pre-filing resolution

**What goes wrong:** The court may dismiss your case if you haven't made a bona fide effort to resolve the dispute before filing.

**How to avoid it:** Send a detailed demand letter and keep records of all communication attempts. [S1]

### Incorrectly naming the defendant

**What goes wrong:** Suing the wrong party can result in dismissal or an unenforceable judgment.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the legal name and status of the defendant, especially for businesses, through the Oregon Secretary of State. [S1]

### Missing the statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the deadline results in automatic dismissal of your case.

**How to avoid it:** Determine the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type and file within that period. [S4]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** Improper service can delay your case or lead to dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Follow Oregon's rules for serving documents, using authorized methods and ensuring proof of service is filed. [S1]

### Not preparing sufficient evidence

**What goes wrong:** Lack of evidence can result in losing your case.

**How to avoid it:** Gather all relevant documents, photos, and witness statements to support your claim. [S1]

### Ignoring post-judgment procedures

**What goes wrong:** Winning a judgment doesn't guarantee payment; you must take steps to collect.

**How to avoid it:** Familiarize yourself with collection methods like garnishment and liens, and act promptly to enforce the judgment. [S1]

- **2025-01-01** — **Increase in small claims filing fees**. Filing fees for small claims cases were increased to $57 for claims up to $2,500 and $102 for claims over $2,500. ORS 46.570. [S11]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**
A: No, attorneys are not allowed to represent parties in Oregon small claims court without the judge's permission. The process is designed for individuals to represent themselves. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**
A: The typical timeline for a small claims case in Oregon is between 30 to 90 days from filing to resolution, depending on the court's schedule and whether mediation is required. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**
A: In Oregon, the maximum amount you can sue for in small claims court is $10,000, excluding court costs and interest. [S1]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**
A: If the defendant fails to respond or appear in court, you can request a default judgment for the amount claimed, plus any applicable fees and costs. [S1]

**Q: Can I appeal?**
A: No, small claims judgments in Oregon are final and not subject to appeal. It's important to present your best case during the initial hearing. [S1]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**
A: A judgment in Oregon is valid for 10 years and can be renewed before it expires to extend its enforceability. [S4]
