## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $15,000 for individuals; $15,000 for businesses [S1, S5].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds $15,000, you cannot split it into multiple small claims cases. Instead, you must file in District Court. [S5].
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S5].

## 3. What you CANNOT sue for

- **Eviction (unlawful detainer)** — Small claims court does not handle eviction proceedings; these must be filed in District Court. Where to go instead: District Court eviction process. [S1]
- **Title to real estate** — Disputes involving ownership or title to real property are beyond the jurisdiction of small claims court. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]
- **Defamation** — Claims involving defamation, such as libel or slander, are not handled in small claims court. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]
- **Malpractice** — Professional malpractice claims require complex evidence and are not suitable for small claims court. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]
- **Family law matters** — Issues such as divorce, child custody, and support are outside the jurisdiction of small claims court. Where to go instead: Family Court. [S1]
- **Probate matters** — Disputes involving wills, estates, and trusts are not handled in small claims court. Where to go instead: Probate Court. [S1]
- **Claims against the federal government** — Small claims court cannot hear cases against federal agencies or the U.S. government. Where to go instead: Federal Court. [S1]
- **Class actions** — Class action lawsuits are complex and exceed the procedural scope of small claims court. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]
- **Injunctions and specific performance** — Small claims court cannot issue orders requiring a party to do or refrain from doing something. Where to go instead: District Court. [S1]
- **Workers' compensation claims** — Disputes over workers' compensation benefits are handled by specialized administrative courts. Where to go instead: Workers' Compensation Court. [S1]

  ### Sheriff personal service
  Sheriff or constable personally hands the papers to the defendant.
  - Pros: Reliable, Official
  - Cons: May incur additional fees, Timing depends on sheriff's availability

  The court clerk sends the documents to the defendant via certified mail.
  - Pros: Cost-effective, Provides proof of service
  - Cons: Defendant may refuse delivery, Slower than personal service

  ### Private process server
  A private individual authorized to serve legal documents delivers the papers to the defendant.
  - Pros: Flexible scheduling, Can be faster
  - Cons: Additional cost, Must ensure server is authorized

File a writ of garnishment with the court, serve the employer, and the employer withholds a portion of the debtor's wages to satisfy the judgment.

### Bank levy

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, serve it on the debtor's bank, and the bank freezes and transfers funds to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor has sufficient funds in the bank account.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $3,000 in bank account is exempt from levy

**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns real property and intends to sell or refinance.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $100,000

### Writ of execution

Authorize the sheriff to seize and sell the debtor's non-exempt property.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, and the sheriff seizes and sells the debtor's non-exempt property to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $50-100 plus sheriff fees
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns valuable non-exempt property.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Various personal property exemptions apply

- **Wage garnishment cap:** 25% of disposable earnings.
- **Judgment lifespan:** 10 years; renewal: File a motion to renew the judgment before the 10-year period expires; if granted, the judgment is extended for another 10 years.
- **Debtor's exam form:** Form 8 (Affidavit of Identification of Judgment Debtor).
- **Exempt assets:**
  - Homestead — up to $100,000
  - Vehicle — up to $2,950
  - Retirement accounts (federally and state-protected)
  - Social Security and federal benefits
- **Bankruptcy effect:** Filing for bankruptcy may discharge the judgment; however, certain debts are non-dischargeable.
- **Satisfaction of judgment form:** Form 9 (Satisfaction of Judgment).
- **Multiple creditors priority:** Judgment liens are prioritized based on the date of recording; earlier liens have higher priority.

## 15. Special rules for businesses

- **Self-representation allowed?** Yes — Businesses may be represented by an officer, owner, director, trustee, or employee in small claims court. [S5]
- **Can a business send an employee instead of an attorney?** Yes [S5].
- **Can a business send an attorney?** Yes [S5].
- **Form for non-attorney representation:** Not specified by statute.
- **Insurance adjuster representation:** No [S5].
- **Sole proprietor employee exception:** A sole proprietor may represent themselves or be represented by an employee [S5].
- **Out-of-state business plaintiffs:** must register with Secretary of State first: Yes — N.D. Cent. Code § 10-19.1-143 [S5].
- **Trade-name (DBA) rule:** Businesses must use their legal entity name when filing or defending a claim. [S5]
- **Licensing notes:** Unlicensed contractors may face challenges in enforcing contracts in court. [S5]

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Filing in the wrong venue

**What goes wrong:** Filing in the incorrect county can lead to dismissal or transfer, causing delays and additional costs.

**How to avoid it:** Ensure you file in the county where the defendant resides or where the business has a place of business. [S5]

---

### Exceeding the monetary limit

**What goes wrong:** Claims exceeding $15,000 are not eligible for small claims court and must be filed in District Court.

**How to avoid it:** Verify that your claim, excluding interest and costs, does not exceed $15,000. [S5]

---

### Missing the statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations has expired will result in dismissal of your case.

**How to avoid it:** Confirm the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type and file within that period. [S5]

---

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** Failure to properly serve the defendant can delay proceedings or result in dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Follow the court's guidelines for serving the defendant, using authorized methods. [S1]

---

### Not preparing sufficient evidence

**What goes wrong:** Lack of evidence can weaken your case and lead to an unfavorable judgment.

**How to avoid it:** Gather all relevant documents, photos, and witness statements before the hearing. [S1]

---

### Failing to collect the judgment

**What goes wrong:** Winning a judgment does not guarantee payment; you may need to take additional steps to collect.

**How to avoid it:** Familiarize yourself with post-judgment collection methods and be prepared to implement them. [S1]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**  
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in small claims court. The process is designed to be accessible for individuals representing themselves. However, you may choose to hire an attorney if you prefer. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**  
A: The timeline varies, but most small claims cases are resolved within 30 to 90 days from filing to judgment. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**  
A: The maximum amount you can sue for in North Dakota's small claims court is $15,000, excluding interest and costs. [S5]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**  
A: If the defendant does not respond within 20 days or fails to appear at the hearing, the court may enter a default judgment in your favor. [S1]

**Q: Can I appeal?**  
A: No, decisions made in small claims court are final and cannot be appealed. [S1]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**  
A: A judgment is valid for 10 years and can be renewed for an additional 10 years if necessary. [S7]
