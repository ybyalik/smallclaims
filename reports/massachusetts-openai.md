## 1. Jurisdictional limits – MAXIMUM AMOUNT YOU CAN SUE FOR  
Massachusetts small claims courts can handle most contract or tort claims for **money damages** up to **$7,000** (excluding court costs and interest) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=hereinafter%20called%20the%20procedure%2C%20for,of%20judgments%20upon%20such%20claims)). This $7,000 cap applies regardless of whether the plaintiff is an individual or a business (corporation, LLC, sole proprietorship, etc.), with **two key exceptions**: 

- **City or town tax claims:** A Massachusetts city or town suing to collect unpaid personal property taxes under M.G.L. c.60 §35 may use small claims for **any amount**, and other city/town claims up to **$15,000** are allowed ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=slander%20and%20libel%2C%20in%20which,of%20judgments%20upon%20such%20claims)). These higher limits apply **only** to municipal plaintiffs for specific tax or municipal claims. 

- **Motor vehicle property damage:** The $7,000 cap **does not apply** to claims for property damage caused by a motor vehicle. Small claims courts in Massachusetts can hear car accident property damage cases **of any dollar amount** ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=slander%20and%20libel%2C%20in%20which,of%20judgments%20upon%20such%20claims)) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=under%20this%20section%20if%20the,the%20amount%20of%20the%20claims)). In other words, if your claim is for damage to a car (or other property) from a collision, you are not limited to $7,000 – you could sue for the full amount of the repair or loss in small claims. (Notably, this exception does **not** cover personal injuries – only property damage. Injury claims are still capped at $7,000 as tort claims.)

Aside from those exceptions, **$7,000** is the hard limit for a small claims initial filing in Massachusetts ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=hereinafter%20called%20the%20procedure%2C%20for,of%20judgments%20upon%20such%20claims)). You cannot demand more than $7,000 in your Statement of Claim (if you do, the clerk will likely reject it or require you to reduce the amount). If you are owed more than $7,000, you have a few options: (a) sue in the regular civil docket of the District or Superior Court (outside of small claims), or (b) **waive** any amount above $7,000 and sue for $7,000 in small claims. It is common for a plaintiff to voluntarily waive the excess in order to use the simpler small-claims process – but note that such a waiver means you **cannot later collect** the remainder in another lawsuit ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/small-claims-standards-501-counterclaims#:~:text=A%20defendant%20may%20raise%20any,session%2C%20he%20or%20she%20waives)). The small claims rules explicitly allow cases to be “commenced under this section if the initial amount of damages claimed is $7,000 or less… notwithstanding that the court may award double or treble damages” under applicable law ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=under%20this%20section%20if%20the,any%20general%20or%20special%20law)). That means as long as your base claim is no more than $7,000, the court can still award statutory **multiple damages** (for example, double or treble damages for certain violations) even if the total ends up over $7,000, and can also award applicable attorney’s fees or costs on top of $7,000 ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-small-claims-and-bringing-a-claim-to-court#:~:text=,exceeding%20%247%2C000%20may%20be%20possible)). For instance, if you sue for a $5,000 loss and the law provides for double damages and your attorney’s fees, the judgment could exceed $7,000 **after** those enhancements – this is allowed as long as the **claim itself** was within $7,000 ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-small-claims-and-bringing-a-claim-to-court#:~:text=,exceeding%20%247%2C000%20may%20be%20possible)).

**No differentiation by plaintiff type:** Except for the municipal claims noted above, Massachusetts does **not** set different small-claims limits for individuals versus businesses. All private parties – whether you’re an individual, a small business, a landlord, or a company – are held to the $7,000 maximum for the claim amount ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=hereinafter%20called%20the%20procedure%2C%20for,of%20judgments%20upon%20such%20claims)). (By contrast, some states like California have lower caps for business plaintiffs, but Massachusetts has one limit for all.) There is also no statutory limit on the **number of small claims cases** you can file per year. A frequent-filer business (like a credit landlord or debt collector) can theoretically file as many cases as needed, though large-volume filers must comply with special filing requirements (see Section 13 on business rules). Massachusetts law does *not* impose California-style annual caps on filings over a certain amount – the main restriction is simply the $7,000-per-case limit.

**No claim-splitting to evade the limit:** You are **not allowed** to split one larger claim into multiple smaller claims to skirt the $7,000 cap. The statute requires that the claim be for “$7,000 or less” in total **initial** damages ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=under%20this%20section%20if%20the,the%20amount%20of%20the%20claims)). Courts will generally treat a series of lawsuits for installments of a single debt or claim as improper “claim splitting.” In practice, if you tried to file two small claims each for $7,000 arising from the same transaction, the second could be dismissed or consolidated. It’s better to either waive the excess above $7,000 or proceed in a higher court. *Example:* if a contractor is owed $10,000 on one contract, they cannot file two separate $5,000 small claims for different portions of that contract – that would violate the rule against claim-splitting. They should either sue for $7,000 in small claims (waiving $3,000) or use the regular civil court for the full $10,000.

**Venue and case transfer:** Jurisdictionally, Massachusetts small claims cases are handled in the state’s District Courts (or the Boston Municipal Court for Boston cases, and the Housing Court for certain housing matters). You must file in an appropriate **venue**: the law allows you to file in the court for the location where either the **plaintiff or the defendant** lives, **or** has their usual place of business or employment ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=Actions%20under%20this%20section%20and,which%20the%20property%20is%20located)). For residential landlord-tenant cases, there’s an additional venue option – the case may also be brought in the court where the rental property is located ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=Actions%20under%20this%20section%20and,which%20the%20property%20is%20located)) (this is helpful if the property is in a different county from where the landlord or tenant reside). If you accidentally file in the wrong court, it’s not fatal. Improper venue can be **waived** by the defendant, or if the defendant objects, the case will be transferred to the correct court rather than dismissed ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=Notwithstanding%20the%20foregoing%2C%20each%20court,An%20action%20may%20be%20commenced)). The Massachusetts small claims statute explicitly gives any District Court jurisdiction to hear the case if venue is waived, and provides for transfer if a timely motion for improper venue is made ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=Notwithstanding%20the%20foregoing%2C%20each%20court,An%20action%20may%20be%20commenced)). Bottom line: pick a permissible venue (usually where the defendant lives or does business), but know that a venue mistake will typically result in a transfer order, not a thrown-out case.

## 2. What types of cases qualify – EVERYTHING YOU CAN SUE FOR  

Massachusetts small claims is available for any suit for **money damages** based in **contract or tort** (with a few specific exceptions for certain torts). The governing statute describes small claims as covering “claims in the nature of contract or tort, other than slander and libel” ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=Section%2021,not%20exceed%20%2415%2C000%3B%20and%20provided)). This means most disputes where one party seeks a **fixed sum of money** can be brought in small claims, including breaches of agreement, property damage, or statutory penalties. You cannot use small claims for purely equitable relief like injunctions or for cases outside the contract/tort realm (see the list of non-eligible cases later in this section). Below is an **exhaustive list** of common claim types and their eligibility in Massachusetts small claims, with notes on state-specific rules:

### Contract & money-owed disputes  
**Breach of written contract:** Yes – small claims can address breaches of written contracts for up to $7,000 in damages ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=hereinafter%20called%20the%20procedure%2C%20for,of%20judgments%20upon%20such%20claims)). This is one of the most typical small claims scenarios. For example, if you hired a contractor under a written agreement to renovate your kitchen, paid a $5,000 deposit, and the contractor never performed the work, you could sue for the $5,000 plus any additional costs incurred. Or if you sold goods to someone under a written contract and they didn’t pay, that’s eligible. **Evidence tip:** bring a copy of the signed contract, any relevant emails or texts, and proof of the breach (e.g. invoices, photos of incomplete work). Massachusetts imposes a **6-year statute of limitations** on contract actions (whether written or oral) in most cases ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=Massachusetts%20laws)), so ensure the breach occurred within the last 6 years when filing (see Section 5 on limitations). 

**Breach of oral/handshake agreement:** Yes – oral contracts are enforceable in Massachusetts (except in certain cases under the Statute of Frauds), and you can sue on an oral agreement up to $7,000 in small claims. Example: You agreed verbally to lend your friend your used car in exchange for $500, but they never paid – you could sue for $500 based on the oral contract. The challenge with oral contracts is proving the terms; be prepared with any witnesses or written corroboration (texts, emails referencing the deal, etc.). The same 6-year statute of limitations applies from the date of breach for oral contracts ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=Massachusetts%20laws)). 

**Unpaid personal loans (friends/family IOUs):** Yes – if you lent someone money informally and they didn’t repay, that’s essentially a contract claim (loan agreement). It doesn’t matter if the loan was documented by a promissory note or was just a personal IOU; both are enforceable if you can prove the loan. Bring any evidence of the loan (cancelled check, Venmo records, a text where they acknowledge the debt, etc.). Personal loans often have no written contract, but Massachusetts small claims courts regularly handle these disputes. Ensure you file within 6 years of the date the loan was due to be repaid ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=Massachusetts%20laws)). Also, **calculate interest** if you had an interest agreement – Massachusetts allows pre-judgment interest on contract claims at 12% per year unless a different rate was agreed ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=plaintiff%20shall%20state%20specifically%20any,procedural%20information%20to%20small%20claims)) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleII/Chapter231/Section6c#:~:text=Section%206C,order%20for%20judgment%20against%20the)), but in personal loan cases, interest is typically awarded only if the original deal included it.

**Unpaid promissory notes:** Yes – a promissory note is a written promise to pay, essentially a written contract. If someone signed a promissory note to repay a loan or debt to you and defaulted, you can sue up to $7,000 on that note (plus allowable interest). The note itself is key evidence. Massachusetts law treats a promissory note breach like any contract breach (6-year limit from the due date or last payment). If the note has an interest rate, the court will usually honor that; otherwise statutory interest (12% per annum) may apply from date of breach ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleII/Chapter231/Section6c#:~:text=Section%206C,order%20for%20judgment%20against%20the)).

**Unpaid invoices or accounts receivable (business debts):** Yes – a business can use small claims to collect on unpaid invoices for goods or services, as long as the claim is within $7,000. Common examples: a freelancer wasn’t paid for work done, a supplier seeking payment for delivered products, or a small business seeking to collect an outstanding customer balance. Massachusetts requires **special filing steps for claims “incurred in the course of trade or commerce,”** which covers most business-issued debts (see Section 13: businesses must include extra information about the original creditor and verify the defendant’s address) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=,commerce%20or%20pursuing%20assigned%20debt)) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=Any%20plaintiff%20pursuing%20a%20claim,be%20served%20on%20the%20defendant)). But the claim itself – breach of contract or account stated – is absolutely allowed. **Note:** If you’re a business suing a consumer for a debt, ensure compliance with any required **30-day demand letter** under the Consumer Protection Act if applicable (e.g. if you plan to seek attorney’s fees or multiple damages under c.93A for a bad check or deceptive conduct by the debtor – more on that below).

**Unpaid rent (residential or commercial):** Yes – a landlord can sue a tenant (or former tenant) in small claims for up to $7,000 of unpaid rent. This is a straightforward contract claim on the lease or rental agreement. For residential tenancies, these cases are often handled in the Housing Court (which has small claims sessions) or District Court. *Example:* A tenant moved out owing 2 months’ rent at \$1,200/month = \$2,400; the landlord can file a small claim for \$2,400 in unpaid rent. Landlords should be aware of the security deposit implications – if the tenant’s security deposit was not properly handled or returned, the tenant may counterclaim for penalties (see “Unreturned security deposits” below). For **commercial rent**, it’s the same idea: if a business tenant failed to pay rent and the amount is under $7,000, small claims works. (If over $7,000, the landlord would need to sue in a higher court or waive the excess.) The statute of limitations on rent claims is generally 6 years (written or oral lease) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=Massachusetts%20laws)). Note that **eviction (possession)** cases cannot be done in small claims – if you seek to evict a tenant, that’s a separate Summary Process action in Housing or District Court, not a small claim. But you can sue for the money owed (and you could do so either during or after an eviction case). 

**Bounced or dishonored checks:** Yes – if someone paid you with a check that **bounced** (was returned for insufficient funds or a closed account), you can sue for the amount of the check **and** Massachusetts law entitles you to additional damages. Under **M.G.L. c.93 §40A**, if a check bounces and the maker doesn’t pay up within 30 days after a written demand, the payee is entitled to at least **$100** and up to **$500** in statutory damages **on top of** the check amount ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXV/Chapter93/Section40a#:~:text=Section%2040A,damages%2C%20as%20determined%20by%20the)) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXV/Chapter93/Section40a#:~:text=who%20fails%20to%20pay%20the,more%20than%20five%20hundred%20dollars)). To get these penalties, you must send a **demand letter by certified mail** giving the check writer 30 days to pay the face amount ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXV/Chapter93/Section40a#:~:text=Section%2040A,criminal%20penalties%20which%20may%20be)). The statute even provides specific wording for the “Demand for Payment of Dishonored Check” notice ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXV/Chapter93/Section40a#:~:text=who%20fails%20to%20pay%20the,more%20than%20five%20hundred%20dollars)) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXV/Chapter93/Section40a#:~:text=following%20form%20and%20shall%20be,the%20English%20and%20Spanish%20languages)). If they still don’t pay, you can sue in small claims for the value of the check plus the statutory damages (minimum $100, max $500 per check) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXV/Chapter93/Section40a#:~:text=who%20fails%20to%20pay%20the,more%20than%20five%20hundred%20dollars)). *Example:* John writes you a $300 rent check that bounces. You send the statutory demand letter. He doesn’t pay. You can sue for $300 + (at least) $100 = $400, and the court could award anywhere from $400 to $800 total (since the law allows up to $500 extra) depending on circumstances. Be sure to bring **proof of the demand letter** (copy of the letter and certified mail receipt) to claim the penalty. (Note: You cannot recover more than $500 extra per bad check by statute ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXV/Chapter93/Section40a#:~:text=imposed%20in%20accordance%20with%20law%2C,more%20than%20five%20hundred%20dollars)), so even if the $7,000 cap isn’t an issue, the penalty tops out at $500 per instrument.)

**Open-book accounts & revolving credit (store accounts, tabs):** Yes – an “open book account” is essentially an ongoing balance where a customer makes multiple purchases or is billed periodically. These are treated as enforceable contracts. For example, if a customer ran up a $3,000 tab with your business over time and hasn’t paid, you can sue for the balance. Itemize the charges and credits to prove the amount due. Massachusetts law doesn’t have a special category for open accounts; they fall under contract. The 6-year limit usually runs from the **date of the last charge or payment** on the account, since that’s typically when the breach (failure to pay) solidifies ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=Massachusetts%20laws)). 

**Co-signer/guarantor disputes:** Yes – if you co-signed a loan or guaranteed someone’s debt and ended up having to pay the lender, you can sue the primary debtor for what you paid (this is essentially a contract of indemnity or reimbursement). For instance, you co-signed a friend’s car loan; the friend defaulted and you paid $5,000 to the bank – you now have a claim against your friend for $5,000. This can be framed as either an **equitable indemnity** claim or enforcing the implied promise that the principal would bear the debt. Either way, it’s allowable in small claims. Provide evidence of the co-signer agreement and proof you paid the debt. Likewise, if you guaranteed a commercial lease and had to pay the landlord, you can small-claim the tenant for what you paid (again up to $7,000). Ensure no more than 6 years have passed since you paid and the debt became due to you ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=Massachusetts%20laws)).

### Consumer disputes  
**Refunds for defective products:** Yes – as a consumer, if you bought a product that turned out to be defective or not as advertised, you can sue the seller for your money back (and possibly additional damages). This could be a breach of warranty or contract claim. Massachusetts law gives consumers an **implied warranty** that new and used goods sold for personal use must be fit for ordinary use (see M.G.L. c.106 §2-314, the U.C.C. warranty of merchantability). If a seller refuses to refund or replace a defective item, a small claim can be used to recover the purchase price and any consequential losses (within the $7,000 cap). *Examples:* a laptop that dies two weeks after purchase, a used car that immediately fails inspection (see Lemon Law below), or furniture that falls apart – in all cases you’d first demand a refund or repair, and if refused, sue. The statute of limitations for sale of goods under the U.C.C. is **4 years** from sale ([caretaker.com](https://caretaker.com/learn/habitability/repair-and-deduct-laws-in-massachusetts#:~:text=Massachusetts%20law%20specifically%20allows%20tenants,they%20can%20spend%20on%20repairs)), so be mindful of that slightly shorter period for product issues (other consumer claims might fall under the 4-year UCC or the general 3- or 6-year limits depending on the theory). In your suit, you might also invoke **Chapter 93A, the Consumer Protection Act**, if the seller’s refusal was an unfair or deceptive practice – doing so could potentially entitle you to **double or treble damages and attorney fees** ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-small-claims-and-bringing-a-claim-to-court#:~:text=for%20statutory%20damages%20or%20attorney%E2%80%99s,exceeding%20%247%2C000%20may%20be%20possible)), but note that you **must send a 30-day demand letter** to the business before suing under c.93A ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/30-day-demand-letter#:~:text=Unfair%20or%20deceptive%20practices%20by,triple%20damages%20and%20attorney%27s%20fees)). Include evidence like receipts, photos of the defect, and any expert statements if available.

**Refunds for services not performed or done poorly:** Yes – if you paid for a service that was not provided, or was performed in a shoddy manner far below expected quality, you can sue for the money you paid (and possibly additional costs to fix the issue). This is essentially a breach of contract or failure of consideration. Common examples: a photographer no-shows at your event that you paid them for, a caterer you deposited money with cancels and won’t refund, or a home contractor does substandard work. All of these are eligible small claims cases. Bring the service contract (if any), proof of payment, and evidence of the lack of performance or poor performance (photos of poor workmanship, etc.). If a service was done poorly, you might claim the cost to have it redone correctly by someone else. Massachusetts allows such consequential damages, though remember the total claim still capped at $7k. If the service provider’s conduct was deceptive or egregious (for example, a contractor took your money knowing he wouldn’t do the work), you might include a **c.93A claim** for unfair practice – again, that requires sending a 30-day demand letter first ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/30-day-demand-letter#:~:text=Unfair%20or%20deceptive%20practices%20by,triple%20damages%20and%20attorney%27s%20fees)). Under c.93A, the court could award **double or triple damages** and legal fees if the conduct was willful or if a reasonable settlement offer wasn’t made ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-small-claims-and-bringing-a-claim-to-court#:~:text=for%20statutory%20damages%20or%20attorney%E2%80%99s,exceeding%20%247%2C000%20may%20be%20possible)).

**Auto repair overcharges or shoddy work:** Yes – disputes with mechanics and auto repair shops are a staple of small claims. If a mechanic charged for repairs not actually performed, or performed substandard work that caused you loss, you can sue for the overcharge or the cost of correcting the work. Massachusetts regulations (940 CMR 5.00, the Auto Repair Regulations under c.93A) require repair shops to provide written estimates and get authorization for extra work. If a shop violated these rules (e.g. charging far above estimate without consent, or doing unnecessary repairs), that can be deemed an “unfair or deceptive act” under consumer law. You could sue not only for the dollar difference or damages, but potentially under c.93A for multiple damages. *Example:* A shop charges you $800 for replacing parts but you later find those parts were never replaced – you could sue for the $800 and perhaps argue for additional punitive damages. Always document what happened: save the invoice, any written estimate, and get a second mechanic’s statement if possible to prove the work was not done or done improperly. There’s a 4-year statute of limitations for c.93A consumer actions ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=MGL%20c,or%20held%20by%20the%20defendant)) and for U.C.C. warranty on services it’s usually 6 years if based on contract. Most such cases will be within a year or two of the repair anyway.

**Home improvement/contractor disputes (incomplete or defective work):** Yes – if you hired a home improvement contractor (for remodeling, repairs, etc.) and they either *failed to complete the work* or did it so defectively that it has to be redone, you can sue for the money you’ve lost. This could include any deposit you paid for work never performed, or the cost to repair the contractor’s poor work. Massachusetts has a Home Improvement Contractor Act (M.G.L. c.142A) which, among other things, requires contractors to be registered and can provide a **Guaranty Fund** recovery up to $25,000 for consumers who obtain a judgment and can’t collect from the contractor ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/file-a-guaranty-fund-claim-for-an-unpaid-judgment-against-a-contractor#:~:text=,arbitration%20award%20or%20judgment%20issued)) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/file-a-guaranty-fund-claim-for-an-unpaid-judgment-against-a-contractor#:~:text=If%20you%20win%20your%20case,25%2C000%20of%20your%20actual%20losses)). In small claims, you can pursue the contractor directly for up to $7,000. Example: You paid a contractor $5,000 to build a deck, but they only did half the work and vanished – you could sue for the $5,000 plus perhaps the extra cost if you had to hire someone else (though any amount above $7k can’t be awarded in small claims, you might waive it or just claim $7k). If the contractor was **unregistered** or violated certain regulations (like not including certain contract terms), that might be a per se violation of c.93A, entitling you to multiple damages and fees – again, you’d need to send the demand letter first in that scenario ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/30-day-demand-letter#:~:text=Unfair%20or%20deceptive%20practices%20by,triple%20damages%20and%20attorney%27s%20fees)). **Special note:** If you do get a small claims judgment against a home improvement contractor who then doesn’t pay, you have up to 7 years from the contract date to apply to the state’s Home Improvement Guaranty Fund for payment (up to $25,000 of your actual loss) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/file-a-guaranty-fund-claim-for-an-unpaid-judgment-against-a-contractor#:~:text=If%20you%20win%20your%20case,25%2C000%20of%20your%20actual%20losses)). That fund is a backup source but requires you to have a court judgment first.

**Appliance/electronics warranty issues:** Yes – if a significant appliance or electronic item fails and the seller or manufacturer won’t honor a warranty, you can pursue a claim for repair costs or a refund. Massachusetts implies a warranty of merchantability on consumer products and also has a **Used Vehicle Warranty Law** (for used cars – see Lemon Law below). For appliances, usually you’ll be dealing with either a store that sold it or the manufacturer’s warranty. If a fridge dies in warranty and the company fails to fix or replace, you can sue for the cost of a new one or the refund, etc. These are contract/warranty claims and fit in small claims if under $7k. Include proof of purchase and any warranty terms. If it’s a **service contract** you bought (extended warranty through a third party) and they refuse coverage, that’s also actionable. Use c.93A if the warranty denial seems in bad faith — as always, give the 30-day demand notice if you intend to claim under c.93A.

**No-show or canceled services (event vendors, etc.):** Yes – if you paid someone to provide services at a specific time (e.g. a wedding photographer, DJ, caterer, limo, or event venue) and they canceled or failed to show/performed nothing, you can sue to get your money back and any related losses. These are straightforward contract breaches. Example: A DJ was prepaid $1,000 for a wedding and never arrived – you’d sue for the $1,000 plus any additional expense you incurred to find last-minute replacement (if within the cap). Or if you reserved a banquet hall with a deposit and they double-booked and denied you the space, you’d claim the deposit and perhaps costs of finding a new venue. Such scenarios might also implicate c.93A if the conduct was willfully unfair (like the vendor knew they couldn’t fulfill but took your money). Always attempt to mitigate damages (e.g. find an alternate provider) and document everything.

**Dry cleaning or property loss by a bailee:** Yes – if a business that took possession of your property for service (dry cleaner, laundry, valet parking, storage facility, repair shop, etc.) loses or damages your property, you can sue for the value of the item or cost of repair. This can be framed as negligence or breach of bailment contract. Example: a dry cleaner ruined your dress; you’d sue for the dress’s value or cleaning cost. Or a parking valet crashed your car – you can claim the repair cost (car damage from a valet is property damage, so note if it involves a vehicle, the $7k limit wouldn’t apply due to the motor vehicle exception ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=slander%20and%20libel%2C%20in%20which,of%20judgments%20upon%20such%20claims)) – but typically valet damages are within that anyway). Bring any tickets or receipts that show you entrusted the item, and evidence of value (receipts or market value for a lost/damaged item). These cases often come down to valuations, so be prepared to justify the amount you demand (original purchase price, etc., maybe depreciated value if older).

**Gym memberships, subscriptions, memberships (cancellation/refund disputes):** Yes – if you canceled a gym or club membership according to the contract but the company keeps billing you (or refuses to refund a sign-up fee), you can use small claims. Massachusetts has specific consumer protections for gym contracts (M.G.L. c.93, § 82 and 940 CMR 5.00) that for instance allow cancellation under certain conditions. Similarly, subscriptions or “automatic renewal” services that don’t honor cancellations can be liable. Claim the amounts wrongfully charged after cancellation, or any refund due for prepaid months. Keep records of your cancellation notice (letters, emails) and the company’s response or lack thereof. These can also be pursued under c.93A as unfair business practices (especially if it’s a systemic issue – Massachusetts law lets consumers recover at least $25 in damages under c.93A even for small losses, and multiple damages for willful violations) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-small-claims-and-bringing-a-claim-to-court#:~:text=for%20statutory%20damages%20or%20attorney%E2%80%99s,exceeding%20%247%2C000%20may%20be%20possible)).

**Auto dealer/used-car disputes (lemon law, odometer fraud, etc.):** Yes – Massachusetts has a **New Car Lemon Law** (M.G.L. c.90 §7N½) and a **Used Car Warranty Law** (M.G.L. c.90 §7N¼). Under the lemon law, if a new car has serious defects and the manufacturer fails to repair after a reasonable number of attempts, you could be entitled to a refund or replacement – the lemon law process often goes through arbitration, but you *could* sue in court if needed. However, many lemon-law refunds exceed $7,000, so small claims would only be suitable for partial issues or very low-value vehicles. The **Used Vehicle Warranty Law** in MA covers used cars sold by dealers for personal use (not private sales) if the purchase price was at least $700 – it mandates a warranty period (30–90 days depending on mileage) during which the dealer must repair any defect impairing use or safety, or if they can’t, to cancel the sale and refund the buyer ([www.stateregstoday.com](https://www.stateregstoday.com/living/consumer-protection/auto-repair-and-service-regulations-in-massachusetts#:~:text=Auto%20Repair%20and%20Service%20Regulations,by%20the%20Office%20of%20Consumer)). If a dealer refuses to honor that, you can sue for your money back. Small claims is a common venue for used-car disputes because the purchase price of a used car is often within the limit. *Example:* You bought a used car for $5,500 and it broke down in 2 weeks with major issues; the dealer won’t fix it – you could demand rescission (return the car, get $5,500 back). Also, **odometer fraud** (rolling back mileage) or misrepresentation of a vehicle’s condition can be a basis for a 93A claim with possible treble damages. Federal odometer law (49 U.S.C. §32710) even provides for treble damages or $10,000, but you’d be limited by the $7k cap in small claims or would file in a higher court for full remedies. For small claims, stick to the refund or actual loss amount. Don’t forget to send a 93A demand letter to the dealer at least 30 days before suing for any deceptive practices ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/30-day-demand-letter#:~:text=Unfair%20or%20deceptive%20practices%20by,triple%20damages%20and%20attorney%27s%20fees)).

**False advertising or other deceptive trade practices:** Yes – if you were induced to purchase something by false advertising or if a business engaged in an unfair or deceptive act that cost you money, you can sue under the Massachusetts Consumer Protection Act (c.93A). Common scenarios: a business guaranteed a result it didn’t deliver, advertised a product as new when it was refurbished, or hid important terms of a sale. In small claims, you would claim your actual damages (the money you lost). If you prevail on a c.93A claim, the court **must** award you your attorney’s fees (if any) and may award **double or triple damages** if the conduct was willful or the business refused a reasonable settlement offer after your demand letter ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-small-claims-and-bringing-a-claim-to-court#:~:text=for%20statutory%20damages%20or%20attorney%E2%80%99s,exceeding%20%247%2C000%20may%20be%20possible)). **Important:** To pursue a c.93A claim, you need to send the business a **30-Day Demand Letter** at least 30 days before filing, stating the facts, your loss, and what relief you request ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/30-day-demand-letter#:~:text=Unfair%20or%20deceptive%20practices%20by,triple%20damages%20and%20attorney%27s%20fees)). This gives the business a chance to offer a settlement. If you skip the demand letter, the court may not allow the 93A damages (with some exceptions, e.g. if the business is out of state, or if it’s a counterclaim) – so it’s a critical step. For example, if a contractor’s ad claimed to be licensed and he wasn’t (false advertising), causing you to hire him and suffer a loss, you could claim whatever you paid him plus perhaps additional costs under c.93A. Provide a copy of the misleading advertisement or any proof of the deceptive representation.

### Property damage  
**Vehicle collisions (property damage only):** Yes – claims for damage to your vehicle (or other property) from a collision are absolutely allowed, and remember **there is no dollar limit** for property damage from a motor vehicle in Massachusetts small claims ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=slander%20and%20libel%2C%20in%20which,of%20judgments%20upon%20such%20claims)) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=under%20this%20section%20if%20the,the%20amount%20of%20the%20claims)). This means even if your car sustained $10,000 in damage, you can still choose small claims court to sue the at-fault driver (or owner). In practice, many car accident property claims fall in the small claims arena especially if insurance refused to pay or you’re seeking an uninsured amount like your deductible. *Example:* Someone hit your parked car causing $4,500 damage not fully covered by insurance – you can sue the driver for the $500 deductible you had to pay. Or if the other driver was uninsured altogether, you could sue for the full repair cost. Bring repair estimates, invoices, and photos of the damage. If liability is contested, bring any witness statements, police report, etc. Note: Personal injury from a car accident is **not** within the motor-vehicle exception, only property damage is. Injury claims are torts subject to the $7,000 cap and are usually filed in regular court due to higher typical values.

**Damage to personal property by another (non-vehicle):** Yes – if someone else (or their child, or their pet, etc.) damaged your belongings, you can sue for the cost of repair or replacement. This is a tort claim (negligence or even intentional damage). For example, a neighbor borrowing your lawnmower wrecked it, or a guest spilled red wine on your expensive rug causing $1,000 in cleaning/replacement costs – those can be brought in small claims. Provide proof of the item’s value and the cost to fix or replace. If it was intentional damage (vandalism), you could still sue in small claims for the monetary loss, though note that if it was willful or malicious, sometimes treble damages are available by statute in higher courts (e.g. M.G.L. c.231 §85J provides *treble damages* for someone who by “fraud or deceit” sells you defective personal property ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/massachusetts/part-iii/title-ii/chapter-231/section-85j/#:~:text=Section%2085J,of%20damages%20sustained%20by%20him)), but for straightforward property damage by negligence you get actual damages). In small claims, you’re basically looking at actual compensatory damages plus perhaps statutory interest from date of damage (in tort cases, pre-judgment interest at 12% runs from the date you filed the case ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=the%20total%20amount%20being%20sought%2C,procedural%20information%20to%20small%20claims))).

**Damage by a tenant to a landlord’s property:** Yes – a landlord can sue a former tenant (or a tenant’s guest) for damage beyond normal wear and tear that the tenant caused to the apartment or house. Usually, the landlord might withhold the security deposit to cover it, but if damages exceed the deposit, or if no deposit was held, a small claim can be filed. For example, if a tenant caused $3,000 of deliberate damage (holes in walls, broken windows), and the security deposit was $1,000, the landlord might keep the deposit and sue for the remaining $2,000. Landlords should carefully document the damages (photos, repair bills). Note: **Avoid claiming “reasonable wear and tear”** items – Mass. law (and a recent SJC case in 2025) makes clear a landlord cannot charge a tenant for routine wear ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-tenants-security-deposits#:~:text=Castenholz%20v,interest%2C%20costs%2C%20and%20attorney%E2%80%99s%20fees)) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-tenants-security-deposits#:~:text=Peebles%20v,specific)). Only actual excessive damage is recoverable. Also, if you retained a security deposit, be sure you complied with M.G.L. c.186 §15B (proper itemized list of damages within 30 days, etc.), or else the tenant might counterclaim for penalties (see security deposits below). 

**Damage by landlord to a tenant’s property:** Yes – if a landlord (or their contractor) damaged or wrongfully withheld a tenant’s personal property, the tenant can sue. This might occur during renovation, negligent maintenance (a roof leak from landlord’s failure to fix damages your furniture), or if a landlord improperly **disposes of a tenant’s belongings** after an eviction or move-out. Massachusetts law (M.G.L. c.186 §14) prohibits a landlord from willfully or negligently destroying a tenant’s property; doing so can even be a defense to rent or a separate claim. In small claims, a tenant would claim the value of items destroyed. Example: Your landlord’s repair crew broke your TV while fixing an electrical issue – you could sue for the TV’s value. Or if a landlord locked you out and threw away your possessions (an illegal “self-help eviction”), you can sue for the value and that act would also violate c.93A and possibly entitle you to multiple damages and attorney fees. Document the items and their value (photos, receipts, etc.). These cases can overlap with housing court issues, but monetary compensation can be obtained via small claims.

**Tree/fence/boundary disputes causing damage:** Yes – property damage arising from neighbor disputes or encroachments can be brought in small claims if the damages are within the limit. For example, if a neighbor’s tree fell onto your property and damaged your fence or shed (assuming it was due to negligence or known rot – purely “Act of God” tree falls might not result in neighbor liability unless they knew the tree was hazardous). Or if a neighbor building a fence accidentally dug up your sprinkler system causing $2,000 damage, you could claim that cost. Small claims can’t resolve *title* or boundary questions (who owns the tree or where the line is – those are equity issues for higher courts), but it can award money for the damage. So ensure your claim is framed as a damage claim, not asking the court to decide the exact boundary. Bring evidence like survey maps (to show whose tree it was if relevant), photos of the damage, and repair estimates.

**Pet damage (dog bite to property, etc.):** Yes – if someone’s pet or animal caused property damage or minor injury, you can sue the owner. For property: maybe the neighbor’s dog chewed up your expensive garden hose or scratched your car, or their pet peacock pecked apart your lawn furniture (hey, it could happen). These are property damage claims. If it’s a **dog bite causing personal injury**, Massachusetts has a strict liability dog bite statute (M.G.L. c.140 §155) making owners liable for bites, but those cases often exceed $7,000 in medical costs; however, if it was a minor bite with small medical bills, a small claim is possible (see “personal injury” section below). For property damage by pets, claim the repair or replacement costs. E.g. veterinarian bills for your pet hurt by their pet could also be claimed as property damage (since legally pets are property, though precious property). Keep receipts for any repairs or vet bills.

**Damage during a move (movers or friends helping):** Yes – if you hired movers and they damaged your furniture or home, you can sue for the damage. Many moving companies have contracts that limit liability (often by weight, e.g. 60 cents per pound) – but those limitations might not be enforceable if not disclosed or if this wasn’t an interstate move governed by federal law. In small claims you can certainly attempt to recover the actual damage value. If friends helped you move and accidentally smashed your TV, you could also file a claim (though you might weigh the relationship strain!). These are negligence claims. Present evidence of the item’s value and extent of damage (photos, repair quotes). For hired movers, also bring the contract terms if any; the judge will decide if any liability cap applies. 

### Landlord–tenant (financial claims only – not eviction)  
Small claims is commonly used by tenants and landlords for **money disputes** at the end of a tenancy. Massachusetts tenants, in particular, often use small claims to recover improperly withheld security deposits or last month’s rent. **Eviction (possession)** cases cannot be heard in small claims (they go to the summary process session in Housing or District Court), but any **monetary aspect** of a tenancy can be. Here are typical claims:

**Unreturned security deposits:** Yes – Massachusetts law on security deposits is very strict. Under **M.G.L. c.186 §15B**, a residential landlord who takes a security deposit must follow detailed rules (holding it in a separate account, giving a receipt and Statement of Condition, only using it for certain damages or unpaid rent, and providing an itemized list of damages within 30 days of tenancy ending) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-tenants-security-deposits#:~:text=Laws)) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-tenants-security-deposits#:~:text=Castenholz%20v,interest%2C%20costs%2C%20and%20attorney%E2%80%99s%20fees)). If the landlord fails to refund the remaining deposit or violates the statute, the tenant can not only claim the deposit but may be entitled to **treble damages** plus court costs and attorneys’ fees ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-tenants-security-deposits#:~:text=Castenholz%20v,interest%2C%20costs%2C%20and%20attorney%E2%80%99s%20fees)). In fact, the Massachusetts Appeals Court and SJC have held that certain violations (like not placing the deposit in an escrow account, or not returning it when required) result in automatic triple damages in the tenant’s favor ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-tenants-security-deposits#:~:text=Castenholz%20v,interest%2C%20costs%2C%20and%20attorney%E2%80%99s%20fees)) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-tenants-security-deposits#:~:text=If%20a%20landlord%20does%20not,interest%2C%20costs%2C%20and%20attorney%E2%80%99s%20fees)). For instance, if your $2,000 security deposit was not returned within 30 days and the landlord had not sent a proper itemized damage list, you can sue for **$6,000** (three times the deposit) plus interest and costs ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-tenants-security-deposits#:~:text=Castenholz%20v,interest%2C%20costs%2C%20and%20attorney%E2%80%99s%20fees)). Small claims **does** have jurisdiction for these claims – the law says the $7,000 cap does not limit the court’s ability to award double or treble damages that statutes require ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=under%20this%20section%20if%20the,any%20general%20or%20special%20law)). (However, note that trebling a $2,000 deposit gives $6,000, still under $7k; if trebled deposit would exceed $7k, the case can still be brought as long as the base deposit is $7k or less, which it will be because collecting more than $7k security is not allowed anyway.) Many security deposit cases in MA small claims result in **automatic judgment for tenant** because landlords often slip up on the procedural requirements. If you’re a tenant bringing such a case, cite the specific violations of c.186 §15B (e.g., “landlord failed to provide a statement of damages and receipts within 30 days, therefore forfeiting any claim on the deposit”). Be aware that if the landlord **did** comply and had legitimate damages, the court might credit those – but if the landlord violated any part of the law, judges often strictly enforce penalties. There is a **3-year statute of limitations** for claims under the security deposit statute (as a subset of contract/consumer protection laws) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-tenants-security-deposits#:~:text=Castenholz%20v,interest%2C%20costs%2C%20and%20attorney%E2%80%99s%20fees)) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=MGL%20c,or%20held%20by%20the%20defendant)) – so don’t wait too long.

**Last month’s rent and interest:** Yes – if you paid last month’s rent in advance and didn’t end up using it (say you left early or properly gave notice and the landlord double-charged you), you can claim a refund. Also, Massachusetts law requires landlords to pay **interest** annually on last-month’s rent and security deposits (5% or the bank rate) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/Partii/Titlei/Chapter186/Section15b#:~:text=,2%29%3B%20and)). If the landlord fails to either apply the last month’s rent to rent or return it when appropriate, that’s a valid claim. Also, if they never paid out the accrued interest on the last-month rent each year or at move-out, the tenant can sue for that interest (and if the tenant demands the interest in writing and the landlord doesn’t pay within 30 days, the tenant can get **triple the interest** as penalty ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-tenants-security-deposits#:~:text=MGL%20c,may%20be%20awarded%20triple%20damages)) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/Partii/Titlei/Chapter186/Section15b#:~:text=amount%20equal%20to%20three%20times,costs%20and%20reasonable%20attorneys%20fees)) – though the dollar amounts for interest are usually small). For example, on a $1,500 last month’s rent held for 2 years, interest might be around $150 – not huge, but it’s the principle! In small claims you could combine that with other claims.

**Habitability and “repair and deduct” counterclaims:** Yes – while issues like breaches of the warranty of habitability often come up as **defenses** in eviction cases, a tenant can affirmatively sue a landlord for money damages if the landlord’s failure to maintain the property caused losses. One common scenario: you had no heat or serious leaks, you notified the landlord, nothing was done, so you paid out-of-pocket to fix something or you suffered monetary loss (like higher utility bills due to a bad furnace). Massachusetts law allows tenants to **repair and deduct** in certain serious situations – per **M.G.L. c.111 §127L**, if code violations endangering health or safety aren’t fixed within 5 days of notice, tenant can arrange repairs and deduct up to 4 months’ rent to pay for them ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXVI/Chapter111/Section127L#:~:text=inspection%2C%20or%20by%20a%20court,tenants%20may%20subsequently%20deduct%20from)) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXVI/Chapter111/Section127L#:~:text=A%20tenant%20may%20not%20deduct,total%20of%20four%20months%27%20rent)). If you went this route and the landlord later tries to collect that withheld rent, you could use small claims to affirm your right to deduct or even proactively sue if, say, you had to move out and want compensation for unsafe conditions. More straightforward is suing for a **rent abatement**: if conditions were bad (vermin, no hot water, etc.), the apartment’s rental value was reduced, and you can claim a reimbursement of part of rent paid. Housing Court is often the venue for these, but nothing stops a tenant from suing in small claims for, e.g., “rent abatement of $1,000 due to landlord’s violation of the State Sanitary Code.” You should document the conditions (inspection reports from the Board of Health are powerful evidence) and the timeline of notices. Note: If you withheld rent without following the proper procedure, the landlord might counterclaim for that rent – habitability issues can then be your defense. It can get complex, so these cases sometimes end up consolidated with an eviction case. But purely monetary claims like “I paid for an exterminator because landlord wouldn’t, and I want reimbursement” can be perfect for small claims. 

**Damage claims by landlords (beyond normal wear):** Yes – as mentioned, a landlord can sue a departed tenant for property damage, unpaid utility bills the tenant was obliged to pay, or other costs due under the lease (cleaning fees if legitimately owed, etc.). These are contractual damages. However, Massachusetts law forbids deducting from a security deposit for anything other than unpaid rent, damage beyond reasonable wear, or unpaid real estate taxes (if applicable) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/Partii/Titlei/Chapter186/Section15b#:~:text=,2%29%3B%20and)). So a landlord cannot, for instance, deduct from deposit or sue for **routine cleaning** or repainting. Nor can they fine the tenant via deposit for breaking a lease (that would be a separate claim for lost rent, not a deposit item). Small claims is suitable if the amount is modest. For example, tenant broke a $300 window and left owing $500 water bill – a landlord could sue for $800. If the tenant disputes the damage, the landlord should bring photos, move-in/move-out inspection checklists, and receipts for repairs. Remember, if the landlord failed to follow the security deposit law, the tenant might turn the tables with a bigger counterclaim (treble deposit), so landlords must approach these carefully (see “pitfalls” section).

**Wrongful retention of tenant’s personal property:** Yes – if a landlord holds a tenant’s possessions after move-out (for example, the tenant left some items and the landlord refuses to return them), the tenant can sue for the value of the items. There’s a statute about property left in a rental: landlords have to store and **inventory** belongings left behind and make reasonable efforts to contact the tenant, and ultimately they can dispose of them following a certain process (M.G.L. c.239, §4). If the landlord doesn’t follow that and effectively converts the tenant’s property, that’s actionable. Small claims can award the fair market value of the items. The tenant should be prepared to prove value (original cost, etc., possibly depreciated). Likewise, if a landlord *legally* stored items and wants reimbursement for storage costs, they could counterclaim that – but in most cases, it’s the tenant seeking their stuff or its value. Massachusetts also treats an illegal lockout (where landlord throws tenant’s stuff out) as a c.93A violation possibly, so multiple damages could come into play if you frame it that way (with the demand letter step). But often it’s simply about recovering the dollar value of missing belongings.

**Fee disputes (late fees, cleaning fees, lock replacement, etc.):** Yes – a tenant can contest fees the landlord charged that were not lawful or reasonable, and a landlord can claim any legitimate fees owed under the lease. Massachusetts has some specific rules: e.g., late fees cannot be charged in a residential lease until rent is 30 days overdue (so a “late fee after 5 days” clause is invalid) – a tenant charged such a fee could sue to get it back under c.93A (viewed as an unfair practice) or as a straightforward money had and received. Also, some leases impose carpet cleaning or re-keying fees automatically – those may not be enforceable unless actual damage beyond wear. In small claims, a tenant might sue for return of a $200 “professionally clean the unit” fee that was deducted despite leaving the place clean. Conversely, if a tenant truly lost keys and agreed to pay for replacements, a landlord could claim that maybe from the deposit; if not covered, they can small-claim it. Typically these amounts are small and well-suited for small claims. Just be sure any fee you claim or contest has a basis in the lease or law.

### Employment & wages  
Small claims court can be a helpful venue for employees seeking relatively small amounts of unpaid wages, though Massachusetts wage laws provide strong remedies often pursued through the Attorney General or superior court. Key points:

**Unpaid final paycheck or earned wages:** Yes – if your employer didn’t pay your last paycheck, or withheld earned wages or accrued vacation pay, you can sue for those amounts in small claims (up to $7,000). **Massachusetts Wage Act (M.G.L. c.149 §§148, 150)** is very employee-friendly: any employer who fails to pay wages (including vacation pay due at termination) on time is liable for **mandatory treble damages** plus the employee’s attorneys’ fees ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter149/Section150#:~:text=An%20employee%20claiming%20to%20be,employee%20files%20a%20complaint%20with)) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter149/Section150#:~:text=final,litigation%20and%20reasonable%20attorneys%27%20fees)). The Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court ruled in 2022 that even a short delay in paying final wages triggers this triple-damages penalty – no excuses ([www.seyfarth.com](https://www.seyfarth.com/news-insights/no-quarter-sjc-mandates-treble-damages-for-any-late-payment-of-wages-even-honest-corrected-mistakes.html#:~:text=No%20Quarter%3A%20SJC%20Mandates%20Treble,Califano%20Click%20to%20share)). In practice, to sue for wages in court, Massachusetts law requires you (the employee) to first file a complaint with the **Mass. Attorney General’s Office** (Fair Labor Division) or obtain their **permission (a “private right of action” letter)** ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter149/Section150#:~:text=An%20employee%20claiming%20to%20be,employee%20files%20a%20complaint%20with)). If the AG doesn’t promptly take action (90 days) or gives you a right-to-sue letter, you can proceed in court ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter149/Section150#:~:text=An%20employee%20claiming%20to%20be,employee%20files%20a%20complaint%20with)). This applies even in small claims. So, an employee might file a wage complaint form with the AG, then after 90 days (or sooner if the AG issues a letter) file a small claim. You’d cite M.G.L. c.149 §150, which entitles you to **triple the unpaid wages** as liquidated damages, plus your filing fee and costs ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter149/Section150#:~:text=final,litigation%20and%20reasonable%20attorneys%27%20fees)). For example, if you are owed $500 in wages, the court must award $1,500, not just $500 ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter149/Section150#:~:text=final,litigation%20and%20reasonable%20attorneys%27%20fees)). Keep in mind $1,500 is still under $7k, but even if triple damages pushes it over $7k, you might still be okay because the **initial claim** (the wages owed) was under $7k ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=under%20this%20section%20if%20the,any%20general%20or%20special%20law)). Bring evidence of hours worked and unpaid (timesheets, pay stubs showing non-payment, etc.). Note: Some wage claims, especially if larger or if you have legal counsel, end up in Superior Court because attorneys’ fees can also be recovered and procedural tools like discovery are needed – but small claims is an option for a self-represented worker owed a moderate sum. Also, Massachusetts law absolutely prohibits firing or penalizing an employee for claiming wages – that would be a retaliation issue possibly giving separate claims.

**Unpaid commissions or bonuses:** Yes – if you earned a commission under a sales plan or met the criteria for a bonus that wasn’t paid, you can sue for it. Commissions and certain bonuses can count as “wages” under the Wage Act if they were definitely determined and due ([www.seyfarth.com](https://www.seyfarth.com/news-insights/no-quarter-sjc-mandates-treble-damages-for-any-late-payment-of-wages-even-honest-corrected-mistakes.html#:~:text=No%20Quarter%3A%20SJC%20Mandates%20Treble,Califano%20Click%20to%20share)) (i.e., not discretionary). For example, you sold a big contract and per your commission agreement you should get $2,000 commission, but your employer never paid it – that’s actionable. If it qualifies as wages under the law, it carries the same treble damages and fee shifting. Even if it doesn’t fall under the Wage Act (say it was a truly discretionary bonus that was promised in writing after performance), it could be a contract claim (though contract claims wouldn’t guarantee treble damages). The statute of limitations for wage claims under c.149 is 3 years ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter149/Section150#:~:text=An%20employee%20claiming%20to%20be,employee%20files%20a%20complaint%20with)) (and is tolled by the AG complaint process), so don’t wait too long. Always start with the demand via AG’s office.

**Unreimbursed business expenses:** Yes – if your employer owed you reimbursement for expenses (mileage, travel costs, equipment you bought for work) and didn’t pay, you can claim those. These are typically contractual (maybe company policy says they’ll reimburse) or quasi-contract (implied they’d cover necessary business expenses). Massachusetts does not classify routine expense reimbursement as “wages” (so treble damages likely do *not* apply unless the failure to reimburse was actually a way of deducting wages unlawfully). But you can still sue for the out-of-pocket amount. For example, you spent $300 on work supplies expecting payback and got nothing – small claims is fine for that $300. Bring receipts and any emails or handbook showing entitlement to reimbursement.

**Misclassification disputes (contractor vs employee):** Small claims can resolve **money** aspects of a misclassification if, for instance, you were treated as an independent contractor but believe you should have been an employee and thus owed certain benefits or expense reimbursements. However, many misclassification issues (like getting overtime or benefits) can exceed $7k or require declaratory/ injunctive relief, which doesn’t fit small claims. That said, if it’s straightforward – e.g., you had to pay self-employment taxes that you wouldn’t have if properly classified, or you didn’t get overtime pay for 100 hours at time-and-half – you could quantify that and sue. Massachusetts has a strict law defining employees vs contractors (c.149 §148B) and misclassifying someone can be a 93A violation in some contexts. Before filing a small claim, it may be better to file with the AG’s Fair Labor Division if it involves unpaid overtime or minimum wage (they handle those). But nothing prohibits using small claims if the amount is moderate and the liability clear. Ensure the claim is framed as one for wages owed (like overtime pay not given) or benefits owed.

**Important:** The Massachusetts Attorney General’s office is often the **“better forum”** for many wage disputes initially. They can issue civil citations and get you your wages without a lawsuit, and they must be notified (by law) before you sue for wages ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter149/Section150#:~:text=An%20employee%20claiming%20to%20be,employee%20files%20a%20complaint%20with)). In some cases (like minimum wage or overtime violations, or larger wage theft issues), the AG might pursue it or authorize a suit in a higher court. Small claims might be most appropriate for relatively small amounts where you don’t have an attorney and just want your wages. If you do go to small claims, follow the required steps (file with AG or get a right-to-sue letter) or the case could be dismissed for “failure to exhaust administrative remedies.”

### Personal services / Professional disputes  
**Hairdressers, barbers, nail salons (botched services):** Yes – if a personal-care service was done negligently and caused damage, you can sue for the cost of fixing it or for a refund. For example, if a hairdresser’s bleaching ruined your hair and you had to pay another stylist $300 to correct it, you could seek that $300 plus maybe a refund of what you paid. Similarly, if a nail salon’s technician injured you (minor injury) or ruined a product (like expensive shoes with spilled polish), you can claim damages. These are typically negligence or breach of contract (you paid for competent service, didn’t get it). The dollar amounts usually fit comfortably in small claims. Document with photos (e.g., of a hair dye disaster or a scalp burn) and any medical receipts if it caused harm requiring treatment. Massachusetts does not have a specific statute for “bad haircuts,” so it’s general consumer protection; you could also threaten a 93A claim if it was really bad and they refused a refund (some courts might see that as overkill, but it’s possible if they acted unfairly).

**Tutoring, lessons, coaching not delivered:** Yes – if you paid for lessons (music class, tutoring sessions, personal training, etc.) and the provider didn’t deliver them or did them incompetently, you can sue for a refund of the unused portion or the value loss. E.g., you paid a tutor $500 for 10 sessions, they only provided 2 before disappearing – sue for the $400 value of the 8 missed sessions. Or if a driving school took your money and never scheduled the lessons. These are straightforward contract claims. Provide proof of payment and the number of sessions promised vs delivered. 

**Pet boarding, grooming, or training disputes:** Yes – if you left your pet with someone for care (kennel, daycare) and something went wrong that cost you money (vet bills due to their negligence, or they didn’t provide services paid for), you can sue. For example, a dog groomer injured your dog and you have $200 in vet costs – that’s a valid claim (property damage or negligence to your “property,” which is the pet – unpleasant way to think of it, but legally pets are property). Or if you pre-paid a dog trainer $1,000 but they only held half the sessions or did nothing, claim the unused portion back. Be aware of any waivers you might have signed – some kennels have liability waivers; they’re not always ironclad if there was gross negligence, but they could be a defense. Massachusetts doesn’t have a special statute for pet service contracts, so it’s general contract/tort law.

**Medical/dental billing disputes (when not covered by insurance or surprise billing protections):** Possibly – if you have a dispute over a medical bill that doesn’t fall under specialized processes (like the federal No Surprises Act covers certain out-of-network surprise bills, which has its own dispute resolution scheme), you could use small claims. Example: you paid out-of-pocket for a procedure that was done improperly and you want a refund, or the billing office charged you for services not provided. Be cautious: true **medical malpractice** claims (seeking damages for injury from treatment) are not suited for small claims due to the need for expert testimony and damages often exceeding the cap. But a **simple billing issue** – say a dentist charged you for a crown they never actually put in – could be small claims material. Or if you were promised that a cosmetic procedure would yield X result, and it was a total failure and the provider refuses any refund, you might claim the fee back under contract theory. However, many healthcare providers will argue any dispute is malpractice, not a fee issue. Still, small claims has seen cases like a chiropractor billing for sessions not given, etc. Keep it framed as a monetary dispute, not malpractice (you’re not claiming injury, just that services paid for were not delivered or were worthless). If the amount is under $7k and you have clear evidence, it’s worth a try. Remember, actual med mal claims require following the malpractice tribunal process in Superior Court, which is outside small claims jurisdiction.

**Daycare or preschool refund disputes:** Yes – if you put down a deposit or pre-paid tuition to a daycare or preschool and then didn’t end up using it (and are entitled to a refund per contract or fairness), you can sue for that money. Many daycare contracts require advance notice for withdrawal to refund a deposit. If you complied and they still didn’t refund, small claims is ideal. Or if the daycare closed abruptly and didn’t refund unused fees, definitely. Also, if a daycare provider violated regulations and you pulled your child out and want a prorated refund, you could attempt that (though not guaranteed by law, it’d be equitable argument). Provide the contract and proof of payment. Massachusetts has some regulations on daycare contracts (licensing, etc.), but refunds are usually contract-specific. Similarly, private schools or classes – if you withdrew properly, you might get a refund depending on their policies; if they refuse but you feel it’s wrongful, you can bring it to small claims.

### Loans, debts, and financial disputes  
**Money lent to friends/family:** Yes – as noted under contract disputes, an informal loan to a friend or relative is enforceable. You become essentially an unsecured creditor. It might feel awkward, but small claims is commonly used to sort out personal loans that went bad. Example: You lent your cousin $2,000 to buy a used car, and they never paid you back as promised – you can absolutely sue for the $2,000 (with perhaps interest if you had an agreement, or at least statutory interest from when you demanded repayment ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleII/Chapter231/Section6c#:~:text=Section%206C,order%20for%20judgment%20against%20the))). Bring any semblance of evidence: emails, a text “I’ll pay you back in 3 months,” or a check you wrote them noted as loan, etc. Even absent that, your sworn testimony that it was a loan (not a gift) can convince a magistrate, especially if the defendant doesn’t show or lacks credibility. There’s no requirement of a written contract; oral and implied agreements are valid. Just be prepared to articulate the terms (amount, date it was to be repaid or that you asked for it back and they refused). The statute of limitations is 6 years from when repayment was due or from the loan date if no specific due date (the court might consider a “reasonable” term in absence of details) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=Massachusetts%20laws)).

**Shared expenses between roommates or ex-partners:** Yes – disputes over splitting bills or repayment of shared expenses are fair game. For instance, roommates agreed to split the electric bill and one never paid their half for several months – you can sue for their portion if you ended up paying it all. Or an unmarried couple who split up and one kept the $3,000 couch that you jointly paid for – you might claim your share of its value. These are often quasi-contract (unjust enrichment) claims if there wasn’t a clear contract. Massachusetts courts can award equitable money damages in small claims “according to substantive law” ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=Section%2021,not%20exceed%20%2415%2C000%3B%20and%20provided)), and unjust enrichment is a recognized basis. Provide any evidence of the arrangement: texts like “I’ll pay you back for my half of rent,” or receipts of you paying for both. Even absent writing, a magistrate will try to get to a fair result. Keep expectations modest and math clear (if there were 3 roommates and you paid the whole $900 utility bill, you might claim $600 for the other two shares). The timeframe is effectively 6 years for unwritten agreements ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=Massachusetts%20laws)), but practically the sooner the better.

**Wedding/engagement gifts and ring returns:** Yes – Massachusetts law now has clear guidance that an **engagement ring** must be returned to the donor if the engagement is called off, regardless of fault ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=Selected%20cases)) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=Johnson%20v,the%20donor%20regardless%20of%20fault)). In late 2024, the SJC in *Johnson v. Settino* adopted the “no-fault” rule for engagement rings ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=Johnson%20v,the%20donor%20regardless%20of%20fault)). So if you gave someone a ring and the wedding is off, you (the donor) can sue to get the ring back or its value. Typically the ring’s value might exceed $7,000, but many are within that range. If the ring was, say, $10,000, you face the choice of suing in Superior Court or waiving some value to fit in small claims. Alternatively, some plaintiffs sue for **return of the ring** via a replevin action (which is not a small claim – small claims can only award money, not order specific return of property). However, you can sue for the **value** of the ring in small claims. You’d argue that it was a conditional gift given in contemplation of marriage, which is not going forward, so the value should be returned to you. With the SJC case in your favor, you have strong legal backing. Provide proof of the ring’s value (receipt or appraisal). Note: If you are the recipient of an engagement ring and your ex-fiancé is suing you – expect that they will win under this rule, so it’s wise to just return the ring to avoid a judgment (plus court costs) against you. Regarding other wedding-related gifts: generally, gifts (like appliance gifts from guests) aren’t expected to be returned if the wedding is off, except possibly by social convention. But an engagement ring is treated specially under the law. Small claims would not handle things like ordering return of family heirlooms (that might require an equitable order), but money equivalent can be sought.

**Jointly titled property after a breakup:** Possibly – if you and an ex jointly own something (car, furniture) and can’t agree how to split, small claims can’t directly partition property, but you can sue for *money* representing your share. Example: You and your ex bought a TV together for $1,200; after breaking up, they kept the TV and you got nothing – you could claim $600 as your share of the value. Or if both names are on a car and one kept it and didn’t compensate the other, the other could sue for half the car’s equity (if within $7k). Bring proof of purchase and contributions. The court won’t change title, but could award a money judgment to even things out. These cases are essentially unjust enrichment claims. If the amount or situation is complex (like jointly owned real estate), that’s beyond small claims – stick to personal property of moderate value.

**Recovery of cash deposits or earnest money:** Yes – if you put down a cash deposit for something (a rental, a purchase of item or vehicle, or anything) and the other side failed to fulfill their part or refused to return it, you can sue. Common example: you gave a $500 “good faith” deposit to hold a used car, then decided not to buy it, and the seller won’t return it. Whether you can legally get it back might depend on if you breached an agreement – but in small claims, both sides can argue fairness. Or a contractor took a $1,000 deposit and then no-showed – clearly you sue for that $1,000 back as unjust enrichment and breach. Massachusetts home improvement contracts above $1,000 are legally not allowed to take more than 1/3 deposit in advance ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/Partii/Titlei/Chapter186/Section15b#:~:text=,2%29%3B%20and)) (except special-order materials), so a contractor taking a huge deposit and doing nothing is also a 93A violation potentially. Provide evidence of payment (canceled check, etc.) and any written agreement or advertisement. 

**Creditors (banks, collections agencies) suing consumers:** Yes, creditors *can* sue in small claims in Massachusetts, but there are special restrictions. Unlike some states that bar collection agencies from using small claims, Massachusetts permits it but imposes those **“trade or commerce” additional requirements** on the filings ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=,commerce%20or%20pursuing%20assigned%20debt)) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=Any%20plaintiff%20pursuing%20a%20claim,be%20served%20on%20the%20defendant)). If a plaintiff is **pursuing a debt that was originally owed to someone else** (e.g., a debt buyer or collection agency) or any claim “incurred in the course of plaintiff’s trade or commerce” (like a company collecting a customer debt), they must: (1) list the original creditor’s name, (2) the last four digits of the account number, and (3) the date and amount of the last payment, *and* they must file a **“Verification of Defendant’s Address”** form certifying they verified the debtor’s current address ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=Any%20plaintiff%20pursuing%20a%20claim,be%20served%20on%20the%20defendant)) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=Statement%20of%20Small%20Claim%20form,be%20served%20on%20the%20defendant)). This is designed to prevent default judgments going to old addresses. If the business plaintiff doesn’t do this, and the defendant doesn’t show up, the court will **not** enter a default and will instead dismiss the claim ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=Any%20plaintiff%20pursuing%20a%20claim,be%20served%20on%20the%20defendant)) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=If%20the%20plaintiff%20fails%20to,shall%20be%20dismissed%20without%20prejudice)). The case can be refiled properly, but it’s a big pitfall. Also, Massachusetts law (Chapter 93 §49 and 940 CMR 7.00) prohibits certain unfair debt collection practices – a debtor could counterclaim under c.93A if the collection suit is filed on a debt that’s beyond the statute of limitations or not actually owed, etc., claiming it’s an unfair practice. Generally though, a bank or credit union or collector can utilize small claims to pursue debts <= $7k. There’s no special monetary cap for businesses; they are held to $7k like everyone else. Creditors often prefer District Court regular civil process for larger debts because they can get attachments and such, but small claims is quicker and cheaper for, say, a $3,000 credit card balance. If you’re a debtor being sued: the small claims court will hear their case, but you can raise any defense (e.g., “I never incurred this,” “It’s past the 6-year statute for contract debts in MA,” ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=Massachusetts%20laws)) etc.) or even counterclaim if the collection efforts violated law. 

### Vehicle-specific disputes  
*(Many of these overlap with earlier categories but focusing on vehicle-related scenarios:)*

**Accident damage (when insurance doesn’t cover):** Yes – small claims is frequently used for car accident property damage, especially if the at-fault party’s insurance is refusing to pay fully. Perhaps the insurance dispute is over fault percentage or the claimant only had liability coverage for their own car. If your own collision coverage denied the claim or you had none, you can sue the other driver. Massachusetts is a no-fault state for personal injury (which affects injury claims via PIP), but for property damage, fault matters. You can sue the at-fault driver for your **out-of-pocket losses**: repair costs, a rental car, towing, etc. Police reports are admissible as evidence (though technically hearsay, many magistrates consider them as a framework; witnesses are better). If the defendant was cited for a traffic violation, mention it. Because Massachusetts small claims treats motor vehicle property claims specially (no cap and often scheduled on **evening/Saturday sessions** by statute ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=Actions%20brought%20under%20sections%20twenty,four%20O%20of%20chapter%20ninety))), you may find the hearing is at a convenient time. Also, note if the defendant’s insurance company sends an attorney – they might in some cases (the insurer has the right to **remove** the case to the regular civil docket in District Court if they enter an appearance ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section23#:~:text=in%20the%20district%20court%20department,costs%20and%20reasonable%20attorney%27s%20fees)), which sometimes they do for high-dollar claims). However, if an insurer does transfer the case out of small claims to a formal session and you win, the law says the insurer has to pay your reasonable attorney’s fees and costs ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section23#:~:text=in%20the%20district%20court%20department,costs%20and%20reasonable%20attorney%27s%20fees)) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section23#:~:text=vehicle%20where%20the%20action%20is,costs%20and%20reasonable%20attorney%27s%20fees)) – a penalty to discourage needless removal. Often for modest claims, insurers let it stay in small claims. Bring itemized repair estimates or paid bills, and testimony (or a mechanic’s letter) about cause if needed.

**Mechanic/body shop disputes:** Yes – if a garage did repair work on your vehicle that was faulty, or overcharged you, or damaged your car, you can sue them for the cost to fix the issue or the overcharge. (This is the analog of the earlier “auto repair disputes” but specifically when your car is the subject.) Massachusetts has an Auto Repair consumer protection regulation requiring written estimates and authorization. If a mechanic violated those (for example, you got a $500 estimate but they did work and billed $1,500 without permission), that’s not only a breach but a 93A violation. In small claims, you could sue for the $1,000 difference as damages, and potentially double/treble if it seems willful ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-small-claims-and-bringing-a-claim-to-court#:~:text=for%20statutory%20damages%20or%20attorney%E2%80%99s,exceeding%20%247%2C000%20may%20be%20possible)). If the car was actually made worse, you can sue for the cost to correct their mistakes. These cases benefit from having an independent mechanic’s written opinion that “previous work was done improperly” or “unnecessary parts were replaced.” It helps the magistrate decide whose story is credible.

**Dealership disputes (car sales):** Yes – similar to the earlier “dealer disputes” in consumer section. This might involve things like undisclosed salvage title, financing misrepresentations, or failure to honor warranty. For example, a **“yo-yo sale”** (dealer lets you take the car, then says financing fell through and tries to charge a higher rate or reclaim the car) – that is illegal and a classic 93A case; a small claim could recover any additional money you were forced to pay. Or if a dealer promised to fix an issue pre-sale but didn’t, and you had to pay to fix it, you could claim that cost. Just keep the claim itself monetary. Massachusetts law requires used car dealers to fix defects that impair use or safety if they appear within the warranty period (usually 30 days for cars with 40k-125k miles, up to $1,250 in repairs) ([www.carlemon.com](https://www.carlemon.com/amp/massachusetts-lemon-law.html#:~:text=Massachusetts%20Lemon%20Law%20,business%20days%20out%20of%20service)) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/laws/generallaws/parti/titlexiv/chapter90/section7n1~4#:~:text=General%20Law%20,by%20this%20section%20shall%20be)). If they don’t and the cost is below $7k, you could sue for reimbursement of what you spent fixing the problem. If a violation is clear, also consider the demand-letter and 93A route for multiple damages. Odometer tampering is both criminal and civil – if you suspect that, an Odometer Act claim allows $10k or treble damages, but you’d likely need to go to a higher court for full remedy unless you waive excess. But you could use small claims for something like “They misrepresented the car’s year or condition, costing me $3,000 in extra repairs.”

**Towing and impound fees disputes:** Yes – if your car was towed improperly or you were overcharged beyond the legal limits, you can sue the towing company or property owner who ordered the tow. Massachusetts law caps private property “trespass” tow rates (the Department of Public Utilities sets max rates – currently around $108 for tow + certain per-mile and storage fees) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/involuntary-trespass-towing-rates-and-regulations#:~:text=,private%20property%20is%20set%20forth)). If a tow operator charged you, say, $300 cash to release your car, in violation of the cap, you could sue for the overcharge. Also, under c.93A, an illegal towing or failure to release a car on owner request can be unfair practice. If your car was damaged during a tow, that’s property damage by the towing company – claim repair cost. Or if it was towed without proper signage or reason, you might claim the towing/storage fees you had to pay as damages due to an improper conversion of your vehicle. Document what happened (photos of tow zone signs or lack thereof, receipts). This often involves suing the tow company; they often don’t show up, and a default may result if your claim is sound.

**Parking lot damage (hit-and-run with known driver):** Yes – if another driver hit your parked car and you identified them (through surveillance or witness), but they refuse to pay for the damage, you can sue them. The motor vehicle damage exception applies, so even if it’s $10k damage, small claims could hear it ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=slander%20and%20libel%2C%20in%20which,of%20judgments%20upon%20such%20claims)) (though practically, for really high amounts, you might prefer a regular court where discovery and insurance involvement are more robust). For moderate damage, small claims is fine. Present evidence linking that driver to the damage (witness statement, a note they left, matching paint, admission via text, etc.). If it’s essentially a subrogation claim (e.g., your insurer paid you and now you’re trying to recover your deductible), just claim your deductible or any uninsured portion.

### Personal injury (minor)  
Small claims is generally not the forum for serious personal injury cases (since $7,000 is the absolute cap on damages, and injuries can easily exceed that in medical bills or pain and suffering). Additionally, personal injury cases often involve complex proof of negligence and causation that might benefit from formal procedures and expert testimony (which small claims doesn’t easily accommodate). **However**, for relatively **minor injuries or losses under $7,000**, you can use small claims to seek compensation. 

Examples of potentially suitable personal injury claims in small claims:  
- **Minor car accident injuries:** If you suffered a minor injury in a car crash (like whiplash or a sprained wrist) and your medical expenses plus lost wages are, say, $3,000, and perhaps modest pain and suffering, you could sue the at-fault driver for up to $7,000. Massachusetts is a “no-fault” state requiring the first ~$2,000 of medical to go through PIP (Personal Injury Protection) and also requiring that you have over $2,000 in medicals or certain serious injuries to step outside no-fault. Assuming you meet that threshold, you could bring a claim. Many injury lawyers would normally take such a case on contingency in regular court, but if you prefer to self-represent and the amount is small, small claims could work. You’d need to prove the other driver’s fault and your damages (medical bills, perhaps a letter from a doctor, and whatever pain and suffering amount you claim – note that small claims judges might be conservative on pain & suffering awards). Also, note that the $7,000 cap might limit full recovery if you have anything but very minor injuries. Interest on personal injury judgments (M.G.L. c.231 §6B) is 12% from the filing date ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=the%20total%20amount%20being%20sought%2C,procedural%20information%20to%20small%20claims)), which the clerk will add if you win ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=plaintiff%20shall%20state%20specifically%20any,procedural%20information%20to%20small%20claims)). 
- **Dog bite or pet-caused injuries:** Massachusetts has a strict liability law for dog bites (the owner is liable for bites to persons or damage to property, with very limited exceptions) – you don’t have to prove negligence, just that you didn’t provoke and were legally allowed where you were. If a dog bite led to a hospital visit with a $1,500 bill and maybe a scar, that is a legitimate claim. If you keep the claim modest (say within $7k), you could do it in small claims. Be aware, though, that valuing things like scars or pain is subjective; a small claims magistrate might award your out-of-pocket medical and a little extra, but it’s not like a jury that might give more for pain. If you want the maximum compensation, you might consider higher court, but nothing stops you from trying small claims for a quick judgment if the injuries are truly minor. Always bring documentation: medical records, proof that the defendant owned the dog, photos of the injury. 
- **Slip-and-fall or minor property injuries:** If you slipped in a store and had a minor injury (e.g., sprained ankle, $500 ER bill), and the store’s insurer denied liability, you could attempt to recover that $500 and related costs in small claims. Proving negligence (that the store was at fault) is on you; you should have evidence of what caused the fall (a wet floor with no sign, etc.). Many slip-and-fall cases are contested and larger, but a minor one could fit. 
- **Intentional minor assaults:** If someone punched you and gave you a black eye, incurred $300 of medical costs, you can sue them for battery. You might get your medicals plus some amount for pain and suffering. While assault and battery is a tort that can yield punitive damages, in small claims you’re limited to $7k and punitive damages are generally not awarded (plus punitive would likely require a higher court finding). But you can certainly seek compensation. If it was an assault, the fact they were charged criminally (if they were) can help (a conviction can be used as evidence of liability in the civil case). 
Remember that **defamation (libel/slander)** is explicitly excluded from small claims by statute ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=hereinafter%20called%20the%20procedure%2C%20for,not%20exceed%20%2415%2C000%3B%20and%20provided)) – so personal injury in the sense of reputational harm cannot be sued for in small claims.

In summary, while you *can* bring small personal injury matters to small claims, do so only for uncomplicated situations where damages are clearly under $7k. Anything involving long-term injury, significant medical treatment, or complex disputes probably doesn’t belong in small claims. Also, attorneys are allowed in Massachusetts small claims, so if the defendant is insured, often their insurance company might send a lawyer to defend even a small case, which could even out the playing field if you don’t have a lawyer. (In practice, insurers might simply try to settle prior or remove it to a jury session if it’s worth their trouble – see appeals/transfer section.)

### Specific statutory claims (state-specific)  
Massachusetts has various statutes that create causes of action with set damages or multipliers, many of which can be pursued in small claims if the amounts are within the jurisdiction. A few notable ones:

- **Bad check statute (M.G.L. c.93 §40A):** As covered, this allows $100–$500 extra damages for a bounced check, plus the value of the check ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXV/Chapter93/Section40a#:~:text=who%20fails%20to%20pay%20the,more%20than%20five%20hundred%20dollars)). This is perfect for small claims. Cite the statute in your claim to alert the court. (You still cap at $7k base check amount for small claims, but one rarely writes a $8k personal check that bounces; if so, you could only claim $7k of it here or use District Court).
- **Consumer Protection Act (M.G.L. c.93A):** This is the main Massachusetts unfair and deceptive acts statute. It provides for **multiple damages** (double or triple) and **attorney’s fees** for consumers who are harmed by unfair business practices, after a 30-day demand letter ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/30-day-demand-letter#:~:text=Unfair%20or%20deceptive%20practices%20by,triple%20damages%20and%20attorney%27s%20fees)) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-small-claims-and-bringing-a-claim-to-court#:~:text=for%20statutory%20damages%20or%20attorney%E2%80%99s,exceeding%20%247%2C000%20may%20be%20possible)). Common 93A small-claims scenarios include: auto dealer fraud, landlord refusing to return deposit (which also separately triggers 3x by the deposit law), debt collector harassment, home improvement scams, etc. While a 93A claim could exceed $7k after multiplying, the rule is you **can** still bring it as long as your single damages (actual loss) are ≤ $7k ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=under%20this%20section%20if%20the,any%20general%20or%20special%20law)). The court can then award the multiplied amount above $7k. For example, your actual loss is $5,000, you sue for that under 93A – if willful, the court could award $15,000, and that’s permissible even though > $7k ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-small-claims-and-bringing-a-claim-to-court#:~:text=for%20statutory%20damages%20or%20attorney%E2%80%99s,exceeding%20%247%2C000%20may%20be%20possible)). The Massachusetts Trial Court’s small claims page confirms this, noting if you sue for ≤$7k under 93A and win double or triple, the award can exceed $7k ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-small-claims-and-bringing-a-claim-to-court#:~:text=accident%2C%20the%20award%20may%20exceed,exceeding%20%247%2C000%20may%20be%20possible)). So small claims is a viable venue for many consumer statute claims. Just remember the **demand letter** requirement – a failure to do that can bar your 93A claim (the court could still give your base loss on another theory, but you’d lose the power to multiply).
- **Lemon Law (new cars, c.90 §7N½):** For new cars with defects, the law often results in a refund or replacement – usually handled outside court or in superior court due to value. But if you had a cheaper new vehicle or the issue is a specific cost (maybe reimbursement for towing or a defective part), you could use small claims. The lemon law also provides that a manufacturer’s failure to comply entitles the consumer to double or treble damages (and attorney fees) under 93A as well ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-small-claims-and-bringing-a-claim-to-court#:~:text=for%20statutory%20damages%20or%20attorney%E2%80%99s,exceeding%20%247%2C000%20may%20be%20possible)). In small claims, double/triple can be awarded if initial claim ≤ $7k.
- **Data Security / Identity theft:** Massachusetts has a data breach law (c.93H) and regulations, but enforcement is mainly through the AG. A victim of identity theft might sue a culprit for financial losses (common law fraud) or possibly a company for negligently allowing a breach (that would likely be a 93A claim if at all). If someone opened a phone account in your name and ran up $1,000, you could sue that individual for fraud (though collecting from an identity thief might be tough). Massachusetts law allows victims of identity theft to get police reports and freeze credit, etc., but no specific small-claims-friendly statutory damage unless it overlaps with 93A (like a credit reporting agency mishandling something – those go to federal law territory typically). So identity theft monetary claims are usually either tiny and not worth suing, or large and complex. But small claims could handle, say, a roommate who stole and used your credit card for $500 – that’s just theft/ conversion for which you can sue.
- **Wage and hour laws:** We discussed the Wage Act (treble damages mandatory) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter149/Section150#:~:text=final,litigation%20and%20reasonable%20attorneys%27%20fees)). There’s also the Massachusetts Minimum Wage and Overtime law (c.151) which also provides mandatory treble damages and fees for violations ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter149/Section150#:~:text=An%20employee%20claiming%20to%20be,employee%20files%20a%20complaint%20with)) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter149/Section150#:~:text=final,litigation%20and%20reasonable%20attorneys%27%20fees)). If you have a small minimum-wage underpayment (e.g., a summer job that paid you $10/hr when the minimum was $14.25, and you’re owed $500 difference), you can claim that and get $1,500 in damages by law. The process (AG complaint first) is the same. Also, Massachusetts has a unique Sunday/holiday premium pay law and others that could yield small sums. These are all fine in small claims, with the same caveat to involve the AG first.
- **Small Claims for violations of specific statutes**: Massachusetts allows some statutory actions in small claims like **M.G.L. c.186 §14** (landlord interference with utilities or access) which gives tenants damages and often 3x rent as damages. That might be done in Housing Court typically, but could be small claims if only money is sought. Another niche: Massachusetts law provides for consumer rights like **ticket resales** (caps on resale fees), **security deposit** (treble damages) which we covered, **utility deposit refunds**, etc. If there’s a monetary statutory penalty that specifically says the person can sue for a certain amount, it’s likely small-claims eligible if within $7k. Always double-check if the statute requires filing in a particular court or with an agency. For example, Massachusetts’ Consumer Protection Act (93A) we know works in small claims. Some states bar their deceptive trade practice claims from small claims, but Massachusetts explicitly contemplates it ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-small-claims-and-bringing-a-claim-to-court#:~:text=accident%2C%20the%20award%20may%20exceed,exceeding%20%247%2C000%20may%20be%20possible)).
- **Treble damages for property theft/fraud (M.G.L. c.231 §85J)**: Massachusetts law says if someone **by fraud or deceit** sells you personal property and you’re injured by this, they are liable for **treble damages** in tort ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/massachusetts/part-iii/title-ii/chapter-231/section-85j/#:~:text=Section%2085J,of%20damages%20sustained%20by%20him)). This could apply if, say, someone knowingly sold you a fake antique for $1,000 claiming it was authentic – you could sue for $3,000 under §85J. Small claims could award that since the base was $1,000 (the court would likely treble it if you prove the fraud, per the statute). You’d want to cite c.231 §85J. This law is not commonly invoked by laypeople, but it exists and could bolster a fraud case.

### Other state-specific claim types  
*(This section is a “catch-all” for any other common disputes in Massachusetts that one might bring in small claims.)* Massachusetts small claims can encompass a wide array of issues as long as they boil down to a claim for money and are not explicitly excluded. Some additional examples and niche situations:

- **Rental brokerage fee disputes:** In Massachusetts, if a tenant paid a broker’s fee but the apartment was never actually rented (e.g., landlord backed out), the tenant might try to get that fee back from the broker. Depending on circumstances, that could be a small claim (maybe under a theory of frustration of purpose or unfair practice if the broker misled them). No specific statute, but it’s a real-world dispute sometimes.
- **MBTA or public transit issues:** If the MBTA (Boston transit) damaged your property (say bus splashes slush causing dry cleaning, or a T accident injures you small-scale), you could claim against them – note that claims against government entities require a proper **presentment** within 2 years to the agency (per M.G.L. c.258, MA Tort Claims Act) and are capped at $100,000 for torts ([www.lexology.com](https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=19846641-e5bc-4f27-9a68-f1a31931445a#:~:text=District%20of%20Massachusetts%20Provides%20Guidance,Log)). Small claims could handle a small property claim against a city or the MBTA if you followed notice requirements (and interestingly, the law often disallows default judgment against government unless certain conditions are met – they usually respond though). If you had, for example, a $500 damage to your car from a city snow plow, you could small-claim the city (after sending a formal §4 presentment letter to the mayor or appropriate official and waiting denied or 6 months). Government entities often remove cases to regular civil or just settle small ones.
- **Volunteer or unpaid work disputes:** If you performed work expecting payment that wasn’t legally wages (maybe a stipend or agreed expense reimbursements) and didn’t get it, you can use small claims. Or if you fronted costs for a community project and the group promised to repay, that’s enforceable.
- **HOA or condo disputes (money portion):** If you live in a condominium or a neighborhood with a homeowners’ association, and there’s a dispute over money – e.g., the HOA overcharged you fees, or you had to pay out for a repair that was the HOA’s responsibility – you can use small claims. Many condo/HOA fights are equitable (injunctions, etc.), but the **financial parts** can be isolated. For example, if the condo association fails to reimburse you for a common-area repair you paid for, or fines you improperly and you pay and want a refund, those could be claims. Check condo docs and M.G.L. c.183A for any special procedures, but nothing stops small claims for straightforward money claims. Be mindful that suing your HOA might not be the best “political” move without trying internal resolution, but it’s available.
- **Towing without authorization (predatory towing):** Already covered that under vehicle disputes. Massachusetts heavily regulates towing fees ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/involuntary-trespass-towing-rates-and-regulations#:~:text=,private%20property%20is%20set%20forth)), so if your car was towed in violation of those regulations (e.g., no sign posted, or towing company didn’t notify police, or overcharged), you can not only get damages in small claims but also report them to the Department of Public Utilities.
- **Telecommunications or utility customer disputes:** If Verizon or Comcast or Eversource (utility companies) overbilled you and won’t correct it, you might bring a claim for the overbilled amount. Often utilities are subject to Department of Public Utilities processes for disputes, but small claims has seen cases of, say, wrongful termination fees for cable service or undelivered services. Some telecom contracts have arbitration clauses that could complicate this. But if not, a small claim can be a way to get attention. For instance, someone charged for a phone line cancellation despite returning equipment could sue for that $200 fee. If there’s a valid contract clause or tariff, the company will defend on that basis.
- **Online purchases and scams:** If you were scammed by an in-state party via online sale (e.g., paid someone on Facebook Marketplace in Massachusetts who never delivered the item), you can sue them in small claims for the money. The challenge may be serving them if you only know an email, but if you have their name/address or if they’re local, you can. If it’s an interstate internet scam, small claims won’t help (you need to find the person and have jurisdiction).
- **Airbnb or short-term rental issues:** If you rented a vacation property in Mass. and it was misrepresented or the host kept your security deposit without cause, you can use small claims (assuming the host is in MA or you file where the property is – which is allowed as rental property venue ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=Actions%20under%20this%20section%20and,which%20the%20property%20is%20located))). Massachusetts’s new short-term rental laws don’t provide individual remedies except maybe through c.93A if it was a business host and deceptive. But you can treat it as a normal contract: you pay, didn’t get what was promised (or damage deposit wrongfully withheld) = lawsuit. Many short-term rental platforms have arbitration clauses, but an individual host might not. 
- **Uber/Lyft driver or gig economy disputes:** For example, if you were a rideshare driver and the company failed to pay you some bonus or if you were a passenger double-charged. There may be arbitration clauses in the terms that the company will invoke. But absent that (or if you opt-out), you could small-claim amounts due. A rideshare driver might use small claims to get reimbursement from a rider who puked in their car if Uber didn’t cover it (though Uber typically bills the rider cleaning fees and pays driver). Or a delivery driver might sue a restaurant for a promised incentive not paid – depends on the setup. Massachusetts is considering making gig drivers employees; at present these disputes are mostly contract issues with the app’s terms.
- **Mass. Chapter 93A “mini-FDCPA” for debt collection:** Massachusetts regulations (940 CMR 7.00) set rules for debt collectors (e.g., no calls at work if forbidden, etc.). If a collection agency violates those, it’s a 93A violation. A consumer could small-claim, say, $1,000 for harassment calls, which might be doubled/tripled by the court. These cases can be done in small claims (the main challenge is proving the conduct; often better to have an attorney in Superior Court, but it’s an option).
- **Mold or environmental hazards costs:** If a landlord ignored a mold issue and you had to buy an air purifier and stay in a hotel for a week, you could sue to recover those expenses. Or if a neighbor’s use of property caused you expenses (like their sump pump floods your yard and you paid to fix damage). As long as you can show they breached a duty, you can claim the cost.
- **Legal malpractice small claims:** Generally, big malpractice claims are beyond scope, but if a lawyer took a small fee and provided essentially no service, you could sue for that fee back. For instance, you paid a lawyer $1,000 to handle a simple matter and they did nothing and won’t refund – a small claim for $1,000 could be made (for breach of contract or unjust enrichment). Note, malpractice as a claim of damages from poor performance (like you lost a case due to lawyer’s error, wanting the lost amount back) is complex and not for small claims. But a straightforward “didn’t do what I paid for” is suitable.
- **Firearm or hunting violations – property damage:** If someone illegally discharged a weapon and hit your property, or a hunter on state land accidentally shot your cow – you can sue them for damage. Massachusetts also has a law allowing treble damages for unauthorized cutting of trees (M.G.L. c.242 §7) – so if a neighbor cut your trees without permission, you can get three times the tree’s value. That value is often appraised; for ornamental or boundary trees it can be significant. If it’s under $7k (tripled), small claims could handle it; otherwise that might go to higher court. 

The above list is not exhaustive but illustrates the breadth of small claims. The guiding principle: if you can translate a grievance into a specific dollar amount of **monetary loss**, and it’s not excluded by statute and doesn’t exceed $7,000 (for the initial claim), you likely can sue for it in small claims.

### Counterclaims in Massachusetts small claims  
If you are sued in small claims as a defendant, you **can file a counterclaim** against the plaintiff to seek your own damages. Massachusetts allows **broad counterclaims** – they do **not** have to relate to the same transaction or occurrence as the plaintiff’s claim ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/small-claims-standards-501-counterclaims#:~:text=A%20defendant%20may%20raise%20any,session%2C%20he%20or%20she%20waives)). For example, a landlord sues a tenant for unpaid rent, the tenant can counterclaim for an **unrelated** issue, like the landlord damaged the tenant’s furniture last year – the court will hear both, as long as the counterclaim is within small claims jurisdiction (contract or tort and under $7k). Counterclaims are **permissive** (optional); you won’t lose a claim forever if you don’t bring it in small claims, except you might miss the chance to easily resolve it. 

**Dollar limit for counterclaims:** The counterclaim is subject to the **same $7,000 cap** usually ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/small-claims-standards-501-counterclaims#:~:text=A%20defendant%20may%20raise%20any,session%2C%20he%20or%20she%20waives)). The small claims rules say a counterclaim can be filed if it’s “within the jurisdiction of the court in small claims cases” ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-3-notice-to-defendant-answer-to-claim#:~:text=In%20the%20answer%2C%20or%20in,If)) – meaning it must not exceed the $7k limit and must be a claim type allowed (contract/tort). If your potential counterclaim exceeds $7,000, you have a few options: you could **waive the excess** and claim only $7,000, or you might consider transferring the case out of small claims (see below). Unlike some states, Massachusetts has no provision allowing a defendant to effectively remove the case to a higher court by filing a counterclaim above the limit. In fact, the statute forbids *removal to Superior Court* entirely ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section23#:~:text=Section%2023,costs%20and%20reasonable%20attorney%27s%20fees)). So a large counterclaim would either be **voluntarily reduced** to $7k or you’d have to pursue it separately in a different forum. The safer approach: if your counterclaim is big, you might not want to litigate in small claims at all – you could either not counterclaim and sue separately in a higher court, or if it’s intimately related, ask the small claims court (which is a District Court department) to transfer the matter to the regular civil docket of the District Court. Massachusetts Uniform Small Claims Rule 5 doesn’t explicitly outline transferring for oversized counterclaims, but practically, a judge might convert it if both parties agree or if fairness demands.

**How to file a counterclaim:** The small claims Answer form (the notice the defendant gets) includes a section to write in a counterclaim ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-3-notice-to-defendant-answer-to-claim#:~:text=The%20Statement%20of%20Small%20Claim,answer%2C%20or%20to%20send%20a)) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-3-notice-to-defendant-answer-to-claim#:~:text=In%20the%20answer%2C%20or%20in,If)). You can also simply send a letter to the court outlining your counterclaim ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/small-claims-standards-501-counterclaims#:~:text=Rule%203%20requires%20that%20a,the%20court%20stating%20the%20counterclaim)). There’s **no extra filing fee** for a counterclaim in small claims ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-3-notice-to-defendant-answer-to-claim#:~:text=In%20the%20answer%2C%20or%20in,If)). However, you should mail a copy of whatever you file to the plaintiff (to avoid ambushing them) – the rules say if you mail notice of it 10 days before trial, it will be heard together ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-3-notice-to-defendant-answer-to-claim#:~:text=set%20forth%20any%20claim%20which,of%20the%20plaintiff%27s%20case%2C%20the)). If you raise it late or only at the hearing, the court can still allow it (counterclaims can technically be brought “at any time, even at the hearing” under the rules ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/small-claims-standards-501-counterclaims#:~:text=Counterclaims%20may%20be%20made%20at,time%20to%20prepare%20for%20trial)) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/small-claims-standards-501-counterclaims#:~:text=plaintiff,with%20respect%20to%20that%20counterclaim))), but if the plaintiff isn’t prepared, the magistrate might grant a continuance. 

**What happens if counterclaim exceeds plaintiff’s claim or total goes over $7k?** In Massachusetts, unlike some states, there is no automatic transfer or dismissal just because the counterclaim is large. The statute explicitly says the $7k limit doesn’t include any court-awarded multiple damages ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=under%20this%20section%20if%20the,any%20general%20or%20special%20law)), and doesn’t mention counterclaims. Typically, if a defendant wanted to claim more than $7k, they would likely forego small claims and either not counterclaim (then possibly appeal the small claim and merge it with their larger claim in the jury-of-six appeal) or they’d file a separate lawsuit. If a counterclaim above $7k is filed in small claims, the magistrate probably would explain that jurisdiction is capped and either ask the defendant to waive the excess or dismiss the counterclaim without prejudice. Massachusetts Lawyers Weekly Q&As have advised that you can file a counterclaim over the limit, but effectively you’d be waiving the amount over $7k by choosing to stay in small claims (and the plaintiff could object and get it transferred). 

**If counterclaim *exceeds* the small claims limit** and you don’t want to waive, one strategy: when you get sued in small claims, you (the defendant) can opt to **transfer the case to the regular District Court docket** by claiming a jury trial. Actually, Massachusetts allows a defendant in small claims to appeal for a trial in the District Court with a jury of 6 after the magistrate’s decision ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section23#:~:text=A%20plaintiff%20beginning%20a%20cause,by%20a%20jury%20of%20six)), but *before* the small claims is heard, there’s not an explicit mechanism to remove it. Some attorneys will actually not engage in the small claims hearing, let a default or magistrate decision go, then appeal by right to a jury trial (which moves it to a more formal setting where your larger counterclaim may be entertained along with the plaintiff’s claim). However, note that as a defendant, if you don’t appear, the court may issue default judgment and you technically lose the right to appeal except by trying to vacate the default ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-7-trials-hearings-and-judgments#:~:text=match%20at%20L319%20Since%20no,on%20the%20date%20specified%20in)). The safer path if you know you have a big counterclaim is to show up and *if* you lose, appeal to the jury session within 10 days – at that point, it’s a new trial in District Court where normal rules apply and you might be allowed to assert your full counterclaim (the statute says in the jury trial, the plaintiff can also assert claims, etc.). Indeed, c.218 §23 states if the case is appealed to the jury session, the plaintiff then also has the right to a jury and it proceeds like a regular civil trial ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section23#:~:text=A%20plaintiff%20beginning%20a%20cause,by%20a%20jury%20of%20six)) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section23#:~:text=Trials%20by%20jury%20of%20six,in%20the%20superior%20court%20department)). In that de novo trial, you likely can file an answer and counterclaim under normal District Court rules (perhaps now not limited to $7k). This is a complicated route, so it’s mentioned for completeness. For most, simply keep counterclaims ≤ $7,000 in small claims.

**Counterclaim examples:** If you are a tenant being sued for rent, you should almost always counterclaim for things like housing code violations, security deposit violations, or damages to you, as applicable – Massachusetts courts often offset judgments (landlord claims vs tenant counterclaims). If you’re being sued by a contractor for unpaid balance, you might counterclaim that his work was defective and cost you to fix. If sued by a credit card company, you might counterclaim under c.93A if they did something unlawful in collection (though be cautious – 93A against an original creditor is limited; against a third-party debt collector, yes you can). 

**Procedure at hearing:** The court will hear the plaintiff’s claim and the defendant’s counterclaim together as one case ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-3-notice-to-defendant-answer-to-claim#:~:text=set%20forth%20any%20claim%20which,If)). They might alternate who speaks, but effectively both sides get to present their claims and defenses. The magistrate will then decide who owes what. It’s possible both claims have merit – e.g., “Plaintiff landlord wins $1,000 rent, but defendant tenant wins $2,000 on counterclaim, net judgment $1,000 to tenant.” In such netting, the court would typically enter a judgment for the difference in favor of the appropriate party (or separate judgments that offset – in practice they net them).

### What you CANNOT sue for in small claims (Massachusetts)  
While small claims is flexible, there are clear exclusions and limitations. Massachusetts law excludes certain subject matters either explicitly by statute or by the nature of the claim. Do **not** use small claims for:

- **Libel or Slander (Defamation):** The statute explicitly says “other than slander and libel” – so defamation claims are *not* allowed in small claims ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=hereinafter%20called%20the%20procedure%2C%20for,not%20exceed%20%2415%2C000%3B%20and%20provided)). If someone harmed your reputation, that belongs in regular civil court (and typically requires showing actual damages or is complex). Even if your defamation claim is small (say you lost $1,000 of work due to a false statement), small claims cannot hear it in Massachusetts.

- **Evictions (possession of real property):** Small claims cannot be used to evict a tenant or otherwise recover possession of real estate. Eviction cases (summary process) go through a distinct procedure in District, Housing, or Superior Court. So you can’t ask the small claims court to order someone out or to grant you a lease or title. You can only sue for rent or damages, not for possession or specific performance.

- **Family law matters:** You can’t use small claims to get a divorce, or child support order, or custody arrangement. Those are strictly for Probate & Family Court. Even if someone owes you money related to family law (like an ex failed to pay court-ordered child support or alimony), you enforce that through family court contempt, not by a separate small claim (the state would likely reject jurisdiction as it would interfere with the family court order). A property settlement from a divorce also can’t be relitigated in small claims.

- **Equitable relief / Specific performance:** Small claims can only award money, not court orders telling someone to do or not do something (other than an order to pay money). For instance, you cannot sue to make your neighbor remove a fence, or to get an injunction against harassment – those require equitable power of the court not available in small claims. (The only slight exception is they can order the other party to pay in installments after judgment, but that’s part of money judgment enforcement ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/massachusetts/part-iii/title-i/chapter-218/section-22/#:~:text=Section%2022,other%20chapters%2C%20sections%20or%20acts)).) If you filed a small claim essentially asking for an action (“make the mechanic give me my car back” without asking for money), it would be dismissed as not cognizable. You’d need to convert that into a dollar amount (“the value of my car that he refuses to return”).

- **Pain and suffering above the cap:** If your personal injury claim’s value clearly exceeds $7,000, you shouldn’t try to circumvent by suing for only $7k without waiving the rest, because you’d be splitting the cause of action. As discussed, claim-splitting is not allowed. So big personal injury or malpractice cases are effectively outside small claims. Also, Massachusetts law requires in medical malpractice cases a detailed procedure and often an affidavit from a medical expert – which you cannot do in small claims. So malpractice (medical, dental, legal, etc.) is functionally off-limits in small claims.

- **Claims against the Commonwealth or Federal Government:** You generally cannot sue the federal government or a federal agency in state small claims – those claims (if permitted at all under the Federal Tort Claims Act or other waivers of sovereign immunity) must go to federal court or through administrative processes. Similarly, claims against the Commonwealth of Massachusetts or a city/town for torts must follow the Massachusetts Tort Claims Act (M.G.L. c.258) procedures with formal presentment and often are handled in Superior Court. However, if you have a straightforward contract claim against a city (e.g., you sold them supplies and they didn’t pay $2,000), you might bring that in District Court civil (not sure if they’d allow small claim – they might, but by law governmental entities can be sued in District Court, and small claim is just a procedure of District Court). The small claims rules say no bond is required of a town/city if they appeal ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/massachusetts/part-iii/title-i/chapter-218/section-23/#:~:text=No%20bond%20shall%20be%20required,four%20D%20of%20chapter%20ninety)), indicating that suits against municipalities in small claims indeed occur. But the **nature** of claim must be one allowed by law – e.g., you can’t sue the Commonwealth for punitive damages or for something not allowed under sovereign immunity. Also, you cannot sue an Embassy or Consulate or another State in state small claims due to sovereign immunity issues.

- **Bankruptcy-related matters:** If someone has filed bankruptcy and is under automatic stay, you cannot start or continue a small claim against them unless the bankruptcy court gives permission (the automatic stay under federal law halts all collection efforts – a small claims suit would be void or in contempt of federal court). Similarly, if you have a claim that properly belongs in a bankruptcy case (like you’re claiming a debtor committed fraud – that often gets handled as an adversary proceeding in bankruptcy court), state small claims wouldn’t be the venue. If a defendant mentions they filed bankruptcy, the small claim will be stayed or dismissed typically.

- **Class actions or multi-plaintiff suits:** Small claims in MA is meant for individual claims. You cannot file a class action in small claims. Each claim must be by a named individual or entity for their own damages. Even if many people each have the same small claim, they each must file separately (or one can file and others might testify, but there’s no class mechanism).

- **Criminal matters/fines:** You cannot use small claims to seek the criminal prosecution of someone or recover court fines or criminal restitution. If you’re owed restitution from a criminal case judgment, you enforce that in the criminal context, not by a new civil suit. Conversely, if you think someone committed a crime against you, small claims only addresses the civil side (money), and it will not result in jail or criminal record for them – it’s not the right venue to “press charges,” you must go to police/DA for that. 

- **Probate and estate claims:** If you are owed money by a deceased person’s estate, you have to file a claim in Probate Court through the estate’s administration. A small claim against a dead person will be dismissed (a deceased can’t be party; you’d have to sue the estate’s executor if allowed, but that again should go through probate process). Also will contests, trusts, etc., all not in small claims.

- **Tax disputes:** You cannot sue the IRS or Mass. Department of Revenue in small claims over your tax bill. Tax appeals have their own forums (IRS appeals, U.S. Tax Court, the state Appellate Tax Board). The only tax-related small claim might be a city/town suing for personal property tax as allowed (and they can use small claims up to any amount for that) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=slander%20and%20libel%2C%20in%20which,of%20judgments%20upon%20such%20claims)), but that’s the government suing a person, not vice versa. If you think the government wrongly kept your refund, you must follow tax appeal processes.

- **Foreclosure or real property title issues:** Small claims can’t decide who owns land or order a foreclosure stopped. Those require equity jurisdiction. If you lost money in a foreclosure due to some misstep, that’s complex and likely beyond $7k or needs injunctive relief – not suitable for small claims (though possibly a 93A claim for a smaller mortgage servicing error could be if purely monetary and under cap). 

- **Admiralty or federal jurisdiction cases:** If your claim is fundamentally federal (like patent infringement, federal civil rights under §1983 seeking injunction, etc.), those don’t belong in small claims. Even if money is involved, state small claims lack jurisdiction over patents, bankruptcy, immigration, etc. A simple rule: if a special court or process exists, small claims likely isn’t appropriate.

- **Claims requiring extensive discovery or expert testimony:** While not legally “forbidden,” any claim that would hinge on expert opinions (medical malpractice needing a doctor’s testimony on standard of care, for instance) is impractical in small claims where formal discovery and expert cross-examination are not available. The court might dismiss such a complex case without prejudice, suggesting you use a regular court. 

In Massachusetts, if you mistakenly file an excluded claim in small claims, the clerk or magistrate will likely dismiss it or advise you it’s not the right forum. For example, if you file for “emotional distress $5,000” without a clear underlying tort, they may throw it out. Or if you sue “John for slander $5,000,” they’ll note defamation is excluded and dismiss. 

### Damages – what you can and cannot recover in small claims  
Small claims courts can award **compensatory damages** and certain statutory damages, but there are limits on punitive damages. 

**Compensatory damages (actual out-of-pocket loss):** Yes, this is the core of small claims – money to cover your proven loss. This can include the amount you paid and didn’t get benefit from, cost to repair or replace damaged property, medical bills from an injury, lost wages, etc. You should quantify these precisely and provide documentation (receipts, estimates, invoices). The court will award either the full proven amount or a portion that seems justified. They won’t speculate beyond evidence.

**Incidental and consequential damages:** If reasonably connected to the breach or tort, you can claim them. E.g., cost of towing and rental car due to an accident caused by defendant, or storage fees you incurred because of someone’s breach. As long as you show they were a foreseeable result, the magistrate can include those.

**Pain and suffering / emotional distress:** These are recoverable in principle if your claim is a personal injury or similar tort that traditionally allows such damages. But you must include them in your claim amount (e.g., “medical bills $1,000 + pain and suffering $2,000 = $3,000 total”). Massachusetts small claims judges (magistrates) may be somewhat conservative in awarding general damages without clear guidance. They can award it, but you should articulate why and perhaps provide any tangible evidence (even personal testimony of how the injury affected you). For purely emotional or punitive aims (like “I want $5,000 for stress”), if no actual loss accompanies it, that likely won’t fly except under a statute (like some consumer protection might have minimum statutory damages or multiples). 

**Punitive or exemplary damages:** Small claims generally will **not** award “punitive” damages unless a statute specifically calls for a multiple of damages or a set penalty. Punitive damages (meant to punish beyond actual loss) typically require a higher court finding of egregious conduct. In Massachusetts, multiple damages under statutes (double or treble) are considered a form of punitive damages but are **allowed** if the statute applies ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=under%20this%20section%20if%20the,any%20general%20or%20special%20law)) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-small-claims-and-bringing-a-claim-to-court#:~:text=for%20statutory%20damages%20or%20attorney%E2%80%99s,exceeding%20%247%2C000%20may%20be%20possible)). But you can’t just ask the magistrate, “Give me an extra $1,000 to punish them” without a statute – that will be denied. The court sticks to actual damages and statutory enhancements where provided. 

**Statutory multipliers and penalties:** Yes, as covered, Massachusetts has many where the court must or may multiply damages (e.g., treble for wage theft ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter149/Section150#:~:text=final,litigation%20and%20reasonable%20attorneys%27%20fees)), security deposit double or triple ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-tenants-security-deposits#:~:text=Castenholz%20v,interest%2C%20costs%2C%20and%20attorney%E2%80%99s%20fees)), 93A double/treble for willful violations ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-small-claims-and-bringing-a-claim-to-court#:~:text=for%20statutory%20damages%20or%20attorney%E2%80%99s,exceeding%20%247%2C000%20may%20be%20possible)), bad check $100–$500 ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXV/Chapter93/Section40a#:~:text=who%20fails%20to%20pay%20the,more%20than%20five%20hundred%20dollars)), etc.). The small claims magistrate can and will award those if proven. Always cite the statute if you are seeking such an enhanced damage so the magistrate knows they have authority to do it. And remember, the initial claim must be within $7k but the **awarded** total can exceed $7k due to these statutory extras ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=under%20this%20section%20if%20the,any%20general%20or%20special%20law)).

**Attorney’s fees and court costs:** In Massachusetts small claims, **court costs** (the filing fee and typically the service or mailing fees you paid) are usually added to a judgment for the prevailing party. For example, if you win $500, you also get your $40 filing fee back in the judgment. If you had to pay a sheriff $50 to serve the defendant because mail failed, you should present that receipt and ask for it – courts often award it. **Attorney’s fees**, however, are generally **not** awarded in small claims unless a specific statute or contract provides for them. Massachusetts follows the American Rule (each pays their own lawyer) except when statutes like c.93A or the Wage Act mandate fee-shifting ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter149/Section150#:~:text=final,litigation%20and%20reasonable%20attorneys%27%20fees)). So if you sued under c.93A and win, the court must award your reasonable attorneys’ fee (if you had an attorney) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter149/Section150#:~:text=final,litigation%20and%20reasonable%20attorneys%27%20fees)). In practice, most individuals in small claims don’t have an attorney, so that’s moot. But if a business sends a lawyer and wins on, say, a contract that had an attorney fee clause, they could potentially get fees – albeit small claims might view that as against the spirit of informal resolution; still, legally, contract fees can be enforced. When attorneys are involved on appeal (in the jury trial session), the normal rules of fees apply. Note: The small claims magistrate **may limit attorney participation** in the hearing ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-7-trials-hearings-and-judgments#:~:text=,hearings)), but if an attorney did significant prep or the rules allow it (like 93A), fees can be asked for. Also, Massachusetts small claims has a unique rule – if the case is *appealed* to the jury session and the plaintiff wins there, they may get up to $150 in attorney’s fees for having to defend the appeal (this is to discourage frivolous appeals by defendants) – but this is at judge’s discretion.

**Interest:** Yes, interest on judgments is allowed and generally *automatic*. Massachusetts has a pre-judgment interest statute for contracts (12% from date of breach or filing) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleII/Chapter231/Section6c#:~:text=Section%206C,order%20for%20judgment%20against%20the)) and for torts (also 12% from date of filing for personal injury/property damage) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=the%20total%20amount%20being%20sought%2C,procedural%20information%20to%20small%20claims)). In small claims, the clerk or magistrate applies these rules: **Contract cases** – interest at 12% from either when the cause of action accrued or from filing (the statute 6C says from breach or demand; if breach date unclear, from filing) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleII/Chapter231/Section6c#:~:text=Section%206C,order%20for%20judgment%20against%20the)). **Tort cases** – interest at 12% from the start of the case (6B) added to judgment. The Statement of Small Claim form even asks if you want interest according to these statutes ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=plaintiff%20shall%20state%20specifically%20any,procedural%20information%20to%20small%20claims)). So remember to request interest in your claim. Post-judgment interest (after judgment until paid) in MA is likewise 12% per annum ([www.lexology.com](https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=3eee6a7c-5804-4788-8089-f4d39fc2b53c#:~:text=Of%20Significant%20Interest%3A%2012,%E2%80%A6)) (the SJC has upheld 12% as applying post-judgment as well in 2023 ([www.lexology.com](https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=3eee6a7c-5804-4788-8089-f4d39fc2b53c#:~:text=Of%20Significant%20Interest%3A%2012,%E2%80%A6))). So if the loser doesn’t pay right away, the amount accrues interest. The clerk will usually handle adding statutory interest when issuing execution for payment. 

**Filing fees and service costs:** As noted, these are typically added to the winning side’s award. Massachusetts law specifically says the small claims entry fee and any allowable **surcharge** (Massachusetts has a $10 surcharge in small claims ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleVI/Chapter262/Section4c#:~:text=Section%204C,collected%20pursuant%20to%20this%20section)) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleVI/Chapter262/Section4c#:~:text=filing%20fee%20is%20payable%2C%20and,credited%20to%20the%20General%20Fund))) can be awarded. Also if you used a deputy sheriff or constable for service, that fee is recoverable as a “cost.” Just show proof of what you spent.

**Collection costs or treble damages for willful non-payment:** Small claims judgment itself doesn’t give you extra for if they delay paying (aside from the interest). There’s no automatic trebling just because someone ignored your demand unless a statute said so (like the bad check law does after 30 days demand). So you can’t ask the court, “if they don’t pay in 30 days, double it” – no, the court’s involvement ends with judgment (except if you seek payment hearings). However, if post-judgment the person still won’t pay, you move to collection enforcement (Section 12 covers that). But there’s no punitive add-on except interest.

To summarize: you can recover **money** you’re owed, statutory penalties, interest, and your court costs. You generally cannot recover **punitive damages** or **non-monetary remedies** in small claims. You also typically cannot get “**pain & suffering**” unless it’s part of a legitimate injury claim. If you win a judgment, the court won’t automatically enforce it – you then have to collect (see next sections on how).

## 3. Where to file  
Massachusetts small claims cases are filed in the state’s **District Courts** or the **Boston Municipal Court (BMC)** for cases in the city of Boston. Each District Court has a small claims session (informal, often handled by a clerk-magistrate). There is also a **Housing Court** department which can hear small claims if the case relates to housing (landlord-tenant matters, etc.) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/small-claims-court#:~:text=Small%20Claims%20sessions%20are%20conducted,where%20the%20property%20is%20located)). Practically, if your case is a regular consumer or debt issue, you’ll file in the District Court for the area. If it’s a landlord/tenant dispute or other housing-related claim, you have the **option** to file in Housing Court (if one serves your area) – housing specialists might handle it with more expertise in rental issues, but District Court works too.

**Venue (which court) rules:** Massachusetts law gives you some choice in venue. By statute, you may bring the action in the judicial district (court) where either the **plaintiff or the defendant** lives, has their usual place of business, or is employed ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=Actions%20under%20this%20section%20and,which%20the%20property%20is%20located)). This is broader than many states that require suing where the defendant lives. So if you live in Suffolk County and the defendant lives in Worcester County, you can file in *either* Suffolk (your district) or Worcester (defendant’s district) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=Actions%20under%20this%20section%20and,which%20the%20property%20is%20located)). If the defendant is a company, “usual place of business” essentially means where it operates (has an office or does substantial business). For example, if you’re in Springfield and you want to sue a Boston company, you could sue in Springfield District Court because that’s where *you* live (plaintiff’s home) – and the law allows it – or you could sue in Boston (where the company is). 

**Special venue for landlord-tenant:** If you are suing a landlord or lessor regarding a rental property, you also have the option of filing in the court where the property is located ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=either%20the%20plaintiff%20or%20the,which%20the%20property%20is%20located)). So a tenant who moved out of an apartment in Barnstable county but now lives in Boston could still file in Barnstable District Court where the apartment is (perhaps convenient if evidence/witnesses are local there). Or they could file in Boston because they live there – multiple choices.

**Filing in the wrong court:** If you file in a court that isn’t allowed by those rules and the defendant doesn’t object, it’s effectively waived and the court can hear it ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=Notwithstanding%20the%20foregoing%2C%20each%20court,therein%2C%20and%20all%20prior%20proceedings)). If the defendant does object (via a motion to transfer for improper venue), the small claims rules say the case will be **transferred** to the correct court, not dismissed ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=should%20have%20been%20brought%20in,is%20%247%2C000%20or%20less%20or)). The clerk will forward the papers to the right court where it will be heard as if originally filed there ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=said%20action%20is%20waived%20or%2C,any%20amount%20or%20any%20other)). So Massachusetts is forgiving about venue errors – you won’t lose your case, it’ll just move and maybe cause a delay. It’s best to pick a proper venue upfront to avoid inconvenience.

**Multiple defendants or locations:** If you have multiple defendants in different counties, venue is proper where *any* one of them lives or does business. So choose a venue that’s allowed for at least one defendant. If one defendant is in-state and another out-of-state, you can file where the in-state one is, and attempt out-of-state service for the other (subject to jurisdiction issues – see Service section). If you have no idea where the defendant is now (moved), you might file where they last lived or where the transaction happened – but you may need to track them down or use alternate service.

**Online filing availability:** Yes – Massachusetts offers an **online filing** process for small claims through the eFileMA system (Tyler Technologies). The Mass.gov site encourages using “File online: the easiest way to file” ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/small-claims#:~:text=File%20your%20small%20claim)). This is essentially a guided interview (sometimes called “Guide & File”) where you input information and it generates the forms for you, and you can submit electronically. The e-filing portal for small claims is [massachusetts.tylertech.cloud](https://massachusetts.tylertech.cloud/) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/small-claims#:~:text=File%20your%20small%20claim)). You need to create an account, then you can fill in the Statement of Claim and pay the filing fee by credit card. Many people still file in person or by mail, but e-filing is convenient and available statewide (as of mid-2020s). 

For example, if you want to file a small claim in Middlesex County, you can do it online and select the appropriate District Court (like Waltham District Court, etc.) during the process. The online system also helps ensure you include necessary info like address verification if required.

**Where physically to file in person:** If you opt to file in person, you’d go to the **Clerk’s Office of the District Court** that serves the town where you’re filing. For Boston, the BMC has divisions (like BMC Central, Roxbury, etc.) depending on where in Boston. For Housing Court, you’d go to that court’s clerk’s office. You’ll fill out a Statement of Small Claim form and pay the fee (cash/check). Clerk-magistrates are generally helpful if you have questions on which court or how to fill forms, though they can’t give legal advice.

**Effect of filing in wrong venue:** As mentioned, not fatal – transfer rather than dismissal is the remedy in Massachusetts if venue is improper and contested ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=Notwithstanding%20the%20foregoing%2C%20each%20court,An%20action%20may%20be%20commenced)). If nobody raises it, the case just goes forward. Judges themselves typically won’t toss it for improper venue if defendant doesn’t object (because law says it’s waived) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section21#:~:text=Notwithstanding%20the%20foregoing%2C%20each%20court,therein%2C%20and%20all%20prior%20proceedings)). So, for strategic reasons, a plaintiff might pick their home court even if defendant is far – knowing defendant might not bother to contest. But be careful: if it greatly prejudices the defendant, they likely will move to transfer.

**Jurisdictional issues:** Small claims in MA only have jurisdiction over Massachusetts parties or out-of-state parties that have sufficient contacts with MA (under the long-arm statute, M.G.L. c.223A). If you try to file against an out-of-state defendant with no ties, the court may lack personal jurisdiction. They might still accept the filing, but the defendant could later contest service or jurisdiction. For instance, if you sue a New Hampshire resident for a car accident that happened in NH, in a MA small claims, the NH defendant could ignore it and later you’d have trouble enforcing because MA lacked jurisdiction. So ensure there is some connection (the cause of action happened in MA, or defendant does business in MA, etc.) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-3-notice-to-defendant-answer-to-claim#:~:text=address%20or%20addresses%20supplied%20by,cases%20as%20is%20deemed%20necessary)). The small claims statute doesn’t change jurisdictional requirements; it just simplifies process. For out-of-state defendants, service must follow the long-arm law (usually personal service out of state or certified mail with return receipt) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-3-notice-to-defendant-answer-to-claim#:~:text=address%20or%20addresses%20supplied%20by,cases%20as%20is%20deemed%20necessary)) – see Service section.

**Choosing Housing Court vs District Court:** If your case is landlord-tenant or involves housing issues, you can file in Housing Court (if one has jurisdiction over the city/town in question). Housing Courts exclusively handle housing matters, often more tenant-friendly and specialized. For example, a security deposit claim in Boston could be filed in Boston Housing Court small claims or in the BMC small claims – either is fine. Some attorneys prefer Housing Court because the clerks and mediators know the laws intimately. Also, Housing Court provides **mediation services** for small claims – often a mediator will try to resolve landlord-tenant money disputes before the magistrate hearing. If you’re a tenant with a 3x deposit claim, Housing Court might be a venue of choice. But District Courts also know these laws (especially since 3x deposit is straightforward now). It’s your choice; legally both have equal power to hear small claims (the plaintiff chooses the forum). 

**Filing against businesses:** To sue a company, you determine venue by where the company has an office or does business, or where you as plaintiff reside. If it’s an out-of-state corporation but registered in MA, they have a registered agent in MA – you could file where you live. Check if the company is registered in MA (via Secretary of State’s website). If not, you may need to sue them in their home state or see if any local presence gives jurisdiction.

In summary: file your case at a District Court (or BMC/Housing Court) that is geographically appropriate as described. Use online filing if possible. And remember to double-check that the court you picked covers the city/town you think – each District Court’s jurisdiction is by town. Mass.gov has a list: e.g., “Cambridge District Court covers Cambridge, Arlington, Belmont” etc. If you file in the wrong District (e.g., you filed in Cambridge but the defendant lives in Somerville which is under Somerville District Court, and neither of you live Cambridge’s area), the court might transfer it to Somerville or ask you to refile. So, a quick check or a call to the clerk can verify venue if unsure.

## 4. Filing fees  
Massachusetts has a two-tiered filing fee system for small claims, plus a mandated surcharge. The fees depend on the amount of the claim:

- For claims up to **$500.00**, the filing **entry fee** is **$20** ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/massachusetts/part-iii/title-i/chapter-218/section-22/#:~:text=Section%2022,shall%20include%20provisions%20for%20early)).  
- For claims over $500 (i.e., **$500.01 up to $7,000**), the entry fee is **$30** ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/massachusetts/part-iii/title-i/chapter-218/section-22/#:~:text=Section%2022,shall%20include%20provisions%20for%20early)).  

These fees are set by statute (M.G.L. c.218 §22) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/massachusetts/part-iii/title-i/chapter-218/section-22/#:~:text=Section%2022,shall%20include%20provisions%20for%20early)). In addition to the entry fee, Massachusetts law imposes a **surcharge** on civil filings. For small claims, the surcharge is **$10** ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleVI/Chapter262/Section4c#:~:text=Section%204C,credited%20to%20the%20General%20Fund)) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleVI/Chapter262/Section4c#:~:text=filing%20fee%20is%20payable%2C%20and,collected%20pursuant%20to%20this%20section)) (lower than the $15 on other civil cases because the statute has a special line for small claims: “A party entering a small claims action shall pay a surcharge of $10” ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleVI/Chapter262/Section4c#:~:text=Section%204C,credited%20to%20the%20General%20Fund))). 

So in total:  
– If your claim is $\le  $500, you pay $20 + $10 = **$30** total.  
– If your claim is > $500, you pay $30 + $10 = **$40** total.  

These are the fees as of 2026 (they’ve remained at these levels for many years; last changed around 2009 when the surcharge was added). 

If you file electronically, the system will calculate and charge the appropriate amount. If you file in person or by mail, you should include a check or money order to the court for the correct total. 

**Multiple defendants:** You pay one filing fee per case (not per defendant). So if you sue two people in one claim, it’s still $30 or $40 depending on claim amount. 

**Service fees:** Massachusetts small claims uses **certified mail or sheriff/constable** for service (the initial default method is the clerk sends notice by first-class mail ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-3-notice-to-defendant-answer-to-claim#:~:text=The%20clerk%20shall%20promptly%20send,cases%20as%20is%20deemed%20necessary)); if that fails or if you request otherwise, then other methods). The cost of service can vary:
- **Certified Mail by Clerk:** The court will send out the Notice of Small Claim to the defendant via first-class mail for free. If that letter is not returned undelivered, service is considered done ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-3-notice-to-defendant-answer-to-claim#:~:text=The%20clerk%20shall%20promptly%20send,cases%20as%20is%20deemed%20necessary)). The court also often sends a second copy by certified mail or certificate of mailing. Some courts might charge a small fee for the mailing (often it’s included in the entry fee & surcharge – historically, the $10 surcharge helps cover mailing). Typically you do *not* have to separately pay for the initial mailing. If the mail is returned as undeliverable, you may have to arrange another method and pay for it yourself (and later recover it as costs if you win).
- **Deputy Sheriff or Constable:** If personal service is needed (for instance, an out-of-state defendant, or the mail didn’t work), you can hire a sheriff/constable. Fees for that in Massachusetts vary by county, but for in-state within a few towns, it might be ~$35-45 for service of one defendant (plus mileage). If you need to serve out-of-state, you might pay an out-of-state sheriff or process server – those costs could be higher. You can ask the court clerk if they will send by certified mail to an out-of-state address – rule says out-of-state service “shall be made pursuant to G.L. c.223A” ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-3-notice-to-defendant-answer-to-claim#:~:text=address%20or%20addresses%20supplied%20by,cases%20as%20is%20deemed%20necessary)), which usually means certified mail with return receipt or personal service. If you opt for sheriff service, you pay them directly.
- **Publication or other alternate service:** Rare in small claims, but if you truly can’t find the defendant, you’d have to motion the court for permission to do alternate service (like publication in a newspaper). This costs money too (newspaper fees). Small claims rules allow court to order other service if necessary ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-3-notice-to-defendant-answer-to-claim#:~:text=that%20it%20is%20not%20returned,cases%20as%20is%20deemed%20necessary)). But this is last resort and you’d pay those publication fees.

**Fee waivers (Indigency):** If you cannot afford the filing fee, Massachusetts law provides for an “**Indigent Court Costs**” waiver (per M.G.L. c.261 §§27A-27G) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=A%20claim%20may%20be%20filed,The%20date%20the%20Statement%20of)). You can file an **Affidavit of Indigency** (this form is available at the court or online) detailing your income, assets, and expenses. If the clerk or judge finds you qualify (generally, if you receive certain public benefits or your income is below 125% of poverty, or you have otherwise inability to pay), the court can waive the entry fee and surcharge (and even service fees if using a sheriff). On the form, you can also request the **sheriff’s fees** to be covered by the Commonwealth if personal service is needed. Massachusetts is quite accommodating to indigent litigants – the standard is if paying the fees would deprive you of necessities or you’re on assistance (MassHealth, SSI, etc.), you get the waiver ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=A%20claim%20may%20be%20filed,The%20date%20the%20Statement%20of)). The clerk usually can approve routine waivers; if there’s a question, a judge might hold an indigency hearing but that’s uncommon for small claims amounts. If approved, you pay $0 to file, and it will be noted that you filed in forma pauperis. 

**Serving fee waivers:** If you’ve had your filing fee waived and you need a deputy sheriff to serve the papers (say, mail failed or it’s out-of-state), you can also ask that the service costs be paid by the state (there’s a section on the Affidavit of Indigency to request that the court “assign the sheriff/constable” and waive those costs too). The court would then issue process and the sheriff would bill the Commonwealth’s committee for public counsel or equivalent fund for indigency. This prevents fees from blocking access to justice.

**Recoverable fees in judgment:** If you paid filing and service fees and you win your case, you can ask that those fees be added to your judgment against the defendant. Massachusetts small claims judges typically award **“court costs”** which include the entry fee and surcharge, and any reasonable service fees ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/massachusetts/part-iii/title-i/chapter-218/section-22/#:~:text=Section%2022,other%20chapters%2C%20sections%20or%20acts)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/massachusetts/part-iii/title-i/chapter-218/section-22/#:~:text=special%20order%20of%20court%2C%20of,extend%20or%20vacate%20such%20order)). (The law explicitly mentions costs can be in court’s discretion in small claims rules ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/massachusetts/part-iii/title-i/chapter-218/section-22/#:~:text=pleading%20and%20practice%2C%20anything%20contained,fees%20and%20costs%2C%20and%20that)), but in practice it’s routine to grant them to the prevailing party.) If you lost, you might have to eat your own fees. If both parties win something (like both have claims), the costs might be offset or each bear their own.

**Other potential fees:** Massachusetts doesn’t have a “motion fee” or separate trial fee in small claims. The initial filing fee covers the whole process (including one appeal to the jury session actually – though note, appealing to a jury session requires a $25 fee and a $100 appeal bond typically ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/massachusetts/part-iii/title-i/chapter-218/section-23/#:~:text=A%20defendant%27s%20claim%20for%20trial,pursuant%20to%20the%20provisions%20of)); that’s different from the initial filing fee – see Appeals section). But for initial filing: it’s just $30 or $40. No fee for filing a counterclaim as noted ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-3-notice-to-defendant-answer-to-claim#:~:text=In%20the%20answer%2C%20or%20in,If)), no fee for requesting a payment hearing, etc.

**Fee if case transferred or appealed:** If a defendant appeals to the jury trial (new trial) after a small claims decision, they must pay $25 entry for the appeal ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/massachusetts/part-iii/title-i/chapter-218/section-23/#:~:text=A%20defendant%27s%20claim%20for%20trial,the%20foregoing%2C%20in%20any%20action)), but that’s not at initial filing stage. If your case ended up transferred to another district due to venue, usually no additional fee (the original fee covers it; they just shift it over).

**Payment of fees:** When filing in person, they take cash, check, money order, possibly credit cards depending on the court (not all accept cards at the counter). If e-filing, payment is by credit/debit card or ACH. If by mail, check or money order to “Commonwealth of Massachusetts” with the court name often acceptable.

In summary, Massachusetts small claims fees are **$30 or $40** including the surcharge, which is relatively low compared to some states. Be sure to file in the correct amount category, because if you accidentally put an amount slightly over a tier (like $500 exactly is the cutoff between $20 and $30 fee?), note it’s “$500 or less” for $20, “greater than $500” for $30 ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/massachusetts/part-iii/title-i/chapter-218/section-22/#:~:text=Section%2022,shall%20include%20provisions%20for%20early)). $500 exactly = $20 fee; $500.01 = $30 fee. If in doubt, clerks will tell you.

Remember, these fees can be waived if you qualify, and can be recovered from the other side if you win.

## 5. Statute of limitations  
Massachusetts statutes of limitations (the deadlines to file a lawsuit) apply to small claims the same as they do in regular civil court. Filing within the limitation period is crucial – if you file late and the defendant raises it as a defense, your case will be dismissed as time-barred. Below are the common statutes of limitations (per M.G.L. c.260) by claim type in Massachusetts:

- **Written contracts:** **6 years** – Actions based on a contract or promise that is in writing must be commenced within 6 years after the cause of action accrues ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=Massachusetts%20laws)). (Cause accrues typically at the time of breach of the contract.) For example, if someone breached a written service agreement on Jan 1, 2020, you have until Jan 1, 2026 to file.

- **Oral contracts (and implied contracts):** **6 years** – Massachusetts does not differentiate between written and oral for the general contract limitation; both fall under the 6-year limit in most cases ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=Massachusetts%20laws)) (c.260 §2 for contracts in writing or not under seal, and §5 for oral contracts, which also gives 6 years). So handshake deals have 6 years from breach.

- **Sale of goods (U.C.C. Article 2 contracts):** **4 years** – If your claim is specifically for breach of a contract for the sale of goods, the Uniform Commercial Code (M.G.L. c.106 §2-725) sets a 4-year statute of limitations from when the breach occurs ([caretaker.com](https://caretaker.com/learn/habitability/repair-and-deduct-laws-in-massachusetts#:~:text=Massachusetts%20law%20specifically%20allows%20tenants,they%20can%20spend%20on%20repairs)). This is an exception to the general 6-year contract rule. For instance, if you bought a product and it was defective, a breach of warranty claim must be within 4 years of the sale. Many consumer product cases would use this. (However, if you frame it as a consumer protection 93A claim, those have 4 years anyway – see below.)

- **Promissory notes:** **6 years** – A promissory note (not under seal) is a written promise to pay money; enforcement is 6 years from the due date or last payment. If the note is under seal, Massachusetts allows 20 years, but most notes aren’t sealed (a seal in MA usually means specific language or notary and is rare nowadays).

- **Property damage (not arising from contract):** **3 years** – For damage or trespass to personal or real property, the limitation is generally 3 years from the date the damage was incurred or discovered ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=Massachusetts%20laws)) (c.260 §2A covers “actions of tort… and replevin” at 3 years ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=MGL%20c,shall%20be%20commenced%20only%20within))). So if someone dented your car or cut your trees, you have 3 years from that event to sue. Massachusetts follows the discovery rule for some torts (for example, if property damage was inherently undiscoverable when it happened, the clock might start when you knew or should have known). But straightforward property damage (car accidents, etc.) is from date of accident.

- **Personal injury (negligence or intentional tort):** **3 years** – Actions for personal injuries (like slip and fall, car accident injuries, assault and battery injuries) must be filed within 3 years of the date of injury or date you discovered the injury ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=Massachusetts%20laws)). Massachusetts c.260 §2A sets 3 years for “actions of tort… to recover for personal injuries” ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=MGL%20c,Replevin%20is%20an%20action)). If an injury was not apparent right away, a discovery rule may apply (e.g., in some toxic exposure cases, though those often not in small claims). But for most small personal injuries, assume 3 years from incident.

- **Medical malpractice:** **3 years** generally, but with a discovery rule and a statute of repose of 7 years (except for foreign object cases). However, med mal is not going to be in small claims, as discussed, due to procedure requiring a tribunal. So not too relevant here except to say you wouldn’t do med mal in small claims.

- **Fraud and conversion:** **3 years** – These are torts, so covered by the 3-year limit of §2A. If someone defrauded you or stole your property, you have 3 years from when the fraud was (or reasonably should have been) discovered ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-engagement-rings#:~:text=MGL%20c,or%20held%20by%20the%20defendant)). For instance, if in 2018 you found out in 2020 that your friend embezzled your funds in 2018, you’d have until 2023 (3 years from discovery in 2020) to sue for fraud. Massachusetts does apply discovery rule to fraud because by nature it’s hidden.

- **Consumer protection (Chapter 93A) claims:** **4 years** – M.G.L. c.260 §5A specifically sets a 4-year limit for unfair or deceptive practices claims under c.93A ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-small-claims-and-bringing-a-claim-to-court#:~:text=,sources%20below%20or%C2%A0discuss%C2%A0with%20an%20attorney)). So if you have a UDAP claim against a business – for example, they sold you something in 2019 and it was deceptive – you need to sue by 2023. The clock starts when the incident or transaction occurred or when you suffered loss from the practice. Note: making a 93A demand letter doesn’t toll the statute, so ensure you send that with enough time to still file within 4 years (the 30-day wait for their response is built in, but you might need to send by year 3.5 to be safe).

- **Security deposit and landlord/tenant statutory claims:** Most of these would fall under the general 3 or 4 or 6-year periods. For instance, an action under c.186 §15B (security deposits) could be viewed as contract or statutory penalty. Case law indicates it is a contract or statutorily-created right with a 3-year limit (some sources treat it as consumer protection – unclear, but likely 3 or 4; however Massachusetts Trial Court guidelines mention security deposit triple-damages claims should ideally be brought within 3 years ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-tenants-security-deposits#:~:text=Castenholz%20v,interest%2C%20costs%2C%20and%20attorney%E2%80%99s%20fees)), since it’s akin to a penalty). For safety, tenants should sue within 3 years of the tenancy’s end for deposit claims. (The Mass.gov site implies 3 years for many housing money claims ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-tenants-security-deposits#:~:text=Castenholz%20v,interest%2C%20costs%2C%20and%20attorney%E2%80%99s%20fees)).)

- **Wage claims (non-payment of wages):** **3 years** – The Massachusetts Wage Act and overtime/minimum wage claims have a 3-year statute of limitations from the date of violation ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter149/Section150#:~:text=An%20employee%20claiming%20to%20be,employee%20files%20a%20complaint%20with)). (Each paycheck violation is separate, but generally final wages or overtime, 3 years.) This is tolled if you file with the AG’s office until they issue a right-to-sue letter ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter149/Section150#:~:text=An%20employee%20claiming%20to%20be,employee%20files%20a%20complaint%20with)). But the safe approach is to act quickly because wages lost time value and the triple damages apply regardless now, but you don’t want to miss the window.

- **Judgments:** If you actually get a judgment and need to enforce it, the judgment is presumed satisfied after 20 years ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/massachusetts/2006/gl-pt3-toc/260-20.html#:~:text=Section%2020,years%20after%20it%20was%20rendered)). But that’s after you win; this is just noted that you have up to 20 years to enforce a judgment (by Massachusetts law, after 20 years it’s presumed paid) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/massachusetts/2006/gl-pt3-toc/260-20.html#:~:text=Section%2020,years%20after%20it%20was%20rendered)). However, that’s beyond scope of “filing” limitations.

**When does the clock start?** Generally:
  - For contract: when the breach occurred. If it’s a loan payable on a certain date, from that due date. If installment payments, each missed payment might start its own clock, or the entire debt’s clock might start at acceleration.
  - For tort: when the injury or damage happened, or when it was discovered (if discovery rule applies due to inherently hidden harm).
  - For consumer protection: when the unfair act occurred or when you knew of it.
  - The phrase “accrues” is key: a cause of action “accrues” at the time the plaintiff could first have sued on it. 

**Tolling exceptions:** Massachusetts can toll (pause) the statute of limitations in a few circumstances:
  - **Minor or legally insane plaintiff:** If you were a minor or declared mentally incompetent at the time of cause, the statute may be tolled until the disability is removed (e.g., until you turn 18). M.G.L. c.260 §7 tolls for minors/mental incapacity but for a maximum of 6 years extension (and not beyond 3 years after disability ends) for some torts.
  - **Defendant out of state:** If the defendant is out of Massachusetts and cannot be served here for a period, that period might not count (c.260 §9) – absence tolls the limit because one couldn’t reasonably sue them here during absence, unless you could serve them by other means. But with long-arm jurisdiction, tolling for absence is less frequently applied now.
  - **Fraudulent concealment:** If the defendant actively concealed the wrongdoing, the statute doesn’t start until the plaintiff discovered or should have discovered the cause (c.260 §12).
  - **Bankruptcy stay:** If the defendant is in bankruptcy, the automatic stay would toll any limitation to bring claims against them.
  - **Negotiation/mediation tolling:** Not statutory, but if parties agree to toll while negotiating, that can extend time.

For small claims specifically, there’s no special extension – the general rules apply. The court won’t check the statute of limitations for you when filing; it’s up to the defendant to raise it as a defense. But magistrates might dismiss if it's obviously way out of time and the defendant points it out. Usually, the defendant has to explicitly invoke the SOL defense, otherwise it’s waived. However, many lay defendants don’t know about that – but a magistrate might still ask, “When did this happen?” and if you say “10 years ago,” they might themselves note it’s too late. So be mindful.

**Practical examples:**
- You loaned money on Jan 1, 2016 with no written contract, repayable on demand. You demand on Jan 1, 2020, they don’t pay. Arguably the clock starts when you demanded repayment (2020). But an alternative view is a loan with no term is due on reasonable time – likely start when loan made or soon after. Err on safe side and don’t exceed 6 years from loan date; here you’d file by Jan 1, 2022 ideally. Massachusetts case law on demand loans says SOL runs from the demand, but some which are indefinite might eventually be deemed stale if no demand for decades.
- Security deposit from a tenancy that ended August 2019, not returned – file by August 2022 (3 years) to be safe.
- Car accident on July 1, 2019 – file injury claim by July 1, 2022 (3 yrs). If property damage only, file by July 1, 2022 as well (3 yrs for property tort).
- Sale of defective goods on March 1, 2018 – warranty claim by March 1, 2022 (4 yrs).
- Unpaid wages for work done in September 2019 – file by Sept 2022 (3 yrs).
- Debt collection: if it’s a credit card debt from last payment in 2015, by 2021 it’s stale (6 years for breach of credit agreement or 4 years if viewed as sale of goods (credit not goods though) – credit cards are written contracts: 6 years in MA). So debt buyers must sue within 6, otherwise a defendant can assert SOL.

**Check specific statutes:** Always good to verify particular causes. For example, **judgment enforcement** (20 yrs we mentioned), **Claim on a debt judgment** from another state – Massachusetts allows those within the time allowed in that state or Massachusetts (whichever shorter, typically).
**Special laws:** M.G.L. c.93 §13 for 93A says 4 yrs ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-small-claims-and-bringing-a-claim-to-court#:~:text=,sources%20below%20or%C2%A0discuss%C2%A0with%20an%20attorney)); M.G.L. c.260 §2B for architects/engineers malpractice is 3 yrs discovery/6 yr repose (but that’s beyond small claims context usually).

In short, most small claims will fall under: **6 years for contracts**, **3 years for torts**, or **4 years for consumer protection**. When in doubt, assume the shortest potentially applicable period to be safe.

If your claim is close to expiring, file as soon as possible. If it’s expired, consider if any tolling or the possibility the other side might not raise it (not a strategy to rely on, but if they default, they waive that defense – however search your conscience/legal ethics; you are allowed to file even if SOL expired because it’s an affirmative defense the other must assert).

## 6. Pre-filing requirements  
Before filing a small claim in Massachusetts, consider if any **pre-filing steps** are required or recommended for your type of dispute. Massachusetts generally does not require a formal demand letter for most small claims, **except** in special circumstances (notably consumer protection claims). However, even when not required, it’s often wise to **send a demand or warning** and attempt to resolve the issue first.

Key pre-filing considerations:

**30-Day Demand Letter (Consumer Protection – c.93A):** If you intend to claim that the defendant’s actions were an unfair or deceptive business practice under the Consumer Protection Act (c.93A) and seek multiple damages or attorney’s fees, you **must** send a written demand to the business at least **30 days** before filing suit ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/30-day-demand-letter#:~:text=Unfair%20or%20deceptive%20practices%20by,triple%20damages%20and%20attorney%27s%20fees)). The letter must state your identity, describe the unfair act, the injury or loss suffered, and what you demand for a resolution ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/30-day-demand-letter#:~:text=Unfair%20or%20deceptive%20practices%20by,triple%20damages%20and%20attorney%27s%20fees)). It’s often called the “93A demand letter.” The business then has 30 days to respond with a settlement offer. If they don’t respond or make a unsatisfactory offer, you can proceed to file suit. If you skip this letter and go straight to suing under 93A, the court can dismiss the 93A portion of your claim (you’d then only potentially recover actual damages under common law, no double or treble or fees) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/30-day-demand-letter#:~:text=Unfair%20or%20deceptive%20practices%20by,triple%20damages%20and%20attorney%27s%20fees)). **Exceptions:** The 93A letter isn’t required if the defendant does not maintain a place of business or assets in MA (e.g., an out-of-state scammer) or if it’s a suit by a business against another business (c.93A §11 doesn’t strictly require a demand, though often one is sent anyway). But for consumer vs business cases – always send it. *Example:* You’re suing an auto dealer for deceptive sale of a car – send a 30-day demand to the dealer’s address (preferably by certified mail for proof). Then after 30 days (or after receiving a refusal letter sooner), you can file your small claim. Include a copy of your demand letter and any response with your court papers to show you complied (the court may ask for it at trial). 

**Notice to Landlord for Security Deposit claims:** Massachusetts doesn’t explicitly require a tenant to demand their deposit back in writing before suing – though it’s good practice. Under c.186 §15B, once 30 days pass after tenancy without the landlord returning deposit or accounting properly, the treble damages provision kicks in automatically ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-tenants-security-deposits#:~:text=Castenholz%20v,interest%2C%20costs%2C%20and%20attorney%E2%80%99s%20fees)). You can sue without further notice. However, many tenants will have already asked informally. No extra notice is needed because the statute sort of acts as notice by outlining deadlines. Similarly, for last month’s rent interest, the tenant can demand the interest in writing after each year and if landlord doesn’t pay in 30 days, landlord forfeits triple interest ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-tenants-security-deposits#:~:text=Castenholz%20v,interest%2C%20costs%2C%20and%20attorney%E2%80%99s%20fees)) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/Partii/Titlei/Chapter186/Section15b#:~:text=amount%20equal%20to%20three%20times,costs%20and%20reasonable%20attorneys%20fees)); but even if you didn’t formally demand interest, you can still sue for the base interest (the treble might not apply unless you demanded). It’s wise to send a letter or email saying “I haven’t gotten my deposit, please return it” before suing – it may lead to payment without court. But legally, by day 31 after move-out, you have a cause of action. 

**M.G.L. c.93A (landlord/tenant issues):** Some tenant claims can also be framed as 93A (e.g., failure to make repairs might be a breach of warranty actionable under 93A if it’s a business landlord). If you plan to invoke 93A on a landlord (assuming the landlord is in business of renting, not an owner-occupied 2-family exempt from 93A for that relationship), you’d do a 30-day letter as well. But common practice is many tenants just sue under the security deposit law directly or use defenses in eviction. For small claims, usually it’s straightforward enough.

**Wage claims – Attorney General complaint:** As noted in Section 5, to sue for unpaid wages or similar under the Wage Act, you must file a complaint with the Massachusetts Attorney General’s Office (Fair Labor Division) and either wait 90 days or receive a right-to-sue letter (sooner if they allow) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter149/Section150#:~:text=An%20employee%20claiming%20to%20be,employee%20files%20a%20complaint%20with)). This is required by M.G.L. c.149 §150. If you file a small claims for wages without doing this, the employer could move to dismiss for failure to exhaust administrative remedy. The court would likely dismiss it without prejudice and tell you to go to the AG. The AG has an online form; after ~90 days you can request a letter to proceed with a private suit (they often grant it quickly unless they’re actively investigating the employer). So for wage cases, **Pre-filing**: file with AG’s office and obtain letter. If time is an issue (like 3-year SOL running out), in practice you can file with AG near the end, and the law tolls the SOL while the AG has the complaint ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter149/Section150#:~:text=An%20employee%20claiming%20to%20be,employee%20files%20a%20complaint%20with)). But you can’t start the court case until that period ends or they give permission.

**Mediation or demand for payment in contracts:** Massachusetts doesn’t require formal pre-suit mediation for small claims. But some contracts might: if you have a written contract with a clause, “the parties will mediate disputes before litigating,” that could be enforceable. It’s rare in consumer contexts for small stuff, but if present, consider at least offering mediation to the other side so you can say you tried. For example, home improvement contracts sometimes have an arbitration clause – if it’s binding arbitration, that could bar a court claim (the contractor might move to compel arbitration). The small claims court might not enforce an arbitration clause rigorously if nobody brings it up, but legally it’s a defense. So check your contract if any, for arbitration or mediation requirements. Massachusetts does allow defendants to compel arbitration if a valid agreement exists, even in small claims (though in small claims scenario, some arbitrations might cost more than the claim – which could be unconscionable). If an arbitration clause exists, you might still file and see if they raise it.

**For debt collection cases (plaintiff side):** Aside from the special verification-of-address requirement (Section 13 covers that), Massachusetts doesn’t require that creditors send a “due process” letter before suit (some other states do, but not here). However, under federal law (FDCPA), third-party debt collectors must send a 30-day validation notice *after* initial communication, typically before suit. In practice, most collection suits are preceded by demand letters or multiple bills. For original creditors, a final demand is just good practice. If you’re a business plaintiff, sending a demand might trigger a Chapter 93A response that could limit your damages if not (since a debtor could counterclaim under 93A if you engaged in unfair collection). But not a requirement.

**For negligence or property damage:** No official demand letter needed. Still, it can be smart to send a polite letter: “On X date you damaged my property, it cost $Y, please pay by Z or I will consider legal action.” This might prod a neighbor or friend to pay without a case. If you plan to sue a municipality for property damage (like a city plow damaged your fence), you should send a **Chapter 84 pothole or defect notice** within 30 days if it’s a road defect claim (for personal injuries from a defect, not so much for property but advisable if trying to hold town liable for road defect property damage – by law, town liability for road defects is limited and requires notice). For example, damage by a pothole to your car, Massachusetts law requires notice to municipality within 30 days or claim may be barred (M.G.L. c.84 §18). So that’s a pre-suit notice if suing a town for highway defects – but note, town’s liability for property damage to vehicles from road defects is capped at $5k by statute.

**Insurance claims:** If your claim involves insurance (like you’re injured and making a claim on someone’s auto policy), you usually don’t sue the insurance company in small claims – you sue the person who caused harm. But Massachusetts does have a consumer protection reg requiring you to send a 93A demand to an insurance company for unfair claim settlement practices at least 30 days before suing them under c.93A/176D. That’s a specific scenario: if you wanted to sue an insurer for bad faith (not typical in small claims because those are big cases, but mentionable). In small claims, you mostly won’t deal with direct suits against insurers except maybe a small property claim if your own insurer didn’t pay (that could be breach of contract – 6 years – and you likely had an internal appeals process to exhaust first).

**For specialized disputes:**
- If suing under the lemon law’s arbitration provision, you must go through state-certified arbitration before suing the manufacturer for a refund on a new car. But rarely would that come to small claims because of amounts.
- If you’re a condo owner suing the condo association, check the condo docs if they require arbitration or internal dispute resolution first.
- If you had a **home improvement contract dispute** and there’s an arbitration clause referencing the state-run program, note Massachusetts had a Home Improvement Contractor arbitration program (administered by OCABR) for disputes, but it’s voluntary. You can opt for their arbitration before suing and it can help with Guaranty Fund recovery. It’s not mandatory though; you can go straight to court if you prefer.

**Recommendation (even when not required):** It’s usually recommended to send a **demand letter or at least an email** to the other party stating your claim and giving them a chance to pay or settle before you file. Why? Because:
  - They might pay and save you both time.
  - It creates a paper trail of you being reasonable, which judges appreciate.
  - In some instances (like 93A), if they make a reasonable settlement offer and you refuse and then get no better result in court, you might not get attorney’s fees or multiples beyond their offer date ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/30-day-demand-letter#:~:text=outline%20your%20complaint%2C%20the%20harm,triple%20damages%20and%20attorney%27s%20fees)). So taking the demand-letter process seriously can benefit you.

**Notice prerequisites (landlord-tenant):** If you were a tenant looking to use the “repair and deduct” remedy we talked about, the law required you to give the landlord written notice and 14 days to fix (or 5 days to commence repairs) ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXVI/Chapter111/Section127L#:~:text=inspection%2C%20or%20by%20a%20court,tenants%20may%20subsequently%20deduct%20from)) ([caretaker.com](https://caretaker.com/learn/habitability/repair-and-deduct-laws-in-massachusetts#:~:text=Landlords%20have%20five%20days%20to,repairs%E2%80%94and%2014%20to%20finish%20them)) and possibly involve the Board of Health to certify the condition ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXVI/Chapter111/Section127L#:~:text=Section%20127L,a%20third%20party%20for%20such)). If you didn’t do those steps, your right to deduct is compromised. But that would come up as a defense if landlord sued for rent or you counterclaimed. In small claims, if you proactively sue for code violations, it’s good that you had Board of Health documentation. Not strictly a “pre-filing requirement,” but a best practice is to call in an inspection during tenancy issues.

**Small Claims Demand for Payment Form:** While not required, the Mass. Trial Court provides a form letter (somewhere) that you can send to someone to demand payment, stating you intend to go to small claims if not resolved. There’s also an official **Procedure of Small Claims notice** that describes fees and so on; sometimes clerks tell people to send a last demand referencing small claims. This isn’t law, just a courtesy sometimes recommended.

So to summarize: 
- **Demand letters are mandatory for c.93A consumer claims ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/30-day-demand-letter#:~:text=Unfair%20or%20deceptive%20practices%20by,triple%20damages%20and%20attorney%27s%20fees)) and wise for any dispute**.
- **AG complaint required for wage claims ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter149/Section150#:~:text=An%20employee%20claiming%20to%20be,employee%20files%20a%20complaint%20with))**.
- **Notice of claim to government if suing a municipality** (within 30 days for road defect, or within 2 years for general tort via presentment letter to city under c.258 §4).
- **Check contract for arbitration/notice clauses**.
- **Otherwise, recommended courtesy demand or mediation attempt.**

Massachusetts has a strong consumer-friendly stance, but also encourages resolution. In fact, many District Courts offer **free mediation** on the day of small claims hearing (and at Housing Court for housing claims) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/small-claims#:~:text=Expand%20all%20Collapse%20all%20the,s)). So even if you didn’t settle beforehand, the court might ask you to step outside with a mediator on hearing day. It doesn’t hurt your case to show you tried to resolve it earlier.

## 7. Forms required  
Filing a small claim in Massachusetts involves a few standard forms. The main form every case needs is the **Statement of Small Claim** (sometimes just called the “Complaint” form for small claims). Additional forms may be needed for particular scenarios (like for businesses or after filing). Below are the forms and their uses, and whether there are form numbers:

- **Statement of Small Claim and Notice of Trial:** This is the primary **complaint form** you file to start the case. It includes sections for plaintiff and defendant information, a brief statement of the claim, the amount claimed, and a notice of trial date (which the clerk will fill in and mail to the defendant). In Massachusetts, this form is often available as a multi-part carbonless form at the clerk’s office or as a PDF online:
  
  - The District Court/BMC version is often titled “Statement of Claim and Notice” with no specific number (it might be referenced as Trial Court form DC-SC-1 or similar internally, but not always labeled on the PDF). The Housing Court may have its variant but content is same.
  
  On this form, you’ll fill in:
    - **Parties:** Your name/address and the defendant’s name/address. If the defendant is a business, use the correct legal name (e.g., “ABC, Inc.” or “John Doe d/b/a Doe’s Painting”). 
    - **Amount claimed:** The sum of money you are asking for (including any interest up to date of filing, but excluding court costs – costs and statutory interest will be added by clerk). Indicate if you seek interest by checking a box or writing “plus interest from [date]”.
    - **Statement of claim:** A concise explanation of why the defendant owes you money. Keep it short: e.g., “Breach of contract: Defendant failed to deliver goods after payment of $___ on [date].” Or “negligence: Defendant’s vehicle hit Plaintiff’s fence causing $___ damage on [date].” The clerks prefer this short; you can attach a page if more detail is needed, but typically a sentence or two suffices. This is not a detailed complaint – it’s meant to give notice.
    - **Date and signature:** You sign and date it, certifying that the statements are true to the best of your knowledge.
    - **Address verification (for business plaintiffs):** If you are a business plaintiff or assignee, some new forms have a section asking for the verification of defendant’s address pursuant to Rule 2(b) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=,commerce%20or%20pursuing%20assigned%20debt)). If not, you’ll attach the separate form for that (see below).
  
  The court clerk will assign a **docket number** and a **trial date** when you file, writing it on the Notice part of the form. A copy of this Notice with trial info will be given/mailed to you and mailed to the defendant(s). In e-filing, the system generates an electronic form and likely you’ll get a PDF.

  You can obtain this form from the Massachusetts Trial Court’s website or at any District Court clerk’s office. The Mass.gov site has **Small Claims Court forms** listing, often including:
  
  - *“Statement of Small Claim” (District Court/BMC Civil Action No. 3 or something on older forms).*
  
  As of now (2026), one multi-use form is used statewide. There is also a separate form for if the **state (Commonwealth) is plaintiff**, but that scenario is rare (like state agencies collecting debts – they typically go through other means).

- **Verification of Defendant’s Address (Rule 2(b) form):** If you are **filing on behalf of a trade or commerce** (business claim) or for an assigned debt, Massachusetts rules require a **Verification of Defendant’s Address by Plaintiff** form ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=Any%20plaintiff%20pursuing%20a%20claim,be%20served%20on%20the%20defendant)) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=Statement%20of%20Small%20Claim%20form,be%20served%20on%20the%20defendant)). This is a one-page form where you, the plaintiff, certify that you have confirmed the defendant’s current address within the past 6 months through some reasonable method (like sending mail that wasn’t returned, checking RMV records, doing a lookup, etc.). It has checkboxes for what method you used (e.g., first class mail not returned, contacting defendant, etc.) and you sign it. This form must be filed **with your Statement of Claim** for any such business-related cases ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=Any%20plaintiff%20pursuing%20a%20claim,be%20served%20on%20the%20defendant)). If you e-file, there will be a section to upload it or an online interview question about verification. If you paper file, attach it. The official form is titled *“Verification of Defendant’s Address (Small Claims Rule 2)”*. It’s available on Mass.gov in the small claims forms list. Failing to file this when required can lead to dismissal if the defendant doesn’t appear ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=If%20the%20plaintiff%20fails%20to,shall%20be%20dismissed%20without%20prejudice)), as discussed.

- **Original Creditor Information (as part of Statement of Claim):** This is not a separate form, but a *requirement* within the claim description if you are suing on an assigned debt or trade debt: you must include in your claim statement or an attachment the name of the original creditor if different, the last 4 digits of account, and date/amount of last payment ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=Any%20such%20plaintiff%20shall%20include,is%20filed%20with%20the%20court)). Some updated Statement of Claim forms have blanks for these. If not, you can add a sentence: “Original creditor: XYZ Bank, Acct ####, last payment 5/10/2021 $50.” This helps the defendant identify the debt. It’s mandated by Rule 2(b) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=Any%20such%20plaintiff%20shall%20include,is%20filed%20with%20the%20court)). It’s especially needed if a debt buyer like “Midland Funding” is plaintiff; they must list the credit card or bank that was original.

- **Military Affidavit (Servicemembers Civil Relief Act Affidavit):** Before a default judgment can enter, federal law requires a statement regarding whether the defendant is in military service (to protect active service members from default judgments). In Massachusetts small claims, if the defendant doesn’t show, the magistrate will often require the plaintiff to file a **Military Affidavit**. This is typically a simple form or written statement under oath stating either: “Defendant is not an active service member in the military, to the best of my knowledge” with basis, or if you’re unsure, you so state, or if they are, the court must appoint an attorney for them before proceeding ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-7-trials-hearings-and-judgments#:~:text=et%20seq,first%20appointing%20an%20attorney%20for)) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-7-trials-hearings-and-judgments#:~:text=before%20a%20default%20judgment%20may,her%20neutrality%20and%20include%20the)). The court may provide a form on the spot. You can also prepare one beforehand if you suspect default (by searching the Department of Defense SCRA website for the name and DOB to see status, and printing the result). Massachusetts form is often called “Affidavit of Defendant’s Military Status.” This is **not needed at filing time**, only at default application. But be aware you’ll need it if the defendant doesn’t answer. (In small claims, judgments aren’t separate from trial; basically, if they no-show at hearing, you present the affidavit then to get judgment ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-7-trials-hearings-and-judgments#:~:text=et%20seq,first%20appointing%20an%20attorney%20for)) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-7-trials-hearings-and-judgments#:~:text=%28d%29%282%29%20Uncertain%20Claim,While%C2%A0Rule)).)

- **Application for Payment Order (Financial Statement):** If you win the case and the defendant doesn’t pay, Massachusetts small claims process may involve a “payment hearing” (Rule 7( f )). The court, upon entering judgment, can schedule a payment review hearing and will often send the defendant a **Financial Statement form** to fill out ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-7-trials-hearings-and-judgments#:~:text=match%20at%20L319%20Since%20no,on%20the%20date%20specified%20in)). The defendant must disclose income, assets, etc., and appear. As a plaintiff, if you want to trigger a payment hearing, you might file a form requesting it. However, nowadays the Notice of Judgment itself often instructs the defendant to complete a financial form and come on an assigned date if not paid ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-7-trials-hearings-and-judgments#:~:text=match%20at%20L319%20Since%20no,on%20the%20date%20specified%20in)). If not automatic, you can request it on a court-provided form post-judgment (it might be called “Application for Supplementary Process” in regular civil, but in small claims it’s just part of the small claims procedure, often automatically set 30 days after judgment if unpaid). The form the defendant fills out is typically included on the Notice of Judgment (with fields like employer, expenses, etc.). As a plaintiff you don’t have to file it; it’s for the defendant. But mention for completeness.

- **Appeal form (claim for trial by jury):** If a defendant (or plaintiff, if defendant appeals first) wants to appeal the small claims decision, they must file a **Claim for Trial by Jury** or **Claim for Trial before a Single Justice** form within 10 days ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section23#:~:text=six)). The court clerk has a form for this. It’s often a simple one-page or even a section on the Notice of Judgment that the defendant can sign and submit. It will require paying the $25 fee and a bond (or indigency waiver, etc.) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/massachusetts/part-iii/title-i/chapter-218/section-23/#:~:text=A%20defendant%27s%20claim%20for%20trial,the%20foregoing%2C%20in%20any%20action)). The form might be labeled “Small Claims Appeal – Defendant’s Claim for Jury Trial.” The plaintiff cannot initiate an appeal, only join if defendant appeals ([malegislature.gov](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIII/TitleI/Chapter218/Section23#:~:text=A%20plaintiff%20beginning%20a%20cause,by%20a%20jury%20of%20six)). If it happens, the clerk will give the necessary paperwork. For the scope here: likely not needed unless you lose and you’re the defendant.

- **Motion or request forms:** While small claims is informal, sometimes you might need to file a motion (e.g., to postpone the trial, or to correct a name). There isn’t a special small claims motion form; you typically can write a letter or use a generic motion form. The Trial Court provides a generic “Motion” form (28-line form) but you can use that or just a letter with case info. Common ones: Motion to Continue (postpone) – you’d state reason and propose a new date. There might be a local form for continuance requests. Always include case number and names. Since attorneys are often not involved, the clerks often accommodate a simple written request.

- **Dismissal form:** If you settle or want to withdraw the case, you can file a Notice of Dismissal. There’s not a mandated form, a letter suffices (“Please dismiss case # X as settled”). The clerks might have a short form for voluntary dismissal you can sign.

- **Execution form:** After judgment, if the defendant doesn’t pay, you can request an **Execution** (the official court order to a sheriff to enforce the judgment). In District Court, this is often automatically generated 30 days after judgment if not paid or if no payment plan is in effect. You may need to go to the clerk and ask for it. There is a form called “Request for Execution” possibly; or an execution itself which the clerk prepares on request. The execution is not something you prepare originally; the clerk does it, but you might have to fill in some details. That’s after judgment, not initial filing. The execution lets you have a sheriff do attachment or wage garnishment (as allowed) and so on. It’s printed on security paper.

**Where to get forms:**
- **Mass.gov**: has a page “Small claims court forms” which includes PDF versions of Statement of Small Claim, Verification of Address, Financial Statement, etc. For example, “Plaintiff’s Verification of Defendant’s Address (PDF 1.14 MB)” and “Statement of Small Claim and Notice of Trial – sample” are usually listed.
- **Court Clerk’s Office**: They will provide the needed forms if you go in person. They often help fill out the Statement of Claim if you need.
- **Housing Court**: If using Housing Court, the forms are essentially same. Housing Court might also have a different header but it’s all under Trial Court.
- Many forms in Massachusetts have form numbers like DC-XX or OCAJ forms, but referencing them by name is fine.

**County differences in forms:** There are no significant county-specific forms; it’s standardized statewide now under the Trial Court. Historically, BMC had their own form vs District, but nowadays they use uniform ones. 

**Electronic filing forms:** The Guide & File system takes your answers and generates the Statement of Claim and Verification forms for you, so you might not physically see the form until after submission when you can download a PDF of what was filed.

**Serving forms to defendant:** When you file, the clerk will usually handle serving the Statement of Claim/Notice on the defendant by first-class mail (no separate summons form needed – the Notice of Trial on the form acts as a summons) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-3-notice-to-defendant-answer-to-claim#:~:text=The%20clerk%20shall%20promptly%20send,cases%20as%20is%20deemed%20necessary)). If that fails, you might need to use the “Service by sheriff” forms or process. If you do that, the sheriff will need an attested copy of the Statement of Claim and likely an “Alias Summons” (some courts generate an alias summons if initial mail fails, which is basically another copy marked alias). But often the Statement of Claim itself is good for service.

**Counterclaim form:** If you’re a defendant wanting to file a counterclaim in advance, you can use a standard Answer form or just write a letter. The small claims rules allow it in any written format ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/small-claims-standards-501-counterclaims#:~:text=Rule%203%20requires%20that%20a,the%20court%20stating%20the%20counterclaim)). The courts don’t have a strict separate counterclaim form; on the trial notice form there’s an “Answer” section where you could include it. The Mass reply form may be, e.g., a tear-off on the Notice saying “Defendant’s Answer/Counterclaim: ____ (mail to court and plaintiff).” If not, just write a letter with case number and state your counterclaim.

**Interpreters and accommodations forms:** If you need an interpreter or ADA accommodation, you can request one ahead. There’s a form for interpreter request on Mass.gov (“Interpreter Services Request Form”). This is not mandatory to file suit but advisable to ensure an interpreter is present at hearing if needed.

In summary, to initiate a case you need essentially **one form** (Statement of Claim) plus possibly an **address verification form** if a business plaintiff ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=Any%20plaintiff%20pursuing%20a%20claim,be%20served%20on%20the%20defendant)). Always check with the clerk or online to ensure you use the latest form. And fully complete all parts (like those original creditor details if needed ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=Any%20such%20plaintiff%20shall%20include,is%20filed%20with%20the%20court))). If something doesn’t fit, attach a page rather than cram too much in the little space.

## 8. Service of process  
After filing your small claim, the defendant must be notified of the lawsuit and the hearing date. Massachusetts has a simplified service process for small claims: the court itself will initially attempt service by mail. Here’s how it works and what you may need to do:

**Service by first-class mail (default method):** When you file the Statement of Small Claim, the clerk will **send a copy to the defendant by first-class mail** to the address you provided ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-3-notice-to-defendant-answer-to-claim#:~:text=The%20clerk%20shall%20promptly%20send,cases%20as%20is%20deemed%20necessary)). If that mailing is **not returned** as undeliverable by the post office, it is considered good service ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-3-notice-to-defendant-answer-to-claim#:~:text=The%20clerk%20shall%20promptly%20send,cases%20as%20is%20deemed%20necessary)). This means if the letter doesn’t come back to the court, the court assumes the defendant received it. The Notice of Trial is part of that mailing. This is a convenient and inexpensive method (the cost is covered by the filing fee/surcharge). You as the plaintiff generally do not have to do anything else initially – the clerk handles it.

**Address accuracy:** It’s crucial you give a correct and current address for the defendant. That’s one reason for the “Verification of Address” requirement for businesses ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-2-filing-a-statement-of-claim#:~:text=Any%20plaintiff%20pursuing%20a%20claim,be%20served%20on%20the%20defendant)); even for individuals, triple-check the address (including apartment number, etc.). The court will mail to that exact address. If you’re not sure the defendant will get mail there (e.g., you only have a work address), you might proactively choose sheriff service instead of mail.

**What if mail is returned?** If the first-class mail comes back marked undeliverable, or is returned for any reason, then service by mail failed ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-3-notice-to-defendant-answer-to-claim#:~:text=The%20clerk%20shall%20promptly%20send,cases%20as%20is%20deemed%20necessary)). In that case, the court will notify you (often by sending you the returned envelope or a notice). The burden is on you to then try an alternative service method. The small claims rules say the court *may* provide any other means of service “as is deemed necessary” ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-3-notice-to-defendant-answer-to-claim#:~:text=that%20it%20is%20not%20returned,cases%20as%20is%20deemed%20necessary)) – often, practically, the clerk might suggest you have a deputy sheriff or constable serve the defendant personally. 

**Service by constable/sheriff (personal service):** If mail fails or if you prefer a more guaranteed method (like if a large sum or you suspect address issues), you can arrange for a sheriff or constable to serve the small claims notice. To do this, you ask the court for an “attested copy” of the Statement of Small Claim and Notice. They will stamp and give you a copy (some courts have prepared a separate summons, but generally the Notice of trial type function as process). You then hire a **deputy sheriff** (each county has one, sometimes with regional offices) or a **constable** (authorized process server) to deliver the papers to the defendant personally or leave at their last and usual residence (**“in-hand or abode” service**). 

Sheriffs in Massachusetts typically charge ~$35-50 plus mileage for service. They will provide a **Return of Service** indicating date/time/method of delivery. You should file that Return with the court as proof the defendant was served. If a constable does it, ensure they are authorized in the jurisdiction and have them return an affidavit of service.

**Service on out-of-state defendants:** For a defendant residing out of Massachusetts, first-class mail by the clerk might be considered insufficient because the court can’t compel them if no jurisdiction. According to Rule 3(a) ([www.mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/trial-court-rules/uniform-small-claims-rule-3-notice-to-defendant-answer-to-claim#:~:text=address%20or%20addresses%20supplied%20by,cases%20as%20is%20deemed%20necessary)), service on out-of-state defendants must comply with the Massachusetts **long-arm statute (M.G.L. c.223A)**. That statute allows service by:
  - Certified mail (return receipt requested) sent to the out-of-state defendant, or
  - Personal service in the other state by an authorized server per that state’s rules, or
  - Other means authorized by a court.
  
So if your defendant is in Florida, you could have a Florida process server hand them the papers, or send by certified mail (if they sign the green card, that’s proof).
In practice, the clerk might still try first-class mail – if it doesn’t come back, maybe they deem it served. But for caution, serving out-of-state by certified mail is wise. You can ask the clerk, or you yourself send a copy by certified mail and bring the signed return receipt letter as proof to the hearing. Massachusetts long-arm law requires either the defendant sign a receipt or a court order for alternate method if not.

**Timing of service:** The notice of trial will have a hearing date typically 2-6 weeks out from filing (depending on court backlog). Service should be accomplished with enough lead time for defendant to prepare. There’s no explicit minimum notice in the small claims rules text, but generally they try to give at least ~14 days notice. If a defendant is served very close to the date and says they didn’t have time, the court often grants a continuance. Try to get them served at least a week or two prior.

**Substituted service:** If personal service is attempted and defendant isn’t home, Massachusetts allows leaving at the last and usual abode with someone of suitable age (and mailing a copy as well). Sheriffs/constables know the routine. For businesses, service can be on an officer, manager, or agent. If defendant has an attorney and consents, you could serve the attorney.

**Service on a corporation/LLC:** The clerk’s mail will usually go to the address you gave, e.g., corporate headquarters. If using sheriff: serve an officer or the registered agent. The registered agent’s name/address can be obtained from the MA Secretary of State’s website. Service on the agent in-state is valid. If out-of-state corp with no MA agent, serving the MA Secretary of State can be done (they accept service in some cases, but that’s complex for small claims – usually just serve whatever business address you have via certified mail; it might suffice if they do business here under long-arm). 

**Proof of service:** If service is by mail and not returned, the proof is simply the absence of return mail plus the clerk’s certificate. If it is returned, you do alternate service. The officer who serves personally will provide proof. You should ensure the proof is filed or bring it to court. The court may not enter judgment without proof of service on record, especially if defendant defaults. They often require a return of service or certified mail green card.

**Special cases:**
- **Minors or incompetents:** If you are suing a minor (under 18) or a legally incompetent person, you should serve their guardian or parent. The rules of civil procedure require serving a guardian ad litem or representative. In small claims, the magistrate might not be strict on that unless raised, but legally that’s how it should be.
- **Multiple attempts:** If one method fails, you can do another. If a defendant is evading, you could ask court permission for **Service by alternate means** – like by leaving at their work, or by email if reasonably calculated (though email service is unusual in small claims
