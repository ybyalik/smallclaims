## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $10,000 for individuals; $10,000 for businesses [S1].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds $10,000, you may choose to file in small claims court and waive the excess amount, or file in regular District or Superior Court to seek the full amount. Splitting one dispute into multiple claims to stay within the small claims limit is not permitted. [S4]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S4].

## 3. What you CANNOT sue for

- **Eviction (unlawful detainer)** — Small claims court does not handle eviction proceedings; these must be filed in regular District Court. Where to go instead: District Court eviction process. [S1]
- **Title to real estate** — Cases involving disputes over the ownership of real estate are excluded from small claims court. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [S4]
- **Defamation** — Claims involving defamation, such as libel or slander, are not suitable for small claims court due to their complexity. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [unverified]
- **Professional malpractice** — Claims alleging professional malpractice require expert testimony and are beyond the scope of small claims court. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [unverified]
- **Family law matters** — Issues such as divorce, child custody, and support are handled by Family Court, not small claims court. Where to go instead: Family Court. [unverified]
- **Probate matters** — Disputes involving wills, estates, and trusts are under the jurisdiction of Probate Court. Where to go instead: Probate Court. [unverified]
- **Claims against the federal government** — Small claims court does not have jurisdiction over claims against federal agencies or the U.S. government. Where to go instead: Federal Court. [unverified]
- **Class actions** — Class action lawsuits are complex and exceed the procedural scope of small claims court. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [unverified]
- **Injunctions and specific performance** — Small claims court cannot issue orders requiring a party to do or refrain from doing something. Where to go instead: Superior Court. [unverified]
- **Workers' compensation claims** — Disputes over workers' compensation benefits are handled by the Workers' Compensation Board. Where to go instead: Workers' Compensation Board. [unverified]

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of the debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File a motion for a disclosure hearing; if the debtor has income, request a wage garnishment order from the court; serve the order on the employer, who will withhold and remit payments.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees  
**Effectiveness:** Most effective when debtor is W-2 employed at a stable job.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings (federal CCPA floor)

### Bank levy

Seize funds from the debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court; serve it on the debtor's bank; the bank freezes the account and remits funds to the court.

**Estimated cost:** $25 plus service fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor has sufficient funds in the account.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- $1,000 in a bank account is exempt from levy

### Property lien

Place a lien on the debtor's real property to secure the judgment amount.

Record the judgment with the county registry of deeds; the lien must be satisfied before the property can be sold or refinanced.

**Estimated cost:** $20-50 recording fee  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns real property and intends to sell or refinance.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption up to $47,500

### Writ of execution

Authorize the sheriff to seize and sell the debtor's non-exempt property.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court; the sheriff seizes and sells the debtor's property; proceeds are applied to the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $25 plus sheriff fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor owns valuable non-exempt property.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Various personal property exemptions apply

- **Wage garnishment cap:** 25% of disposable earnings.
- **Judgment lifespan:** 20 years; renewal: File a motion to renew the judgment before it expires; court may grant renewal for an additional period.
- **Debtor's exam form:** SC-005 (Disclosure Subpoena).
- **Exempt assets:**
  - Homestead — $47,500
  - Vehicle — $5,000
  - Retirement accounts (federally and state-protected)
  - Social Security and federal benefits
- **Bankruptcy effect:** Filing for bankruptcy imposes an automatic stay on collection efforts; certain judgments may be discharged in bankruptcy.
- **Satisfaction of judgment form:** SC-010 (Satisfaction of Judgment).
- **Multiple creditors priority:** Judgment liens are prioritized by the date of recording; earlier liens have higher priority. [S1]

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Filing in the wrong venue

**What goes wrong:** Filing in the incorrect District Court may result in dismissal or transfer, causing delays and additional costs.

**How to avoid it:** Ensure you file in the correct venue based on where the defendant resides, where the events occurred, or where the defendant's registered agent is located. [S1]

### Exceeding the monetary limit

**What goes wrong:** If your claim exceeds $10,000 and you file in small claims court, you must waive the excess amount or risk dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Consider filing in regular District or Superior Court if your claim exceeds the small claims limit. [S1]

### Ignoring service requirements

**What goes wrong:** Improper service can lead to delays or dismissal of your case.

**How to avoid it:** Follow Maine's rules for serving the defendant, using authorized methods and filing proof of service with the court. [S1]

### Missing the statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations has expired will result in dismissal of your claim.

**How to avoid it:** Be aware of the specific statute of limitations for your type of claim and file within the required timeframe. [S4]

### Failing to appear at the hearing

**What goes wrong:** If you do not appear, the court may dismiss your case or enter a default judgment against you.

**How to avoid it:** Attend all scheduled hearings or notify the court in advance if you cannot attend. [S1]

### Not collecting the judgment

**What goes wrong:** Winning a judgment does not guarantee payment; you must take steps to collect.

**How to avoid it:** Familiarize yourself with collection methods such as wage garnishment, bank levies, and property liens. [S1]

## 18. Recent changes

- **2026-01-01** — **Increase in small claims jurisdictional limit**. The monetary limit for small claims cases increased from $6,000 to $10,000, allowing for higher-value disputes to be resolved in small claims court. Me. Rev. Stat. tit. 14, § 7482. [S4]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in small claims court. The process is designed to be accessible for individuals representing themselves. However, you may choose to hire an attorney if you prefer. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**
A: The timeline varies, but most small claims cases are resolved within 30 to 90 days from filing to judgment, depending on court schedules and whether mediation is successful. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**
A: As of January 1, 2026, the maximum amount you can sue for in Maine's small claims court is $10,000, excluding interest and costs. [S1]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**
A: If the defendant fails to appear at the hearing, the court may enter a default judgment in your favor. You will still need to present evidence to support your claim before the judgment is granted. [S1]

**Q: Can I appeal?**
A: Yes, both parties have the right to appeal a small claims judgment. Defendants can appeal on questions of law or fact, while plaintiffs can appeal on questions of law only. The appeal must be filed within 30 days of the judgment. [S1]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**
A: In Maine, a small claims judgment is valid for 20 years. If the judgment is not satisfied within that period, you may need to take additional legal action to enforce it. [S1]
