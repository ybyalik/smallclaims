## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $10,000 for individuals; $10,000 for businesses [S1, S13, S4].
- **Splitting a claim:** If a claim exceeds the $10,000 cap, you cannot split it into multiple small claims cases; you must file in a higher court. [S4]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S4].

## 10. Service of process

- **Who can serve:**
  - Sheriff
  - Certified mail by clerk
  - Private process server

- **Methods accepted:**

  ### Sheriff personal service
  Sheriff or constable personally hands the papers to the defendant.
  - Pros: Most reliable, Official
  - Cons: May be more expensive, Can be slower

  ### Certified mail by clerk
  Clerk sends the papers via certified mail to the defendant.
  - Pros: Less expensive, Convenient
  - Cons: Defendant must sign for delivery, May be refused

  ### Private process server
  A private individual serves the papers to the defendant.
  - Pros: Flexible timing, Can be faster
  - Cons: More expensive, Less official

- **Timing:** 8 days (in county); 12 days (out of county).

- **Business service:** Serve the registered agent or an officer of the corporation; for sole proprietors, serve the individual directly.

- **If the defendant is dodging service:** If the defendant is avoiding service, you may request alternative service methods from the court.

Have a portion of debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File a garnishment action, serve the employer, and the employer withholds a portion of the debtor's wages to satisfy the judgment.

**Estimated cost:** $15 plus service fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if the debtor is employed and earns above the exemption threshold.

- 80% of disposable earnings or 30 times the federal minimum wage per week, whichever is greater.

Seize funds from the debtor's bank account.

Obtain a writ of execution, serve it on the bank, and the bank freezes and turns over funds to satisfy the judgment.

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Incorrect defendant information

**What goes wrong:** Suing the wrong party or using incorrect information can result in dismissal or unenforceable judgments.

**How to avoid it:** Verify the defendant's legal name and address before filing. [S1]

---

### Missing statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing after the statute of limitations has expired will lead to dismissal of the case.

**How to avoid it:** Confirm the applicable statute of limitations for your claim type before filing. [S4]

---

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** Failure to properly serve the defendant can delay the case or result in dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Follow the court's rules for service of process carefully. [S1]

---

### Insufficient evidence

**What goes wrong:** Lack of evidence can lead to losing the case, even if your claim is valid.

**How to avoid it:** Gather all relevant documents, photos, and witness statements before the hearing. [S1]

---

### Ignoring court deadlines

**What goes wrong:** Missing deadlines can result in default judgments or dismissal.

**How to avoid it:** Keep track of all court dates and deadlines, and respond promptly. [S1]

---

### Overlooking counterclaims

**What goes wrong:** Failing to file a counterclaim may waive your right to seek damages related to the same dispute.

**How to avoid it:** Consider any potential counterclaims and file them within the required timeframe. [S1]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in small claims court. Many individuals represent themselves successfully. [S1]

**Q: How long does it take?**
A: The typical timeline for a small claims case is between 30 to 90 days from filing to resolution. [S1]

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**
A: You can sue for up to $10,000 in small claims court. [S1]

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**
A: If the defendant fails to appear, the court may grant a default judgment in your favor. [S1]

**Q: Can I appeal?**
A: Yes, either party can appeal a small claims judgment within 15 days. [S15]

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**
A: A judgment is valid for 20 years and can be renewed before it expires. [S4]
