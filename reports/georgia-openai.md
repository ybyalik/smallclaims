## 7. Forms Required  
**Statement of Claim (Complaint):** To start a Georgia small claim, you file a **Statement of Claim** in Magistrate Court ([www.gwinnettcourts.com](https://www.gwinnettcourts.com/magistrate/civil-filing#:~:text=,suitable%20age%20and%20discretion%20then)). This is the simple one- or two-page **complaint form** where you identify the parties, state what you’re suing for, and verify the claim under oath. Every county Magistrate Court has a Statement of Claim form (sometimes called a *Small Claims Complaint*). You can usually obtain it from the Clerk’s Office or download it from the court’s website (for example, Fulton County provides a PDF “Statement of Claim” form on its Magistrate Court site ([magistratefulton.org](https://magistratefulton.org/175/Small-Claims#:~:text=The%20Plaintiff%20,with%20the%20reason%20for%20the))). The form will ask for:  

- **Plaintiff’s name and address:** Your full name (or business name, if a business is suing) and mailing address. Use an address where you can receive court notices. If you’re suing on behalf of a business as a non-attorney agent (e.g. you’re the owner or an employee), you’ll typically indicate your title or capacity (some courts may also require an authorization form – see below).  
- **Defendant’s name and address:** The correct legal name of each person or entity you’re suing and their street address for service. Be very careful here: if suing a business, use the exact registered name (e.g. “ABC, Inc.” rather than a trade name) and include the name of the owner (for a sole proprietorship) if needed. For a corporation/LLC, you can list the company name and serve its registered agent. If you’re unsure, look up the business on the Georgia Secretary of State’s **Corporations Division** site to get the proper name and registered agent address. An improper name can complicate collection later, so get it right (e.g., don’t sue “Joe’s Plumbing” if the legal entity is “Joe’s Plumbing, LLC” – instead sue **“Joe’s Plumbing, LLC”**, and you can mention d/b/a if relevant). Include a physical address for each defendant – a P.O. Box is not sufficient for personal service[^5]. If an exact street address isn’t known, you may need to find it (through online directories, utility records, etc.) before filing.  
- **Amount of claim:** The total dollar amount you are suing for. This should include any principal amount owed, plus any allowable interest or statutory penalties you intend to claim (but **do not** exceed $15,000 in the total). For example, “$4,500.00 plus court costs” or “$1,000 plus $300 interest.” Georgia’s form typically has a blank to fill in the dollar figure. If you’re asking for interest, you might either include a specific sum to date or say “plus interest as allowed by law” – but it’s better to be specific if you know the amount up to filing. Court costs (the filing and service fees) are generally added by the court if you win, so you can write “plus court costs” on the form.  
- **Statement of claim (reason):** A brief, concise description of **why** the defendant owes you money ([www.gwinnettcourts.com](https://www.gwinnettcourts.com/magistrate/civil-filing#:~:text=,suitable%20age%20and%20discretion%20then)). This can be very short – Magistrate Courts expect a “plain language” explanation, not legalese. One or two sentences is often enough. For example: “Defendant breached a lease agreement by failing to pay $2,000 rent for June–July 2026” or “Money had and received – defendant borrowed $500 on March 1, 2026 and has not repaid it despite due date of April 1, 2026” or “Negligence in auto accident on 5/10/2025 – seeking $4,300 for vehicle damage caused by defendant.” Be sure to include **dates**, **locations**, and specific items as relevant. If it’s a contract, mention the contract date and breach date. If it’s a tort, mention the date of injury and what happened. The goal is to give the defendant and judge a clear idea of the nature of the dispute in a few lines. You do *not* need to cite code sections or argue law in this form. Simplicity and clarity are key (the form is “free from technicalities” by law ([www.gwinnettcourts.com](https://www.gwinnettcourts.com/magistrate/civil-filing#:~:text=,suitable%20age%20and%20discretion%20then))).  
- **Verification:** At the bottom of the Statement of Claim, you (the plaintiff) must sign it **under oath** in front of a notary public or the court clerk ([www.gwinnettcourts.com](https://www.gwinnettcourts.com/magistrate/civil-filing#:~:text=,suitable%20age%20and%20discretion%20then)) ([www.gwinnettcourts.com](https://www.gwinnettcourts.com/magistrate/civil-filing#:~:text=technicalities,include%20the%20address%20at%20which)). By signing, you swear the facts in the claim are true to the best of your knowledge. This verification is required by O.C.G.A. § 15-10-43, and it essentially turns your complaint into an affidavit ([www.gwinnettcourts.com](https://www.gwinnettcourts.com/magistrate/civil-filing#:~:text=,suitable%20age%20and%20discretion%20then)). If you mail in the form, you’ll need it notarized before sending. If you file in person, many Clerk’s offices will notarize or have you swear before a deputy clerk at the counter (bring a photo ID). Remember that making false statements on a verified claim is perjury – so be truthful.  

The clerk will assign a **case number** and fill in the court information on the form when you file. In many counties, the Statement of Claim also serves as the official **summons**, meaning it notifies the defendant of the lawsuit and their deadline to respond. It will contain language directed to the defendant, for example: “You are hereby notified that a claim has been filed against you in the Magistrate Court of __ County. You are required to file an answer within 30 days after service of this claim.” ([www.franklincountyga.gov](https://www.franklincountyga.gov/magistrate-court/pages/frequently-asked-questions#:~:text=If%20a%20civil%20case%20is,days%20after%20being%20served%20and)) Some courts include the **hearing date** on the form or will mail a notice of hearing later. (Procedures vary: in some counties, the clerk sets a court date as soon as the defendant’s answer is filed; in others, a hearing date might be pre-assigned and written on the summons.) In any case, once you file the Statement of Claim, the Clerk’s office will take care of preparing a summons or using your form as a summons, and arranging service.  

**Where to get the forms:** Many Magistrate Courts post fillable forms on their official websites (often under “Forms” or “Small Claims”). For instance, the **Georgia Judicial Council** website and some county sites have generic Magistrate Court forms. It’s a good idea to use your county’s version if available, as it may have that court’s specific address and instructions. If online access is hard, you can pick up printed forms at the Magistrate Court clerk’s office for free or a small copy fee. (Some libraries or legal aid offices also stock Magistrate Court packets.)  

**Filing the Statement of Claim:** Once you’ve filled it out and notarized it, you will submit it to the Magistrate Court clerk (in person, by mail, or via e-filing if offered) along with the filing fee (see Filing Fees section above). If you have exhibits (like a copy of a contract, promissory note, or invoice), you are **not required** to attach them to the complaint – the rules don’t require attaching evidence – but you *may* attach copies if you think it helps clarify the claim. For example, some plaintiffs attach a copy of a dishonored check or a written contract as a reference. The defendant will see those attachments when served. It’s not mandatory (you can simply bring the documents to the hearing instead), but attaching key documents can sometimes prompt a quicker settlement. **Do not attach overly lengthy materials**; keep it relevant if you include anything.  

When filing, prepare at least **one original and one copy** of the Statement of Claim for each defendant, plus a copy for your records. The clerk will keep the original and serve the copies on the defendant(s). If you file electronically, you’ll upload the form and the system will generate the necessary copies.  

**Other Forms and Documents:**

- **Civil Case Filing Information Form:** Georgia typically requires a Civil Case Filing Information Form in State or Superior Court, but **Magistrate Courts do not require** the statewide civil case filing form. Instead, the Statement of Claim itself gathers the needed info. (A few Magistrate Courts might have a cover sheet, but most don’t for small claims.) Always ask the clerk if any cover sheet is needed – in Magistrate it’s usually not.  

- **Authorization of Agent (for businesses):** If the plaintiff is a business represented by a non-lawyer agent (owner, officer, or employee), some courts require a simple authorization. For example, a corporation might file a one-page **Corporate Authorization** stating “John Doe is authorized to represent XYZ Corp. in this case.” Many courts don’t ask for it unless a question arises, since O.C.G.A. § 15-10-43 allows an agent to file and sign the claim ([www.gwinnettcourts.com](https://www.gwinnettcourts.com/magistrate/civil-filing#:~:text=,suitable%20age%20and%20discretion%20then)). But to be safe, you can attach or file a letter on company letterhead or a corporate resolution naming the agent. Some counties (like DeKalb in some instances) have an “Affidavit of Authority” form for this purpose. Check your local Magistrate rules – the Clerk can tell you if they need a separate authorization. If you’re a sole proprietor suing in your own name (e.g. Joe Smith d/b/a Joe’s Plumbing), no separate form is needed beyond showing you are the owner if questioned.  

- **Military Service Affidavit (for default judgments):** This isn’t filed at the outset, but if the defendant fails to answer and you seek a default judgment, federal law (Servicemembers Civil Relief Act) requires you to provide an affidavit stating whether or not the defendant is in active military service. Courts have a short form for this – often called a **Non-Military Affidavit**. You can obtain this form from the clerk or ahead of time (some courts include it in the initial filing packet). It asks how you determined their military status (for example, by checking the Department of Defense’s online SCRA database or personal knowledge). If the defendant is in active service, the court will not enter a default until certain protections are addressed ([www.franklincountyga.gov](https://www.franklincountyga.gov/magistrate-court/pages/frequently-asked-questions#:~:text=Answering%20a%20Claim%20in%20Magistrate,Court)). If not, the affidavit allows the case to proceed to default judgment. Keep in mind to prepare this form if you end up in a default situation. (You do not need it when you file the case, only later if no answer is received – we mention it here so you’re aware.)

- **Answer Form (for defendant):** When the defendant is served, they will need to file an **Answer** within 30 days. Many Magistrate Courts provide a pre-printed Answer form (sometimes on the back of the summons or as a separate form) for the defendant’s convenience. As the plaintiff, you don’t file the answer, but be aware the summons will inform the defendant how to answer. For instance, some summons have a tear-off portion titled “Answer” where the defendant can write a brief response and mail or hand-deliver it to the court. The defendant can also draft their own answer letter. This is not your responsibility, but knowing the process helps; once the answer is filed, the court will set a hearing. If the defendant fails to answer, you may move for default judgment after the 30+15 day period (there’s usually a **default judgment form or motion** the court may require – often, Magistrates will schedule a default hearing or simply enter judgment if evidence is straightforward). The **motion for default judgment** might not have a standardized form in all counties; some courts just request a letter or an oral motion at a default hearing. Check with the clerk on how defaults are handled (some require you to put up evidence at a default hearing especially if unliquidated damages).

- **Counterclaim (Defendant’s Claim) Form:** If the defendant wants to file a counterclaim against you, they typically can use the same Statement of Claim form format (but captioned as a counterclaim, listing themselves as “counter-claimant”). Some courts have an actual **Counterclaim form** or allow the defendant to write the counterclaim within their Answer. For example, the answer form might have a section: “□ I assert a counterclaim in the amount of $___ for the following reasons: ___.” If a formal separate form is needed, the clerk can provide it. As a plaintiff, you don’t file the counterclaim, but if you receive one, you should answer it at trial (Magistrate doesn’t require a written answer to a counterclaim – your appearance at the hearing and contesting it serves as your response).

- **Dismissal or Settlement Forms:** If you settle the case before the hearing or decide to dismiss it, there’s a simple **Dismissal form** (Voluntary Dismissal) you can file. Magistrate Courts often have a one-page form where you fill in case info and check “dismissed with prejudice” or “without prejudice.” *Without prejudice* means you could refile later (useful if you’re dismissing to correct a venue or name issue), *with prejudice* means it’s permanently resolved. If you and the defendant agree to a settlement that you want entered as a judgment, you can ask the court for a **Consent Judgment form**. The clerk may have a template where both parties sign off on an agreed amount and payment terms, and the judge will sign it to make it an official judgment. This often happens at mediation or just before trial. These forms aren’t needed at filing, but it’s good to know they exist. 

**Note:** The Magistrate Court clerks are generally very helpful with providing and explaining forms (they can’t give legal *advice*, but they can tell you which blanks to fill). By Georgia law, the clerk or judge *may assist individuals in preparing the statement of claim or other documents* if asked ([www.gwinnettcourts.com](https://www.gwinnettcourts.com/magistrate/civil-filing#:~:text=,suitable%20age%20and%20discretion%20then)). Take advantage of that if needed – Magistrate Courts strive to be user-friendly. Just ensure all names and amounts are correct on your forms, and that you sign where required. 

Once your Statement of Claim is filed and the fees paid, the clerk will issue the **summons** and forward the papers for service on the defendant (via sheriff, marshal, or approved process server). Keep a copy of everything you file for your own records. Now your case is officially underway, and you can move on to serving the defendant (next section).
