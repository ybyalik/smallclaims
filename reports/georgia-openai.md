## 1. Jurisdictional Limits – Maximum Amount You Can Sue For  
Georgia’s Magistrate Courts (commonly called small claims courts) have a **$15,000 cap** on the amount in controversy for civil claims[^1]. This $15,000 limit applies to the principal amount of the claim (not including court costs or interest) and generally applies **equally to all plaintiffs** – individuals, businesses (corporations, LLCs, partnerships), and other entities[^2]. Unlike some states, Georgia does **not** set a lower cap for businesses or frequent filers; the $15,000 limit is uniform. A plaintiff may **voluntarily reduce a larger claim** to $15,000 in order to sue in Magistrate Court – however, any amount above $15,000 is **forfeited** if you choose this route[^3]. For example, if you are owed $18,000, you could waive $3,000 and sue for $15,000, but you cannot later recover the waived portion.  

**No annual filing limit:** Georgia law does not impose a formal limit on the number of small claims cases one plaintiff can file per year. High-volume filers (such as landlords or debt collectors) are common in Magistrate Court, and there is no rule like California’s “two cases over $2,500 per year” restriction. Each case is assessed on its own merits under the $15,000 cap.  

**No claim-splitting to evade the cap:** You **cannot split one larger claim into multiple smaller suits** to circumvent the $15,000 limit. Georgia courts have long held that a single “cause of action” or debt cannot be broken into pieces to fit under the jurisdictional threshold[^4]. If you tried to sue for $15,000 now and reserve the rest for later, the judgment on the first case would likely bar any later claim for the remainder (this is the doctrine of res judicata). In short, for one unified dispute, you get only **one bite at the apple** in Magistrate Court. If your claim is truly divisible (e.g. two unrelated contracts or separate incidents), you can file separate suits – but one continuous claim can’t be artificially divided.  

**Counterclaims:** The defendant’s counterclaim in a Georgia small claims case is also capped at $15,000 principal[^3]. If a defendant believes they have a claim against the plaintiff that exceeds $15,000, they have a few options: (a) **limit the counterclaim to $15,000** and proceed in Magistrate (waiving the rest), (b) file a separate lawsuit in a higher court, or (c) ask to transfer the entire case to a court of general jurisdiction (State or Superior Court). By statute, if a counterclaim exceeding the small claims limit is filed, the Magistrate Court **lacks jurisdiction** to decide it, and it may result in transfer of the case to the appropriate court (with a possible transfer fee) or dismissal of the over-limit counterclaim. In practice, a Magistrate judge will often explain this to the defendant: the counterclaim can be **voluntarily reduced or withdrawn**, or the case can be moved up to a higher court. If transferred, the case proceeds anew under regular civil procedures, and a transfer fee (equal to the filing fee of the higher court) may be assessed[^5]. Failure to pay a required transfer fee within the deadline can lead to dismissal of the case, so defendants should carefully consider the best forum for large counterclaims.  

**Examples:**  
- *Individual plaintiff:* Jane is an individual owed $12,000 on an unpaid loan. She can sue for the full $12,000 in Magistrate Court (since it’s ≤ $15k). If she were owed $20,000, she could either waive $5,000 and sue for $15,000 in Magistrate, or sue for the full amount in State or Superior Court.  
- *Business plaintiff:* ABC LLC is owed $9,000 on an invoice. ABC can file in Magistrate Court for $9k. If ABC is owed $25,000, it must either cap the claim at $15k or file in a higher court – there’s no special higher cap for businesses. Georgia does **not** follow the practice of some states that give lower caps to corporate plaintiffs; it’s $15k across the board[^2].  
- *Multiple small claims:* There is no prohibition on filing many small claims (e.g. a business suing many customers for smaller debts). A landlord with 50 tenants each owing $1,000 could theoretically file 50 separate cases. However, each case must be for a distinct transaction/claim – you can’t split one tenant’s $3,000 debt into three separate $1,000 suits.  

## 2. What Types of Cases Qualify – Everything You Can Sue For  

Georgia Magistrate Courts can hear **any civil dispute for money or recovery of personal property** not exceeding $15,000, **unless exclusive jurisdiction is elsewhere** or the law forbids that type of claim in small claims court[^1]. The vast majority of ordinary money disputes are eligible. Below is an exhaustive breakdown by category: each entry describes whether the claim is allowed in Georgia small claims, examples of real-life scenarios, relevant Georgia laws, and any special rules or requirements. If your situation involves someone owing you money or property valued ≤ $15k, it likely can be resolved in Magistrate Court **unless noted as an exclusion** in the later section. Use the following list to find your specific type of dispute:

### Contract & Money-Owed Disputes  
- **Breach of Written Contract:** *Eligible.* If someone fails to honor a written agreement (e.g. a signed contract for services or purchase), you can sue for the financial loss. **Examples:** (1) You paid a contractor $5,000 under a written home renovation contract, but they never performed the work. (2) A client who signed a contract for consulting services refuses to pay the $3,000 balance due. These are classic small claims. Georgia allows enforcement of written contracts in Magistrate Court up to $15k. Written contracts have a **6-year statute of limitations** in Georgia[^6] (so you must sue within 6 years of the breach in most cases). Tip: Bring a copy of the signed contract and any proof of breach (emails, etc.) to your hearing.  
- **Breach of Oral/Handshake Agreement:** *Eligible.* Oral contracts are generally enforceable in Georgia (except certain transactions that must be written under the Statute of Frauds). If a friend verbally agreed to sell you something or repay a loan, and didn’t follow through, you can sue on that promise. **Examples:** (1) A handshake deal where your neighbor agreed to sell you a used boat for $2,000, took your money, but never delivered the boat. (2) You agreed to lend equipment to a colleague in exchange for $500, but they never paid. Oral contract cases often boil down to credibility and evidence of the agreement (texts, witnesses, etc.). The statute of limitations for unwritten (oral or implied) contracts in Georgia is **4 years**[^7]. There’s no special bar to suing on oral agreements in small claims – just be prepared to prove the terms of the deal.  
- **Unpaid Personal Loans (Friends/Family IOUs):** *Eligible.* Informal loans are one of the most common small claims. If you lent someone money on the promise of repayment, you can sue to recover the balance if they default. **Examples:** (1) You loaned your cousin $1,500 to fix his car, with a verbal promise he’d repay in 3 months, but a year has passed with no payment. (2) A friend borrowed $500 “until payday” and has since ghosted you. In court, you’d outline the loan terms and any proof (Venmo records, emails, or testimony). Even without a formal promissory note, these are enforceable as oral contracts (4-year limit)[^7]. It helps to have something in writing or a witness to confirm the loan if possible.  
- **Unpaid Promissory Notes:** *Eligible.* A promissory note is a written promise to pay (often used for loans). If someone signed a note and isn’t paying as agreed, you can sue on that note. **Example:** You sold a car to an acquaintance for $10,000 and they signed a promissory note to pay you $500/month, but they stopped after $2,000. You can sue for the remaining $8,000 (assuming it’s within the $15k cap). A promissory note is a written contract (6-year limit unless it’s under seal, which would be 20 years[^8]). Bring the original note as evidence; if it’s under seal (often indicated by the word “Seal” next to the signature), note that a 20-year limit applies[^8].  
- **Unpaid Commercial Invoices / Accounts Receivable:** *Eligible.* Businesses can use Magistrate Court to collect unpaid customer bills or invoices for goods delivered or services rendered. **Example:** A freelance graphic designer has $4,000 of unpaid invoices for work done for a client. Or a wholesaler delivered products to a retailer on account, and the retailer never paid the $12,000 balance. These are essentially contract claims (written or oral, depending on the documentation). Many business debt claims are on “open accounts,” which in Georgia have a 4-year limitation period[^7]. Attach copies of invoices, delivery receipts, and any purchase orders or communications showing the client’s agreement to pay. Georgia Magistrate Courts regularly handle collection suits by businesses, including by assignees and collection agencies (unlike some states, Georgia does **not** prohibit collection companies from filing in small claims, as long as they are the real party in interest).  
- **Unpaid Rent (Residential or Commercial):** *Eligible.* While eviction (dispossessory) actions have a separate process, a landlord can sue in Magistrate Court solely for unpaid rent or damages after a tenant has vacated. **Examples:** (1) A residential tenant moved out owing two months’ rent ($2,000) and additional damages beyond the security deposit; the landlord can file a claim for the money. (2) A commercial tenant broke a lease and owes $10,000 in remaining rent – the landlord may sue for that rent (though if it’s an ongoing lease, the landlord might need to accelerate the rent per the lease terms). Note: If the tenant is still in possession, the landlord should file a dispossessory (eviction) in Magistrate – which can include a claim for rent but follows a different procedure ([www.franklincountyga.gov](https://www.franklincountyga.gov/magistrate-court/pages/frequently-asked-questions#:~:text=contracts%2C%20personal%20property%20foreclosures%2C%20garnishments%2C,00%20does%20not%20apply)). Once the tenancy is over, claims for money (unpaid rent, damages, etc.) can be pursued as regular small claims. Georgia law requires landlords to mitigate damages (make reasonable efforts to re-rent) in some cases, so landlords should be prepared to show they tried to re-rent if claiming future rents. Residential landlords must also follow **security deposit** laws – see Landlord/Tenant section below for how improper handling of deposits can affect claims.  
- **Bounced or Dishonored Checks:** *Eligible.* If someone paid you with a bad check (insufficient funds or closed account), you can sue for the amount of the check **and** potentially additional damages under Georgia’s bad check statute. Georgia law provides a civil penalty for writing bad checks: if the check writer doesn’t pay up within **10 days** after you send a written demand, they are liable for **double the amount owed, up to $500 extra**, plus court costs[^9]. **Example:** A customer gave you a $300 check that bounced. You send the statutory demand letter (via certified or overnight mail) and 10 days pass without payment. In small claims, you can sue for $300 + $300 penalty (double) + any bank fees you incurred (capped at $500 penalty max)[^9]. If the check were $1,000, double would be $2,000 but the statute caps the penalty at $500 – so you’d sue for $1,000 + $500 = $1,500. To claim this, you *must* have given the 10-day written demand as required[^9]. Bring a copy of the demand letter and proof of mailing (and note when the 10 days expired). Georgia also criminalizes bad checks (“deposit account fraud”)[^1], but the civil route in Magistrate Court lets you recover the money and penalty without involving criminal courts.  
- **Open Accounts / Running Tabs:** *Eligible.* An open account is an ongoing balance where the parties expect continuing transactions (e.g. a running tab at a store or a line of credit a customer uses over time). If the account is unpaid when closed, you can sue for the balance. **Example:** A feed supply store had a running account for a farmer to buy supplies on credit; the farmer stopped paying when the balance hit $4,500. The store can sue for $4,500 as an open account. Georgia treats open account debts as contracts not in writing (unless there’s a signed credit agreement), so the **4-year statute** applies[^7]. It’s important not to split such an account into pieces – if all the items were due by the time of suit, you must sue for the entire balance, not several smaller suits[^4]. Present an account statement showing all charges and payments, and be ready to authenticate it as the custodian of records (if a business).  
- **Co-signer / Guarantor Liability:** *Eligible.* If you co-signed a loan or guaranteed someone’s debt and ended up having to pay, you can sue the primary borrower for what you paid (or sue a guarantor if you’re a lender and they didn’t pay). **Example:** You co-signed an apartment lease for a friend. The friend skipped out, and you paid the landlord $2,000 to cover the unpaid rent. You can sue your friend in Magistrate Court for $2,000 as reimbursement – effectively enforcing the indemnity implied by the co-signer arrangement. Or, if you were the lender: say you loaned $5,000 to Person A, and Person B signed as guarantor – if neither pays, you can sue either or both for the $5,000 (but only collect once). Georgia law allows contribution claims among co-debtors and reimbursement for guarantors, generally under contract/implied contract theories (typically 4-year limit if not in writing)[^7]. Bring copies of any guaranty agreements or proof you paid on their behalf.  

### Consumer Purchase & Service Disputes  
- **Defective Product – Refund or Repair Cost:** *Eligible.* If you bought a product (vehicle, appliance, electronics, etc.) that turned out to be defective and the seller or manufacturer won’t refund or fix it, you can sue for your money back or the cost of repair (as long as it’s under $15k). **Examples:** (1) You purchased a new laptop for $1,200 that died after two weeks. The store refuses to refund or replace it, claiming you mishandled it. You can sue the store for a refund due to breach of the implied warranty of merchantability (assuming it was a consumer sale). (2) You bought a used washing machine “as is” from a local shop for $500, and it broke down immediately due to an internal defect that the seller possibly knew about. You might sue for the repair cost or partial refund, alleging misrepresentation. Georgia recognizes implied warranties in the sale of goods (unless disclaimed) and prohibits deceptive sales practices under the Fair Business Practices Act (FBPA)[^10]. For consumer product issues, you could claim breach of warranty (4-year statute under UCC for goods)[^11] or unfair/deceptive act if the seller’s conduct was misleading (if pursuing an FBPA claim, remember to send a **30-day demand letter** before suing to potentially recover attorney’s fees or treble damages if it was intentional)[^10]. As evidence, bring the product (if portable) or photos, receipts, and any warranty documentation.  
- **Services Not Performed or Performed Poorly (Non-Delivery):** *Eligible.* If you paid for a service that was never provided, or was done in a shoddy manner far below reasonable standards, you can sue for a refund or the cost to fix the problem. **Examples:** (1) You paid a wedding photographer a $2,000 deposit, but they never showed up on the wedding day and won’t return your money. (2) You hired a landscaper to plant trees and paid $800, but they either didn’t do it at all or planted the wrong trees and ruined your yard. These are breaches of service contracts. In Georgia, even unwritten service agreements are enforceable (4-year limit if oral)[^7]. You can claim the amount you paid and, if applicable, any additional losses caused by the poor service (though intentional damage might be a tort as well). If the service was licensed (e.g. a contractor or repairman), also consider complaining to the licensing board – but that doesn’t get you money; the court does. In your case, bring any receipts, contracts, text messages, or before-and-after photos demonstrating the lack of performance or poor quality.  
- **Auto Repair Overcharges or Shoddy Work:** *Eligible.* Auto repair disputes are common in small claims. If a mechanic charged for work not actually done, or did substandard repairs causing you loss, you can sue for the overcharge or cost of proper repairs. **Examples:** (1) You paid a mechanic $600 for new brake pads and rotors, but later discover they never replaced the parts (the old parts are still there) – you can sue to recover what you paid and possibly the cost of having the job done correctly. (2) A body shop repaired accident damage for $5,000, but the paint is peeling and the parts were installed incorrectly, and the shop refuses to fix it – you can sue for the cost of redoing the work properly. Georgia’s FBPA also prohibits certain deceptive practices by auto repair facilities (e.g. misrepresenting that repairs are needed when they aren’t). If you can show the shop engaged in deceptive or bad-faith conduct, you might also invoke the FBPA for potential 3x damages and fees[^10], but note that pursuing FBPA in small claims still requires the 30-day demand letter and the judge’s willingness to award more than actual damages. Generally, you’ll sue for breach of contract or negligence. Bring your repair invoices, any warranties given, and perhaps an expert statement from another mechanic (even a written estimate for corrective work can help prove the prior work was bad).  
- **Home Repair / Contractor Disputes:** *Eligible.* If you hired a contractor or handyman for home repairs or improvement and they didn’t do the work, did it incorrectly, or took your money and ran, you can sue for what it will cost to finish or fix the job (up to $15k). **Examples:** (1) You paid a contractor a $10,000 down payment to remodel your bathroom, but they never started or did only demo and then disappeared. You can sue to recover the $10k. (2) A roofer repaired your roof for $4,000, but it still leaks because the work was substandard – you can sue for the cost of a competent roofer to fix it properly. Georgia does not require contractors to be licensed for basic residential work (except certain trades like plumbing, electrical, HVAC require state licenses), but if your contractor was licensed (or required to be), you can also file a complaint with the Georgia State Licensing Board for Residential and General Contractors. For your small claim, focus on the money: breach of contract and possibly fraud if they never intended to perform. Document the timeline: contract, payments made (canceled checks or receipts), and what (if anything) was done. Photographs of incomplete or defective work and any expert inspection reports are valuable. If the contractor gave excuses or promises in texts/emails, print those as evidence of their failure to perform.  
- **Appliance or Electronics Warranty Failures:** *Eligible.* If you bought an appliance or electronic device with a warranty (manufacturer or extended warranty) and it failed within the covered period, but the company is not honoring the warranty (e.g. refusing repair or replacement), you can sue for the cost of repair or replacement. **Example:** You bought a refrigerator with a one-year warranty. It died after 8 months. The manufacturer’s service center can’t fix it after multiple attempts, and the company won’t replace it or refund you. You could sue for a refund of the purchase price ($1,200) or the cost of a new fridge. Another example: you paid for a 3-year extended warranty on a TV, but when it broke, the warranty provider never sends a technician or claims your issue isn’t covered, contrary to the contract. You can sue for breach of the warranty contract. Under Georgia law, warranty is part of the contract of sale; breach of express warranty or the implied warranty of merchantability for goods is actionable (the UCC’s 4-year limit from purchase applies for breach of warranty[^11], though the warranty period itself might be shorter). If it’s an **automobile** under warranty (lemon law scenario, see below), note that new car warranty issues fall under Georgia’s Lemon Law which has its own process. But for smaller items or used cars sold “as-is,” small claims is appropriate. Bring the warranty documentation and evidence of your requests for service and their refusal or failure to fix.  
- **Canceled or No-Show Service Providers:** *Eligible.* If you hired a service provider for an event or job (photographer, caterer, DJ, movers, etc.) who then canceled at the last minute or simply didn’t show up, causing you financial loss, you can sue to get your deposit back and any extra costs you incurred. **Examples:** (1) A wedding caterer took a $3,000 deposit but never arrived, forcing you to order emergency pizzas – you can sue for the $3,000 plus the pizza costs and other related damages (though emotional distress is not compensable). (2) You booked a moving company for $800, they never came on moving day, and you had to hire another last-minute for $1,200 – you can sue the original mover for the $800 deposit and potentially the $400 difference you paid, as consequential damages for breach. Georgia contract law allows recovery of reasonably foreseeable extra costs caused by the breach. In such cases, document the agreement (contract or emails), any cancellation notice (or lack thereof), and receipts for the substitute services. If a vendor simply refused to refund a deposit despite *them* canceling, the court will likely side with you that the deposit should be returned.  
- **Dry Cleaning / Property Loss or Damage in Custody:** *Eligible.* If a business lost or damaged your property that you entrusted to them (bailee situations), you can sue for the value of the loss. **Examples:** (1) A dry cleaner lost your expensive suit or returned it with irreparable damage. You could file a claim for the value of the suit (fair market value, not necessarily original price if it was used). (2) A valet parking service returned your car with a dent or scratches that weren’t there before, and refuses to pay for the repair – you can sue for the repair cost. (3) A storage unit facility’s roof leaked and ruined your stored furniture (though check the contract: many storage contracts try to waive liability for leaks – but you can still attempt a claim if negligence is involved). These cases can be framed as negligence or breach of bailment duty. Georgia law imposes a duty of care on bailees (i.e., the service holding your property) – generally they must exercise ordinary care or what’s agreed in the contract. If they fail, they’re liable for resulting damage. Be aware of any disclaimers you signed (e.g. dry cleaners often post limited liability per garment). Those are not absolute shields, but can limit recovery. Still, Magistrate judges often use basic fairness – if your $300 dress shirt was ruined, a disclaimer limiting liability to $50 might or might not be enforced fully. Present receipts or credible evidence of the item’s value, and proof it was in their possession and came out damaged or not at all (photos, testimony, etc.).  
- **Gym/Subscription/Membership Cancellations:** *Eligible.* If you canceled a service (gym membership, cable service, alarm monitoring, etc.) according to the contract terms but the company keeps charging you or refuses to refund a prepaid amount, you can sue for the unauthorized charges or owed refund. **Examples:** (1) You mailed a proper cancellation letter to a gym to end your membership, but the gym kept auto-drafting $50/month for 6 more months – you can sue for $300 in wrongly charged fees. (2) You paid upfront for a year of a pest control service and canceled after 3 months due to issues, and they promised a pro-rata refund but never paid – you can claim the unused portion. Key in these cases is the contract: many have specific notice requirements or early termination fees. If you complied with the terms (or if the terms are unfair or not properly disclosed, possibly raising an FBPA issue), the court can grant relief. Georgia’s FBPA might apply if the company engaged in deceptive practices (like hiding cancellation procedures or continuing to charge knowingly). Provide documentation of your cancellation (dated letter, email, or notes from an in-person cancellation) and proof of continued charges. Also bring the membership contract or service agreement if available.  
- **Auto Dealer / Used-Car Sales Disputes:** *Eligible in many cases.* Disputes over used cars or even new cars (beyond Lemon Law) often end up in small claims – e.g. undisclosed defects, odometer fraud, or breach of a purchase contract. **Examples:** (1) You bought a used car “as is” for $6,000 from a small dealer, and the next day the transmission fails. If you can prove the dealer committed fraud (for example, they knew of the issue or tampered with the car), you could sue for misrepresentation or violation of Georgia’s **Used Car Sales** rules (odometer tampering is specifically illegal under federal and state law, for instance). Without fraud, an “as-is” sale is tough – no implied warranty if properly disclaimed. But if any short dealer warranty was given (e.g. 30-day limited warranty) and they refuse to honor it, you can claim under that. (2) You bought a car and later discovered it had a salvage title that wasn’t disclosed – that’s a clear unfair practice; you could sue for rescission (your money back in exchange for returning the car) or damages, under the FBPA as a deceptive act. (3) A dealership failed to pay off your trade-in loan as promised, and you incur charges – you can sue for that financial harm. **Georgia’s Lemon Law:** Note that for **new cars**, Georgia’s Lemon Law (O.C.G.A. § 10-1-780 et seq.) provides a specialized process: you must give the manufacturer chances to repair and possibly go through a state-run arbitration. Lemon Law covers new vehicles in their first 2 years/24,000 miles that have serious defects. If your new car qualifies as a “lemon,” you generally would not go straight to small claims; you’d file a Lemon Law complaint with the Georgia Department of Law Consumer Protection Division and likely proceed to **state-operated arbitration**, not court ([consumer.georgia.gov](https://consumer.georgia.gov/resolve-your-dispute/georgia-lemon-law/georgia-lemon-law#:~:text=to%20the%20superior%20court,shall%20be%20admissible%20in%20evidence)). Only if arbitration is unsuccessful would you potentially file a lawsuit, and that would typically be in Superior Court and often for a repurchase or replacement (exceeding $15k). **However**, for *used cars or issues outside Lemon Law*, small claims is the venue for monetary disputes under $15k. Always send a written demand and consider an FBPA claim if the dealer’s conduct was dishonest – Georgia’s FBPA allows consumers to recover triple damages and attorney fees for intentional deceptive acts[^10]. In one famous scenario, an Atlanta used car dealer was sued in small claims for selling a car with a rolled-back odometer; the buyer won treble damages under the odometer fraud statutes. Bring any evidence of the misrepresentation (advertisements, CarFax report, mechanic’s inspection) and the sale documents.  
- **False Advertising / Deceptive Trade Practices:** *Eligible (with caveats).* If you were misled by a business’s false advertising or unfair business practice and suffered a financial loss, you can sue under Georgia’s Fair Business Practices Act (FBPA)[^10] or general fraud principles. **Examples:** (1) A furniture store advertised “100% solid wood” cabinets, but what you got after paying $2,000 are particle board – you might claim this was a deceptive trade practice and seek a refund or damages for the difference in value. (2) A travel agency’s brochure promised a luxury package but delivered a subpar experience far below what was advertised – if you can quantify the loss (e.g. the difference in value or extra expenses you incurred), you could sue. Georgia’s FBPA (O.C.G.A. § 10-1-393) broadly prohibits unfair or deceptive acts in consumer transactions, but to use it privately you must follow the process: send a **written demand letter at least 30 days before suing** describing the unfair practice and injury[^10]. This gives the business a chance to offer a settlement. If they don’t, or the offer is unreasonable, and you win in court, the judge can award you **treble damages** (3 times your actual loss) if the violation was intentional, plus **attorney’s fees**[^10]. In small claims (where you likely have no attorney fee, since you’re pro se), treble damages can still be awarded for willful deception. Not every bad business act qualifies – the FBPA primarily covers acts that impact the public (not a purely private one-off breach). But many court decisions say a practice doesn’t have to injure lots of people if it could have harmed the public. For Magistrate Court, you might or might not get a judge versed in FBPA intricacies, but you can certainly assert it. Even if not, you always have a common-law fraud or breach of contract claim. Bring any ads, marketing materials, or written representations that were false, and evidence of your reliance (e.g. you bought specifically because of that claim). Also bring your 30-day demand letter and any response or lack thereof, to show you fulfilled that requirement.  

### Property Damage  
- **Vehicle Collisions (Property Damage Only):** *Eligible.* If someone crashes into your car (or other vehicle) and causes damage within the $15k range, you can sue them for the repair costs, diminished value, and related expenses. **Example:** A driver ran a red light and hit your car, causing $4,500 in damage. If insurance didn’t pay (or perhaps the at-fault driver was uninsured or disputes liability), you can sue the at-fault driver in Magistrate Court. You’ll need to prove they were negligent (police report, witness statements, photos of the scene) and the amount of damage (repair estimates or bills, or a professional appraisal if claiming diminished value). Georgia allows recovery of **diminished value** for vehicles – the post-repair loss in value – but you’d need an appraisal to support that. Note: If you personally were injured in the accident, small claims may not be suitable if your injury damages exceed $15k (see Personal Injury section). But for pure property damage, or minor injuries with total under cap, small claims works. Always consider that if the other driver had insurance that denied or lowballed your claim, a small-claims judgment might motivate a settlement or can be sent to their insurer for payment.  
- **Damage to Personal Property by Another Person:** *Eligible.* Whether it’s intentional or accidental, if someone damages or destroys your belongings, you can sue for the cost of repair or replacement (fair market value). **Examples:** (1) A neighbor’s kid threw a baseball through your $300 window and the parents refuse to pay for a new window – you can file a claim. (2) During a house party, a guest spilled red wine on your antique rug causing $2,000 in damage; if they won’t cover professional cleaning or value diminution, you can sue. (3) A friend borrowed your power tools and returned them broken; you can seek the cost to repair or replace them. These claims can be framed as negligence (if accidental) or even trespass to chattels/vandalism (if intentional). In Georgia, intentional property damage could also allow you to ask for punitive damages in higher courts, but Magistrate Courts **generally do not award punitive damages** – they stick to actual compensatory damages. (Magistrate judges technically can award up to the cap, but punitive damages would be unusual in small claims and likely not granted without a statute). So focus on the dollar value of what was damaged. Provide receipts, repair estimates, or expert statements on value. If the act was egregious (say, someone keyed your car out of malice), you can mention it – the judge might be sympathetic – but expect to receive the repair cost, not extra for “punishment,” in small claims.  
- **Damage by a Tenant to a Landlord’s Property:** *Eligible.* If a former tenant caused damage to your rental property beyond normal wear-and-tear, you can sue for the cost of repairs (if it exceeds what the security deposit covered, or if you lawfully did not return the deposit due to those damages). **Example:** A tenant punched holes in walls and let pets ruin the carpet, causing $5,000 in repairs, while their security deposit was $1,500. You could file a claim for the remaining $3,500. **Important:** Landlords in Georgia must follow the **security deposit law** (see Landlord–Tenant section) – you must provide an itemized damage list and return any remaining deposit within 30 days of move-out[^12]. If you fail to do so, you forfeit the right to keep any of the deposit **and** risk a penalty of triple the amount wrongfully withheld[^13]. In a scenario where the tenant sues you for the deposit, you’d better have complied. But when *you* sue the tenant for excess damage, make sure you can show you documented the damages and properly accounted for the deposit. Small claims is the proper venue for landlords seeking money for property damage, as long as the amount is ≤ $15k. Bring before-and-after photos of the rental, move-in/move-out inspection reports, repair invoices, and the lease (to show any clauses on tenant responsibility). Likewise, tenants can counterclaim if they feel deposits were wrongly withheld.  
- **Damage by a Landlord to a Tenant’s Property:** *Eligible.* Conversely, if you’re a tenant and your landlord (or their negligence) caused damage to or loss of your personal property, you can sue for that. **Examples:** (1) The landlord’s careless repair work started a fire that destroyed some of your furniture, or they left your apartment door unlocked during a showing and items were stolen – you could file a claim for the value of your belongings. (2) During an improper eviction, a landlord wrongfully disposed of your possessions. In Georgia, after an eviction, landlords must handle tenant belongings according to law (often by moving them out to a safe place or following the court’s writ – simply throwing stuff out could expose them to liability). If a landlord removes or damages tenant property illegally, the tenant can sue for conversion. Magistrate Court can handle these claims if under $15k. Present evidence of the landlord’s action (texts, witnesses) and proof of each item’s value (photos and receipts if available).  
- **Tree/Fence/Boundary Disputes Resulting in Damage:** *Eligible.* Property line issues often lead to small claims when one neighbor’s actions damage the other’s property. **Examples:** (1) Your neighbor cut down a tree and it fell onto your fence, causing $1,200 in damage – you can sue the neighbor to pay for fence repair. (2) A neighbor’s tree limb crashed onto your shed during a storm; typically, falling trees are considered an “act of God” unless the tree was dead or the neighbor’s negligence contributed (e.g. they knew it was rotten and ignored it). If you can show the neighbor’s fault, you can claim the damage ($5,000 to rebuild the shed). (3) Your neighbor built a fence that encroaches and damaged your sprinkler system – small claims can address the cost to repair your system (though not to *force them to move the fence* – that would be injunctive relief, which Magistrate Court cannot grant). So you could get money for damage, but issues like exact boundary lines or an order to remove an encroachment would be for higher courts. Georgia has specific provisions about cutting timber on another’s land (which can allow triple damages in Superior Court), but in small claims you’re limited to monetary loss. Document the damage with photos, survey if relevant, and any prior notices (e.g. if you warned the neighbor about the dangerous tree). If the dispute is over a shared tree on the boundary, generally each landowner has rights – it can get tricky, but for a simple damage claim, it’s within the court’s power.  
- **Pet Damage (Animal Caused Property Damage):** *Eligible.* If someone’s pet causes damage to your property, the owner can be held liable. **Examples:** (1) Your neighbor’s dog chewed up your expensive lawn furniture or dug massive holes in your yard causing $800 in landscaping costs – you can sue the dog’s owner. (2) A pet-sitter’s negligence led to your cat escaping and being lost or injured; you might claim the value of the pet (pets are considered property in law) or vet bills. (3) Your dog and another person’s dog got into a fight due to the other owner’s negligence (e.g. they let their aggressive dog run loose), resulting in $1,000 vet bills for your dog – you can seek those vet costs. Georgia’s laws on dog bites and animal liability (O.C.G.A. § 51-2-7) require showing the owner’s negligence or that the dog was known to be vicious. For property damage by animals, it’s usually straightforward if the animal trespassed or was uncontrolled. Provide evidence of the damage (photos, receipts for repairs or vet bills) and, if needed, evidence of the animal’s access (witness statements that the dog was loose, etc.). Note: if an animal injured *you* (personal injury), that’s covered in Personal Injury below – you can claim medical costs in small claims if under the cap, but severe injuries often exceed $15k. For property and minor pet injuries, Magistrate is appropriate.  
- **Damage During a Move (Movers or Friends Helping):** *Eligible.* If you hired professional movers and they damaged your furniture or home, you can sue for the damage. **Example:** A moving company dropped your big-screen TV, breaking it. If they refuse to compensate or only offered a low amount (some moving contracts limit liability to e.g. 60 cents per pound unless you bought extra insurance), you can still bring them to small claims. Be mindful of the contract terms – many moving companies have arbitration clauses or liability limits. If there’s an arbitration clause, the moving company can move to dismiss the court case in favor of arbitration (Magistrate judges will generally enforce valid arbitration clauses). But if no such clause or you argue they were grossly negligent, you can try in court. For friends who helped you move and accidentally smashed something, you might hesitate to sue a friend – but legally, you could if the loss is significant. It would likely be a negligence claim: they had a duty to handle your stuff carefully, breached it, causing damage. Provide proof of item value and that the damages occurred during the move. If the movers’ contract limited them to a certain payout and you didn’t opt for higher coverage, the judge might take that into account but isn’t absolutely bound by contractual limitations if they find it unconscionable or if the mover was reckless. It can go either way.  

### Landlord–Tenant (Financial Claims Only – Possession/Eviction is Separate)  
*Note:* Magistrate Courts handle **eviction cases (dispossessories)** as a distinct process, often on a faster track. Those cases decide who has the right to possession of rental property and can include past-due rent claims. However, **small claims actions** between landlords and tenants usually arise either after the tenancy for money issues or as separate claims for monetary relief only. The court and forms might be the same, but it’s important not to file a general small claim when what you need is an eviction, or vice-versa. Below are common landlord-tenant money disputes suitable for Magistrate Court, aside from evictions:

- **Unreturned Security Deposits:** *Eligible (very common).* If you’re a tenant and your landlord failed to return your security deposit without proper justification, you can sue for the deposit and potentially penalties. Georgia’s security deposit law is *tenant-friendly* on this point. **Landlord requirements:** For landlords who own more than 10 rental units (or who use a management company), the deposit must be held in escrow and an inspection list provided at move-in and move-out (O.C.G.A. § 44-7-33). The landlord must return the deposit (minus any lawful deductions for damage or rent owed) **within 30 days** after the tenant moves out, along with an itemized statement of deductions[^12]. If the landlord doesn’t comply – e.g., no itemized damage list, or they miss the 30-day window – they **forfeit the right to keep any deposit funds**[^13]. If they **intentionally** fail to return what’s owed, the tenant can get **treble damages (3× the amount)** wrongfully withheld **plus attorney’s fees** in court[^13]. **Example:** Your $1,000 deposit wasn’t returned, and the landlord never sent any itemized list of damages within 30 days. In small claims, you can sue for $3,000 (triple the deposit) and reasonable attorneys’ fees (if you had one, though in Magistrate you likely don’t, so you’d just get $3,000). The landlord’s only defense would be to show it was a *bona fide* error (like they mailed the list to the wrong address by mistake) – if they prove it wasn’t intentional, the court can limit damages to the actual amount withheld[^13]. **Important:** If you owe unpaid rent that the landlord could legitimately deduct, the net amount wrongfully withheld might be less than the full deposit. Magistrates often calculate the treble on the net wrongful portion. Provide evidence: your lease (to show deposit amount), proof of move-out date, and that you gave a forwarding address if required. If the 30 days passed with no refund or statement, that’s essentially slam-dunk liability for the landlord under the statute. Landlords should be aware of this law – a small claims judge will enforce the triple penalty for bad-faith deposit withholding.  
- **Refund of Prepaid Last Month’s Rent:** *Eligible.* Some leases collect first and last month’s rent at the start. If you properly ended the lease (or the lease ended) and the landlord was supposed to apply that last month’s rent or refund it and did neither, you can sue to get it back. **Example:** You paid $1,200 “last month rent” at move-in. You fulfill the lease term and move out at the end. The landlord, however, double-charged you for the last month (maybe they demanded a payment not realizing you’d prepaid) or simply never returned the prepayment. That $1,200 is a straightforward debt owed back to you. Unlike a security deposit, a last-month rent is not subject to damage deductions (it was meant to cover rent). If the landlord tries to treat it like a deposit, that’s improper. You can explain to the judge that this was rent, not a damage deposit. With your lease and receipt showing you paid in advance, the court should order the refund. This often happens when tenants leave early on good terms and landlord re-rents – any unused rent should be pro-rated and returned. If you broke the lease early, you might not be entitled to it (because you didn’t actually have a “last month” for it to apply to), except possibly as offset against what you owed for early termination. Each scenario varies, but small claims can sort it out.  
- **Habitability Repairs / Rent Abatement (Repair-and-Deduct):** *Eligible as a counterclaim or direct claim, within limits.* Georgia law (unlike some states) doesn’t have a statutorily defined “repair and deduct” right for tenants, but tenants do have the right to a livable premise (warranty of habitability) and can make arguments for rent abatement or reimbursement for repairs they had to undertake. **Examples:** (1) Despite repeated notices, your landlord refused to fix a leaking plumbing fixture, so you paid a plumber $300 yourself and want to deduct that from rent or get reimbursed. You can sue for that $300 as damages for landlord’s breach of duty to maintain. (2) Extreme case: the heating in your apartment failed in winter and landlord ignored requests, so you had to spend $500 on space heaters and high electric bills – you might claim that as consequential damages. Generally, major habitability issues could be a defense to rent (constructive eviction), or grounds to move out without penalty. In small claims, you often see these as **counterclaims**: landlord sues for unpaid rent, tenant counterclaims for damages due to terrible conditions. Magistrate judges can offset the amounts. While Georgia’s warranty of habitability is implied in residential leases, for a tenant to claim a rent reduction or reimbursement, you should show: you gave the landlord notice, a reasonable time passed, you took reasonable action, and the costs were necessary. There’s no guarantee of full reimbursement, but courts aim for fairness. **Note:** If conditions are bad, you can also contact local code enforcement; but to get your money back, court is the way. Document everything: photos of the disrepair, copies of letters or texts to landlord about it, receipts for any repairs or expenses you incurred. If you simply withheld rent because of conditions, be prepared to show why the value of the unit was diminished (e.g. no heat for a month might justify not paying that month). The judge might reduce what you owe rather than order the landlord to pay you. Each case is fact-specific.  
- **Former Landlord Suing for Unpaid Rent or Damages:** *Eligible.* If you are a landlord who didn’t file an eviction during the tenancy (or did but also want additional damages), you can sue a former tenant in Magistrate Court for any unpaid rent, late fees, or property damage after move-out. **Examples:** (1) Your tenant left two months early owing $2,500 in rent, and also $800 in repairs beyond the deposit – you can sue for $3,300 (assuming deposit was applied appropriately). (2) Tenant stayed but never paid last month’s rent and the deposit didn’t cover it – claim the unpaid rent. Just ensure you follow the deposit law: if you kept deposit for damages without sending an itemized list, you might **lose the right to claim those damages**[^13]. In fact, Georgia’s law says if you fail to give the required damage list within 3 business days of move-out (for inspection) and 30 days for return, you **forfeit any right to retain the deposit and also the right to sue the tenant for those damages**[^13]. Yes, part (b) of O.C.G.A. 44-7-35 specifically bars a landlord from suing for damages if the required lists/statements weren’t provided in time[^13]. So a landlord coming to small claims with a damages claim should have their paperwork in order. If you did everything by the book (made move-in/move-out checklists, sent the itemized letter, etc.), you can ask for whatever the tenant legitimately owes above the deposit. Present the lease, evidence of the unpaid amounts (ledgers, invoices for repairs), and copies of the notices you sent the tenant. Keep in mind the **statute of limitations**: written lease breaches are 6 years, oral arrangements 4 years[^6][^7]. So don’t wait too long.  
- **Wrongful Retention of Tenant’s Personal Property:** *Eligible.* If a landlord or property manager kept or disposed of your personal belongings after you moved out (or were evicted), contrary to law, you can sue for the value of those items. Georgia law on evictions provides that after a lawful eviction (writ of possession), any property the tenant leaves can be placed on the landlord’s property line (essentially curbside) – which effectively means it can be picked over or disposed. Some cities or counties might have more protective ordinances, but generally Georgia is not very protective of evicted tenants’ belongings once out. However, **outside** of a formal eviction scenario (e.g. landlord locked you out illegally or removed your items without a court order), you have strong rights to reclaim your stuff or be compensated. **Example:** You moved out voluntarily but accidentally left a box of expensive memorabilia in the garage. The landlord finds it and refuses to return it, or threw it away – you can sue for conversion. Or if the landlord promised to hold items for you to pick up and then didn’t, that could be wrongful. If there was an illegal **self-help eviction** (landlord changed locks and put your stuff on the street without a marshal), you can not only sue for your property loss but that action itself violates Georgia law (landlords must use the court eviction process). Small claims can handle the damage aspect (value of items); it can’t directly penalize the illegal eviction beyond that. Bring testimony or other evidence of what happened and list/value of items. If you have receipts or photos of the items, great; if not, do your best to estimate and describe (the judge will determine a reasonable value – usually garage-sale value, not brand-new cost, for used items).  
- **Disputes Over Fees (Cleaning, Late Fees, Pet Fees, Utilities):** *Eligible.* These often come up when a landlord withholds a deposit for various fees or a tenant is charged excessive fees. **Examples:** (1) Landlord charged a $300 “cleaning fee” after move-out even though you left the place broom clean, and deducted it from your deposit – you can sue to recover that $300 if you believe it’s wrongful. (2) Repeated late fees or nonsufficient funds (NSF) fees added up during tenancy and the landlord is trying to collect them now – you might dispute them if they were not in the lease or were unreasonably high. Georgia generally enforces lease provisions for late fees as long as they aren’t an illegal penalty (e.g. an extremely high late fee might be deemed punitive). Pet fees: Nonrefundable pet fees are allowed if disclosed in lease. Utility charges: If the landlord was supposed to pay utilities but didn’t, causing you to pay, or vice versa, that can be a claim. Small claims allows either party to hash out these miscellaneous charges. Judges often take an equitable approach – if a cleaning fee seems unwarranted (or the amount seems arbitrary), they may side with the tenant. Conversely, if the tenant clearly trashed the place, a cleaning fee is appropriate. Present your lease (which should detail fees) and any evidence (photos of cleanliness, records of payments). If you’re a landlord defending fees, show invoices (e.g. professional cleaning cost) or how the fee was agreed in lease.  

### Employment & Wages  
*(Georgia is an at-will employment state with relatively fewer wage/hour laws than some states. Many wage disputes can be handled in small claims, but some might be better handled administratively or under federal law. Below we cover what an individual can sue for directly.)*

- **Unpaid Final Paycheck / Wages:** *Eligible.* If an employer failed to pay your last paycheck or other earned wages, you can absolutely sue them in Magistrate Court for the wages owed. Georgia has **no specific state statute mandating when final pay is due**, so generally it defaults to the regular pay schedule or “reasonable time.” However, once that time has passed, it’s simply a breach of the employment agreement (or violation of the federal Fair Labor Standards Act if minimum wage/overtime issues). **Examples:** (1) You quit or were fired from a job and the employer never mailed your final check of $800. You can file a wage claim for $800. (2) Your employer implemented a bonus plan that you earned, say $1,500, but after you resigned they refuse to pay the bonus because you weren’t employed on payout date (contrary to what the plan said) – you might sue for that if the bonus was earned. Georgia doesn’t provide a state penalty for late wage payments (unlike California’s daily penalties), but you are entitled to the actual wages and possibly interest. Additionally, if the amount is small or the employer is uncooperative, you can also contact the U.S. Department of Labor (Wage & Hour Division) for assistance – especially if it involves minimum wage or overtime. But the USDOL usually won’t chase one-off final pay if it’s not a widespread issue. Thus, small claims is often the quickest route. Provide evidence of hours worked and pay rate – pay stubs, timesheets, offer letter, etc. If you were salaried, show your salary and how you pro-rate it for the final period. If the employer has a history of not paying, mention any communications or admissions. **Tip:** If your claim is for minimum wage or overtime under FLSA and is significant, note that under federal law you might recover liquidated damages (double the wages) and attorney fees in a higher court. But in small claims (state court), you’ll likely just ask for the base wages owed; Magistrates typically won’t apply federal liquidated damages unless you explicitly frame it as an FLSA claim and the judge is receptive. For simplicity, most individuals just sue for the gross amount owed (you won’t get into tax withholdings stuff – the court awards a gross amount and the employer should still properly withhold taxes on a late wage payment, but that’s outside the scope of court).  
- **Unpaid Commissions or Bonuses:** *Eligible.* If you’re a salesperson or employee owed commissions on sales, or a performance bonus that was contractually promised, you can sue for those earnings. **Examples:** (1) You sold several big accounts before leaving a company, which generated $10,000 in commissions per your commission plan, but the company didn’t pay after you left. As long as you can show the commissions were earned under the terms (e.g. the sales finalized, and the commission plan doesn’t say you forfeit if not employed), you have a good claim. (2) You hit all targets for an annual bonus of $5,000, but the employer refuses to pay citing budget cuts – you can claim the bonus if it was part of your agreed compensation. Georgia honors commission agreements as contracts. If nothing was in writing, it can be trickier, but past practice and industry standards can be evidence. The **Georgia Wage Payment Act** (note: Georgia does **not** have a general wage payment statute with penalties; attempts to pass one haven’t succeeded) – so you rely on contract law. You should have any offer letter, commission plan document, emails detailing the pay arrangement, etc. If the commission was large, some salespeople opt to sue in State Court to also get attorney fees for bad faith under O.C.G.A. § 13-6-11 (if they hire a lawyer). But if it’s within $15k and you want to DIY, small claims is fine. Keep in mind the 6-year limit for written or 4-year for oral contracts[^6][^7]. Also, check if the company required arbitration (some employment contracts have arbitration clauses – though wage claims might be exempt in some instances). If arbitration clause exists, the employer could ask the Magistrate to dismiss in favor of arbitration. Many small employers don’t have that.  
- **Unreimbursed Business Expenses:** *Eligible.* If your job required you to pay certain expenses (travel, supplies) with the promise of reimbursement and your employer didn’t reimburse you, you can sue for those expenses. **Example:** A salesperson paid $1,000 for airfare and hotel to attend a work conference, on the understanding the company would reimburse, but after her resignation, the company stalled and never paid. Or an employee used their personal car for deliveries and wasn’t reimbursed mileage as agreed. These out-of-pocket costs are recoverable as a form of wage (some argue they’re basically unpaid compensation). There’s no specific law forcing reimbursement unless it was agreed, but if your employment or company policy says they reimburse, that’s enforceable. Present receipts and any written policy or emails approving the expenses. If it’s a clear entitlement, Magistrate judges will award it. If not clearly agreed, it may be tougher (some employers claim “we never authorized that expense”). Having an approval email from a manager makes your case strong. The time limit is again contract-based (4 or 6 years). Such claims are often small and ideal for small claims court.  
- **Misclassification as Independent Contractor (seeking wages/overtime):** *Limited eligibility.* If you were treated as an independent contractor but believe you were an employee and are owed overtime or minimum wages, you *can* attempt to recover those in Magistrate Court, but these cases can get complex. Often, these are handled under the federal Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), which can be pursued in federal court or by the Department of Labor. However, small claims has been used by some workers to simply claim “unpaid wages” for overtime hours. **Example:** You worked 60 hours/week as a delivery driver but were paid a flat rate that ends up below minimum wage per hour. You could sue for the difference to minimum wage for all those hours, arguing you were effectively an employee. The challenge: FLSA and misclassification law can involve legal tests beyond the simplicity of small claims. The Magistrate judge might not be well-versed in FLSA and could dismiss if it seems like a federal matter. But they do have jurisdiction as long as it’s money under $15k. If you go this route, frame it as breach of contract or failure to pay for hours worked. Alternatively, filing a complaint with the **U.S. Department of Labor** might be more effective if it’s a clear wage violation affecting others too. Georgia has no additional state overtime law, so it’s all federal. In summary, while you *can* bring it, be prepared to simplify: e.g. “I worked X hours, was paid $Y, at minimum wage I should have been paid $Z, so I’m owed $Z–$Y.” Provide your time records and pay records. The court can award actual unpaid wages, but likely will not handle statutory liquidated damages or civil penalties. Many misclassification situations exceed small claims cap if it’s long-term, but smaller cases (a few thousand dollars) fit. If your goal is just your wages, small claims could suffice; if you want double damages and to set precedent, consult an employment attorney for a bigger case.  
- **Better Forums for Wage Claims:** Georgia lacks a state Labor Commissioner process for individual wage claims (unlike some states). The Georgia Department of Labor handles things like unemployment benefits and some very limited labor law aspects, but not typically wage theft claims. Therefore, your choices are basically small claims court or federal remedies. The **U.S. Department of Labor** (federal Wage & Hour Division) can investigate minimum wage/overtime complaints. If your claim is clearly under federal law (e.g. unpaid overtime) and is significant, the DOL might pursue it and can get you back wages and liquidated damages. But this can take time and they may decline small-dollar cases or cases where law is ambiguous (like independent contractor status disputes). Also, if it’s an employer with multiple affected employees, DOL is more likely to step in. Another route is a private lawsuit in State or Superior Court for breach of contract or under FLSA (which allows attorney fees). But that requires hiring a lawyer, which may not be practical for smaller amounts. In summary: *small claims is usually the most accessible forum for straightforward wage disputes in Georgia*, and many workers use it to successfully recover final pay or expenses. Just be aware of what it can’t do (it won’t issue civil penalties against the employer beyond your owed money, and it can’t order the employer to change its practices).  

### Personal Services / Professional Services Disputes  
- **Hairdressers / Barbers / Nail Salons (Botched Service):** *Eligible.* If a personal care service goes awry and causes you financial loss, you can sue for a refund or cost to fix the issue. **Examples:** (1) A hair stylist severely damaged your hair (chemical burn or wrong color) and you had to pay another salon $300 to correct it – you could sue the original salon for that $300 plus perhaps any additional damages if, say, your hair was ruined and you had to cut it short (valuing hair damage is subjective, but you could claim the cost of treatments, etc.). (2) A nail salon’s technician injured you (cut you leading to infection) – you could sue for the medical bills (small claims can cover minor injury treatment costs). These fall under breach of contract (you paid for a competent service and did not receive it) or negligence. Georgia requires some cosmetology licenses for hair and nail services; an unlicensed provider could also be violating state law (you could report them to the GA Board of Cosmetology and Barbers), but for money damages you go to court. Keep evidence: photos of the bad result or injury, receipts for what you paid and what you spent to fix it, and any communications where the provider acknowledged the issue or refused to refund.  
- **Lessons or Coaching Not Provided:** *Eligible.* Whether it’s tutoring, music lessons, personal training, or any kind of lessons/coaching service, if you paid and the instructor didn’t deliver the sessions (or did far fewer than promised), you can sue for the unused portion of your money. **Examples:** (1) You pre-paid a tutor $500 for 10 sessions, and they only did 4 sessions then disappeared – you can claim the value of the 6 missed sessions ($300 if pro-rated). (2) You hired a tennis coach for a summer program for $800, but they canceled most classes – you seek a refund for the canceled days. These are straightforward contract issues. If you have a written agreement, great; if not, the court will look at proof of payment and whatever terms you can show (texts or emails discussing the number of sessions). If some value was received, the judge might award a partial refund. If none at all (coach ghosted from start), likely a full refund. The statute of limitations is 4 years on oral service contracts[^7]. If the provider gave excuses (illness, etc.), you could still get a refund for what wasn’t provided – their circumstances aren’t your fault, though a judge might consider if they offered make-up lessons. Ultimately, if you paid for a service not rendered, you are entitled to your money back in proportion.  
- **Pet Boarding / Grooming / Training Disputes:** *Eligible.* Pet services can lead to claims if something goes wrong (though our pets are beloved, remember legally they are property – you typically recover economic loss, not emotional distress, for issues with pets). **Examples:** (1) You boarded your dog for a week for $200, but when you returned, the dog was sick or injured due to the kennel’s negligence, leading to $600 in vet bills. You can sue the boarding facility for those vet costs (and possibly a refund of the boarding fee). (2) A dog groomer accidentally cut your dog’s ear causing a vet visit – claim the vet bill and maybe a refund of the grooming charge. (3) A pet trainer guaranteed certain results or used harmful methods that caused damage – you might claim the cost of retraining or treatment for your pet. Georgia has **no “pet lemon law” or specific consumer statute for pet services**, so these are basic negligence or breach of contract claims. You must show the service provider failed to use reasonable care. Many boarding contracts try to limit liability (e.g. “we’re only liable for up to $X or not liable for vet bills unless grossly negligent”). Those clauses can be challenged – courts often won’t enforce a complete waiver if the provider truly was negligent. In small claims, judges often cut through boilerplate if fairness dictates. Provide records: the contract, vet reports linking injury to incident at the facility, photos of the pet’s condition, etc. The measure of damages is usually the vet cost and related expenses. You generally can’t get “pain and suffering” for your pet’s distress in Georgia – it’s limited to economic loss (the law views it as damage to property). One exception: if someone **willfully** killed your pet, Georgia law allows **some additional measures of value**, but that would likely be above small claims and potentially a criminal matter. For typical service mishaps, stick to bills and refunds.  
- **Medical / Dental Billing Disputes (non-insurance matters):** *Eligible.* Small claims can address disputes like being overcharged for a procedure, or paying for a treatment you didn’t receive. It won’t handle malpractice (that requires a higher court and expert witnesses usually), but it can deal with straightforward billing issues. **Examples:** (1) You went to a dentist for a procedure quoted at $500, but you were billed $1,200 without explanation of extra charges – after trying to resolve, you can sue for the difference, arguing you only agreed to $500. (2) You paid a cosmetic clinic in advance for a series of laser treatments for $1,000, but they closed after providing only half – you’d sue for the unused $500. (3) A doctor’s office charged you for lab tests that were never performed. These cases often revolve around what was agreed and what was done. If you have an **itemized bill** that includes erroneous charges (e.g. double billing or services not rendered), that’s strong evidence. If insurance was involved, be careful: sometimes insurance adjustments and contractual write-offs complicate it. But often uninsured or out-of-network patients end up in disputes. Georgia has a new law to address **surprise medical bills** (from out-of-network providers), which gives a process for arbitration – that might be outside small claims if applicable. But if you knowingly went out-of-network and just think the charge is unfairly high, small claims won’t second-guess price reasonableness unless it’s egregious and possibly unconscionable. It mostly enforces agreements. If you can argue there was a contract (explicit or implicit) for a certain price or that the additional charges were unauthorized, you have a case. Include any written estimates, consent forms, or lack thereof. Note: If it’s a hospital bill dispute, you might also reach out to the Georgia Department of Law Consumer Protection or the billing department for negotiation – by the time it hits small claims, usually negotiation failed. Small claims judges can sometimes find equity (e.g. if you truly were misled about cost), but if you simply don’t want to pay and they did the service, you might lose. Understand the distinction: billing *errors or misrepresentations* are good cases; simply high bills are harder.  
- **Daycare / Preschool Refunds:** *Eligible.* If you paid a daycare or preschool tuition and withdrew your child, whether you get a refund depends on the contract terms. But if a refund is due or was promised and not given, you can sue. **Examples:** (1) You paid $900 for a month of daycare, but after a week you had to pull your child out because you moved. The daycare agreed to refund the unused 3 weeks but hasn’t – you can claim the ~$675 for 3 weeks. (2) You put down a $300 enrollment deposit for preschool and then decided not to enroll well before the term, and they refuse to return it contrary to what was told to you. Daycares often have contracts that specify notice required for withdrawal and whether unused tuition is refunded. If the contract says “no refunds,” you might be out of luck unless you can show they didn’t uphold their side (e.g. the daycare closed or lost its license and couldn’t provide service). If the daycare kicked your child out without good cause, you’d likely get a prorated refund. Always check the written agreement. For small claims, bring the enrollment agreement or receipt and any communications about withdrawal or promises of refund. If the contract is silent on the issue, courts lean toward refunding prepayments for services not delivered, minus any proper fees. If they claim your deposit was non-refundable but never stated that in writing when you paid, you have a strong case to get it back under FBPA for unfair practice (taking money without disclosure of terms could be deceptive).  

### Loans, Debts, and Financial Disputes Among Individuals  
- **Money Lent to Friends or Family:** *Eligible (and common).* Personal loans between friends/relatives often lead to small claims when they go unpaid. As noted earlier, an informal IOU is enforceable. **Example:** You lent your cousin $2,000 to help with a down payment, with an understanding he’d pay you back in a few months. It’s been a year with no payments. You can sue for $2,000 plus any nominal interest you maybe agreed on (if none agreed, you generally just get principal; court might or might not award statutory interest from the date due). It doesn’t matter that it was a handshake or “family helping family” – if you can convince the judge it was indeed a loan, not a gift. How to prove it? Ideally a text or email where they mention the loan or repayment, or a notation like “Loan” on a check or Venmo. Lacking that, your testimony and any pattern (did they make any small payments? did they acknowledge owing you to anyone else?). Georgia’s 4-year limit for oral contracts applies[^7]. The judge may ask the borrower, “Did you borrow this money?” If they say yes but “I thought I didn’t have to pay back,” it usually doesn’t fly absent evidence it was a gift. So, yes, you can sue a friend for a loan and many do – though weigh the relationship fallout. Legally, it’s straightforward debt collection once you show the money was given and expected back.  
- **Shared Expenses (Roommates, Ex-Partners):** *Eligible.* When people share expenses (rent, utilities, travel costs) and one party doesn’t pay their part, the other can sue to recoup the owed share. **Examples:** (1) You and a roommate each signed a lease but you ended up covering the roommate’s portion of rent ($600) for the last two months after they lost their job. You can sue the roommate for $1,200 – even if the landlord doesn’t care because you paid full rent, between you two there was an implicit agreement to split rent. Bring proof you paid the full rent and perhaps communications where roommate agreed to “I’ll pay you back” or at least acknowledged the situation. (2) You went on a group trip and fronted the Airbnb cost of $1,000 on the understanding your three friends would each pay you $250. If one friend never paid, you can sue for $250. This is basically a mini-contract among friends. Often texts or emails show the agreed split. Judges see many roommate and trip money cases – they enforce fair divisions if evidence supports there was such an agreement. If nothing was explicitly agreed, the court might infer an agreement based on what’s customary (splitting evenly, etc.). If one person argues “I didn’t pay because they did X,” the judge might consider offsets (like if one roommate did all cleaning or bought other stuff – but those are fuzzy). It’s best to have clear numbers. If it’s emotional (like an ex-now-enemy refusing to pay a joint bill), stick to the math and evidence of the arrangement.  
- **Wedding / Engagement Gift Disputes (including Rings):** *Eligible.* When engagements break off or weddings are canceled, disputes arise over expensive gifts, especially the engagement ring or shared wedding expenses. Georgia follows the majority rule that an engagement ring is a **conditional gift** – if the marriage doesn’t occur, the ring should be returned to the giver, regardless of fault (Georgia case law supports return of the ring to the proposer if the engagement is ended)[^4]. So if your ex-fiancée refuses to return the ring, you can sue for the ring or its value. Magistrate Court can technically order return of personal property (they have jurisdiction to issue judgments for recovery of personal property up to $15k value)[^1]. Usually, if you just want the ring back, you’d sue for “return of the ring or $X value.” The value should be within $15k – if it’s a $20k ring, Magistrate can’t hear it. But many rings are under $15k (if above, you might consider waiving value above 15k and ask for max $15k). Provide proof of purchase and that it was an engagement gift. If she shows up with the ring, judge can order it handed over. If not, a money judgment for value might be given. For wedding costs: say you and your ex each paid certain vendors deposits and now you’re sorting who bears the loss after cancellation. Small claims can divide those if there was an agreement. Often each party eats what they paid unless there was a specific pact (“we’ll split all losses 50/50”). If you paid all deposits and the other person promised to share the loss, that promise is enforceable. Keep any messages about sharing costs. Another scenario: gifts between couples (like one gave the other an expensive laptop during the relationship and now wants it back). Generally, completed gifts can’t be reclaimed just because you broke up (unlike engagement rings, which have that special conditional nature). So if you sue an ex for random gifts, expect to lose; but if you can frame it as a loan or conditional (“I gave you this car on condition you’d refinance and put it in your name, which you didn’t”), you might have a shot. Magistrates often see these and follow common sense: engagement ring – returnable; other gifts – not returnable unless clearly a loan.  
- **Jointly Titled Property After Breakup:** *Eligible (if seeking money or partition under $15k).* If unmarried people bought something together (car, furniture, etc.) and break up, each has an interest. Small claims can’t adjudicate real estate title, but for personal property, it can effectively award money to compensate one party if, say, one keeps the item. **Example:** You and your former partner co-own a car (both names on title). After splitting, the partner keeps the car. You could either agree they buy out your half or you sue for half its value in small claims. The court can’t transfer title or force a sale in Magistrate, but it can award you a monetary judgment for your interest. It’s like a simplified partition: value of car $10k, you get $5k. Or if you contributed significantly to an asset solely in their name expecting to share ownership, you can attempt a claim for unjust enrichment. These cases are a bit complex legally (perhaps equitable partition or resulting trust issues, usually for higher courts). But for simpler property splits where value under $15k, many ex-couples use small claims for resolution. If the item can be easily divided (e.g. each take certain furniture items), do that outside of court. Use court for those that can’t be split and where a cash equalization is needed. Provide evidence of co-ownership or contributions (title, receipts showing who paid). The judge will likely try for an equitable solution. Note: If it’s real estate (house) or something over $15k, Magistrate has no jurisdiction – those must go to Superior Court as partition actions.  
- **Recovery of Cash Deposits / Earnest Money:** *Eligible.* If you put down a cash deposit in a transaction that didn’t go through, you may sue to get it back (depending on contract terms). **Examples:** (1) You gave a $1,000 cash “good faith” deposit to hold a used car, but then decided not to buy within the agreed time. Whether you get it back depends on the agreement: if it was a nonrefundable deposit, you might lose it; if nothing was said, typically a deposit to hold should be returned if sale not completed (especially if seller didn’t incur loss). You can argue for return in small claims. (2) You paid $5,000 earnest money for a house purchase and the deal fell through due to the seller’s fault (like they couldn’t provide clear title). You can definitely sue for that earnest money if the seller or broker refuses to release it. Often earnest money is held by a broker and there’s a procedure (Georgia real estate law: broker can interplead the funds or require mutual release). If it’s stuck, you might need to sue the seller and possibly the broker (though typically the broker will deposit it with the court if there’s a dispute – an **interpleader** – beyond small claims scope). But if seller straight-up kept it wrongly, small claims can handle the claim of $5k. Always read the purchase contract: it often says what happens to earnest money if buyer backs out vs seller default. Judges will follow that. If seller was at fault, you get refund; if buyer breached contract, seller often gets to keep it (liquidated damages). If it’s ambiguous, the small claims judge will decide based on equity. Provide the contract and any communications on why deal failed.  
- **Consumer vs. Creditor: Can Banks or Collection Agencies Sue in Small Claims?** *Yes, generally.* In Georgia, original creditors and even debt collectors (assignees of debt) frequently use Magistrate Court to sue consumers for loan defaults, credit card debt, etc. Unlike some states, Georgia has no blanket prohibition on **assignees** filing in small claims. So as a consumer, you might find yourself a defendant in Magistrate Court if you owe money. (More on defendants’ response in section 9.) From the **plaintiff** perspective: If you are a small business or individual owed money by a customer or even by a friend on a promissory note, you are allowed to sue in Magistrate Court to collect (no requirement to be the original creditor, as long as you prove the debt and that it was assigned to you if applicable). But if you are an **unlicensed “debt adjustment” or collection agency collecting for others, be careful**: Georgia law (Fair Business Practices Act and industrial loan act etc.) may require certain licenses or may restrict attorneys representing collection companies in Mag Court if the actual party is not present. Usually, collection agencies hire attorneys and go to State or Superior Court for larger amounts. For ≤$15k, many credit card companies and hospitals file directly in Magistrate with an affidavit. So the playing field is open. If you are a **consumer wanting to sue a bank or lender** (maybe for wrongful fees or breach of contract), you can too, but many banking relationships have arbitration clauses. If not, small claims can hear, for example, “My bank overcharged $500 in fees contrary to our account agreement.” Or “I paid off my loan but the lender failed to record the satisfaction and I incurred $300 in costs – I want that back.” Those are within jurisdiction.  

### Vehicle-Specific Disputes  
- **Accident Damage Where Insurance Won’t Pay:** *Eligible.* Often if you have a **fender-bender** with modest damages and the other party’s insurance denies liability or offers too little, you can sue the at-fault driver directly in Magistrate Court for the full amount of your damages (repair costs, rental car, possibly diminished value). **Example:** Your car sustained $4,000 damage in a crash clearly the other’s fault, but their insurer only offers $2,500 claiming prior damage, etc. You can sue the driver for $4,000 and let the judge decide. If you win, the driver’s insurance should actually cover the judgment up to policy limits (insurers often end up paying once their insured is found liable). Georgia allows you to recover **the reasonable value of repair** or if totaled, the fair market value of the vehicle pre-accident. You can also claim towing and rental car expenses. **Pain and suffering or medical** from injuries are separate – those typically push the case out of small claims if significant. But minor injury costs (like a $1,500 ER visit) could be added if you are under the cap and not seeking general pain and suffering damages (those are hard to quantify in small claims; some judges might award small amounts if you prove inconvenience, but generally they focus on out-of-pocket losses). Bring evidence: the police report (if it supports your side), photos of the scene and damage, repair estimates or bills, and any medical bills if claiming those. If liability is contested, witness testimony is crucial. You can have witnesses come or even written statements (though live is better). The other party might counter-argue or even counter-sue you for their damages if they believe you were at fault – effectively the judge then decides liability like a mini trial. Small claims is a common way to resolve minor car accidents when insurance fails to.  
- **Mechanic / Body Shop Disputes:** *Eligible.* Aside from overcharges (covered above), disputes might include: the mechanic’s work made things worse, or they charged for new parts but used used parts, etc. **Examples:** (1) You paid a body shop $2,000 to repair collision damage, but they used bondo and duct tape instead of actually replacing parts as billed – sue for the cost of proper repair or a refund. (2) A mechanic installed a rebuilt engine incorrectly, ruining it; you seek the value of that engine and labor. These can be complex if expert testimony is needed to prove the work was faulty. In Magistrate Court, you usually rely on common-sense evidence or maybe an inspection from another mechanic. If you have an inspection report from a second shop saying “previous repair was done improperly causing damage,” that helps. You could even bring the second mechanic as a witness (though that can be hard to arrange). But often, photos of poor workmanship (e.g. sloppy welding, parts held by wire) can sway the judge. Also, any warranties given by the shop (e.g. “1-year guarantee”) are useful – if they refused to honor their own warranty, mention that. Legally, it’s breach of contract or negligence. Georgia’s **Motor Vehicle Service and Repair Act** (O.C.G.A. § 10-1-430 et seq.) provides some protections – e.g., an estimate must be given if requested, and no charges beyond estimate without consent. If the shop violated that (did extra work and charged more without approval), you can use that to claim the extra charge was unlawful. Also, charging for parts not actually installed is a form of fraud/FBPA violation. If you prove that, you might even get treble damages under FBPA (though small claims judges rarely calculate treble, they might just say “refund the bogus charges”). Present any document from the shop: estimate, final invoice, etc.  
- **Dealership Disputes (Repairs or Sales):** *Eligible.* If your dispute is with a vehicle dealership for a repair or a warranty issue on a used car, or something like being sold add-on services you didn’t want, you can use small claims. **Examples:** (1) A dealership’s service department charged $800 for a repair that didn’t fix the issue (and maybe they refuse to refund or correctly diagnose without more money) – you could sue for the $800. However, typically you’d give them a chance to fix under warranty. If they utterly failed, a judge might side with you for a refund due to lack of results. (2) You bought a certified pre-owned car with the understanding it had a clean history, then discovered it had prior accident damage – you might claim that’s a misrepresentation and seek compensation (possibly return of car or a diminished value difference). That could be above $15k if car is expensive, but if it’s within range or you’re willing to settle for that, you can try. Many of these could be FBPA claims for deceptive practice. Another common one: **Yo-yo sales** (dealership lets you take the car then financing falls through and they demand the car back or different terms) – if they misled you and you incurred costs, you could sue for say your down payment or any damages. There are federal and state laws on that, but small claims can address the immediate money issue (like “I want my $2,000 down payment back because the deal was rescinded”). Car sales are a big area – be specific in what monetary relief you want. If it’s a **warranty repair on a new car under Lemon Law**, that goes to the Lemon Law process as noted. But if it’s a used car with a dealer-provided 3-month warranty that they aren’t honoring, small claims is perfect to enforce that. Include the written warranty and evidence of the defect.  
- **Towing / Impound Fees Disputes:** *Eligible.* If your car was towed or booted and you believe it was illegal or improper, you can sue the towing company or property owner to get your money back. Georgia has laws regulating non-consensual tows (from private lots, etc.), including fee caps and signage requirements (See O.C.G.A. § 44-1-13). For instance, if they overcharged beyond the allowed fees or didn’t have proper signs, you might have a claim. **Example:** Your car was booted in a parking lot with no visible warning sign, and you had to pay $150 to get the boot off. Atlanta city law (for example) caps booting fees (often around $75). So you could sue the booting company for the overcharge or entire amount if they violated regulations. Or if a tow company damaged your vehicle during a tow, you can sue for those damages. Or if it was towed without legal cause (say you actually had a permit displayed), sue for wrongful tow fees. Small claims won’t formally decide if a law was broken in a broad sense, but they will evaluate if *you* were wronged and money should be returned. If you cite the specific code or ordinance the tow company violated, that helps. Provide receipts, photos of the location (to show inadequate signage or that you were not illegally parked). If you already filed a complaint with local authorities, mention the outcome. Many won’t help individual refunds – they just fine the company – so court is how you get your money. Under Georgia law, towing companies that violate certain provisions may even owe treble damages in Superior Court, but in small claims you’re likely to just get actual fees back. Still worth pursuing to deter bad actors.  
- **Parking Lot Damage (hit-and-run or known driver):** *Eligible.* If your car was damaged in a parking lot and you identified the responsible party (like through surveillance or witness), you can sue them as in any property damage scenario. If it’s a mystery (“someone dented my car and I suspect the next-door neighbor”), you need evidence to tie it to them or you’ll lose. For known incidents: e.g. your apartment’s security gate malfunctioned and smashed your car – you could sue the apartment complex (or their management) for repair costs, asserting negligence in maintaining the gate. Or if an employee at a valet service scratched your car, the valet company is liable. Provide repair estimates and evidence linking the damage to their action (photos of the gate on your car, etc.).  

### Personal Injury (Minor Cases)  
Small claims courts **can hear minor personal injury cases** (like minor car accident injuries, slip-and-fall with small medical bills), but be cautious: the $15,000 cap in Georgia often is far below the value of serious injury claims. If your injuries are even moderately severe, you could easily exceed $15k in medical costs, and you’d be waiving any excess by going to Magistrate. Also, negligence and injury cases can be complex (issues of proof, expert testimony about injuries, etc.). Still, for *small* injuries or where liability is very clear and damages modest, Magistrate Court is a viable option for quicker resolution without attorney fees.

- **Minor Injuries (fits under $15k):** *Eligible.* **Examples:** (1) You slipped on an unmarked wet floor at a store and sprained your ankle, incurring $1,200 in ER and physical therapy bills, and some lost wages taking a couple days off. The store’s insurer lowballed you or denied responsibility – you can sue the store for, say, $1,500 (your costs) plus maybe a bit for the inconvenience (though don’t expect large pain & suffering awards in small claims; some judges might award a token amount if the injury was painful, but usually the focus is economic damages). (2) A dog bit you causing a minor injury – $300 urgent care bill – you can sue the dog’s owner for that medical cost (and under Georgia’s dog bite law, if the dog was known vicious or off-leash in violation of ordinance, you could also claim other damages, but likely you’ll stick to actual costs). (3) You got food poisoning from a restaurant, documented by a doctor visit $500 and lab tests – sue for your medical cost and possibly refund of the meal. In these cases, you must prove the defendant’s liability (negligence, etc.) just like in a bigger court, but in a simplified way. Bring any incident reports, photos (e.g. the wet floor with no sign), and medical records/bills. Magistrate judges will scrutinize causation – e.g., is it clear the store’s negligence caused your fall? Without a lawyer, you’ll present the situation in narrative. Defendants (businesses) often send an insurance adjuster or representative, or sometimes no one (leading to default if you served properly). If liability is disputed, it can become a “he-said, she-said.” Judges try their best to sort it out. If the evidence isn’t solid, you may get nothing. But if it is (like a witness saw the spill and no warning, etc.), you stand to recover your out-of-pocket losses. Georgia’s **statute of limitations** for personal injury is **2 years**[^14]. So you must file within two years of the injury.  
- **Why Not Larger Injuries:** For context, if you had a bigger injury (e.g. a broken bone, $20k hospital bill, significant pain), you’d normally hire a personal injury attorney who might take it to State Court, because you could recover much more (including pain & suffering, which often far exceed medical costs). In Magistrate, you’re capped at $15k total and it’s unlikely to cover the intangible damages well. Also, serious cases often involve expert testimony (doctors), which isn’t practical in small claims. So, *use small claims for small injuries where the main issue is reimbursing expenses.*  

### Specific Statutory Claims in Georgia  
Certain Georgia statutes create causes of action or special remedies that can be pursued in small claims if within the amount limit. We’ve mentioned some already (bad check penalties, FBPA treble damages, security deposit triple damages). Here’s a summary of key ones:  

- **Bad Check Double Damages:** *Yes.* Under O.C.G.A. § 13-6-15, if a check bounces and the maker doesn’t pay within 10 days of demand, the payee can recover **double the amount owed, up to $500 extra** plus court costs[^9]. This is a statutory penalty to discourage writing bad checks. This can be claimed in Magistrate Court. (We discussed this under Contract disputes – e.g. a $200 bad check yields $400 judgment). Remember the certified mail demand is a prerequisite.  
- **Consumer Protection (UDAP) – Fair Business Practices Act (FBPA):** *Yes, with process.* O.C.G.A. § 10-1-390 et seq. allows consumers to sue for unfair or deceptive acts. Remedies: **actual damages**, and if intentional, the court **shall** award **treble damages** (3×) and **attorney’s fees**[^10]. The catch: a 30-day prior demand letter is required[^10]. In small claims, you can certainly allege a violation of the FBPA. Example uses: car sales fraud, misleading advertising, scams, etc. If you prove the act was deceptive and you were harmed, the judge can grant triple your losses. Some Magistrate judges might not automatically know to do so – you should explicitly request treble damages in your complaint if pursuing FBPA. Show a copy of your demand letter and the fact that 30 days passed with no adequate offer. Note: The FBPA doesn’t apply to all transactions – it exempts e.g. regulated industries and maybe isolated one-on-one non-public transactions. It mainly covers consumer transactions that could impact the public. But many scenarios do qualify (auto sales, home improvement scams, etc.). If in doubt, you can try – the worst is the judge just gives single damages.  
- **Lemon Law (New Car Warranty Rights):** *Small claims generally **not** used.* Georgia’s Lemon Law (O.C.G.A. § 10-1-780 to 10-1-786) covers new motor vehicles with defects. It provides for **mandatory arbitration** through a state-run program and, if that fails, a right to sue in Superior Court for replacement or refund. The amounts and relief (replace the car or refund purchase price) are way beyond Magistrate jurisdiction. So you would **not file a lemon law claim in small claims**. However, if you have a smaller related claim (like you paid out-of-pocket for towing or repairs that should be reimbursed under warranty), you might sue for those costs alone if the manufacturer refuses. But typically, the Lemon Law has its own procedure including reimbursing certain costs if you win in arbitration. In short: treat lemon law issues outside the small claims forum. Only use small claims for auto issues not covered by lemon law (used cars or incidental expenses as noted).  
- **Identity Theft / Credit Fraud Losses:** *Possibly.* If someone stole your identity and you suffered out-of-pocket losses (for example, you had to pay certain charges or you lost funds), you can sue the perpetrator for damages – but often the perpetrator is unknown or not caught. Georgia has an Identity Theft statute (O.C.G.A. § 16-9-130) that allows victims to recover **$5,000 in damages or three times the actual damages, whichever is greater, plus fees** from the thief, but that’s more a criminal law with civil remedy rarely used (and you’d have to catch the person). In practice, victims usually rely on banks to absorb fraudulent charges. If a credit bureau or company negligently allowed the theft (e.g. released your info improperly), you might have a claim under the federal Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) or state law, but that’s beyond small claims complexity. Usually small claims identity theft cases might involve suing a known individual who, say, opened a utility account in your name that cost you money – you could certainly sue them for that money citing fraud. If you have a police report confirming the identity theft by that person, that’s strong evidence. There’s also a provision for getting a declaration of innocence from a court, but that’s not what Magistrate Court does – that’s more for clearing your record. So yes, you can sue an identity thief for monetary loss in small claims if you know who it is and it’s worth under $15k.  
- **Wage/Hour Statutory Damages:** (Addressing again) Under the FLSA (federal), an employer who underpays must pay the shortfall plus an equal amount as liquidated damages, plus attorney’s fees. If you sue in Magistrate for say $1,000 unpaid overtime, you could ask for $2,000 citing FLSA. The court might say it can’t award the extra without a proper federal case (Magistrate Courts can apply federal law though). It’s hit or miss. If you clearly cite FLSA and the employer has no defense (they likely won’t show up if it’s small), the judge could award double – some have. But it’s safer to expect only actual wages unless you really educate the judge on the federal law. There’s no state liquidated damage law since GA has no separate wage statute.  
- **Predatory Lending / Unlicensed Lending:** Georgia has laws capping interest on certain loans and requiring licenses for others (Industrial Loan Act for loans < $3k, etc.). If you fell victim to an unlicensed lender charging illegal interest, you might have a case to void the interest or the loan. Those are complicated and might be in higher court. But if it’s a small illegal online payday loan, small claims could be used to get your money back beyond principal. It would involve showing the loan violated Georgia law (which caps interest at 10% unless licensed). Possibly consult with Georgia Consumer Protection Division in such cases. As a borrower, usually you defend against their collection rather than affirmatively suing. But if you did pay a bunch of illegal interest, you might sue to recover the usurious portion. It’s novel but plausible under O.C.G.A. § 7-4-10 (which allows forfeiture of interest over 5% per month).  

### Other State-Specific Claim Types  
*(This section ensures any niche but realistic disputes that Georgians bring to small claims are covered. If it’s a common real-world problem and not excluded by law, you can usually bring it in Magistrate Court for monetary relief. Below are various examples not already covered above.)*

- **Auto Towing without Authorization:** Georgia’s towing law (O.C.G.A. § 44-1-13) requires certain signage and authorizations for towing from private property. If your car was towed in violation of these rules, you can sue for the tow and storage fees. For instance, if the tow truck didn’t notify law enforcement within 30 minutes of the tow (required by law) or lot had no signs, that law says the tow company can’t charge you. Magistrate Court is where you’d enforce that by getting a refund.  
- **Predatory towing or booting in parking lots:** As touched on, many Georgia cities have ordinances capping boot fees (e.g. Atlanta Code § 162-227 sets max boot fee $75). If charged more, small claims to get the difference or full refund is effective.  
- **Telemarketer or Spam Call Violations:** Georgia consumers have a law against certain telemarketing practices (O.C.G.A. § 46-5-27) and there’s also the federal Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA) which gives $500 per illegal call/text. However, TCPA cases are typically in federal or state courts, not small claims (though technically you could sue for a few spam calls at $500 each in Magistrate; some have done so elsewhere). But identifying the caller and proving violation can be tricky for a layperson. It’s possible if, say, a known local business kept robocalling you after you revoked consent. You could ask for the statutory $500 per call. There’s no Georgia-specific private right of action on calls except federal TCPA. Magistrate Courts in some states have seen pro se TCPA claims. If you try, be sure to have evidence (call logs, that your number was on Do Not Call list or you told them to stop).  
- **Debt Collection Harassment (State FDCPA):** Georgia doesn’t have a state Fair Debt Collection Practices Act giving consumers a private right; it relies on the federal FDCPA, which allows you to sue debt collectors (not original creditors) for up to $1,000 statutory damages for harassment or false collection tactics, plus actual damages and attorney fees. You could bring an FDCPA suit in Magistrate for up to $1,000 (statutory) + maybe any actual loss (like phone bill, etc.). But FDCPA is federal; you can file it in state court if you want. It might be a bit technical (you have to show the collector is third-party and violated specific FDCPA provisions). If it’s straightforward (e.g. they kept calling at 3am or used profanity threatening you – clear violations), you might scare them into settling by a small claims filing. They might remove it to federal court, but for $1k they likely won’t. If you win, you’d get up to $1k; attorneys’ fees are not relevant since you won’t have one. Some Georgia consumers have successfully done this. Just ensure to document the harassment (recordings or logs and any witnesses).  
- **HOA / Condo Disputes (monetary):** Homeowners association issues like wrongful fines or not returning a refundable deposit (for clubhouse rental or whatever) can be small-claimed. If your HOA charged you $500 in fines that you believe are improper under the covenants, you can challenge them in Magistrate. The court will interpret the HOA bylaws or covenants like a contract. Similarly, if you as an HOA are trying to collect a small assessment or fine, you might go to Magistrate (though HOAs often file liens and foreclose – bigger deal). For a homeowner, suing the HOA for, say, damages to your property due to their negligence (like they failed common area maintenance and it flooded your unit costing $3k) – you can sue for that $3k. Many HOA fights, however, seek injunctions (“stop this interference”) or declaratory relief, which Magistrate can’t grant. But pure money claims fall under its jurisdiction.  
- **Mold or Mildew Damage in Rental:** If your apartment had a mold problem due to landlord’s negligence causing you health expenses or property damage, you can claim those costs (e.g. $800 for doctor and ruined clothing). Provide evidence of landlord’s knowledge and failure to act. It’s basically property damage or personal injury (which we covered). No specific mold statute in GA awarding automatic damages, you must prove negligence.  
- **Security Deposit Interest:** Georgia doesn’t require interest on deposits (some states do if held long enough). So no claim for interest unless lease promised it.  
- **Newly effective laws:** Always check if any new GA law gives a small-dollar remedy. For instance, Georgia recently updated laws on **abandoned mobile homes** (O.C.G.A. § 44-7-113) giving landlords a procedure to obtain title or dispose of tenants’ abandoned mobile homes via Magistrate Court. That’s something a landlord or park owner can petition Magistrate for – a new jurisdiction category. Not a typical “small claim” for money, but a special proceeding now allowed in Magistrate. For completeness: if you have an abandoned mobile home in your lot, you as landowner can go to Magistrate for an order permitting sale or disposal, and for up to $3,000 in past due rent from its owner if applicable. This is a 2020+ new process in GA.  
- **Agricultural disputes:** If you sold livestock or crops and buyer didn’t pay, it’s a contract – definitely can sue for the price. Georgia farming communities use Magistrate for things like a neighbor’s livestock damaging your crops (property damage), or disputes over the sale of a cow at auction (if check bounced – tie in bad check law for double damages). Or if someone borrowed your tractor and broke it ($2k repair) – that’s property damage claim. These all fit general categories already covered (contracts or negligence).  
- **Hunting/Fishing violation causing damage:** If someone trespassed to hunt on your land and damaged your property (cut your fence, etc.), you can sue for those damages. Could also get punitive if you sued in higher court for willful trespass, but in small claims you’ll get actual damages.  
- **Ride-share / Gig Economy Issues:** If you’re a driver for Uber/Lyft and they didn’t pay an incentive they promised, that could be a wage/contract issue – might try small claims for the amount (though driver agreements often have arbitration clauses). Or if you were deactivated and had credit in the app (like $200 of earnings not paid out), you could sue to recover that balance. For customers: if a ride-share driver attacked you or wrecked and you had minor damage, you might sue the driver (though ride-share companies have insurance that should cover third-party injuries; small claims is more likely used by drivers vs. the company for payments). Food delivery gig: have a missing payment? Could use small claims if no arbitration. Many gig contracts do require arbitration unfortunately. But if not, small claims is accessible.  
- **Cell Phone Early Termination Fees / Billing Disputes:** These big carriers often have arbitration clauses (and FCC rules). But if not, or you opt out of arbitration, you could sue for a wrongful fee (like they charged $300 ETF contrary to contract). It’s tough to beat them in small claims because they’ll invoke the contract. However, sometimes they don’t show and you could win default. People have tried with mixed success.  
- **Ticket Scalping or Event Refunds:** If you bought concert tickets from someone and they turned out fake, you can sue that seller for what you paid (fraud). Or if an event was canceled and the promoter didn’t refund, and it’s a local entity, small claims for your ticket cost is feasible (class action covers many, but individually you can pursue your own refund).  
- **AirBnB / Short-term rental Damage Deposits:** If you rented a vacation home and the host wrongly kept your security deposit or charged you for damage you didn’t cause, you can sue the host (if they’re in GA or you file where the property is). AirBnB’s platform has its own dispute process, but it’s not binding if you want to go to court. Provided you can establish jurisdiction over the host or vice versa, you can litigate those claims like any deposit dispute. Similarly, hosts could sue guests for damages if AirBnB resolution center didn’t pay out but you have real damages under $15k. Usually AirBnB collects from guest’s card, but if not, small claims is backup.  

*(In summary, virtually any scenario where one party owes money to another in Georgia and the amount is $15,000 or less can be brought in Magistrate Court, **except** those involving types of relief or defendants that the court can’t handle – which we cover next.)*  

### Counterclaims in Small Claims  
In Georgia Magistrate Court, a defendant has the right to file a **counterclaim** against the plaintiff if the defendant believes the plaintiff owes *them* money or that the plaintiff caused them harm related to the same transaction. Key points about counterclaims:  

- **Counterclaim Limit:** The counterclaim is subject to the **same $15,000 jurisdictional limit** as the main claim[^3]. If a defendant’s counterclaim *exceeds* $15,000, the Magistrate Court **cannot** decide it. The defendant would either have to **waive the amount over $15k** and claim only $15,000, or move the case to a higher court. Often, if a counterclaim over the limit is filed, the court will transfer the entire case to State or Superior Court for consolidation. A defendant who tries to slyly split the counterclaim (e.g. sue for $15k now and reserve the rest) won’t be able to recover the remainder later – it’s effectively waived unless they object and seek transfer up front.  
- **Filing a Counterclaim:** The counterclaim process is straightforward. The defendant can file a written counterclaim with the court **at or before the hearing**, typically as part of their Answer or as a separate statement of claim. Georgia law (O.C.G.A. § 15-10-43) provides that the answer may contain a counterclaim[^5]. Most Magistrate Courts have a **counterclaim form** or allow the defendant to write a brief statement of their counter demands. **Form**: There isn’t a statewide numbered form, but many counties provide a generic “Counterclaim” form or an Answer form with space for counterclaims. For instance, Fulton County’s Magistrate website mentions that if a defendant has a claim against the plaintiff, they should file a counterclaim for the judge to consider at trial ([magistratefulton.org](https://magistratefulton.org/175/Small-Claims#:~:text=match%20at%20L88%20charge%20and,collect%20damages%20from%20the%20plaintiff)). The counterclaim should be related to the same facts (it doesn’t strictly have to – Georgia allows permissive counterclaims – but if it’s unrelated, the court might wonder why it’s in this case).  
- **Deadline:** A counterclaim should ideally be filed within the **30 days** answer period (which is the time a defendant has to respond to the suit after service)[^14]. However, even if the answer is late, Georgia Magistrate Courts often allow counterclaims up until the hearing as long as the plaintiff isn’t prejudiced (some courts might allow it on the day of hearing if the facts merit). To be safe, file it by the answer deadline so the plaintiff is on notice. If a default has already been entered (after 30 days with no answer), a counterclaim won’t help unless the default is opened (which Georgia Magistrate Courts can allow within 15 days of default with payment of costs)[^15].  
- **Effect of Counterclaim Over Limit:** If a defendant insists their counterclaim is worth more than $15,000 and does not want to waive the excess, the Magistrate **must transfer** the case. Under the Uniform Magistrate Court Rules, the magistrate should halt proceedings and send the case to a court of competent jurisdiction (usually State Court) because the court lacks subject matter jurisdiction over the excessive counterclaim ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2020/title-15/chapter-10/article-1/section-15-10-2?current=1#:~:text=No%20division%20of%20running%20open,account)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2020/title-15/chapter-10/article-1/section-15-10-2?current=1#:~:text=Recovery%20on%20part%20of%20account,decided%20under%20former%20law)). The defendant might have to pay transfer costs (the receiving court’s filing fee) per O.C.G.A. § 15-10-42 if ordered[^5]. If the defendant doesn’t pay the transfer fee, the case could be dismissed or remain in Magistrate with the counterclaim not considered. Sometimes, practical approach: a judge will ask the defendant at the hearing, “Are you seeking over $15,000?” If yes, and they want to persist, the judge will stop – often defendants then opt to waive the excess to get it done in small claims. Waiving excess means if your damages are $20k, you give up $5k and sue for $15k – and you can’t later sue for that $5k difference.  
- **Counterclaim Examples:** Suppose a contractor sues a homeowner for $10,000 unpaid balance. The homeowner might counterclaim that the work was defective and cost $6,000 to fix, so they seek $6,000. The net effect could be that even if contractor proves the $10k bill, the homeowner might win on the $6k defect claim, resulting in a judgment of $4k for the contractor (or $0 if the defect fully negates the balance due). Another example: a tenant is sued for $3,000 back rent; the tenant counterclaims for $3,000 for property damage from a leaky roof the landlord ignored. The Magistrate will hear both and might decide the landlord did breach duties causing $1,500 damage, so deduct that from rent owed, etc. This flexibility encourages defendants to raise all related issues now.  
- **No counter-sue limit on frequency:** Georgia doesn’t have a limit like “only 5 counterclaims a year” or such – you can counterclaim whenever sued, as needed.  
- **Counter-suing third parties:** Magistrate Court generally handles claims between the plaintiff and defendant. If a defendant believes a third party is actually responsible (like you’re sued and you think someone else owes you that amount), the process to add them (impleader) is not typical in Magistrate though technically possible by adding them as a third-party defendant in your counterclaim if the court allows. But that gets complicated and may be denied. Usually, Magistrate will only deal with existing parties.  
- **Default on counterclaim:** If a plaintiff files a suit and the defendant counterclaims, and then if the plaintiff (the original) *doesn’t show up to court*, the plaintiff’s claim can be dismissed for want of prosecution, but what about the counterclaim? The defendant can still potentially get a default judgment on their counterclaim if they gave proper notice to the plaintiff to appear for it. So, yes, a plaintiff who abandons their case could end up having a judgment against them on the counterclaim – a reason to take counters seriously.  

### What You **Cannot** Sue for in Georgia Small Claims  
While Magistrate Courts cover a wide range, there are explicit **limitations**. You **cannot** use small claims for certain types of cases either because another court has exclusive jurisdiction or because the relief sought is not monetary or not within the court’s power. Here is a list of claims **not allowed or practical** in Georgia Magistrate Court, with specifics:

- **Eviction / Possession of Real Property:** You cannot file a standard small claims case to evict a tenant or recover possession of property (land). Evictions (called **dispossessory proceedings**) are handled in Magistrate Court but under a different process (O.C.G.A. § 44-7-50 et seq.) ([www.franklincountyga.gov](https://www.franklincountyga.gov/magistrate-court/pages/frequently-asked-questions#:~:text=contracts%2C%20personal%20property%20foreclosures%2C%20garnishments%2C,00%20does%20not%20apply)). They require a Dispossessory Affidavit, not a Statement of Claim, and have accelerated timelines. So if you’re a landlord wanting a tenant out, don’t file a “$0 claim for possession” in small claims – instead, file a dispossessory action (which the same Magistrate Court will hear, but it’s separate from the small claims calendar). Similarly, if you’re a homeowner wanting to eject a squatter or holdover occupant, you might need an ejectment in Superior Court or use the new unlawful squatting process if applicable ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/title-15/chapter-10/article-1/section-15-10-2/#:~:text=,33)). Magistrate small claims can **only** address the money side (like back rent), not actual possession. One minor exception: Magistrate Court can handle **foreclosure of personal property liens** (like liens on cars, etc.) and some niche possessory warrants (e.g., abandoned vehicles, as per code list) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2020/title-15/chapter-10/article-1/section-15-10-2?current=1#:~:text=5,exceeding%20ten%20days%20or%20both)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2020/title-15/chapter-10/article-1/section-15-10-2?current=1#:~:text=as%20provided%20by%20Article%2013,The%20Abandoned%20Motor%20Vehicle%20Act)), but not real estate title or possession disputes beyond landlord/tenant.  
- **Divorce, Child Support, Alimony, or Child Custody:** Absolutely not. These are family law matters reserved for Superior Court. Magistrate Court cannot grant a divorce or adjudicate child custody or support. You cannot sue your ex for “child support owed” in Magistrate – those orders must come from a Superior or Juvenile Court. (If you already have a support order, enforce it through the proper process, not small claims). Similarly, property division in a divorce is for Superior Court. In short, **no family law disputes** can be resolved in small claims. If you try (e.g. suing your ex-boyfriend for “breaking up with me” or for child support claimed as “money owed”), the case will be dismissed. The court may treat a narrow monetary agreement between ex-spouses as a contract (like “he agreed to pay me $5,000 in our separation agreement and didn’t” – but that agreement itself likely is part of a divorce decree enforceable in Superior Court, not separate). So, keep anything involving marriage or children out of Magistrate.  ([www.franklincountyga.gov](https://www.franklincountyga.gov/magistrate-court/pages/frequently-asked-questions#:~:text=Magistrate%20courts%20cannot%20try%20individuals,refrain%20from%20doing%20an%20act))Georgia Magistrate Courts expressly lack jurisdiction over divorce, alimony, child custody, and related equitable relief.  
- **Name Changes or Identity/Status Declarations:** You cannot file in small claims to change your name or establish paternity or anything that is not about a money judgment. Those are done via petitions in Superior (name change) or other appropriate courts.  
- **Defamation (Libel/Slander) for amounts over $15k:** Technically, defamation is a tort that could be pursued in State or Superior Court (with a one-year statute of limitations for slander and libel[^14]). Could you sue for defamation in Magistrate if you only want, say, $10,000 in damages? In theory, yes – nothing in the jurisdictional statute explicitly forbids defamation claims as long as damages sought are within $15k. However, defamation cases are tricky: you must prove statements, falsity, harm to reputation, etc. It’s rare to see them in small claims because damages are often speculative and require evidence of reputational harm which is hard to quantify in a quick hearing. Also, often defamation plaintiffs want injunctive relief (retraction or stop slandering) which small claims cannot grant – only money can be awarded. If you filed one, a Magistrate judge might dismiss it as inappropriate or require clear proof of actual financial loss from the slander (like “I lost a $5k contract because of his false statement”). Without actual economic loss, it’s mostly an emotional/reputation injury, which small claims won’t measure well. So practically, **defamation cases do not belong in small claims** unless it’s basically a small business claiming a competitor’s false statements cost $10k in lost sales – even then, proving that causation in a 15-minute hearing is nearly impossible. It’s best to take defamation to a higher court or not at all if under $15k. Many Magistrates would say it’s beyond their scope (and indeed it might implicitly fall under needing equity or complex damages). Additionally, a **2008 Georgia constitutional amendment** and subsequent law changes allowed *State* Court to have jurisdiction over certain non-monetary cases but left Magistrate as strictly monetary or specific property recovery. So safe to assume defamation is not handled.  
- **Malpractice (Professional Negligence):** Practically no. If you claim a doctor’s or lawyer’s malpractice caused you harm, those cases require expert affidavits under Georgia law (O.C.G.A. § 9-11-9.1). You cannot realistically do that in small claims. Magistrate Court would dismiss a professional malpractice claim lacking the required expert affidavit (which is jurisdictional in Georgia for any suit against a professional requiring expertise). Also damages usually exceed $15k. For minor cases like “my tax preparer made a $1,000 error and I had to pay fees,” you could frame it as breach of contract and sue for the $1,000 perhaps. But anything involving alleging failure to meet professional standard of care – that’s beyond Magistrate’s informal process. Particularly medical malpractice – not in small claims at all. If say a vet harmed your pet, legally that’s property damage, you can sue for vet bills (like we covered), but you wouldn’t call it “malpractice”; you’d call it negligence and claim the monetary loss. If a lawyer mishandled your case causing $10k loss – you’d have to sue in State Court with an affidavit; Magistrate can’t waive that requirement. So no true malpractice suits in Magistrate.  
- **Federal Claims Exclusive to Federal Courts:** Certain matters are exclusively federal jurisdiction: e.g. bankruptcy filings (only federal bankruptcy courts handle those), federal tax disputes, patent or copyright infringement (federal courts have exclusive jurisdiction on patents and copyrights). So you cannot use small claims to sue someone for patent infringement or to resolve a bankruptcy issue or ERISA benefits claim. If you attempt a copyright claim for under $15k (maybe someone used your photo without pay), technically copyright must be in federal court (after registration) – Magistrate would dismiss for lack of subject matter jurisdiction. Trademark infringement can be in state court (concurrent jurisdiction), but small claims might not be adequate for injunctive relief you usually seek. In summary, any case grounded in a federal statute that says “exclusive jurisdiction in federal court” is off-limits.  
- **Criminal Matters / Fines:** You cannot sue to prosecute someone or to impose criminal penalties. If a crime occurred, you go to police. Magistrate Court does issue criminal warrants in Georgia (Magistrates handle warrant applications) but that’s separate from civil small claims. Also, you can’t sue someone to pay a criminal fine or ticket – the state handles that.  
- **Class Actions or Multiple Plaintiffs beyond simple joinder:** Magistrate Court is not set up for class action lawsuits or large-scale litigation. If a bunch of people have the same claim, each generally files their own small claim (e.g. a dozen tenants each sue a landlord individually). There’s no provision for a true class action in Magistrate.  
- **Equitable Relief (Injunctions, Specific Performance):** Magistrate Courts **cannot issue injunctions** (orders to do or not do something) or decrees ordering someone to perform a contract (specific performance). They can only award money or *possibly* order the return of specific personal property (via a writ of possession for personal property in some cases). For instance, you can’t ask Magistrate Court to order your neighbor to remove a fence or stop a nuisance – you’d need an injunction from Superior Court. You can’t ask them to make a contractor finish the job – you can only ask for money to pay someone else to finish.  ([www.franklincountyga.gov](https://www.franklincountyga.gov/magistrate-court/pages/frequently-asked-questions#:~:text=Magistrate%20courts%20cannot%20try%20individuals,refrain%20from%20doing%20an%20act))This is a firm limitation. So any case whose primary goal is non-monetary (restraining order, enforcement of a non-compete agreement, forcing someone to issue a deed, etc.) is not allowed. One nuance: Magistrate can **foreclose certain liens** (like mechanics liens on personal property, see code list) which results in an order to sell property – that’s sort of equitable but directly given by statute. Generally, though, injunctive powers are absent.  
- **Suing the Government (Sovereign Immunity):** You usually cannot sue the **State of Georgia** or its agencies in Magistrate due to sovereign immunity (they can only be sued if they waived immunity under acts like the Georgia Tort Claims Act, which requires suing in State or Superior Court and following strict notice procedures). Same for federal government – if you have a claim against the U.S. (like a small refund owed), that typically must go through federal claims processes. Local governments (cities/counties) have some waiver for certain claims but require ante-litem notice (we covered 6-month notice to city, 12-month to county)[^16]. If you properly gave notice, you could sue a city or county in an appropriate court. Can you sue a city in Magistrate for, say, $2,000 vehicle damage due to a city vehicle hitting you? Possibly, since the amount is in range and it’s essentially a negligence claim – but because it’s a government, you had to send the ante-litem notice first. If you didn’t, the court will dismiss for failure to meet a condition precedent. Also, certain claims against cities (like those arising from discretionary functions or above certain amounts) might be transferred or dismissed due to immunity. Practically, many smaller claims against local governments do end up in Magistrate once notice is done – e.g., someone might sue a county for the cost of a tire blown out by a pothole after notifying the county. Magistrate might hear it, though counties often assert immunity. It can go either way: maintenance of roads is usually a government function with some immunity unless they had notice of the hazard. In short, **suing government entities is complicated and often not successful in small claims unless clearly allowed**. If you do, strictly follow notice statutes ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2020/title-36/chapter-33/section-36-33-5/#:~:text=1,courts%20against%20the%20municipal%20corporation)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2020/title-36/chapter-33/section-36-33-5/#:~:text=2,the%20governing%20authority%20shall%20consider)). If it’s the State, likely a no-go (needs to go to State Court via the Claims Advisory Board or such route). Federal agencies – definitely not in state small claims; they remove cases to federal court and likely dismiss on immunity (like suing USPS for property damage would be under FTCA in federal court).  
- **Workers’ Compensation:** If you’re injured on the job, you cannot sidestep the Workers’ Comp system by suing your employer in small claims for medical bills or lost wages. Workers’ comp is an exclusive remedy system handled by the State Board of Workers’ Compensation. So those claims cannot be brought in Magistrate (and employers pretty much have immunity from civil suit for workplace injuries, except very limited circumstances). If an employer didn’t have comp insurance illegally, you still usually pursue it through the Board or a Superior Court action under the comp statute – not a small claim.  
- **Hiring someone to do something (specific performance)**: For example, if you paid a contractor and they didn’t do the work, you can sue for money, but you **cannot** ask the court to force them to do the work. The court will only give money; it won’t order someone to complete a contract.  
- **Intangible disputes without monetary value:** e.g. you can’t sue someone because “they broke a promise and hurt my feelings” without a quantifiable financial loss. Emotional distress is not compensable in Magistrate (unless tied to a physical injury in a tort claim, and even then it’s part of the damages calculation not separate). So if there’s no dollar amount you can attach to a claim, it doesn’t belong in small claims.  

**Georgia-Specific Exclusions and Notes:**  
In addition to the above, Georgia Magistrate Courts are explicitly limited as follows ([www.franklincountyga.gov](https://www.franklincountyga.gov/magistrate-court/pages/frequently-asked-questions#:~:text=Magistrate%20courts%20cannot%20try%20individuals,refrain%20from%20doing%20an%20act)):  

- They do **not have equity jurisdiction**, meaning anything requiring fairness orders (injunctions, specific performance, reformation of a contract, quieting title) is out.  
- They do **not handle mental incompetency proceedings** (that’s probate court for guardianships, etc.).  
- They do **not handle cases involving title to real estate**. If your dispute is who owns a piece of land or an interest in land, you must go to Superior Court. (Magistrate can handle landlord-tenant because that’s mere possession, not title). They also can’t officially partition real property or foreclose real property mortgages (those in Superior Court). They can foreclose certain liens on personal property (like abandoned vehicle liens per code).  
- They cannot issue restraining orders or protective orders – those come from Superior (or in stalking cases, also State Court can issue civil stalking protective orders).  
- **Probate matters** (wills, estates) are exclusive to Probate Court or Superior in some instances – can’t sue in Magistrate to, say, remove an executor or get your inheritance (that goes through probate process).  
- **Tax disputes:** If you want to dispute a property tax assessment or state income tax bill, those have specific appeal routes (Board of Equalization for property tax, Georgia Tax Tribunal for state taxes, etc.). Magistrate is not the venue to fight a tax bill. 

To put it succinctly, Magistrate (small claims) deals with **money judgments for civil claims**. If your desired outcome is not “Defendant pays me $X,” then small claims is likely not the right place.  

## Damages – What You Can and Cannot Recover in Small Claims  
When suing in Georgia small claims court, you should understand the types of **damages** you are allowed to win. Magistrate Court can award **compensatory monetary damages** and certain statutory damages, but there are limitations on punitive damages and other categories:  

- **Compensatory Damages (Actual Damages):** Yes – this is the core of nearly every small claim. Compensatory damages are meant to reimburse you for your actual loss: money you are out-of-pocket, the cost to repair or replace property, the amount of an unpaid debt, etc. Georgia Magistrate judges will award proven **direct losses** readily. For example, the cost of repairs, medical bills, lost wages from an incident, the amount someone unjustly enriched themselves at your expense – these are all compensable. You should bring evidence to justify the dollar figure (receipts, estimates, bills, contracts). The court usually awards either the exact amount proven or a reasonable estimate if evidence is conflicting but a loss is clear. Note: If there’s **future damages** (like ongoing treatment costs or installments not yet due on a contract), Magistrate might only award what has accrued up to the hearing. They typically don’t do speculative future losses.  

- **Punitive/Exemplary Damages:** Typically **not available** in Georgia Magistrate Court. Punitive damages (to punish wrongdoing and deter others) can only be awarded in certain tort cases in higher courts and require specific findings (like willful misconduct, fraud, malice). Magistrate Courts have no jurisdiction to award **punitive damages exceeding the $15,000 cap**, and even within cap it’s generally outside their scope. The code giving Magistrate jurisdiction doesn’t mention punitive damages, and punitive by nature often would exceed the cap if warranted. In practice, Magistrate judges **do not** award separate punitive damages. The one exception might be if a statute effectively includes a punitive element (like treble damages under FBPA or double under bad check law – those are technically punitive in effect but statutorily authorized and within the cap). But classic punitive for, say, a deliberate trespass – you won’t get that in small claims. If you ask for some extra “for aggravation,” the judge will likely say no, stick to your measurable losses. So do **not expect any award just to make an example of the defendant**. If the conduct was egregious, your route is to sue in State/Superior Court for punitive (and remember GA has caps on punitive in most cases at $250k except specific intent harm).  

- **Statutory Multipliers or Penalties:** *Sometimes, if statute allows and within cap.* We have discussed a few: double damages for bad checks (which is actually compensatory up to $500 cap), treble for consumer protection, triple for security deposit bad faith. These are **allowed because Georgia statutes explicitly provide them**. If you prove the conditions, Magistrate can award them as part of compensatory relief (the statute defines it). For example, if you win an FBPA claim and prove it was intentional, the law says treble damages *shall* be awarded[^10]. The Magistrate judge, if aware, should apply that (and it would still be ≤$15k as long as your base damages ×3 ≤ $15k, otherwise you’d limit to cap). Another penalty: if a landlord fails deposit rules, statute says 3× deposit + fees[^13]. That can be granted by Magistrate because it’s within their monetary jurisdiction and specifically authorized. They can also award court costs (discussed below) as provided by statute. In contrast, if some law says a civil penalty of $50,000 (like certain federal penalties), that’s beyond them. Keep multipliers limited to those designed for small consumer cases.  

- **Attorney’s Fees and Court Costs:** Generally, in small claims, **each party bears their own attorney’s fees** (and most people don’t have attorneys there anyway). Magistrate Court does **not** routinely award attorney fees to the winning side, except if a specific law allows it or in very rare cases of bad faith. Georgia law O.C.G.A. § 13-6-11 allows prevailing plaintiffs in contract/tort cases to get attorney fees if the defendant acted in bad faith or was stubbornly litigious, etc., but that typically requires a jury or judge finding – Magistrate judges might be hesitant to add attorney fees unless a clear showing of bad faith (even then, how to quantify if you mostly self-represented?). Most likely, **no attorney fees** will be awarded in Magistrate, especially if neither side is represented or if you didn’t actually incur fees. If you did hire a lawyer (rare, since they can’t formally appear in hearing without permission, though they can help prep), you could ask for fees under §13-6-11, but don’t count on it. One scenario: if a statute like FBPA or security deposit law mandates attorney fees for prevailing consumer/tenant[^10][^13], the court could award a reasonable sum – but if you had no attorney, there’s none to award (you can’t get paid for your own time as a pro se). So practically, attorney fee awards are not a feature of small claims except statutory triggers that mainly benefit those who had counsel. **Court costs**, however, are routinely awarded to the prevailing party. Court costs include your filing fee and sometimes service fee. Georgia law (O.C.G.A. § 15-10-82) often allows the winning party to recover **costs of the action**. In Magistrate Court, if you win, you should request court costs in your judgment. Usually the judge will add like “+$___ costs” in the judgment. That covers what you paid to file and serve. It’s not automatic but standard. For example, you sued for $1,000 and paid $60 filing + $35 service = $95 costs. If you win, you get $1,000 + $95. If you settled before judgment, no automatic cost shift. And if each side wins something (like you both had claims), the court might offset or let each cover their own.  

- **Interest (Pre-judgment and Post-judgment):** In Georgia, **pre-judgment interest** on an unliquidated claim (one not fixed by contract or certain) generally is not awarded except from the date of judgment (because until the amount is determined, it’s not owed yet). On liquidated claims (like a known debt amount due on a date), you can claim interest from the due date if provided by law or contract. For instance, if a promissory note had 5% annual interest specified, you can calculate interest up to the hearing and include that in your claim (ensuring total still ≤ $15k). Or if no contract interest but it was a liquidated sum due on a date, Georgia’s statutory post-judgment interest rate (which is prime rate + 3% per annum as set by O.C.G.A. § 7-4-12) could arguably apply from default date as pre-judgment interest – but Magistrates often keep it simple and just award principal. You can ask for pre-judgment interest explicitly if you have a basis (say, “I’m owed $5,000 since Jan 1 last year, I ask for interest at Georgia’s legal rate of 7.5% for one year, about $375”). The judge may or may not add it. If it’s small and clearly due, they might. If not mentioned, once judgment enters, **post-judgment interest** accrues **automatically** on civil judgments in Georgia at the statutory rate (which changes annually; e.g. around 7.25% for 2022, 8.25% for 2023 depending on prime). You don’t need to ask for it; it’s by law. The judgment form might not list it, but it’s understood that interest accrues from the date of judgment until paid. So if the defendant drags out payment, they owe interest. As a practical matter, for small amounts, interest is often trivial, but for larger near-cap judgments, it can be meaningful if unpaid for long. If you want to be precise, you can calculate and state an interest amount up to trial date. But know that interest cannot push the total beyond $15k in jurisdiction – however, post-judgment interest accruing after judgment is not limited by the cap; the cap is only for the judgment principal. For pre-judgment interest, to avoid any argument, keep principal+interest claimed ≤$15k.  

- **Pain and Suffering:** Typically **not separately awarded** in small claims. If you have a personal injury, pain and suffering is an element of damages you could theoretically claim (no fixed formula). But Magistrate judges are hesitant to award intangible amounts. They tend to stick to medical bills and lost wages that you proved. It’s not that they can’t give anything for pain – it’s within power to award general damages in a tort – but with no jury and an informal process, any request for, say, “$5,000 for pain” will be met with scrutiny. If your case is modest (a sprained ankle example), you might ask for a small sum beyond bills to cover inconvenience. The judge might give something nominal or might say “No, we only grant actual expenses.” It’s case-by-case. For very minor injuries, don’t expect a windfall. For somewhat more serious but still under cap, you might convince the judge to round up the award a bit to account for suffering. But since these are not economic damages, it’s discretionary. Also, if awarding would take total over $15k, they legally cannot. So you might actually limit your ask to keep within jurisdiction. E.g., if you had $14k medical and clearly lots of pain, you’re capped at $15k anyway, so you’d likely just get $15k.  

- **Property Value vs. Repair Cost:** In property damage cases, you **cannot recover more than the property’s value**. For instance, if your 10-year-old car is worth $5,000 and it was totaled, you can’t get $8,000 just because repairs would have cost that – you’re limited to the $5k market value (plus perhaps rental car or towing). Magistrates will follow that principle of making you whole, not giving you a brand-new item. Similarly, if a used item was destroyed, you get its used value, not original price. If repairs are possible, you usually get the lesser of repair cost or value. In some car cases, diminished value after repair can be added until it equals pre-accident value. Just be prepared to justify any measure you claim.  

- **Recoverable Costs (Filing, Service):** As noted, these are usually awarded to the winner. Also, if you had to pay a **private process server** because the sheriff couldn’t serve, you can ask for that cost as part of court costs (with a receipt). Court costs are small but it’s your right as prevailing party.  

- **Collection Costs and Post-judgment Fees:** If your contract said the debtor will pay collection costs or a percentage for attorney fees, enforcing that in Magistrate might require proving those costs. For example, some promissory notes say “if in default, debtor owes 15% attorney fees after 10-day notice.” Even if no actual attorney used (or one was used to file the case), you might attempt to claim that contractual attorney fee. Legally, under O.C.G.A. § 13-1-11, to enforce an attorney fee provision, you must send a specific notice and the debtor can avoid fees by paying within 10 days. If you did that, Magistrate can award the contractual 15% as part of judgment. But many pro se folks skip that or find it complicated, so they just sue for principal and interest and drop attorney fee clause.  

In summary, Georgia small claims will give you money to cover your proven loss and statutory additions, but **will not** give you non-monetary relief or speculative/punitive extras except where explicitly allowed. Always tailor your claim to a dollar amount you can justify. If something is important to you but not measurable in money (e.g. apology, vindication, making them stop doing something), small claims isn’t the forum – it only deals in dollars. 

## 3. Where to File  
**Georgia’s small claims courts are the Magistrate Courts of each county**. Every county in Georgia (159 counties) has a Magistrate Court, which is where you file your small claims case. Unlike some states that have separate “Small Claims Court” signs on the door, in Georgia you will be dealing with the Magistrate Court Clerk’s Office. Sometimes they call it the “Civil Division” of Magistrate. For example, Fulton County’s Magistrate Court handles small claims (up to $15k) and dispossessories, etc., all under the Magistrate umbrella ([magistratefulton.org](https://magistratefulton.org/175/Small-Claims#:~:text=Jurisdiction)). 

**Court Name:** You should file in the Magistrate Court of the county that has proper **venue** (the right location for the case). 

- **Venue Rules:** Generally in Georgia, you must file in the county where the **defendant** resides (if the defendant is a person) or has its principal office (if a business)[^5]. This is a constitutional venue requirement for civil cases: a defendant is entitled to be sued in their home county, with some exceptions. For small claims: 
  - For an **individual** defendant, file in their county of residence. If they recently moved, it’s wherever they legally reside at filing time. 
  - For a **business** defendant:
    - If it’s a **sole proprietorship** or an unincorporated business (like a d/b/a), you sue the owner in their county of residence (because the owner is the legal entity).
    - If it’s a **partnership**, you can sue in any county where at least one partner resides[^5].
    - If it’s a **corporation** or **LLC**, you file in the county where its **registered agent** is located or where the corporation’s office is. Every corporation/LLC registered in Georgia has a registered office and agent on file with the Georgia Secretary of State. For example, if you’re suing a corporation, you look up its registered agent’s address (often in the county of its main office) and file there. If the company is out-of-state with no Georgia registration, and jurisdiction is appropriate, you might file where they do business or where the contract was made – but that can get tricky; better to consult a lawyer on unregistered foreign companies. (For Magistrate, practically, they often only handle in-state defendants that they can serve).
    - If suing a **county** or **city** government (we caution again about sovereign immunity, but say it’s for a allowed claim like a refund of a fee), venue would lie in that county (for county) or where the city is located (for city).
  - **Multiple defendants:** If you have more than one defendant, venue is okay in a county if at least one of the defendants resides there and the others reside in Georgia. (If one defendant is in Fulton and one in Cobb, you could file in Fulton if the Fulton defendant is centrally involved). Georgia law allows suit in a county if one “joint obligor” defendant resides there and others are elsewhere in GA, per joint action venue rules. But if one defendant is out-of-state entirely, it introduces complications (potentially removal to federal court or personal jurisdiction issues).
  - **Special contracts:** Some contracts have a clause specifying venue or consent to jurisdiction in a certain county – but those are usually for higher courts. Magistrate likely still requires defendant’s residence or where contract was made/ performed in some cases. Generally stick to defendant’s residence for simplicity.
  
Georgia Magistrate Courts also adhere to the rule that **venue must be correct** or the case can be transferred or dismissed. If you file in the wrong county, the defendant can motion to transfer for improper venue. The Magistrate judge will likely transfer it to the correct county (and may require you to pay a transfer fee within 20 days as noted by Liberty County Magistrate FAQ ([ww2.libertycountyga.com](https://ww2.libertycountyga.com/civilefiling/public/ClaimFAQ.aspx#:~:text=6,is%20a%20fee%20for%20the)) ([ww2.libertycountyga.com](https://ww2.libertycountyga.com/civilefiling/public/ClaimFAQ.aspx#:~:text=transfer%20fee%20within%20twenty%20,a%20dismissal%20of%20the%20case))). If you don’t pay that fee, your case might be dismissed. So it’s important to file in the right place initially to avoid delay and extra cost. In sum, **file where the defendant lives** (or does business). 

**Example:** You live in Cobb County, the person who owes you money lives in DeKalb County – you must file in DeKalb Magistrate Court. It doesn’t matter where you live; plaintiff’s residence isn’t usually relevant to venue (except some minor exceptions not usually for Magistrate). The key is defendant’s location. 

There is one notable exception in landlord-tenant: **Dispossessory (eviction) actions must be filed in the county where the property is located**, which typically coincides with where the tenant lives. For small claims about rent after move-out, you still use defendant’s current residence. 

Also, if the dispute arose out of a **contract specifying a county for suit**, Georgia might honor that if it’s a proper forum selection clause. For example, many big companies have “any suit shall be filed in X County.” If X County is where they are or if clause is enforceable, you could file there, but in Magistrate context such clauses are less common and sometimes unenforceable against consumers. Best to ignore that nuance unless clearly binding.

**Online Filing (E-Filing):** Many Georgia Magistrate Courts now have **electronic filing** available for small claims, but it varies by county:
  - **Statewide system?** Georgia does not have a unified statewide e-filing portal for Magistrate courts as a whole. Some counties have adopted e-filing through private vendors (like PeachCourt or eFileGA (Tyler)) for Magistrate cases, some have their own system, others still require paper filing. 
  - For instance, **Fulton County** explicitly allows electronic filing for Magistrate small claims ([magistratefulton.org](https://magistratefulton.org/175/Small-Claims#:~:text=Filing%20Fee)). They direct users to eFileGA (Tyler’s system) to submit online. Similarly, **DeKalb Magistrate** has information on e-filing on their website and uses a system. **Gwinnett County** Magistrate has an online filing link as well. Smaller counties may not have the volume or resources, so you may need to file in person or by mail.
  - To find out, check the Magistrate Court’s website or call the clerk. The **Georgia Courts** website might not list each, but often a quick search "X County Magistrate efile" yields info. If e-filing is provided, you’ll need to create an account, upload PDFs of the Statement of Claim form, and pay filing fees online. 
  - If online filing is available, it’s convenient, but ensure you follow up on service of process (some e-filing lets you pay for sheriff service online too).
  - If not, you must **file in person or by mail** at the Magistrate Clerk’s office for that county. Many Magistrate clerks are located in the county courthouse or judicial building. Some have multiple locations (like Fulton has a physical filing location downtown).
  - Keep in mind even with e-filing, *some counties might still require you to show up to get a hearing date or ensure service.* But usually, once e-filed, the clerk will issue a hearing date and arrange service.

**Consequences of Wrong Venue:** If you file in the wrong county:
  - The defendant can file a **motion to transfer or dismiss**. Under Uniform Magistrate Court Rule 36, if a Magistrate Court lacks jurisdiction or venue, it should transfer the case to a court that has jurisdiction (which could be Magistrate of another county or State/Superior if needed) ([law-journals-books.vlex.com](https://law-journals-books.vlex.com/vid/ga-benchbook-ch-1-1035026980#:~:text=1,court%20would%20have%20jurisdiction%20to)). The Liberty County Magistrate FAQ confirms they’ll transfer improper venue cases, and no additional fee for transfer to another Magistrate, but a fee if transfer to State/Superior ([ww2.libertycountyga.com](https://ww2.libertycountyga.com/civilefiling/public/ClaimFAQ.aspx#:~:text=6,is%20a%20fee%20for%20the)). 
  - If somehow it’s completely wrong (like defendant out-of-state with no GA contacts, the case could be dismissed for lack of personal jurisdiction).
  - If the defendant doesn’t object and appears, sometimes the issue is waived in practice – but they could later contest enforceability of a judgment if venue was improper, so better not risk it. 
  - Usually, Magistrate judges on their own won’t check venue unless obvious (like you list defendant lives in another county). But if noticed, they might flag it and transfer.
  - It’s not like your case is automatically lost; it will just be moved, delaying resolution. But if the statute of limitations was close and you filed in wrong county, you might face a dismissal and need to refile, possibly missing the deadline. So always pick correct venue to preserve your claim.

**To recap venue**: **File in defendant’s county.** For businesses, that means their registered agent’s county for corporations, or an owner’s residence for sole proprietors. If uncertain, you can search the GA Secretary of State’s business registry (for corp/LLC) to find their county of reg. agent ([magistratefulton.org](https://magistratefulton.org/175/Small-Claims#:~:text=Venue)). For an individual, use their home address on bills or as known. If you truly don’t know where the person lives (e.g. a transient), venue is tricky – you might have to file where the incident happened and hope no objection, or hire an investigator. There is no state-run service by publication in Magistrate for small claims (some allow if defendant cannot be found and case is about property, but not for money judgments typically – personal service or substitute service is needed).

**Subject Matter Jurisdiction:** As covered in Section 1, Magistrate can hear civil claims up to $15k, and certain special matters like garnishments, foreclosures of liens on personal property, distress warrants (landlord pre-eviction remedy) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2020/title-15/chapter-10/article-1/section-15-10-2?current=1#:~:text=5,exceeding%20ten%20days%20or%20both)). So ensure your case fits. If you accidentally file something outside that (e.g. replevin of land, or a $20k claim), the clerk might reject it or the judge will handle accordingly. Most often, you as the plaintiff ensure it’s ≤$15k and one of the allowed claim types. The clerk’s office can often advise if your form looks off (though they can’t give legal advice, they might say “We can’t process a divorce here, you need Superior Court”).

**Military or Out-of-State Defendants:** If the defendant is active duty military stationed in GA, venue is still where they reside (their domicile might be elsewhere, but if they’re stationed in a base in GA and live off-base, arguably that county). If they live on a base or deployed, you might have to handle Service Members Civil Relief Act issues if they don’t appear. If the defendant moved out of state entirely, Georgia courts might lack personal jurisdiction unless the claim arises from something in GA (like they lived in GA at time of contract or accident in GA). If you manage to serve them out of state (via long-arm statute conditions), venue might be considered the county where the claim arose or your county with using GA long-arm to serve out-of-state. But that’s complicated for Magistrate; practically, Magistrate suits are usually defendant is in GA. If not, consider other courts or sue in their state.

**Summary:** *File your small claim in the Magistrate Court for the county that is right for the defendant. Double-check that location before you prepare your forms.* Magistrate Court addresses and phone numbers are listed on each county’s website or the Georgia Courts site. Many provide downloadable Statement of Claim forms as well. 

## 4. Filing Fees  
Georgia Magistrate Courts charge a **filing fee** to start a small claims case, which varies slightly by county. The fee is generally in the range of **$50 to $60 for one defendant**, plus an additional fee for serving each defendant. The exact schedule often looks like:
- Base filing fee (covers filing and the first defendant’s service by sheriff or marshal).
- Fee for each additional defendant.
- Fee for service if by marshal or sheriff, often separate or included depending on county.

For example:
- **Fulton County:** Filing fee is $60 for one defendant, plus $8 for each additional defendant (and in Fulton, service by the Marshal is $50 per party) ([magistratefulton.org](https://magistratefulton.org/175/Small-Claims#:~:text=Filing%20Fee)). So in Fulton, suing one person with marshal service costs $60 + $50 = $110. If two defendants, $60 + $8 add’l + $50×2 service = $168.
- **DeKalb County:** Filing fee is $54, service fee $35 per defendant by sheriff ([dekalbcountymagistratecourt.com](https://dekalbcountymagistratecourt.com/civil-matters/small-claims/#:~:text=The%20filing%20fee%20is%20%2454,checks%2C%20debit%20or%20credit%20cards)). So one defendant = $54 + $35 = $89. Two defendants = $54 + $35×2 = $124.
- **Gwinnett County:** (From general info) They often align with around $56 filing + $50 service or similar, but let’s see if we have data: I recall around $58.
- Typically, **smaller counties** have a fee around $50-55 and sheriff service about $25-35. 
  - Eg. Liberty County says “If claim exceeds $15k must file elsewhere” but likely their fee is around $50 (not in snippet).
  - Often, counties in Georgia set Magistrate filing fees via local court fee schedules, which include state surcharges (like a $5 Judicial Operations fund fee, etc.). That’s why it slightly differs.
  
A helpful resource is the **Magistrate Court clerk** or county website which usually lists the filing fees. Some counties have them posted as PDF or on the FAQ:
- Ex: **DeKalb Magistrate** [listed above].
- **Cobb County** (if we looked, maybe around $56 and $50 service).
- **Chatham County**: likely similar (maybe $60 and $10 + $25 service).
  
Because these are local, it’s wise to check the specific county’s info:
- If not easily found, you can call the Magistrate Court Clerk and ask, “What’s the cost to file a small claim and have it served on X defendants?”
  
**Service of Process Fees:** In Georgia, you typically have the county **Sheriff’s Department** or a court **Marshal** serve the summons/claim. The service fee is separate from filing:
  - Many counties require you to pay the service fee up front with filing. Some include one service in the filing fee, but usually they list it separately.
  - *Sheriff vs. Marshal:* Only a few counties (like Fulton, maybe DeKalb) have Marshals for civil process. Most use the Sheriff’s civil division. The fees are set by county or statute (O.C.G.A. § 15-16-21 maybe sets some Sheriff fees).
  - $35 is a common sheriff service fee (like DeKalb).
  - $50 in Fulton (because they use Marshals who charge that).
  - $25 in some smaller jurisdictions historically (though many increased to around $35 in recent years).
  - Plus if you need service out of county, you might pay that county’s fee via second original (discussed below in Service section).
  
**Multiple Defendants:** Most counties charge an **incremental fee per additional defendant** served. E.g. $8 in Fulton (small admin fee plus separate service fee per additional). Others just require you to pay another service fee for each. Always clarify: If two defendants at same address, do you pay twice? Yes, because they need to serve each individually.

**Fee Waiver / In Forma Pauperis:** If you cannot afford the filing fee, Georgia law allows you to file **In Forma Pauperis** (IFP), meaning as an indigent person. Each Magistrate Court has a procedure for this. Typically, you must fill out an **Affidavit of Poverty** (a sworn statement of your income, assets, and inability to pay). The court might have a form for this. If the court finds you truly cannot pay, they may waive the fees and allow you to file without cost. O.C.G.A. § 9-15-2 generally governs proceeding in forma pauperis in Georgia courts. Magistrates often just require the affidavit; some might have a judge review it briefly or ask for evidence of benefits (like if you receive food stamps or SSI, that usually suffices to prove indigence). Once approved, your case is filed and the sheriff will still serve process without cost to you (the county covers it). **Important:** If you win and recover money, sometimes the court may require that out of the proceeds, the waived fees be paid back (especially in higher courts, they have a lien for costs). But in Magistrate, that isn’t typically enforced unless maybe a large amount was recovered. If you lose, you owe nothing for fees per IFP. If you falsely claim poverty, you could face dismissal or perjury charges, so only use this if truly needed.

**Are fees recoverable?** Yes, as mentioned, if you win, you can ask for your filing and service fees as part of judgment (so effectively the defendant reimburses them). If you filed IFP, there were no upfront fees to reimburse, but the court might then not tack any fee on defendant either (though it could add normal costs that would have been due – tricky situation, usually if IFP, the county eats the cost unless they decide to tax it to defendant upon judgment). But normally, paid fees get included in judgment on request.

**Payment methods:** In-person, clerks accept cash, money order, sometimes credit/debit (plus a surcharge for card). By mail, you use money order or cashier’s check typically to “Magistrate Court of X County.” For e-filing, you pay by credit card online. Ensure funds are ready because they won’t file until fee is processed (except if IFP approved).

**Hearing scheduling and fees:** Once you pay and file, the court will assign a hearing date. There’s no separate hearing fee. If you need a **continuance**, some courts might charge a small fee to reschedule (some charge $10 or so for a continuance motion – it varies). Check local rules on that.

**If case is transferred to State/Superior:** and you already paid Magistrate, often the transfer order will require you to pay the difference up to the higher court’s filing fee. E.g., State Court filing might be ~$200; if you paid $58 in Magistrate, you might have to pay $142 more to State Court within 20 days or the case can be dismissed in State Court for nonpayment. Liberty Co. FAQ indicated no extra fee if transferring to another Magistrate, but a fee (i.e. the receiving court’s fee) for transfer to State/Superior ([ww2.libertycountyga.com](https://ww2.libertycountyga.com/civilefiling/public/ClaimFAQ.aspx#:~:text=6,is%20a%20fee%20for%20the)). So if your case gets bumped up due to counterclaim or something, be prepared to pay that or negotiate withdrawal of counterclaim to stay in Magistrate.

**Other Fees:** 
- **Jury trial demand:** not applicable in Magistrate (Magistrate trials are bench trials only—no jury). If a party wants a jury, they have to appeal (see appeals section).
- **Appeal fee:** If you appeal the Magistrate judgment to State or Superior (called “de novo appeal”), you’ll have to pay that court’s filing fee and possibly a supersedeas bond. That’s not a Magistrate fee but relevant if you lose/win and someone appeals.
- **Writ of Fi.Fa (Fieri Facias) issuance:** After you get a judgment, if you want to record a lien, the Magistrate clerk can issue a FiFa (basically an execution on judgment) for a small fee (like $9 or $25, depends on county) that you then file in Superior Court deed records. That is post-judgment collection cost. If you ultimately collect, you can add those costs to what you collect from defendant possibly. 
- **Garnishment filing fee:** If you have to file a garnishment to collect your judgment (like to garnish wages or bank account), that’s a new case with separate fee (varies but often ~$105 including service). That’s a separate Magistrate process after judgment.  

**Fee Recap:** 
Costs to start are modest ($50-100). Georgia’s fees are lower than many states. There’s no sliding scale by claim amount like in some states (CA charges more for >$5k claims; GA doesn’t, as long as ≤$15k it’s the same base fee). Only number of defendants and service choices influence it.

Always verify current fees because counties may update them due to state surcharges or local add-ons (law library fee, ADR fund fee – though ADR not in Magistrate usually, etc.).

## 5. Statute of Limitations  
A **statute of limitations** is a law setting the deadline by which a lawsuit must be filed. If you file too late, the defendant can raise the statute of limitations as a defense and get the case dismissed. In Georgia, the statute of limitations depends on the type of claim (the cause of action). The clock usually starts when the claim “accrues” – typically the date of the breach, injury, or discovery of the issue in some cases. Here are Georgia’s main limitation periods for the kinds of claims commonly in small claims, summarized:

- **Written Contracts:** **6 years**. (O.C.G.A. § 9-3-24) Actions based on a simple written contract must be filed within 6 years from the date the contract was broken (breach) or the payment became due[^6]. For example, if someone signed a promissory note or service agreement and breached it on Jan 1, 2020, you have until Jan 1, 2026 to sue. This covers most loans, leases, or service contracts in writing that are not under seal. *Note:* If the contract is for the sale of goods, the Uniform Commercial Code (UCC) provides a 4-year limit (see below for sale of goods).
- **Oral Contracts / Implied Contracts / Open Accounts:** **4 years**. (O.C.G.A. § 9-3-25) For agreements not in writing and signed by the party charged, Georgia allows 4 years from breach[^7]. This includes handshake deals, unwritten promises to pay, and open account debts (like ongoing credit accounts) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2020/title-9/chapter-3/article-2/section-9-3-25/#:~:text=All%20actions%20upon%20open%20account%2C,Article%202%20of%20Title%2011)). Also, if part written part oral, often treated as oral for limitations. E.g., you loaned money with no signed note – 4 years from when repayment was due. Open accounts (like revolving credit or running tab) are 4 years from the last time they gave credit or from demand for payment if contract implied that. (If it’s an open account for sale of goods, UCC might still be 4 years, effectively the same, but more on UCC below).
- **Contracts “Under Seal”:** **20 years**. (O.C.G.A. § 9-3-23) If a contract is executed under seal (and it recites that it’s under seal in the text, and typically has a seal by the signature)[^8], the limitation is 20 years. This is uncommon in small claims because it’s mostly for formal documents like certain secured loans, deeds, or a contract where they intentionally put “(Seal)” near signature. But keep in mind if you have, say, a sealed apartment lease (some Georgia leases are signed under seal), then rather than 6 years, the landlord might technically have 20 years to sue for breach. Most everyday contracts are not under seal unless explicitly stated. If you suspect a contract is under seal, look for words “sealed and delivered” or the letters “L.S.” or “(Seal)”. The presence of a corporate seal or notary doesn’t automatically mean under seal; it must say it.
- **Sale of Goods (UCC contracts):** **4 years**. (O.C.G.A. § 11-2-725) The sale of goods (movable items) falls under Article 2 of the UCC, which Georgia has adopted. O.C.G.A. § 11-2-725 sets 4 years from the breach of a sales contract[^9]. For example, you bought equipment and the seller breached the warranty or failed to deliver – you have 4 years. This UCC provision overrides the general 6-year for written contracts specifically for goods. So if you have a written contract to buy a car, it’s 4 years (since a car is goods).
- **Injury to Person (Personal Injury):** **2 years**. (O.C.G.A. § 9-3-33) If you were physically injured or had a personal tort like a car accident injury or a slip-and-fall injury, you have 2 years from the date of injury to file a lawsuit for personal injury[^14]. This also covers injuries like medical malpractice (but those not in small claims), product liability injuries, etc. For example, a minor car wreck on March 1, 2024 – you must sue by March 1, 2026. Georgia does have a discovery rule in limited cases (e.g. malpractice or latent illness from exposure, etc.), but generally for clear injuries, it’s 2 years from incident. Also included in this code section: **injury to reputation (defamation)** is 1 year[^14], and **loss of consortium** (spouse’s claim for injury to partner) is 4 years, but that’s rarely in small claims. So remember: bodily injury = 2 years.
- **Damage to Property:** **4 years**. (O.C.G.A. § 9-3-31) This statute states actions for trespass or damage to real or personal property have a 4-year limit. Example: someone dented your car or cut your trees – 4 years from the damage date to sue for property damage. Many property damage claims (like car accidents property damage) effectively you have 4 years to sue for the car repair, but only 2 for any personal injury from that accident (they often file together within 2 to cover both). So for small claims focusing on property damage, you get up to 4 years. Franklin County’s info also noted property damage is 4 years ([www.franklincountyga.gov](https://www.franklincountyga.gov/magistrate-court/pages/frequently-asked-questions#:~:text=match%20at%20L63%20Magistrate%20Court,Felony%20cases%20differ%20from%204)). 
- **Fraud:** **4 years**. (O.C.G.A. § 9-3-31 also covers injuries to personalty and likely torts not otherwise specified like fraud). Georgia doesn’t have a separate code section for fraud, so the courts apply the 4-year limit for fraud claims (from when the fraud was or should have been discovered, possibly – Georgia does apply a discovery rule to fraud because by nature fraud is concealed). If you were defrauded, you likely have 4 years from discovery. For example, someone sold you a fake painting on Jan 2020 but you discovered it in Jan 2022 – you’d argue you have until Jan 2026 (4 years from discovery) to sue for fraud. Georgia case law does allow that tolling until discovery in actual fraud cases, but if it’s a simple misrepresentation you could have known, they might not toll. 
- **Conversion (theft of property) or Civil Theft:** also treated as property damage – 4 years would apply. 
- **Debt Collection (on account or judgment):** 
   - An ordinary unsecured debt (credit card, personal loan not under seal) is contract – 6 years if written, 4 if oral or open account depending on documentation. Many credit card agreements are considered written contracts but actually often signed when opened so likely 6, but some argue credit cards are open accounts (courts have debated; in practice, credit card suits often cited 6 if signed application, or 4 if just implied by use).
   - **Judgments:** If you already have a judgment (not applicable to initial small claim but to enforcement later), Georgia judgments are valid for 7 years and can be renewed.
- **Judgments from other states:** To domesticate in GA, you have time equal to that state’s judgment life or 5 years from judgment to domesticate under Uniform Enforcement of Foreign Judgments. But that’s beyond small claims context usually.

- **Special Cases:** 
   - **Medical malpractice:** 2 years (with specific rules, but anyway not in small claims).
   - **Wrongful death:** 2 years typically, but again not small claims domain.
   - **State tax collection**: not relevant here; also government often exempt from limitations for themselves (but they apply to them sometimes differently).
   - **Security deposit claims by tenant:** treated as written or oral contract depending on lease, likely 6 if written lease, but arguably a statutory penalty claim might have shorter limit (some states have 1 year for deposit suits; Georgia’s deposit law doesn’t specify a statute, so default contract terms – if lease under seal possibly 20, but practically treat it as contract).
   - **Wage claims (unpaid wages)**: If based on oral promise to pay wages (no written contract), 4 years. If a written employment contract about pay, 6 years. If under federal law (FLSA) – that’s 2 years for non-willful, 3 for willful. But if you’re suing in state court for wages as breach of contract, Georgia’s 4 or 6 might apply. Many do the safe approach and adhere to FLSA 2-year if it’s overtime/min wage, but for a straight final paycheck with no FLSA issue, contract limit applies.
   - **Consumer protection (FBPA):** The Fair Business Practices Act has a statute of limitations of **2 years** after the person knew or should have known of the violation (O.C.G.A. § 10-1-401). So if you bring a claim under FBPA, ensure it’s within 2 years of the deceptive act or discovery. E.g. you bought a car with odometer rollback discovered 1 year later; you have 2 years from discovery. 
   - **Bad check statute**: No special SOL beyond treating it as written instrument? Usually you use the contract or statutory penalty context, likely 4 years from dishonor (though it’s arguably conversion – but it’s simply to recover money, so contract 4 or written if check is considered a signed instrument – one could argue 6 since it’s a negotiable instrument which is written and signed – likely 6 years).
   - **Lemon Law**: There’s a hard deadline within which you must file for arbitration after the lemon law rights period, but since lemon law claims aren’t in small claims, skip detail.

**Tolling and Exceptions:** Sometimes statutes are tolled (paused):
  - If the defendant is a minor or legally incompetent, the period may not run until they are of age or competent (but for contract claims, incompetency might not toll – minors just can’t be sued until maybe majority for some matters).
  - If the defendant is out of state (absent from GA) continuously, that period of absence might toll the statute (O.C.G.A. § 9-3-94). E.g. if they left GA right after breach and you couldn’t serve them because they were gone for a year, that year might not count. But careful: if you can still serve them by long-arm, some say absence doesn’t toll as much nowadays. It’s arguable.
  - Fraud can toll until discovery as mentioned (O.C.G.A. § 9-3-96: if defendant concealed the cause of action by fraud, limitation tolled until discovered).
  - Partial payments or written acknowledgments of debt can restart the clock for debt cases (O.C.G.A. § 9-3-110). If a debtor makes a payment or writes a signed acknowledgment of owing the debt, the statute resets from that date. So an old debt from 5 years ago, if they paid $50 two years ago, the 6-year starts from that payment.
  - If someone dies, sometimes the clock tolls for a period until an estate is opened. Or if there’s a pending bankruptcy by defendant, limitations might be tolled due to automatic stay (though filing suit during stay is barred).
  - **Judgment to Magistrate**: not applicable, but if appealing a magistrate dismissal and refiling etc., that’s separate.

**When does clock start?**  
- Contracts: from date of breach or when payment was due and not paid.
- Injury: from date of injury (or date of death for wrongful death).
- Damage: from date damage incurred.
- Fraud: from date discovered or reasonably should have.
- Open account: often from date of last item or when demand made if contractual credit with no set term.
- Note: If there’s a continuing contract (like installment payments), each missed installment might be its own breach starting clock for that installment. But usually, for an accelerated loan or closed account, one period from default covers all. Georgia has some case law about multiple notes or installments ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2020/title-15/chapter-10/article-1/section-15-10-2?current=1#:~:text=,decided%20under%20former%20law)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2020/title-15/chapter-10/article-1/section-15-10-2?current=1#:~:text=Recovery%20on%20part%20of%20account,decided%20under%20former%20law)).

**Important**: Statute of limitations is an affirmative defense – if you file late and the defendant doesn’t raise it, the court might not dismiss automatically. Many Magistrate judges will still be mindful and might ask about dates. But if defendant doesn’t invoke the defense, it can be considered waived and they might lose the chance. That said, you cannot count on that – if it’s obviously time-barred, they’ll likely mention it. Defendants in credit card cases often get advice to mention SOL. So best not to knowingly file a stale claim hoping the other side won’t notice – that’s risky and arguably unethical.

**If uncertain**, err on side of earlier filing. If you’re past the limit, magistrate will likely dismiss your case with prejudice (meaning you can’t refile). If you truly have a reason it should be tolled, you’d have to argue it, which is complex in small claims. 

Thus, before filing, ensure your claim is within these periods. If one claim is partially outside, e.g. some invoices older than 4 years and some newer, you can only recover the ones within the limit.

If multiple cause of action apply (like a breach of contract and a fraud regarding same act), the safer route is the longest period that fits. But e.g. if contract limit passed but fraud discovered later still within 4 of discovery, maybe you could plead fraud to salvage it. That gets legally heavy for Magistrate, though.

**Real example:** Franklin County Magistrate FAQ explicitly lists: *“statute of limitations on open accounts, property damage and oral contracts is 4 years; for simple contracts in writing it is 6 years; for a contract under seal it is 20 years; for personal injury suits it is 2 years”* ([www.franklincountyga.gov](https://www.franklincountyga.gov/magistrate-court/pages/frequently-asked-questions#:~:text=match%20at%20L63%20Magistrate%20Court,Felony%20cases%20differ%20from%204)). This aligns with what we have outlined, confirming the typical periods.

**Tolling note for Magistrate**: Magistrate Court doesn’t handle tolling hearings or such, you’d raise any tolling as part of your argument if defendant moves to dismiss on SOL. Given informal nature, it’s better to be clearly within the period to avoid that fight.

## 6. Pre-filing Requirements  
Before filing a small claims case in Georgia, it’s wise (and sometimes legally required) to take certain **pre-filing steps**:

- **Demand Letter (Not legally required, but strongly recommended):** Georgia law does not require a general “demand letter” for most small claims, with a couple of exceptions for specific statutes (like the **FBPA 30-day letter** for consumer fraud, or **bad check 10-day notice**). However, it is **good practice** to send the person or business a written demand for payment before suing. A demand letter can:
  - potentially resolve the issue without court,
  - serve as evidence that you tried to settle amicably,
  - and in some cases is necessary to claim extra damages or fees (e.g. for attorneys fees under O.C.G.A. § 13-6-11, some argue a prior demand and refusal can show stubborn litigiousness).
  
  For example, if a friend hasn’t paid back your loan, a simple letter or even email saying, “Please pay by X date or I’ll consider legal action” might prompt payment. Many people settle when they realize you’re serious about going to court.
  
  **Specific instances required by statute:** 
    - **Consumer Protection (FBPA):** Before suing under the Georgia Fair Business Practices Act for a deceptive act, you **must send a 30-day written demand** to the prospective defendant outlining your claim and giving them opportunity to cure ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2020/title-10/chapter-1/article-15/part-2/section-10-1-399/#:~:text=2,relation%20to%20the%20injury%20actually)). Without it, your FBPA claim may be dismissed or at least you can’t get attorneys fees and treble damages. So if you plan to invoke the FBPA in your case (e.g. a car dealer fraud case), mailing that letter 30+ days before filing is required. The letter should identify you, describe the unfair act and injury, and state the specific monetary damages sought ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2020/title-10/chapter-1/article-15/part-2/section-10-1-399/#:~:text=2,relation%20to%20the%20injury%20actually)).
    - **Bad Checks:** To get the double-damage penalty, you must send a **10-day demand letter** by certified or overnight mail to the check maker after the check bounces, demanding they pay the amount plus any service charge (<= $30) within 10 days ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2020/title-13/chapter-6/section-13-6-15/#:~:text=1,liable%20to%20the%20payee%2C%20in)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2020/title-13/chapter-6/section-13-6-15/#:~:text=certified%20mail%2C%20or%20statutory%20overnight,be%20a%20rebuttable%20presumption%20that)). If they don’t, then you can sue for double. If you fail to send the letter, you can still sue for the base check amount as a debt, but not the statutory double damages. So always do that certified letter for NSF checks and keep proof of mailing and receipt (or that letter returned unclaimed – law allows first-class with affidavit too after trying certified).
    - **Contractual Attorney Fee Clause:** If you have a contract that grants attorney’s fees upon default (common in promissory notes or service contracts), Georgia law (O.C.G.A. § 13-1-11) says the debtor can avoid paying those fees if they pay the principal and interest within 10 days of receiving notice of your intent to enforce the attorney fee provision. So essentially you must send a letter after default saying “you owe $X plus an additional $Y (usually 15%) in attorney fees unless you pay within 10 days.” If they don’t pay, you can then claim the fees in your suit. This is a specific pre-suit notice to get contractual attorney fees. In small claims, few bother unless an attorney drafted the contract. But if you want that 15%, you need to send this letter and wait 10 days.
    - **Evictions:** For dispossessory (again separate summary process), residential landlords in Georgia often must demand possession or rent **before** filing – typically a landlord’s demand for possession (often a simple pay-or-quit notice) is recommended but not strictly required by statute except in certain circumstances (some older case law suggests demand is required unless lease waived it). Many leases have a clause waiving demand. But eviction is separate anyway (but if you try to do self-help or skip, the dispossessory might be thrown out – not relevant to small claims, but note for context).
    - **Late fees on consumer loans (GA Installment Loan Act)** etc. – rarely relevant in small claims unless you’re a lender.
    - **Vehicle repair shops**: Not exactly a “notice” but under GA Motor Vehicle Repair Act, if you intend to charge more than estimate, etc. you must get authorization – that’s pre-charge, not pre-suit. But if you’re the consumer, no pre-suit beyond normal demand needed.
    - **Labor/Employment**: Not required to demand wages, but practically, the Georgia Dept. of Labor or US DOL often expects you to have asked the employer. Many attorneys also send a wage demand letter citing potential FLSA claims before filing, sometimes prompting payment.
    - **Surety bonds or claims on bonds (like a contractor’s bond)**: possibly required to give notice to surety or claimant; not typical in small claims.

- **Mandatory Mediation or ADR:** Georgia Magistrate Courts can encourage or even require mediation in some cases. There’s no statewide requirement to mediate before filing, but some counties have pre-trial mediation programs. For instance, some Magistrate Courts automatically schedule a mediation session on the hearing date to see if parties can settle before seeing the judge. However, **before filing**, there’s no general requirement that you try mediation. It’s voluntary unless the court orders after filing. Still, if both parties are willing, you could attempt a community mediation center instead of court. Notably, in some counties (Cobb, for example) there are Magistrate mediation programs where on court day a mediator is present – not something you arrange pre-filing but you should be aware you might be mediated on the spot. If you settle at mediation, great. If not, then immediate trial.
  
  No law requires “notice period” to the other party except those specific demand letters. For landlord-tenant, beyond dispossessory (which requires a demand for possession), some states require a demand letter for security deposits (Georgia doesn’t require tenant to demand deposit back before suing, but it’s a good idea to request it in writing anyway to show landlord’s bad faith).
  
- **Notice Periods in Certain Industries:** 
   - For example, **new home construction defects** often by contract require giving the builder a chance to cure. GA has a Right to Repair Act (O.C.G.A. § 8-2-35 et seq.) requiring homeowners to give builders notice and opportunity to inspect and offer repairs for construction defects before suing. But those suits would be in State Court and likely above $15k, not magistrate. Still worth noting: if someone tries to sue a contractor for a small construction defect without following that law (which applies to residential construction), they could face dismissal. That law covers new home or renovation defect claims and requires a written notice 90 days before litigation detailing defects, then builder has time to respond with an offer. If you skip it, case can be stayed to allow it. Probably rare in small claims because such cases are usually bigger and go to higher courts. But a minor renovation job might arguably fall under it; be aware if so.
   - **Auto Lemon Law:** you must go through manufacturer’s repair attempts and arbitration as pre-filing steps, but again lemon law repurchase suits aren’t in small claims anyway.
   - **Homeowner Association disputes:** sometimes covenants require arbitration or internal dispute resolution first. If so, failing to do that could get your case dismissed. This is contract-specific. If you sue your HOA in small claims for $500 and the bylaws mandated mediation first, the HOA might move to dismiss based on that. A magistrate judge might or might not enforce that contractual requirement, but probably should if clear. So check any contract for required ADR clauses before filing.
   - **Credit Contracts:** Many consumer contracts (cell phone, credit card, etc.) have arbitration clauses waiving court. If such a clause exists and the defendant invokes it, the Magistrate must stay/dismiss in favor of arbitration according to Federal Arbitration Act. So though not a “pre-filing requirement” per se, be aware if the contract has that, the defendant can derail your suit by moving to compel arbitration. If you anticipate that, you might initiate the contract’s arbitration process rather than file in court. But arbitration might cost money or be burdensome, yet some companies have consumer-friendly programs. Up to you. But yes, if there’s a valid arbitration clause and the opponent doesn’t waive it, your small claim might not proceed. Some small claims courts in other states are exempt from FAA due to a small claims carve-out in the contract (some contracts allow small claims despite arbitration clause); see if contract says you can still go to small claims. If so, you’re fine.
  
- **Notice to Government or Corporations:** 
   - **Ante-litem notice** to municipal or county government we discussed in Sec. 2 (under “cannot sue”), but important here: If you intend to sue a city or county (e.g. car damage from a city truck), you must serve a written notice of claim within **6 months for city, 12 months for county** after the incident ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2020/title-36/chapter-33/section-36-33-5/#:~:text=first%20giving%20notice%20as%20provided,the%20governing%20authority%20for%20adjustment)). This is a strict prerequisite in Georgia (O.C.G.A. § 36-33-5 for cities ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2020/title-36/chapter-33/section-36-33-5/#:~:text=first%20giving%20notice%20as%20provided,the%20governing%20authority%20for%20adjustment)), § 36-11-1 for counties). It should be delivered to the governing authority (e.g. City of Atlanta – send to the Mayor or City Clerk; for counties, likely County Commission Chair or County Attorney). The notice must state the time, place, and extent of injury and negligence cause, and the specific amount of monetary damages being sought ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2020/title-36/chapter-33/section-36-33-5/#:~:text=first%20giving%20notice%20as%20provided,the%20governing%20authority%20for%20adjustment)) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2020/title-36/chapter-33/section-36-33-5/#:~:text=5,the%20case%20may%20be%2C%20by)). If you fail to do this timely, your lawsuit will be barred, even if filed within the statute of limitations. The claim letter is basically a pre-suit requirement. After you present it, the city has 30 days to consider (the law says they shall act within 30 days) ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2020/title-36/chapter-33/section-36-33-5/#:~:text=authority%20for%20adjustment,b%29%20of)), after which you can file suit. The statute of limitations is tolled during that consideration period ([law.justia.com](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2020/title-36/chapter-33/section-36-33-5/#:~:text=no%20sense%20be%20a%20bar,and%20the%20claimant%20litigates%20such)). So for example, you had an incident with a city on Jan 1, 2024, you must send notice by June 30, 2024; after sending, they have 30 days; then you can sue. If you didn’t send by June 30, even if you sue within 2 years, it will be dismissed.
   - **Suing State of GA or US:** these require special ante-litem notices or administrative claims (e.g. if you had a claim against State under Georgia Tort Claims Act, you must send notice to Department of Administrative Services within 12 months of loss; for a claim vs US (Federal Tort Claims Act), you must file administrative claim with the agency within 2 years). These processes are beyond small claims typically, as claims vs state must be filed in State or Superior Court after administrative denial, and vs US in U.S. District Court. So small claims won’t apply.
  
- **Contractual Cure or Notice provisions:** Some contracts themselves say “if one party believes the other is in breach, they shall give written notice and 30 days to cure before filing litigation.” If your contract has such a clause, you should comply before suing. If you ignore it, the defendant can argue the suit is premature. A Magistrate judge might enforce that or might consider notice given by the act of suing as substantial compliance. But safer to send a formal notice letter referencing that clause and allow the cure period. If time-sensitive, at least send notice and wait the required days, then file. Example: many home improvement contracts require written notice of defects and opportunity to fix. If you sue without giving them chance, court might say you jumped the gun.
  
- **Professional Malpractice pre-suit steps:** Not relevant in small claims due to complexity, but if it were, note med mal and some others require an expert affidavit filed with complaint (in State Court). Magistrate likely to dismiss such a claim anyway.
  
- **Check for Settlement or ADR resources:** Some counties might provide pre-filing mediation services through community mediation centers. It’s optional but can save time. Also, some industries have complaint processes (e.g. used car issues could go through the GA Used Car Lemon Law (but GA doesn’t have a used car lemon law, just new car), or contacting the GA Consumer Protection Division which sometimes mediates consumer complaints with businesses, possibly yielding a resolution without court).
  
**Summary of key recommended steps before suing:**
  - Write a clear **demand letter** to the potential defendant, giving them a short period (10-30 days) to pay or resolve the matter, and state that otherwise you intend to pursue legal action. Be polite but firm. Keep a copy and send it ideally by a method you can track (certified mail, or at least email with read receipt).
  - Fulfill any **statutory notice** (FBPA 30-day, bad check 10-day, city/county ante-litem, etc.) if applicable to your situation.
  - Review your contract for any **notice or cure requirement or arbitration clause** and act accordingly (send required notices, consider if arbitration must be pursued, etc.).
  - If the dispute might be resolved by a regulator or **complaint to an agency** (e.g. you might complain to GA Dept of Law Consumer Protection for certain scams – sometimes they mediate or take action), you can try that route before court. It might not yield money directly but could pressure the business.
  - For landlord/tenant deposit: not required, but a **courtesy demand** for the deposit summarizing why you think you’re owed can demonstrate landlord’s bad faith if they ignore it. If going for triple damages, showing you tried to resolve makes you look reasonable.
  - If claim is against someone in the military on active duty, consider the Soldiers and Sailors Civil Relief Act – you will have to address that (through an affidavit at filing about defendant’s military status) and they could get stay if on deployment. Sometimes contacting their military legal office can solve things without court. 

**No mandatory pre-suit mediation in GA small claims,** but voluntary is beneficial. 
**No requirement to use a collection agency or anything** – you can go straight to court if you want (though some businesses do one final collection attempt to show they tried everything).
 
**Final note:** Pre-filing steps can strengthen your case. For instance, a well-documented demand letter that is ignored demonstrates the defendant’s lack of response. If you show the judge the letter and certified mail receipt, it can implicitly support your narrative that the defendant left you no choice. Conversely, if you never asked for payment and immediately sued, the judge may ask, “Did you try to work this out?” It doesn’t bar your claim but might influence how the judge perceives the case’s necessity. Many Magistrates appreciate when litigants first attempted to resolve the matter. So it’s both strategic and, in some cases, legally mandated to do these pre-filing notifications.

## 7. Forms Required  
To start a small claims case in Georgia, you’ll need to prepare and file certain forms. The exact form names/numbers can vary by county (some counties have their own formats), but most follow a similar structure. Key forms and documents include:

- **Statement of Claim (Complaint):** This is the primary form that initiates the lawsuit. It is sometimes literally called “Statement of Claim” in Georgia Magistrate Courts. It serves as your **complaint** – you (the plaintiff) state who you are suing (the defendant), for how much, and why.  
  - **What to include:** The Statement of Claim requires: names and addresses of plaintiff(s) and defendant(s), the amount of money sought, a brief description of the claim (often a few lines like “Defendant failed to repay loan of $___ made on [date]” or “Breach of contract for repair services – paid $___, no work done”), and date of claim. You also sign it under oath, so it’s verified. It effectively acts as both complaint and your affidavit of the claim’s truth. ([www.gwinnettcourts.com](https://www.gwinnettcourts.com/magistrate/civil-filing#:~:text=,suitable%20age%20and%20discretion%20then))O.C.G.A. § 15-10-43 requires the plaintiff or their agent to sign and verify it under oath ([www.gwinnettcourts.com](https://www.gwinnettcourts.com/magistrate/civil-filing#:~:text=,shall%20be%20served%20on%20the)) ([www.gwinnettcourts.com](https://www.gwinnettcourts.com/magistrate/civil-filing#:~:text=technicalities,include%20the%20address%20at%20which)). 
  - **Form availability:** Many Magistrate Courts have fill-in-the-blank forms for the Statement of Claim. For example, Fulton County provides a PDF titled “Statement of Claim” ([magistratefulton.org](https://magistratefulton.org/175/Small-Claims#:~:text=The%20Plaintiff%20,with%20the%20reason%20for%20the)). DeKalb’s site lists “Small Claims Form” which likely is their statement of claim form ([dekalbcountymagistratecourt.com](https://dekalbcountymagistratecourt.com/civil-matters/small-claims/#:~:text=Small%20Claims%20,cashier%E2%80%99s%20check%20and%20other%20certified)). If not found online, the Magistrate Clerk’s office can give you one. Some courts also allow you to draft your own complaint form as long as it contains all required info (but using the official form is simpler because it ensures you include everything).
  - **Filing number or code:** There isn’t a statewide standardized form number (like one size for all counties). It’s not like “Form MAG-1” (Magistrate forms aren’t numbered statewide like that). The Judicial Council of Georgia provides some general forms but multiple counties customize their own. It will simply be labeled “Statement of Claim” or “Complaint.” For example, Cobb Magistrate’s form may look a bit different from Chatham’s, but all require the same info. 
  - If e-filing, you often upload a PDF of your Statement of Claim. Some e-filing portals might have an online questionnaire instead of needing to fill a PDF. 
  - **Notice of hearing and answer:** Many Statement of Claim forms double as the Summons or incorporate a Notice to Defendant portion. At the bottom or on the second page, it often says: “You are hereby summoned to answer within 30 days... a trial date will be set... if you fail to answer, judgment by default may be granted.” This means the same document, once filed, gets served to defendant and contains their instructions to respond. It’s unlike higher courts where you have a separate summons. In Magistrate Court, typically the clerk issues your Statement of Claim as the summons. (Though some counties still attach a Magistrate summons form).
  
- **Summons (if separate):** If the county uses a separate summons form, the clerk will prepare it. In most cases, the Statement of Claim itself is stamped and serves as notice. For example, some counties have a one-page Statement of Claim with an area the clerk fills in the court date or instructions to answer. If the clerk sets a trial date at filing, the form might list it. If not, they’ll mail notice later. Usually, the defendant gets served with the complaint and a notice of how to answer, either on the complaint or attached.
  - For purposes of forms you must file, focus on the Statement of Claim. The clerk will ensure a summons or notice of hearing goes out. 
  - If you are e-filing, the e-filing system may automatically generate a summons form. If filing by paper, the clerk might ask you to complete a top part and they’ll complete the rest. 
  - Notably, Uniform Magistrate Court Rule requires the claim to include a hearing notice or at least after answer, they’ll send hearing date. But initial service includes a standard notice about the 30-day answer period ([www.franklincountyga.gov](https://www.franklincountyga.gov/magistrate-court/pages/frequently-asked-questions#:~:text=Answering%20a%20Claim%20in%20Magistrate,Court)) that Franklin’s text references: “If a defendant fails to file an answer within thirty days... default” ([www.franklincountyga.gov](https://www.franklincountyga.gov/magistrate-court/pages/frequently-asked-questions#:~:text=If%20a%20civil%20case%20is,days%20after%20being%20served%20and)).
  
- **Verification/Affidavit:** On the Statement of Claim form, you will sign a verification (under penalty of perjury) that the facts are true. No separate affidavit required if it’s built into the form. But if you are an agent (like filing on behalf of a business or as property manager), you typically sign as agent and maybe attach a power of attorney or corporate resolution proving authority, though Magistrate often accepts the agent’s signature as long as they’re identified. 
  - e.g., if a corporation is plaintiff, an officer or authorized agent can sign. Some courts require an “Authorization of Agent” form (especially if a non-lawyer employee is representing a business). More on that in Special Rules for Business section, but basically, a corporation can file via an officer or employee (no lawyer needed). 
  - If you are an attorney (allowed in GA Magistrate), you might sign on plaintiff’s behalf because attorneys are officers of court (some courts still require client verify, but generally attorneys can sign).

## 7. Forms Required  
**Statement of Claim (Complaint):** To start a Georgia small claim, you file a **Statement of Claim** in Magistrate Court ([www.gwinnettcourts.com](https://www.gwinnettcourts.com/magistrate/civil-filing#:~:text=,suitable%20age%20and%20discretion%20then)). This is the simple one- or two-page **complaint form** where you identify the parties, state what you’re suing for, and verify the claim under oath. Every county Magistrate Court has a Statement of Claim form (sometimes called a *Small Claims Complaint*). You can usually obtain it from the Clerk’s Office or download it from the court’s website (for example, Fulton County provides a PDF “Statement of Claim” form on its Magistrate Court site ([magistratefulton.org](https://magistratefulton.org/175/Small-Claims#:~:text=The%20Plaintiff%20,with%20the%20reason%20for%20the))). The form will ask for:  

- **Plaintiff’s name and address:** Your full name (or business name, if a business is suing) and mailing address. Use an address where you can receive court notices. If you’re suing on behalf of a business as a non-attorney agent (e.g. you’re the owner or an employee), you’ll typically indicate your title or capacity (some courts may also require an authorization form – see below).  
- **Defendant’s name and address:** The correct legal name of each person or entity you’re suing and their street address for service. Be very careful here: if suing a business, use the exact registered name (e.g. “ABC, Inc.” rather than a trade name) and include the name of the owner (for a sole proprietorship) if needed. For a corporation/LLC, you can list the company name and serve its registered agent. If you’re unsure, look up the business on the Georgia Secretary of State’s **Corporations Division** site to get the proper name and registered agent address. An improper name can complicate collection later, so get it right (e.g., don’t sue “Joe’s Plumbing” if the legal entity is “Joe’s Plumbing, LLC” – instead sue **“Joe’s Plumbing, LLC”**, and you can mention d/b/a if relevant). Include a physical address for each defendant – a P.O. Box is not sufficient for personal service[^5]. If an exact street address isn’t known, you may need to find it (through online directories, utility records, etc.) before filing.  
- **Amount of claim:** The total dollar amount you are suing for. This should include any principal amount owed, plus any allowable interest or statutory penalties you intend to claim (but **do not** exceed $15,000 in the total). For example, “$4,500.00 plus court costs” or “$1,000 plus $300 interest.” Georgia’s form typically has a blank to fill in the dollar figure. If you’re asking for interest, you might either include a specific sum to date or say “plus interest as allowed by law” – but it’s better to be specific if you know the amount up to filing. Court costs (the filing and service fees) are generally added by the court if you win, so you can write “plus court costs” on the form.  
- **Statement of claim (reason):** A brief, concise description of **why** the defendant owes you money ([www.gwinnettcourts.com](https://www.gwinnettcourts.com/magistrate/civil-filing#:~:text=,suitable%20age%20and%20discretion%20then)). This can be very short – Magistrate Courts expect a “plain language” explanation, not legalese. One or two sentences is often enough. For example: “Defendant breached a lease agreement by failing to pay $2,000 rent for June–July 2026” or “Money had and received – defendant borrowed $500 on March 1, 2026 and has not repaid it despite due date of April 1, 2026” or “Negligence in auto accident on 5/10/2025 – seeking $4,300 for vehicle damage caused by defendant.” Be sure to include **dates**, **locations**, and specific items as relevant. If it’s a contract, mention the contract date and breach date. If it’s a tort, mention the date of injury and what happened. The goal is to give the defendant and judge a clear idea of the nature of the dispute in a few lines. You do *not* need to cite code sections or argue law in this form. Simplicity and clarity are key (the form is “free from technicalities” by law ([www.gwinnettcourts.com](https://www.gwinnettcourts.com/magistrate/civil-filing#:~:text=,suitable%20age%20and%20discretion%20then))).  
- **Verification:** At the bottom of the Statement of Claim, you (the plaintiff) must sign it **under oath** in front of a notary public or the court clerk ([www.gwinnettcourts.com](https://www.gwinnettcourts.com/magistrate/civil-filing#:~:text=,suitable%20age%20and%20discretion%20then)) ([www.gwinnettcourts.com](https://www.gwinnettcourts.com/magistrate/civil-filing#:~:text=technicalities,include%20the%20address%20at%20which)). By signing, you swear the facts in the claim are true to the best of your knowledge. This verification is required by O.C.G.A. § 15-10-43, and it essentially turns your complaint into an affidavit ([www.gwinnettcourts.com](https://www.gwinnettcourts.com/magistrate/civil-filing#:~:text=,suitable%20age%20and%20discretion%20then)). If you mail in the form, you’ll need it notarized before sending. If you file in person, many Clerk’s offices will notarize or have you swear before a deputy clerk at the counter (bring a photo ID). Remember that making false statements on a verified claim is perjury – so be truthful.  

The clerk will assign a **case number** and fill in the court information on the form when you file. In many counties, the Statement of Claim also serves as the official **summons**, meaning it notifies the defendant of the lawsuit and their deadline to respond. It will contain language directed to the defendant, for example: “You are hereby notified that a claim has been filed against you in the Magistrate Court of __ County. You are required to file an answer within 30 days after service of this claim.” ([www.franklincountyga.gov](https://www.franklincountyga.gov/magistrate-court/pages/frequently-asked-questions#:~:text=If%20a%20civil%20case%20is,days%20after%20being%20served%20and)) Some courts include the **hearing date** on the form or will mail a notice of hearing later. (Procedures vary: in some counties, the clerk sets a court date as soon as the defendant’s answer is filed; in others, a hearing date might be pre-assigned and written on the summons.) In any case, once you file the Statement of Claim, the Clerk’s office will take care of preparing a summons or using your form as a summons, and arranging service.  

**Where to get the forms:** Many Magistrate Courts post fillable forms on their official websites (often under “Forms” or “Small Claims”). For instance, the **Georgia Judicial Council** website and some county sites have generic Magistrate Court forms. It’s a good idea to use your county’s version if available, as it may have that court’s specific address and instructions. If online access is hard, you can pick up printed forms at the Magistrate Court clerk’s office for free or a small copy fee. (Some libraries or legal aid offices also stock Magistrate Court packets.)  

**Filing the Statement of Claim:** Once you’ve filled it out and notarized it, you will submit it to the Magistrate Court clerk (in person, by mail, or via e-filing if offered) along with the filing fee (see Filing Fees section above). If you have exhibits (like a copy of a contract, promissory note, or invoice), you are **not required** to attach them to the complaint – the rules don’t require attaching evidence – but you *may* attach copies if you think it helps clarify the claim. For example, some plaintiffs attach a copy of a dishonored check or a written contract as a reference. The defendant will see those attachments when served. It’s not mandatory (you can simply bring the documents to the hearing instead), but attaching key documents can sometimes prompt a quicker settlement. **Do not attach overly lengthy materials**; keep it relevant if you include anything.  

When filing, prepare at least **one original and one copy** of the Statement of Claim for each defendant, plus a copy for your records. The clerk will keep the original and serve the copies on the defendant(s). If you file electronically, you’ll upload the form and the system will generate the necessary copies.  

**Other Forms and Documents:**

- **Civil Case Filing Information Form:** Georgia typically requires a Civil Case Filing Information Form in State or Superior Court, but **Magistrate Courts do not require** the statewide civil case filing form. Instead, the Statement of Claim itself gathers the needed info. (A few Magistrate Courts might have a cover sheet, but most don’t for small claims.) Always ask the clerk if any cover sheet is needed – in Magistrate it’s usually not.  

- **Authorization of Agent (for businesses):** If the plaintiff is a business represented by a non-lawyer agent (owner, officer, or employee), some courts require a simple authorization. For example, a corporation might file a one-page **Corporate Authorization** stating “John Doe is authorized to represent XYZ Corp. in this case.” Many courts don’t ask for it unless a question arises, since O.C.G.A. § 15-10-43 allows an agent to file and sign the claim ([www.gwinnettcourts.com](https://www.gwinnettcourts.com/magistrate/civil-filing#:~:text=,suitable%20age%20and%20discretion%20then)). But to be safe, you can attach or file a letter on company letterhead or a corporate resolution naming the agent. Some counties (like DeKalb in some instances) have an “Affidavit of Authority” form for this purpose. Check your local Magistrate rules – the Clerk can tell you if they need a separate authorization. If you’re a sole proprietor suing in your own name (e.g. Joe Smith d/b/a Joe’s Plumbing), no separate form is needed beyond showing you are the owner if questioned.  

- **Military Service Affidavit (for default judgments):** This isn’t filed at the outset, but if the defendant fails to answer and you seek a default judgment, federal law (Servicemembers Civil Relief Act) requires you to provide an affidavit stating whether or not the defendant is in active military service. Courts have a short form for this – often called a **Non-Military Affidavit**. You can obtain this form from the clerk or ahead of time (some courts include it in the initial filing packet). It asks how you determined their military status (for example, by checking the Department of Defense’s online SCRA database or personal knowledge). If the defendant is in active service, the court will not enter a default until certain protections are addressed ([www.franklincountyga.gov](https://www.franklincountyga.gov/magistrate-court/pages/frequently-asked-questions#:~:text=Answering%20a%20Claim%20in%20Magistrate,Court)). If not, the affidavit allows the case to proceed to default judgment. Keep in mind to prepare this form if you end up in a default situation. (You do not need it when you file the case, only later if no answer is received – we mention it here so you’re aware.)

- **Answer Form (for defendant):** When the defendant is served, they will need to file an **Answer** within 30 days. Many Magistrate Courts provide a pre-printed Answer form (sometimes on the back of the summons or as a separate form) for the defendant’s convenience. As the plaintiff, you don’t file the answer, but be aware the summons will inform the defendant how to answer. For instance, some summons have a tear-off portion titled “Answer” where the defendant can write a brief response and mail or hand-deliver it to the court. The defendant can also draft their own answer letter. This is not your responsibility, but knowing the process helps; once the answer is filed, the court will set a hearing. If the defendant fails to answer, you may move for default judgment after the 30+15 day period (there’s usually a **default judgment form or motion** the court may require – often, Magistrates will schedule a default hearing or simply enter judgment if evidence is straightforward). The **motion for default judgment** might not have a standardized form in all counties; some courts just request a letter or an oral motion at a default hearing. Check with the clerk on how defaults are handled (some require you to put up evidence at a default hearing especially if unliquidated damages).

- **Counterclaim (Defendant’s Claim) Form:** If the defendant wants to file a counterclaim against you, they typically can use the same Statement of Claim form format (but captioned as a counterclaim, listing themselves as “counter-claimant”). Some courts have an actual **Counterclaim form** or allow the defendant to write the counterclaim within their Answer. For example, the answer form might have a section: “□ I assert a counterclaim in the amount of $___ for the following reasons: ___.” If a formal separate form is needed, the clerk can provide it. As a plaintiff, you don’t file the counterclaim, but if you receive one, you should answer it at trial (Magistrate doesn’t require a written answer to a counterclaim – your appearance at the hearing and contesting it serves as your response).

- **Dismissal or Settlement Forms:** If you settle the case before the hearing or decide to dismiss it, there’s a simple **Dismissal form** (Voluntary Dismissal) you can file. Magistrate Courts often have a one-page form where you fill in case info and check “dismissed with prejudice” or “without prejudice.” *Without prejudice* means you could refile later (useful if you’re dismissing to correct a venue or name issue), *with prejudice* means it’s permanently resolved. If you and the defendant agree to a settlement that you want entered as a judgment, you can ask the court for a **Consent Judgment form**. The clerk may have a template where both parties sign off on an agreed amount and payment terms, and the judge will sign it to make it an official judgment. This often happens at mediation or just before trial. These forms aren’t needed at filing, but it’s good to know they exist. 

**Note:** The Magistrate Court clerks are generally very helpful with providing and explaining forms (they can’t give legal *advice*, but they can tell you which blanks to fill). By Georgia law, the clerk or judge *may assist individuals in preparing the statement of claim or other documents* if asked ([www.gwinnettcourts.com](https://www.gwinnettcourts.com/magistrate/civil-filing#:~:text=,suitable%20age%20and%20discretion%20then)). Take advantage of that if needed – Magistrate Courts strive to be user-friendly. Just ensure all names and amounts are correct on your forms, and that you sign where required. 

Once your Statement of Claim is filed and the fees paid, the clerk will issue the **summons** and forward the papers for service on the defendant (via sheriff, marshal, or approved process server). Keep a copy of everything you file for your own records. Now your case is officially underway, and you can move on to serving the defendant (next section).
