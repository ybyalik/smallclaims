## 1. Jurisdictional limits

- **Most you can sue for:** $10,000 for individuals; $10,000 for businesses [S1, S10].
- **Splitting a claim:** If your claim exceeds the $10,000 limit, you may choose to waive the excess and file in small claims court, or file in the Civil Division for the full amount. Splitting one dispute into multiple claims to fit under the cap is not permitted. [S1]
- **What's excluded from the cap:** interest and court costs are excluded [S1].

## 2. What you can sue for

### Contracts and money owed

Vermont allows you to file claims for breaches of contract and money owed up to $10,000. The statute of limitations varies depending on the type of contract.

- **Breach of a written contract** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A contractor signed a $4,000 contract to remodel your kitchen, took the deposit, and never finished.
  - **Statute:** 12 V.S.A. § 511. 6-year deadline from breach. Bring the signed contract, payment proof, and any correspondence showing the breach. [S4]
- **Breach of an oral contract** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You agreed verbally to lend a friend $2,000, which they promised to repay in six months, but they haven't.
  - **Statute:** 12 V.S.A. § 511. 6-year deadline from breach. Gather any evidence of the agreement, such as emails or witness statements. [S4]
- **Unpaid personal loan** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You lent $3,000 to a colleague who agreed to repay in installments but stopped after the first payment.
  - **Statute:** 12 V.S.A. § 511. 6-year deadline from breach. Provide documentation of the loan agreement and any repayment history. [S4]
- **Default on a promissory note** — eligible.
  - **Example:** A borrower signed a promissory note for $5,000 but failed to make any payments.
  - **Statute:** 12 V.S.A. § 511. 6-year deadline from breach. Present the original promissory note and records of missed payments. [S4]
- **Unpaid invoice for services rendered** — eligible.
  - **Example:** You provided consulting services worth $4,500, invoiced the client, but haven't received payment.
  - **Statute:** 12 V.S.A. § 511. 6-year deadline from breach. Submit the invoice, service agreement, and any communication regarding payment. [S4]

- **Unpaid rent by tenant** — eligible.

  - **Statute:** 12 V.S.A. § 511. 6-year deadline from breach. Present the lease agreement and records of missed payments. [S4]

- **Defamation** — Claims for slander or libel are excluded from small claims court jurisdiction. Where to go instead: Superior Court Civil Division. [S1]

- **Probate matters** — Disputes involving wills, estates, and trusts are outside the jurisdiction of small claims court. Where to go instead: Probate Division of the Superior Court. [unverified]

- **Class actions** — Class action lawsuits are beyond the procedural scope of small claims court. Where to go instead: Superior Court Civil Division. [unverified]
- **Injunctions or specific performance** — Small claims court can only award monetary damages and cannot order someone to do or stop doing something. Where to go instead: Superior Court Civil Division. [unverified]

## 14. Post-judgment collection

- **Payment deadline expectation:** 30 days.
- **Post-judgment interest rate:** 12% — 12 V.S.A. § 2903.

### Wage garnishment

Have a portion of debtor's wages withheld until the judgment is paid.

File a motion for trustee process against earnings; if granted, serve the employer, who will withhold a portion of the debtor's wages and remit to the court.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees  
**Effectiveness:** Most effective when debtor is W-2 employed at a stable job.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- 75% of disposable earnings (federal CCPA floor)

### Bank levy

Seize funds from debtor's bank account to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, serve it on the bank, which will freeze and remit funds up to the judgment amount.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor maintains sufficient funds in bank accounts.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Certain amounts may be exempt under state law

### Property lien

Place a lien on debtor's real property to secure the judgment.

Record the judgment with the town clerk where the property is located; the lien must be satisfied before the property can be sold or refinanced.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50  
**Effectiveness:** Effective for securing payment, especially if debtor plans to sell property.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Homestead exemption may apply

### Writ of execution

Authorize seizure of debtor's non-exempt property to satisfy the judgment.

Obtain a writ of execution from the court, which allows the sheriff to seize and sell debtor's non-exempt property.

**Estimated cost:** $30-50 plus sheriff fees  
**Effectiveness:** Effective if debtor owns valuable non-exempt property.

**What's protected (exemptions):**
- Certain personal property may be exempt under state law

- **Wage garnishment cap:** 25% of disposable earnings.
- **Judgment lifespan:** 8 years; renewal: File a motion to renew the judgment before it expires; if granted, the judgment is extended for another 8 years.

- **Multiple creditors priority:** Judgment liens are prioritized based on the date of recording; earlier liens have higher priority.

## 17. Common pitfalls and dismissal reasons

### Wrong defendant naming

**What goes wrong:** If you sue 'John's Garage' but the legal entity is 'JG Auto Repair LLC', the judgment may be unenforceable against the LLC's assets.

**How to avoid it:** Search the Secretary of State business records before filing and use the exact registered name; for sole proprietors, use the individual's full legal name. [unverified]

### Missed statute of limitations

**What goes wrong:** Filing one day after the deadline gets the case dismissed with prejudice.

**How to avoid it:** Check the statute of limitations for your specific claim type before filing; for fraud, the discovery rule applies but be prepared to prove when you reasonably could have discovered it. [unverified]

### Improper service of process

**What goes wrong:** If the defendant isn't properly served, the case may be dismissed or delayed.

**How to avoid it:** Follow Vermont's rules for service of process carefully; consider using the sheriff or a professional process server. [unverified]

### Filing in the wrong venue

**What goes wrong:** The case may be transferred or dismissed, causing delays and additional costs.

**How to avoid it:** File in the county where the defendant lives or has a business, or where the cause of action arose. [unverified]

### Failing to collect judgment

**What goes wrong:** Winning a judgment doesn't guarantee payment; collection can be challenging.

**How to avoid it:** Be prepared to take post-judgment collection actions like wage garnishment or bank levies. [unverified]

### Overlooking exemptions in collection

**What goes wrong:** Certain debtor assets are protected from collection, limiting recovery options.

**How to avoid it:** Understand Vermont's exemption laws to assess the feasibility of collecting on a judgment. [unverified]

## 19. FAQs

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**
A: No, you are not required to have a lawyer in Vermont small claims court. The process is designed to be simple and accessible for individuals representing themselves. However, you may choose to hire an attorney if you prefer.

**Q: How long does it take?**
A: The timeline varies, but typically, a small claims case in Vermont is resolved within 30 to 60 days from filing to judgment. Factors such as court scheduling and whether the defendant contests the claim can affect the duration.

**Q: What's the maximum I can sue for?**
A: In Vermont, you can sue for up to $10,000 in small claims court. For claims involving medical debt or consumer credit transactions, the limit is $5,000.

**Q: What happens if the defendant doesn't show up?**
A: If the defendant fails to appear in court after being properly served, the judge may issue a default judgment in your favor. You may still need to provide evidence to support your claim before the judgment is granted.

**Q: Can I appeal?**
A: Yes, either party can appeal a small claims judgment to the Civil Division of the Superior Court. The appeal must be filed within 30 days of the judgment, and a filing fee applies.

**Q: How long is a judgment good for?**
A: In Vermont, a small claims judgment is valid for 8 years. If the judgment is not satisfied within that period, you may need to take additional legal steps to renew it.
